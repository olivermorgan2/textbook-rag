## Introduction to Emerging Global Health Challenges

### The Changing Global Health Landscape

Global health faces unprecedented transformation driven by converging environmental, technological, demographic, and social forces. The twenty-first century presents challenges fundamentally different from those addressed by twentieth-century public health infrastructure, requiring new conceptual frameworks, governance mechanisms, and intervention strategies (1). Traditional approaches focused on individual diseases or risk factors prove insufficient when confronting interconnected challenges spanning climate change, technological disruption, demographic transition, and evolving infectious disease threats (2).

The complexity of contemporary challenges defies simple solutions. Climate change simultaneously affects vector-borne disease distribution, food security, water availability, and extreme weather mortality. Demographic transitions create aging populations requiring fundamentally different health services while reducing the workforce available to provide care. Technological advances offer unprecedented diagnostic and therapeutic capabilities while raising profound questions about equity, privacy, and human autonomy (3). These challenges demand integrative approaches recognizing that health outcomes emerge from complex interactions among biological, environmental, social, and political determinants.

### Systems Thinking and Complexity

Systems thinking provides essential frameworks for understanding emerging health challenges characterized by nonlinear dynamics, feedback loops, and emergent properties (4). Health systems function as complex adaptive systems where interventions produce unexpected consequences and small changes can trigger cascading effects. The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated how infectious disease emergence, globalized travel, health system capacity, political governance, and social behavior interact in ways impossible to predict from analyzing components individually (5).

Complexity science recognizes that many global health challenges cannot be solved definitively but must be continuously managed through adaptive responses. Antimicrobial resistance exemplifies this dynamic: interventions addressing one aspect may create selection pressures producing new resistance patterns. Climate adaptation requires ongoing adjustment as climate impacts evolve and interact with changing social conditions. Effective responses embrace uncertainty and build adaptive capacity rather than seeking permanent solutions to inherently dynamic challenges (6).

### Anticipatory Governance Frameworks

Anticipatory governance represents a shift from reactive policy-making to proactive identification of emerging challenges and development of response capacity before crises materialize (7). This approach combines foresight methods including horizon scanning, scenario planning, and early warning systems with governance mechanisms enabling rapid, coordinated responses. The concept emerged from recognition that traditional governance processes, developed for relatively stable conditions, prove inadequate for rapidly evolving challenges like emerging technologies or accelerating climate change (8).

Effective anticipatory governance requires institutional capacity for systematic future analysis, mechanisms for translating foresight into policy, and flexibility to adapt as circumstances evolve. Countries implementing robust anticipatory governance for health have demonstrated improved pandemic preparedness, more effective climate adaptation, and better integration of emerging technologies (9). However, anticipatory governance faces challenges including political short-termism, uncertainty about future developments, and difficulty mobilizing action for non-immediate threats.

## Planetary Health and Environmental Sustainability

### Conceptual Framework for Planetary Health

Planetary health emerged as a conceptual framework recognizing that human health and human civilization depend on flourishing natural systems that are being degraded by human activities (10). The Rockefeller Foundation-Lancet Commission on Planetary Health established this framework, emphasizing that unprecedented human development gains have come at a previously unrecognized cost to Earth's natural systems. Human health cannot be sustained in the long term without addressing the underlying environmental determinants threatening the stability of Earth systems (11).

The framework positions human health within ecological context, recognizing that ecosystem services including clean air, water purification, food production, climate regulation, and disease control underpin all health outcomes. Unlike traditional environmental health focused on specific pollutant-disease relationships, planetary health addresses systemic environmental changes threatening the conditions that enabled human civilization to develop and flourish (12). This perspective demands fundamental reconsideration of development models, economic systems, and consumption patterns driving environmental degradation.

### Planetary Boundaries and Health Implications

The planetary boundaries framework identifies nine Earth system processes with scientifically established thresholds that should not be crossed to maintain a safe operating space for humanity (13). These boundaries include climate change, biosphere integrity, land system change, freshwater use, biogeochemical flows, ocean acidification, atmospheric aerosol loading, stratospheric ozone depletion, and novel entities. Current evidence indicates that at least four boundaries have been transgressed, with potentially severe implications for human health (14).

**Climate change** represents the most extensively studied boundary with direct health implications including heat-related mortality, extreme weather events, food insecurity, and altered infectious disease patterns. **Biosphere integrity**, encompassing biodiversity loss, threatens food security, pharmaceutical discovery, and ecosystem services supporting health. **Biogeochemical flows** disruption, particularly nitrogen and phosphorus cycles, affects water quality, air pollution, and food safety. **Land system change** through deforestation increases zoonotic disease emergence risk while reducing carbon sequestration capacity (15).

The interconnected nature of planetary boundaries means that transgressing one boundary may trigger effects cascading across others. For example, climate change accelerates biodiversity loss, which reduces ecosystem resilience to climate impacts, creating reinforcing feedback loops with compounding health consequences. Addressing planetary health challenges thus requires integrated strategies considering boundary interactions rather than addressing individual boundaries in isolation (16).

### Biodiversity Loss and Human Health

Biodiversity loss constitutes an emerging health crisis of potentially catastrophic proportions. Current extinction rates exceed background rates by orders of magnitude, with one million species facing extinction risk (17). Beyond intrinsic ecological value, biodiversity loss threatens human health through multiple pathways including reduced food system resilience, loss of potential pharmaceuticals, degraded ecosystem services, and increased infectious disease emergence risk.

The relationship between biodiversity and infectious disease emergence illustrates complex ecological dynamics. Biodiversity loss may increase disease risk through the dilution effect, whereby diverse host communities reduce pathogen transmission by including species less competent as disease reservoirs (18). Simultaneously, habitat destruction and wildlife trade increase human-animal contact, facilitating zoonotic spillover events. An estimated 60% of emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic in origin, with risk concentrated in tropical regions experiencing rapid land use change (19).

### Sustainable Healthcare Systems

Healthcare systems paradoxically contribute to the environmental degradation threatening health. The healthcare sector generates approximately 4.4% of global net carbon emissions, exceeding the aviation industry's contribution (20). If healthcare were a country, it would rank as the fifth-largest emitter globally. Additional environmental impacts include pharmaceutical pollution of waterways, medical waste generation, and resource-intensive supply chains (21).

Sustainable healthcare addresses this contradiction through systematic reduction of healthcare's environmental footprint while maintaining or improving care quality. Strategies include transitioning to renewable energy, implementing circular economy principles for medical supplies, redesigning clinical pathways to reduce unnecessary interventions, and shifting care to lower-carbon settings (22). Importantly, many sustainability interventions deliver co-benefits including cost savings, improved resilience, and better health outcomes through prevention emphasis.

## Climate Change and Health

### Direct Health Impacts of Climate Change

Climate change produces direct health impacts through increasing ambient temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and more frequent extreme weather events. Heat-related mortality represents the most immediate direct impact, with heat waves causing significant excess mortality particularly among elderly populations, outdoor workers, and those with preexisting conditions (23). The 2003 European heat wave caused over 70,000 excess deaths, demonstrating vulnerability of even high-income health systems to extreme heat (24).

Extreme weather events including floods, storms, droughts, and wildfires cause direct mortality and morbidity while disrupting health services. The frequency and intensity of extreme events have increased measurably, with attribution studies demonstrating climate change contribution to specific events (25). Infrastructure damage from extreme weather compounds immediate casualties by disrupting healthcare access, displacing populations, and creating conditions favorable for disease outbreaks. Mental health impacts of extreme weather, including post-traumatic stress disorder, anxiety, and depression, affect far larger populations than physical injuries (26).

### Indirect and Ecosystem-Mediated Effects

Indirect climate impacts operate through ecosystem changes affecting food security, water availability, air quality, and livelihood stability. Food system impacts include reduced crop yields, changing nutritional content of staple foods, and disrupted food distribution. The relationship between climate and nutrition operates through multiple pathways: direct effects of temperature and precipitation on crop production, elevated CO2 effects on crop nutrient content, and extreme weather disruption of food systems (27).

Water security faces compounding threats from changing precipitation patterns, glacial melt affecting river flows, sea level rise contaminating coastal aquifers, and increased drought frequency. Water scarcity affects health through inadequate drinking water, compromised sanitation, reduced agricultural production, and conflict over resources (28). Air quality degradation from increased wildfire frequency, changed atmospheric chemistry affecting ozone formation, and dust storm intensification adds respiratory disease burden to climate impacts.

### Climate-Sensitive Infectious Diseases

Climate change alters the geographic distribution, seasonality, and transmission intensity of climate-sensitive infectious diseases. Vector-borne diseases including malaria, dengue, Zika, and Lyme disease show sensitivity to temperature and precipitation affecting vector survival, reproduction, and biting behavior (29). Modeling studies project substantial expansion of suitable transmission zones for major vector-borne diseases, potentially exposing billions of additional people to infection risk (30).

Waterborne diseases respond to flooding events, which overwhelm water treatment systems and contaminate water supplies, and to drought, which concentrates pathogens in reduced water sources. Climate effects on food safety include temperature impacts on pathogen proliferation and extreme weather disruption of food cold chains (31). The complex interactions between climate variables, ecosystem changes, and human behavior make precise prediction of disease impacts challenging, but the direction of change consistently suggests increased infectious disease burden.

### Climate Change Adaptation in Health Systems

Climate adaptation in health systems encompasses vulnerability assessment, preparedness planning, infrastructure resilience, and service delivery modifications. Effective adaptation requires understanding local climate projections and health vulnerabilities, which vary substantially across geographic and social contexts (32). Coastal communities face different challenges than landlocked regions; urban heat islands differ from rural temperature patterns; and vulnerability concentrates among populations with least adaptive capacity including the poor, elderly, and marginalized.

Health system adaptation strategies include climate-resilient infrastructure design, early warning systems for climate-sensitive health threats, health workforce training for climate-related conditions, and integration of climate considerations into health planning (33). The World Health Organization's Operational Framework for Building Climate Resilient Health Systems provides structured guidance for national adaptation planning, emphasizing leadership, health workforce, vulnerability assessment, and financing components (34). Successful adaptation requires sustained political commitment and integration across health and non-health sectors.

## Technological Disruption in Health

### Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning

Artificial intelligence and machine learning applications in healthcare have expanded rapidly, demonstrating capability matching or exceeding human performance for specific diagnostic tasks (35). Applications span medical imaging interpretation, disease prediction, drug discovery, clinical decision support, and health system management. AI-enabled diagnostics show particular promise for settings with specialist shortages, potentially democratizing access to expert-level interpretation (36).

However, AI deployment raises significant challenges including algorithmic bias, data privacy, accountability for errors, and equity implications. AI systems trained on biased data may perpetuate or amplify existing disparities, as demonstrated in studies finding algorithms underestimating illness severity for Black patients (37). The opacity of some machine learning approaches creates accountability challenges when AI-informed decisions produce adverse outcomes. Furthermore, AI benefits may concentrate in well-resourced settings with high-quality digital infrastructure, potentially widening rather than narrowing health inequities (38).

### Genomics and Precision Medicine

Genomic technologies have transformed from research tools to clinical applications offering personalized prevention, diagnosis, and treatment. Declining sequencing costs have made genome-wide analysis increasingly accessible, enabling identification of disease risk, pharmacogenomic optimization of drug therapy, and targeted cancer treatments (39). Population genomics initiatives in multiple countries are building databases supporting research and clinical translation.

Precision medicine promises more effective interventions tailored to individual genetic profiles, but realizing this promise equitably presents challenges. Genetic databases overrepresent populations of European ancestry, limiting applicability of findings to other populations. The cost and infrastructure requirements of genomic medicine risk creating two-tier systems where precision approaches benefit only wealthy populations (40). Ethical considerations include genetic privacy, potential for discrimination based on genetic information, and implications of genetic knowledge for family members who may not desire such information.

### Digital Health Transformation

Digital health encompasses mobile health applications, telemedicine, electronic health records, wearable devices, and digital therapeutics. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated digital health adoption by years, demonstrating feasibility of remote care delivery while revealing digital divides limiting access for disadvantaged populations (41). Digital health offers potential for extending healthcare access, improving efficiency, enabling patient engagement, and generating real-world evidence.

Implementation challenges include interoperability between systems, data quality and standardization, digital literacy requirements, and regulatory frameworks struggling to keep pace with innovation (42). Privacy and security concerns intensify as health data becomes more comprehensive and valuable. The potential for digital health to either reduce or exacerbate health inequities depends critically on implementation approaches ensuring equitable access and addressing digital determinants of health.

### Governance of Health Technologies

Governing rapidly evolving health technologies requires balancing innovation enablement with protection against potential harms. Traditional regulatory approaches designed for pharmaceuticals and medical devices prove poorly suited to AI systems that learn continuously, digital applications updated frequently, and technologies combining multiple regulated categories (43). Adaptive regulatory frameworks emphasizing continuous monitoring and iterative improvement show promise for technologies where pre-market assessment cannot fully characterize real-world performance.

International harmonization of health technology governance gains importance as technologies and data flow across borders. Efforts including the International Medical Device Regulators Forum aim to align regulatory approaches, but significant variation persists (44). Governance must address not only safety and efficacy but also ethical considerations including consent, equity, transparency, and accountability. Meaningful stakeholder engagement, including patient and public involvement in governance processes, strengthens legitimacy and responsiveness of regulatory frameworks.

## Demographic Transitions and Health

### Global Aging and Health System Implications

Population aging represents one of the most significant demographic transformations in human history. The global population aged 65 and over is projected to double from 761 million in 2021 to 1.6 billion by 2050, with the most rapid increases in low- and middle-income countries (45). This demographic shift fundamentally alters disease burden, healthcare demand, and health system requirements.

Aging populations experience higher prevalence of non-communicable diseases, multimorbidity, and functional limitations requiring ongoing care rather than episodic treatment. Health systems designed around acute, hospital-centric models require transformation toward integrated, community-based approaches addressing chronic conditions and maintaining functional independence (46). The economic implications are substantial: aging increases healthcare expenditure while reducing the working-age population financing health systems through taxation and insurance contributions.

### Migration and Health

International migration has reached unprecedented levels, with over 280 million international migrants and over 100 million forcibly displaced persons globally (47). Migration and health interactions are complex: migration patterns are shaped partly by health considerations, while migration profoundly affects health outcomes of migrants, origin communities, and destination populations.

The healthy migrant effect, whereby migrants tend to be healthier than destination populations upon arrival, attenuates over time as health advantages diminish (48). Migrants face specific health challenges including occupational hazards in informal employment, barriers to healthcare access, mental health impacts of displacement and discrimination, and continuity of care disruption. Healthcare systems in destination countries require cultural competence, language access, and understanding of health conditions prevalent in origin regions but rare locally.

### Urbanization and Health Challenges

Urban population has grown from 30% of global population in 1950 to over 55% today, projected to reach 68% by 2050 (49). Urbanization creates health opportunities through improved access to services, economic opportunities, and infrastructure. However, rapid unplanned urbanization, particularly in low- and middle-income countries, produces informal settlements lacking adequate housing, water, sanitation, and health services.

Urban health challenges include air pollution, heat island effects, road traffic injuries, physical inactivity, mental health impacts of crowding and social fragmentation, and infectious disease transmission facilitated by population density (50). Healthy city approaches integrate health considerations into urban planning, transportation, housing, and environmental policy. The New Urban Agenda and Sustainable Development Goal 11 provide frameworks for sustainable urban development promoting health equity.

### Changing Family Structures and Care Systems

Traditional family-based care systems face pressure from demographic and social change including declining fertility, increased female workforce participation, urbanization dispersing family networks, and changing family formation patterns (51). These trends reduce availability of informal caregivers precisely as aging populations increase care needs.

Health systems must adapt to reduced informal care capacity through formal long-term care systems, community-based services, and technology-assisted care. Countries at different stages of demographic transition can learn from those further advanced: Japan's experience with super-aged society, European long-term care reforms, and emerging innovations in middle-income countries all offer relevant lessons (52). Sustainable care systems require balancing formal and informal care, supporting family caregivers, and creating financing mechanisms for long-term care.

## Emerging Infectious Disease Threats

### Pandemic Risk and Preparedness

The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated catastrophic consequences of inadequate pandemic preparedness despite decades of warnings from public health experts. The pandemic caused millions of deaths, unprecedented economic disruption, and profound social impacts globally (53). Post-pandemic analysis reveals persistent preparedness gaps including surveillance limitations, surge capacity constraints, supply chain vulnerabilities, and coordination failures at national and international levels.

Strengthening pandemic preparedness requires sustained investment in core public health infrastructure, health system surge capacity, stockpiling of essential supplies, and international cooperation mechanisms. The proposed pandemic treaty under negotiation at WHO aims to strengthen the international framework, though substantial disagreements persist regarding financing, equity, and sovereignty implications (54). Modeling studies suggest the economic benefits of preparedness investments substantially exceed costs, yet political and fiscal constraints continue to limit preparedness funding.

### Antimicrobial Resistance as Global Threat

Antimicrobial resistance represents an existential threat to modern medicine, potentially rendering common infections untreatable and routine surgeries dangerous. An estimated 1.27 million deaths were directly attributable to bacterial antimicrobial resistance in 2019, with 4.95 million deaths associated with resistant infections (55). Without effective action, projections suggest antimicrobial resistance could cause 10 million annual deaths by 2050.

Addressing antimicrobial resistance requires action across human health, animal health, agriculture, and environmental sectors. Strategies include antimicrobial stewardship to optimize use, infection prevention to reduce need for antimicrobials, surveillance to track resistance patterns, and research and development for new antimicrobials and alternatives (56). The market failure in antimicrobial development—where new drugs face rapid resistance emergence limiting commercial returns—requires innovative financing mechanisms including push and pull incentives for research.

### Zoonotic Disease Emergence and One Health

An estimated 75% of emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic, originating in animal populations before spillover to humans (57). Drivers of zoonotic emergence include land use change increasing human-wildlife contact, intensive animal agriculture concentrating hosts and pathogens, wildlife trade creating novel transmission opportunities, and climate change altering species distributions. Understanding and addressing these drivers requires moving beyond health sector silos to embrace One Health approaches integrating human, animal, and environmental health.

One Health implementation involves cross-sectoral coordination, joint surveillance systems, integrated outbreak response, and collaborative research addressing the animal-human-environment interface (58). The Quadripartite collaboration among WHO, FAO, WOAH, and UNEP provides international leadership for One Health, but implementation at national level remains uneven. Building One Health capacity requires investment in veterinary public health, environmental monitoring, and mechanisms for routine cross-sectoral collaboration.

### Global Health Security Architecture

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed weaknesses in global health security architecture, prompting extensive debate about reforms needed to prevent future catastrophic outbreaks. The International Health Regulations (2005) provide the legal framework for outbreak notification and response, but compliance proved inconsistent during COVID-19 (59). Proposed reforms include strengthening IHR implementation, establishing sustainable financing mechanisms including a pandemic fund, improving pathogen and benefit sharing arrangements, and enhancing WHO's authority and capacity.

Regional health security mechanisms complement global frameworks, with organizations including the Africa CDC, ECDC, and ASEAN health security mechanisms providing tailored regional coordination (60). National health security capacity varies enormously, with Joint External Evaluation assessments revealing critical gaps in many countries. Sustainable health security requires both strengthening international mechanisms and building domestic public health infrastructure across all countries.

## Social and Political Determinants of Future Health

### Globalization and Health Equity

Globalization produces contradictory health effects: expanding economic opportunities that could finance health improvements while simultaneously creating vulnerabilities through interconnected systems, race-to-the-bottom regulatory competition, and market volatility affecting health-promoting policies (61). Trade agreements increasingly incorporate provisions affecting health, from intellectual property rules influencing medicine prices to regulatory cooperation potentially constraining health policies.

Health equity implications of globalization depend critically on governance choices. Countries maintaining strong social protection systems and health investments have achieved health improvements amid globalization, while others have seen benefits concentrated among elites. Ensuring globalization serves health equity requires active policy intervention including progressive taxation, universal social protection, and regulation of global markets affecting health (62).

### Conflict, Fragility, and Health

Armed conflict and state fragility constitute major obstacles to health progress, with the health impacts extending far beyond direct casualties to include health system destruction, displacement, food insecurity, and ongoing trauma (63). An estimated 1.8 billion people live in fragile and conflict-affected states where achieving health-related Sustainable Development Goals appears unlikely without fundamental improvements in security and governance.

Health in conflict settings requires specialized approaches addressing immediate humanitarian needs while building toward sustainable health systems. Humanitarian health response increasingly emphasizes protection of healthcare facilities and workers, continuation of essential services, and attention to mental health and protection needs (64). Long-term recovery requires rebuilding health infrastructure, addressing population trauma, and establishing governance systems capable of sustained health provision.

### Changing Governance Landscapes

Governance of global health is evolving with shifts in geopolitical power, emergence of new actors including philanthropies and civil society, and changing dynamics of multilateral institutions. The concentration of global health governance authority that characterized the early WHO period has given way to a more fragmented landscape with multiple overlapping initiatives (65). This fragmentation creates coordination challenges while potentially enabling innovation and flexibility.

Democratic backsliding and rising authoritarianism in multiple countries threaten health through weakened accountability, suppressed civil society, and subordination of public health to political interests. Conversely, citizen movements demanding health rights and accountability have achieved significant gains in some contexts (66). The future of health governance depends substantially on broader political trajectories affecting state capacity, accountability, and prioritization of population welfare.

### Misinformation and Health

Health misinformation has emerged as a significant public health challenge, amplified by social media platforms enabling rapid spread of false claims. Vaccine hesitancy fueled partly by misinformation has contributed to declining immunization coverage and disease resurgence (67). During the COVID-19 pandemic, the WHO declared an "infodemic" accompanying the pandemic, with misinformation undermining public health responses.

Addressing health misinformation requires multi-faceted strategies including platform accountability, media literacy, trusted messenger engagement, and rapid response to emerging false claims. Research demonstrates that prebunking (inoculating against misinformation before exposure) may be more effective than debunking false claims after spread (68). Building public trust in health institutions and expertise provides foundational protection against misinformation, requiring transparent communication and accountability for past failures.

## Case Studies in Emerging Health Challenges

**Case Study 34.1: European Heat-Health Action Plans**

The 2003 European heat wave caused over 70,000 excess deaths, exposing critical gaps in public health preparedness for extreme heat events. France alone experienced approximately 15,000 excess deaths during a two-week period in August 2003, with mortality concentrated among isolated elderly individuals (1). The catastrophic toll prompted comprehensive reassessment of heat-health preparedness across Europe.

Following 2003, WHO EURO developed guidance for heat-health action plans (HHAPs), and multiple European countries implemented comprehensive systems (2). Key HHAP components include: meteorological early warning systems providing advance notice of dangerous conditions; health system preparedness including surge capacity and cool facilities; targeted outreach to vulnerable populations; public communication campaigns; and real-time mortality surveillance enabling response adjustment.

France's Plan National Canicule exemplifies comprehensive HHAP implementation. The system includes four alert levels triggering escalating responses, mandatory registration of vulnerable persons, activation of air-conditioned refuge centers, and deployment of personnel for door-to-door welfare checks (3). Health system adaptations include heat-adjusted staffing, stockpiling of rehydration supplies, and protocols for heat-related presentations.

Evaluation of European HHAPs demonstrates significant mortality reduction despite continued heat wave occurrence. The 2006 European heat wave, comparable in intensity to 2003, caused substantially fewer deaths in countries with implemented HHAPs (4). Modeling studies estimate that HHAPs prevented between 30% and 70% of expected excess mortality during subsequent heat events, depending on system maturity and specific conditions.

Challenges persist including reaching isolated individuals, addressing nighttime heat exposure, managing chronic conditions during heat waves, and adapting to progressively more extreme events as climate change advances (5). Urban planning adaptations including green infrastructure, cool roofs, and heat-resilient building design increasingly complement acute response systems.

The European HHAP experience demonstrates that systematic preparation can substantially reduce climate-related health impacts, but requires sustained political commitment, intersectoral coordination, and continuous adaptation as conditions evolve.

**Case Study 34.2: NHS England Greener NHS Programme**

The National Health Service in England generates approximately 4-5% of England's total carbon emissions, including direct operational emissions and supply chain contributions (6). Recognizing healthcare's climate contradiction--contributing to the environmental degradation threatening health--NHS England committed to achieving net zero carbon emissions, becoming the world's first health system to establish such a target.

The Greener NHS programme encompasses comprehensive decarbonization across all aspects of health system operations. For direct emissions (Scope 1 and 2), strategies include transitioning to renewable energy, electrifying the NHS vehicle fleet, modernizing building systems, and reducing anesthetic gas emissions (7). The 2040 target for direct emissions net zero requires complete transformation of energy systems and infrastructure.

Supply chain emissions (Scope 3) constitute the majority of NHS carbon footprint and present greater challenges given limited direct control over suppliers. Strategies include sustainable procurement requirements, supporting suppliers in decarbonization, shifting to lower-carbon alternatives, and redesigning care pathways to reduce resource intensity (8). The 2045 target for comprehensive net zero requires fundamental transformation of global health supply chains.

Early implementation demonstrates feasibility while revealing challenges. Energy efficiency improvements and renewable energy procurement have reduced direct emissions, while sustainable procurement standards increasingly influence supplier practices (9). Clinical pathway innovations including remote consultations, optimized prescribing reducing pharmaceutical waste, and reduced unnecessary investigations generate both environmental and efficiency benefits.

Challenges include capital investment requirements for infrastructure transformation, complexity of supply chain engagement, and balancing environmental objectives with other priorities including cost containment and service delivery (10). Staff engagement through sustainability champions and integration of environmental considerations into clinical education support cultural transformation necessary for comprehensive change.

NHS England's experience demonstrates that comprehensive health system decarbonization is achievable but requires long-term commitment, substantial investment, and integration of sustainability into all aspects of health system governance.

**Case Study 34.3: Singapore's AI Governance Framework for Healthcare**

Singapore has positioned itself as a leader in both AI development and AI governance, developing frameworks to enable innovation while managing risks. The healthcare sector presents particularly high stakes given potential for AI to improve care quality and access alongside risks of harm from algorithmic errors or bias (11). Singapore's approach seeks to balance innovation enablement with patient protection.

Singapore's AI governance for healthcare operates within the broader Model AI Governance Framework first released in 2019 and subsequently updated. The framework emphasizes principles including human oversight, transparency, fairness, and accountability (12). For healthcare specifically, the Health Sciences Authority developed regulatory guidance for AI medical devices, distinguishing between different risk categories requiring different levels of regulatory scrutiny.

The governance approach emphasizes proportionality, applying more stringent requirements to higher-risk applications while enabling streamlined pathways for lower-risk innovations. Post-market surveillance mechanisms require ongoing monitoring of deployed AI systems, enabling detection of performance degradation or unexpected harms (13). Sandbox arrangements allow controlled testing of innovative approaches before broader deployment.

Multiple AI healthcare applications have navigated Singapore's governance framework, including diagnostic imaging algorithms, clinical decision support tools, and population health management systems. Implementation experience has informed iterative framework refinement, with updates addressing emerging challenges including generative AI applications (14).

Key implementation challenges include ensuring algorithmic transparency when AI systems utilize proprietary methods, maintaining human oversight without negating efficiency benefits, and addressing equity implications when AI development concentrates expertise in well-resourced institutions (15). Singapore's approach emphasizes collaborative development involving regulators, developers, healthcare providers, and patient representatives.

Singapore's experience demonstrates that proactive AI governance can enable beneficial innovation while managing risks, but requires continuous adaptation as technology evolves and implementation experience accumulates.

**Case Study 34.4: Japan's Community-Based Integrated Care System**

Japan faces the world's most advanced demographic aging, with over 29% of the population aged 65 and above and the proportion continuing to increase (16). This super-aged society presents unprecedented challenges for health and social care systems designed for younger populations with different disease burdens and care needs. Japan's response provides important lessons for countries approaching similar demographic transitions.

Japan's Community-based Integrated Care System (Chiiki Hokatsu Care System) was established through 2012 legislation with full implementation targeted by 2025. The concept integrates five components--medical care, nursing care, preventive care, housing, and livelihood support--within geographic communities of approximately 30 minutes' travel radius (17).

The system emphasizes enabling aging in place by bringing services to where people live rather than institutionalizing elderly populations. Care management coordinates across multiple service providers, while community support centers serve as local hubs for information, assessment, and service coordination (18). Prevention programs aim to maintain functional capacity and delay care needs.

Implementation has progressed substantially though unevenly across Japan's diverse municipalities. Successful examples demonstrate reduced hospitalization, improved care coordination, and maintained quality of life for elderly populations (19). Integration of medical and long-term care has improved, though professional boundaries and separate financing streams continue to create coordination challenges.

Technology increasingly supports integrated care, including remote monitoring, care coordination platforms, and robotic assistance for caregivers and care recipients. However, the technology is supplementary to human care relationships rather than replacing them (20). Workforce challenges persist given Japan's overall labor shortage and particular difficulty attracting workers to elderly care.

Japan's experience demonstrates both possibilities and challenges of transforming health systems for aging societies, emphasizing the importance of community-based approaches, integration across care sectors, and sustained political commitment to long-term system transformation.

## Future Directions and Strategic Responses

### Scenario Planning for Global Health

Scenario planning provides methodology for exploring multiple plausible futures rather than predicting a single outcome. Unlike forecasting, which extrapolates current trends, scenario planning deliberately considers divergent possibilities including discontinuities and surprises (69). For global health, scenario planning helps identify robust strategies performing well across different possible futures and early warning indicators signaling which scenarios are materializing.

Global health scenario exercises have explored futures shaped by climate trajectories, technological development, geopolitical evolution, and pandemic emergence. The World Economic Forum, WHO, and academic institutions have conducted scenario exercises informing strategy development (70). Effective scenario planning requires diverse participant perspectives, willingness to consider uncomfortable possibilities, and mechanisms for translating insights into actionable strategy.

### Innovation and Adaptation Strategies

Navigating emerging challenges requires both technological innovation developing new solutions and adaptive capacity enabling effective response to changing circumstances. Innovation strategies should address the full pipeline from basic research through implementation, with particular attention to translating innovations to resource-limited settings where need is greatest (71). Adaptive capacity requires flexible institutions, diverse knowledge systems, and learning mechanisms enabling continuous improvement.

Global health innovation increasingly emphasizes frugal approaches developing effective solutions at dramatically reduced cost, enabling deployment where traditional technologies remain unaffordable. Examples include low-cost diagnostics, simplified treatment protocols, and digital health applications functioning on basic mobile devices (72). Innovation ecosystems fostering collaboration between traditional and non-traditional health actors show promise for accelerating development and deployment of needed solutions.

### Global Cooperation Mechanisms

The transboundary nature of emerging health challenges demands strengthened global cooperation even as geopolitical tensions complicate collective action. Climate change mitigation and adaptation, pandemic preparedness, antimicrobial resistance, and technology governance all require coordinated international responses (73). Existing mechanisms including WHO, multilateral financing institutions, and bilateral cooperation provide foundations requiring strengthening and adaptation.

Emerging cooperation mechanisms include challenge-focused coalitions convening diverse actors around specific objectives, regional health security arrangements tailored to local contexts, and multi-stakeholder partnerships engaging private sector and civil society alongside governments (74). Equitable participation of low- and middle-income countries in global health governance ensures that cooperation mechanisms address diverse needs rather than reflecting only powerful countries' priorities.

## Conclusion

This chapter examined emerging challenges that will shape global health in coming decades. Planetary health provides a framework recognizing human health dependence on natural systems currently being degraded. Climate change produces direct and indirect health impacts requiring systematic adaptation. Technological disruption offers unprecedented capabilities while raising governance challenges. Demographic transitions transform health needs and health system requirements. Emerging infectious disease threats demand continued vigilance and preparedness investment. Social and political determinants including globalization, conflict, and governance evolution shape the context within which health challenges unfold.

Case studies illustrated responses to these challenges: European heat-health action plans demonstrating climate adaptation effectiveness; NHS England's decarbonization programme modeling sustainable healthcare transformation; Singapore's AI governance enabling innovation while managing risks; and Japan's integrated care system addressing super-aged society needs. Throughout, effective responses share characteristics including systems thinking, anticipatory governance, sustained political commitment, and adaptive learning from implementation experience.

The path forward requires embracing complexity and uncertainty rather than seeking false certainty. Building adaptive capacity, strengthening global cooperation, and maintaining commitment to health equity despite competing pressures will determine whether emerging challenges are navigated successfully or produce avoidable suffering. The knowledge, technologies, and governance approaches to address these challenges largely exist; the critical factor is political will to deploy them effectively and equitably.
