## Introduction and Historical Context

Global health partnerships represent a defining feature of contemporary efforts to address health challenges that transcend national boundaries. These collaborative arrangements bring together governments, multilateral organizations, civil society, private sector actors, and philanthropic foundations to mobilize resources, coordinate interventions, and strengthen health systems worldwide. Understanding the architecture, governance, and effectiveness of these partnerships is essential for public health professionals working in an increasingly interconnected global health landscape.

### Evolution of Global Health Partnerships

The landscape of international health cooperation has undergone profound transformation over the past century. Early international health efforts emerged from colonial medicine and commercial interests in controlling epidemic diseases that threatened trade and economic activity (1). The establishment of the World Health Organization in 1948 marked a pivotal shift toward multilateral cooperation, embedding health within the United Nations framework and establishing principles of technical cooperation and normative guidance (2).

The latter decades of the twentieth century witnessed significant expansion in **development assistance for health (DAH)**, growing from approximately $5.6 billion in 1990 to over $40 billion by 2019 (3). This growth coincided with a fundamental paradigm shift from traditional bilateral aid relationships toward more complex multi-stakeholder partnerships. The HIV/AIDS pandemic served as a catalyst for this transformation, revealing limitations of existing institutions and creating demand for new mechanisms capable of mobilizing unprecedented resources (4).

The period from 2000 to 2010 represented a watershed era in global health partnership formation. The Millennium Development Goals established health-related targets that galvanized international attention, while new institutions including GAVI, the Vaccine Alliance (2000), the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria (2002), and the U.S. President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR, 2003) fundamentally reshaped the global health architecture (5). These initiatives introduced innovations in governance, financing, and program implementation that distinguished them from traditional multilateral organizations.

### Current Partnership Landscape

Contemporary global health financing involves a complex ecosystem of actors and mechanisms. The Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation estimates that DAH reached $40.6 billion in 2019, though the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted established patterns and generated unprecedented mobilization for pandemic response (6). Bilateral government assistance accounts for approximately 60% of DAH, with the United States contributing roughly one-third of global totals. Private philanthropy, led by the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, provides approximately 15% of DAH, while multilateral organizations and global health initiatives account for the remainder (7).

The proliferation of actors in global health has created both opportunities and challenges. While new institutions have mobilized additional resources and introduced governance innovations, fragmentation has emerged as a significant concern. Recipient countries must navigate relationships with multiple funding sources, each with distinct reporting requirements, programming priorities, and accountability mechanisms. Recognition of this fragmentation has prompted efforts to improve coordination, harmonization, and alignment with country priorities—principles codified in the Paris Declaration on Aid Effectiveness and subsequent agreements (8).

## Multilateral Organizations in Global Health

Multilateral organizations remain foundational actors in global health governance, providing normative guidance, technical assistance, and convening functions that complement their financing roles. Understanding the mandates, comparative advantages, and limitations of key multilateral institutions is essential for effective partnership engagement.

### World Health Organization: Mandate and Functions

The **World Health Organization (WHO)** serves as the directing and coordinating authority on international health within the United Nations system. Established by its Constitution in 1946 and commencing operations in 1948, WHO's mandate encompasses setting norms and standards, articulating evidence-based policy options, providing technical support to countries, and monitoring health trends (9).

WHO's normative functions include development of the International Health Regulations, which provide the legal framework for global health security, as well as prequalification programs for medicines, vaccines, and diagnostics that facilitate procurement by global health initiatives. The organization's technical guidance on disease prevention and treatment informs national programs worldwide, while WHO-led initiatives such as the Expanded Programme on Immunization have achieved remarkable success in reducing vaccine-preventable disease burden (10).

Despite its unique legitimacy as a member-state organization, WHO faces persistent resource constraints. Assessed contributions from member states have remained essentially frozen for decades, forcing the organization to rely increasingly on voluntary contributions earmarked for specific purposes. This financing structure has raised concerns about WHO's ability to set independent priorities and respond flexibly to emerging challenges (11). The COVID-19 pandemic exposed both the indispensable role of WHO in health emergency response and limitations in its authority and resources for coordinating global pandemic preparedness.

### World Bank Group and Health Financing

The **World Bank Group** has become a major financier of health systems in low- and middle-income countries, providing both concessional lending through the International Development Association (IDA) and technical assistance for health sector reform. World Bank health portfolio commitments averaged approximately $15 billion annually between 2020 and 2023, making the institution one of the largest sources of development financing for health (12).

The World Bank's engagement in health has evolved significantly from early structural adjustment programs that often mandated user fees and health sector retrenchment. Contemporary World Bank health programming emphasizes universal health coverage, primary health care strengthening, and human capital development. The Human Capital Project, launched in 2018, positions health and education investments as essential for economic development, providing a framework for sustained engagement with finance ministries (13).

The World Bank's comparative advantages include substantial financing capacity, analytical expertise in health economics and health financing, and convening power that engages finance ministries as well as health sectors. However, critics have raised concerns about conditionality requirements, policy prescriptions that may not fit all contexts, and limited representation of affected communities in project design (14).

### UNICEF, UNFPA, and UN Agencies

The **United Nations Children's Fund (UNICEF)** plays a critical role in child health and survival, operating in over 190 countries with emphasis on immunization, nutrition, water and sanitation, and emergency response. UNICEF serves as the world's largest vaccine buyer, procuring over 2 billion doses annually through its Supply Division and leveraging procurement scale to negotiate reduced prices for developing countries (15).

The **United Nations Population Fund (UNFPA)** leads international efforts in reproductive health and rights, providing family planning commodities, supporting maternal health programs, and advocating for women's and adolescents' health needs. UNFPA's mandate explicitly addresses the demographic, development, and human rights dimensions of reproductive health (16).

Effective coordination among UN agencies operating in health remains an ongoing challenge. The UN Reform process has sought to strengthen country-level coordination through UN Country Teams and joint programming, while the Global Action Plan for Healthy Lives and Well-being brings together 13 multilateral health organizations to align efforts toward Sustainable Development Goal 3 (17).

### Regional Development Banks and Organizations

Regional development banks including the African Development Bank, Asian Development Bank, and Inter-American Development Bank have expanded health sector engagement, often emphasizing regional priorities and providing financing modalities suited to middle-income country contexts. Regional health organizations such as the Africa Centres for Disease Control and Prevention, established in 2017, have assumed increasing prominence in coordinating regional health security and supporting national public health capacities (18).

Regional integration initiatives increasingly address health cooperation, exemplified by the European Union's health programming, ASEAN health cooperation frameworks, and the African Union's health strategy. These regional mechanisms offer opportunities for **South-South learning** and peer support while addressing transboundary health challenges through coordinated responses (19).

## Major Global Health Initiatives

The emergence of dedicated global health initiatives transformed the landscape of international health cooperation beginning in 2000. These initiatives introduced governance innovations, mobilized unprecedented resources, and demonstrated capacity for rapid program scale-up, though they have also faced criticism regarding their impact on health systems and country ownership.

### GAVI, the Vaccine Alliance

**GAVI, the Vaccine Alliance** was established in 2000 to address declining immunization rates and ensure introduction of new vaccines in the world's poorest countries. GAVI's founding represented a novel public-private partnership model, bringing together developing country and donor governments, WHO, UNICEF, the World Bank, the vaccine industry, research and technical agencies, civil society organizations, and the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation (20).

GAVI's programmatic approach combines direct vaccine financing with market-shaping strategies to accelerate vaccine introduction and reduce prices. Through the **Advance Market Commitment** mechanism, GAVI provided guaranteed demand for pneumococcal vaccines, incentivizing manufacturer investment in production capacity and enabling rapid price reductions from over $100 per dose to under $3.30 for GAVI-supported countries (21). Between 2000 and 2023, GAVI support contributed to vaccination of over 1 billion children, preventing an estimated 17.3 million future deaths (22).

GAVI's **co-financing policy** requires recipient countries to contribute increasing shares of vaccine costs as their economies develop, promoting sustainability and domestic resource mobilization. Countries graduate from GAVI support upon reaching income thresholds, though this transition process has proven challenging for some countries facing fiscal constraints or competing health priorities (23).

### The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria

**The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria** was established in 2002 as a financing mechanism to mobilize and disburse resources for combating three major infectious disease epidemics. As of 2023, the Global Fund has disbursed over $60 billion to programs in more than 100 countries, supporting HIV treatment for over 25 million people, TB treatment for over 7 million people, and distribution of over 2.2 billion insecticide-treated bed nets for malaria prevention (24).

The Global Fund introduced several governance innovations that distinguish it from traditional development institutions. **Country Coordinating Mechanisms (CCMs)** bring together government, civil society, private sector, and affected communities to develop grant applications and oversee implementation, representing a significant departure from government-only decision-making in development assistance. The inclusion of people living with HIV, TB, and malaria in governance structures reflects principles of meaningful involvement that emerged from the HIV activist movement (25).

The Global Fund operates through a **performance-based funding** model, disbursing resources based on demonstrated results rather than expenditure-based reimbursement. This approach has incentivized results-oriented programming but has also been criticized for potentially favoring easily measurable disease-specific outcomes over health system strengthening investments with longer-term impact (26). In response to such criticism, the Global Fund has progressively increased emphasis on resilient and sustainable systems for health, with approximately 25% of recent funding allocations dedicated to health system strengthening activities (27).

### PEPFAR and PMI

The **U.S. President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR)** represents the largest bilateral global health initiative, having invested over $110 billion since its inception in 2003. PEPFAR's emergency response orientation enabled rapid scale-up of HIV prevention and treatment services, supporting antiretroviral therapy for over 20 million people by 2023 (28).

PEPFAR's evolution illustrates broader trends in global health partnership development. Initial programming emphasized service delivery through implementing partners, often bypassing national health systems. Subsequent phases have prioritized country ownership, health system strengthening, and sustainability planning. The PEPFAR 3.0 strategy emphasized the "90-90-90" treatment cascade targets, while more recent strategic directions emphasize epidemic control, sustainability, and integration with broader health system priorities (29).

The **President's Malaria Initiative (PMI)**, launched in 2005, complements Global Fund investments in malaria-endemic countries, providing approximately $770 million annually for prevention and treatment activities. PMI's implementation model relies heavily on coordination with national malaria programs and other partners, exemplifying efforts toward alignment and harmonization (30).

### UNITAID and Innovative Health Financing

**UNITAID** was established in 2006 with a distinctive mandate to use innovative financing and market interventions to accelerate access to health products for HIV, TB, and malaria. UNITAID pioneered the airline solidarity levy, a small tax on airline tickets implemented by France and subsequently adopted by additional countries, generating predictable funding independent of traditional government budget processes (31).

UNITAID's market intervention approach targets bottlenecks in access to health commodities, investing in product development, quality assurance, and demand creation to sustainably reduce prices and expand availability. Investments in HIV viral load diagnostics contributed to price reductions exceeding 50%, while support for pediatric antiretroviral formulations addressed critical gaps in treatment options for children (32).

The International Finance Facility for Immunisation (IFFIm), another innovative financing mechanism, leverages donor pledges to issue bonds in capital markets, frontloading resources for GAVI programs. Debt-for-health swaps, social impact bonds, and pandemic financing instruments represent additional innovations in global health financing that complement traditional official development assistance (33).

## Public-Private Partnerships in Global Health

Public-private partnerships (PPPs) in global health encompass diverse arrangements between public sector entities and private companies, ranging from product development collaborations to service delivery partnerships. These partnerships have demonstrated capacity to address specific market failures while also raising important questions about governance, accountability, and alignment with public health objectives.

### Product Development Partnerships

**Product Development Partnerships (PDPs)** emerged to address the persistent gap between disease burden and research investment for neglected tropical diseases and other conditions predominantly affecting low-income populations. These non-profit organizations coordinate drug, vaccine, and diagnostic development by managing portfolios of candidates, leveraging pharmaceutical industry expertise, and ensuring access provisions are integral to development agreements (34).

The **Medicines for Malaria Venture (MMV)**, established in 1999, exemplifies the PDP model. Operating as a virtual research organization, MMV manages partnerships with pharmaceutical companies, academic institutions, and contract research organizations to advance antimalarial candidates through the development pipeline. MMV's portfolio has contributed to development of 14 new antimalarials since 1999, compared with only four in the preceding 25 years, including novel treatments active against drug-resistant parasites (35).

The **TB Alliance** (Global Alliance for TB Drug Development) applies similar approaches to tuberculosis drug development, addressing the critical need for shortened treatment regimens and drugs effective against drug-resistant TB. PDP investments in TB drug development contributed to approval of bedaquiline and delamanid, the first new TB drugs in over 40 years (36).

The **Drugs for Neglected Diseases initiative (DNDi)**, established in 2003 following the precedent of Médecins Sans Frontières' campaign for access to essential medicines, has developed new treatments for sleeping sickness, leishmaniasis, and Chagas disease. DNDi's approach explicitly emphasizes access as integral to the development model, with licensing agreements ensuring availability at affordable prices in endemic countries (37).

### Implementation Partnerships

Beyond product development, public-private partnerships in global health encompass implementation collaborations for service delivery, health worker training, supply chain strengthening, and health system support. These arrangements range from corporate social responsibility initiatives to comprehensive partnerships addressing specific health priorities (38).

Pharmaceutical industry access programs, including tiered pricing arrangements and voluntary licenses, have expanded availability of essential medicines in low-income countries. The Medicines Patent Pool, established with UNITAID support, facilitates voluntary licensing of HIV, hepatitis C, and tuberculosis medicines, enabling generic production and price competition (39).

Private sector engagement in health service delivery presents both opportunities and governance challenges. Partnerships with private healthcare providers can extend coverage and introduce efficiency innovations, while also raising concerns about equity, quality assurance, and alignment with public health objectives. Effective governance frameworks, regulatory capacity, and monitoring systems are essential prerequisites for successful public-private partnerships in service delivery (40).

### Philanthropic Foundations

Private philanthropic foundations have assumed unprecedented roles in global health governance and financing. The **Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation** has disbursed over $50 billion since its establishment in 2000, with health constituting the largest programmatic area. Foundation investments have catalyzed development of new health products, supported global health initiatives including GAVI and the Global Fund, and influenced research priorities across the global health field (41).

The **Wellcome Trust**, with assets exceeding £38 billion, represents another major philanthropic actor in global health, emphasizing research funding and capacity strengthening. Wellcome's investments in African research institutions, climate and health research, and pandemic preparedness complement its traditional biomedical research portfolio (42).

The influence of large private foundations on global health priorities has generated debate regarding democratic accountability and governance. Critics argue that foundation priorities may not align with expressed needs of affected communities and that substantial resources concentrated in private hands warrant greater transparency and accountability mechanisms. Foundation supporters emphasize flexibility, risk tolerance, and capacity for innovation that complement more constrained public sector funding (43).

## South-South Cooperation and Emerging Partnerships

South-South cooperation represents an alternative paradigm for international health cooperation, emphasizing solidarity, mutual benefit, and horizontal relationships between developing countries. As emerging economies have assumed greater roles in global health, South-South cooperation has expanded both in scale and in recognition as a complement to traditional North-South development assistance.

### Principles and Modalities of South-South Cooperation

South-South cooperation is grounded in principles articulated through the Buenos Aires Plan of Action (1978) and subsequent UN resolutions, emphasizing respect for national sovereignty, non-interference, equality among partners, and mutual benefit. Unlike traditional development assistance characterized by donor-recipient relationships, South-South cooperation positions partners as co-learners sharing relevant experiences and appropriate technologies (44).

Modalities of South-South health cooperation include technical cooperation and knowledge sharing, training and capacity building, provision of health commodities, and joint research initiatives. **Triangular cooperation** involves collaboration between two developing countries supported by a traditional donor or international organization, combining Southern expertise with Northern resources (45).

The comparative advantages of South-South cooperation include relevance of shared development experiences, cultural and linguistic affinity that may facilitate knowledge transfer, and technologies and approaches developed for resource-constrained settings. However, South-South cooperation also faces challenges including capacity constraints among provider countries, limited documentation and evaluation, and questions about quality and sustainability of some initiatives (46).

### Brazil, China, and India in Global Health

**Brazil** emerged as an influential actor in global health through its domestic HIV/AIDS response, which demonstrated that universal access to antiretroviral therapy was feasible in middle-income country settings. Brazil's strategy of compulsory licensing and domestic generic production challenged pharmaceutical industry pricing and contributed to global price reductions. Brazil subsequently engaged in South-South health cooperation, sharing expertise with Lusophone African countries and establishing the LAPROMAL pharmaceutical facility in Mozambique for local ARV production (47).

**China's** engagement in African health has expanded substantially, encompassing medical team deployments, infrastructure construction, pharmaceutical exports, and training programs. Chinese medical teams have operated in African countries since the 1960s, while more recent initiatives have emphasized health system infrastructure and technology transfer. China's role in COVID-19 vaccine diplomacy, including donations and commercial exports to developing countries, illustrated both the scale of Chinese health engagement and associated geopolitical dimensions (48).

**India's** pharmaceutical industry produces approximately 20% of global generic medicines and provides the majority of antiretrovirals used in developing countries. Beyond commercial exports, India has engaged in technical cooperation, training programs, and health infrastructure support, particularly in neighboring South Asian countries and through historical relationships with African nations (49).

The BRICS grouping (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) has explored health cooperation as a dimension of broader South-South engagement, establishing working groups on health issues and pledging cooperation on pharmaceutical production and health security (50).

### Regional Health Cooperation

Regional organizations have assumed increasing roles in health cooperation, addressing shared challenges and strengthening collective capacities. The **African Union's Africa Health Strategy** articulates continental health priorities, while the Africa Centres for Disease Control and Prevention coordinates regional health security and supports member state public health capacities. Regional economic communities including ECOWAS, SADC, and EAC have developed health cooperation frameworks addressing cross-border health challenges (51).

**ASEAN health cooperation** encompasses regional disease surveillance networks, harmonization of pharmaceutical regulations, and cooperation on pandemic preparedness. The ASEAN Health Sector has developed regional frameworks for emergency response and health workforce development, building on principles of regional solidarity (52).

## Aid Effectiveness and Global Health Governance

The proliferation of global health actors and initiatives has prompted sustained attention to aid effectiveness—ensuring that development assistance achieves intended outcomes efficiently while strengthening country capacities and systems. Principles articulated in the Paris Declaration and subsequent agreements provide a framework for assessing and improving partnership effectiveness.

### Paris Declaration and Aid Effectiveness Principles

The **Paris Declaration on Aid Effectiveness** (2005), Accra Agenda for Action (2008), and Busan Partnership for Effective Development Cooperation (2011) established internationally agreed principles for development cooperation. Five core principles guide effective partnerships: ownership by recipient countries, alignment with country systems and priorities, harmonization among donors, results orientation, and mutual accountability (53).

Application of these principles to health has proven challenging given the complexity of the global health architecture and disease-specific vertical initiatives that may operate outside national health systems. The International Health Partnership (IHP+), subsequently transformed into UHC2030, was established specifically to apply aid effectiveness principles in health, promoting alignment with national health strategies and use of country systems for planning, budgeting, and monitoring (54).

Evidence on progress toward aid effectiveness goals in health presents a mixed picture. While some indicators show improvement—such as increased use of program-based approaches and reduced transaction costs through pooled funding mechanisms—significant fragmentation persists. Studies document continuing proliferation of project implementation units, parallel reporting systems, and donor-driven priority setting that may not align with country needs (55).

### Country Ownership and Leadership

**Country ownership** represents a foundational principle of effective partnership, reflecting recognition that sustainable health improvements ultimately depend on national capacities, resources, and political commitment. Ownership encompasses national leadership in health strategy development, domestic resource mobilization, and governance of health systems—not merely implementation of externally designed programs (56).

Strengthening country ownership requires concerted effort from both countries and development partners. Country governments must articulate clear health priorities through national health strategies, develop domestic financing mechanisms, and build institutional capacity for health system governance. Development partners must genuinely align with country priorities, reduce parallel systems, and support country-led coordination mechanisms (57).

**Domestic resource mobilization** for health has emerged as a critical priority as countries transition from external support. While development assistance for health has increased substantially, it remains a small fraction of total health expenditure in most countries. Sustainable health financing ultimately depends on increased government health expenditure, expanded risk pooling through social health insurance or tax-based financing, and improved efficiency in resource utilization (58).

### Accountability and Transparency

Effective partnerships require robust accountability mechanisms ensuring that resources achieve intended purposes and that all stakeholders can access information necessary for informed participation. The **International Aid Transparency Initiative (IATI)** established standards for publishing development assistance information in open, comparable formats, enabling improved tracking of resource flows and coordination among actors (59).

**Mutual accountability** extends beyond traditional donor accountability for resource utilization to encompass reciprocal commitments from both development partners and country governments. Results frameworks, joint annual reviews, and multi-stakeholder oversight mechanisms provide structures for accountability, though power imbalances may limit genuine mutuality in practice (60).

## Critical Perspectives and Future Directions

Critical scholarship on global health partnerships has highlighted power dynamics, unintended consequences, and limitations of dominant partnership models. Engaging with these critiques is essential for designing more equitable and effective partnerships that genuinely advance health equity and system strengthening.

### Power Dynamics and Decolonizing Global Health

Growing attention to **decolonizing global health** has foregrounded historical and contemporary power imbalances in international health cooperation. Critics argue that global health partnerships often reproduce colonial patterns of knowledge extraction, with research questions and methodologies defined in high-income countries, data collected in low- and middle-income countries, and publications and careers accruing to Northern-based researchers (61).

**Epistemic justice** in global health requires recognition and valuation of knowledge produced by researchers and practitioners in affected communities and countries. This encompasses support for research institutions in low- and middle-income countries, equitable partnerships that share decision-making authority, and publication practices that ensure appropriate recognition for local collaborators (62).

Power dynamics in global health governance extend beyond research to priority setting, resource allocation, and program design. While initiatives such as the Global Fund have introduced innovations in stakeholder participation, significant decisions regarding global health architecture, funding allocations, and programmatic approaches remain concentrated among high-income country governments and institutions. Meaningful inclusion of affected community voices and Southern leadership requires structural changes in governance arrangements, not merely consultative processes (63).

### Sustainability and Transition Challenges

As countries experience economic growth and progress toward development thresholds, they face **transition** from external support—a process that has revealed significant challenges. Countries graduating from GAVI support have struggled to maintain immunization program financing and coverage, while Global Fund transition has in some cases been associated with service disruptions and coverage declines (64).

Effective transition requires advance planning, graduated phase-out timelines, and sustained investment in domestic financing capacity and health systems. Evidence suggests that transitions occurring too rapidly or without adequate preparation can undermine achievements gained through external support. Conversely, indefinite dependence on external financing is neither sustainable nor compatible with country ownership principles (65).

**Sustainability** extends beyond financing to encompass institutional capacity, technical expertise, and political commitment. Building sustainable health systems requires long-term investments in human resources, information systems, supply chains, and governance structures—investments that disease-specific initiatives may not prioritize and that require extended timeframes to mature (66).

### Future Partnership Models

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed limitations in global health partnership architecture and generated proposals for substantial reform. The inadequacy of pandemic preparedness financing, inequitable vaccine distribution through COVAX, and coordination challenges in the emergency response have prompted calls for strengthened global health governance, new financing mechanisms, and reformed partnerships (67).

Emerging partnership models address climate change and health, digital health cooperation, and health system resilience—priorities that transcend traditional disease-specific framing. The Pandemic Fund, established in 2022 with World Bank hosting, represents an effort to address financing gaps for pandemic preparedness, though questions remain about its scale, governance, and relationship to existing institutions. Proposals for an International Pandemic Treaty negotiated through WHO would establish binding commitments for pandemic preparedness and response, potentially reshaping global health governance (68).

**Case Study 27.1: GAVI and Pneumococcal Vaccine Introduction in Low-Income Countries**

Pneumococcal disease represents a leading cause of pneumonia deaths in children under five, with highest burden concentrated in low-income countries that historically lacked access to pneumococcal conjugate vaccines. At vaccine launch in 2000, pneumococcal vaccines were priced at over $100 per dose, prohibitively expensive for developing country health systems. GAVI developed an innovative Advance Market Commitment (AMC) mechanism to accelerate introduction and reduce prices (69).

The AMC, launched in 2009 with $1.5 billion in donor commitments, provided manufacturers guaranteed demand for pneumococcal vaccines meeting specified criteria, thereby incentivizing production scale-up and price reduction. Under the AMC, manufacturers committed to supply vaccines at subsidized prices ($3.50 per dose) in exchange for guaranteed purchase volumes (70).

GAVI support enabled introduction of pneumococcal vaccines in over 60 countries, increasing coverage from essentially zero in 2009 to over 50% in GAVI-eligible countries by 2019. Modeling studies estimate that GAVI-supported pneumococcal vaccination prevented approximately 700,000 child deaths between 2010 and 2019. Price reductions achieved through the AMC and subsequent negotiations reduced procurement costs by over 90% from pre-GAVI prices (21). Beyond vaccine coverage, GAVI support strengthened immunization systems through investments in cold chain infrastructure, health worker training, and data systems. The co-financing policy required recipient countries to contribute increasing shares of vaccine costs, promoting domestic resource mobilization and sustainability planning (22).

**Case Study 27.2: Medicines for Malaria Venture - A Product Development Partnership Model**

Prior to establishment of the Medicines for Malaria Venture (MMV) in 1999, antimalarial drug development had essentially ceased due to commercial market failure. Between 1975 and 1999, only four new antimalarials reached the market, even as parasite resistance to existing treatments spread. The estimated market for antimalarials in endemic developing countries was too small and uncertain to justify pharmaceutical industry R&D investment (71).

MMV was established as a non-profit product development partnership to address this market failure, with founding support from the World Bank, WHO, Dutch and Swiss governments, and the Rockefeller Foundation. The organization operates as a "virtual" R&D organization, managing a portfolio of drug candidates through partnerships with pharmaceutical companies, academic institutions, and contract research organizations (34).

MMV's portfolio has contributed to development of 14 new antimalarials since 1999, representing more new treatments in 25 years than in the previous half-century. Key products include pyramax (pyronaridine-artesunate), eurartesim (dihydroartemisinin-piperaquine), and tafenoquine for malaria prevention and radical cure. MMV partnerships have ensured availability of artemisinin-based combination therapies at less than $1 per treatment through the Affordable Medicines Facility-malaria and subsequent mechanisms (35). The MMV model demonstrates that non-profit coordination can successfully advance drug development for neglected diseases by managing risk across a portfolio of candidates, providing expertise in regulatory strategy and clinical development, and integrating access provisions into development agreements from inception (72).

**Case Study 27.3: Brazil-Africa Health Cooperation - South-South Partnership in HIV/AIDS**

Brazil's domestic response to HIV/AIDS, which achieved universal access to antiretroviral therapy through a combination of political commitment, civil society mobilization, and domestic generic production, positioned the country as a model for other developing nations. Beginning in the early 2000s, Brazil leveraged this experience for South-South cooperation, particularly with Portuguese-speaking African countries sharing linguistic and cultural ties (73).

The flagship initiative was establishment of the LAPROMAL pharmaceutical facility in Mozambique, designed to enable local production of antiretroviral medications. The project involved technology transfer, infrastructure construction, and training of Mozambican technical personnel. Brazil also provided technical cooperation in health policy, disease surveillance, and health workforce development (47).

The LAPROMAL facility achieved capacity for local ARV production, though production volumes remained limited by raw material constraints and regulatory challenges. Beyond pharmaceutical manufacturing, the partnership strengthened Mozambican technical capacity in pharmacology, quality assurance, and pharmaceutical regulation. Similar cooperation with Angola and Cape Verde encompassed training programs, policy dialogue, and technical exchange (74). Brazil's South-South health cooperation demonstrated both the potential and limitations of this partnership modality. Cultural and linguistic affinity facilitated knowledge transfer, while Brazil's relevant experience developing a middle-income country health response resonated with partner countries. However, sustaining cooperation proved challenging amid Brazil's economic difficulties and political transitions, and some initiatives did not achieve anticipated scale or sustainability (75).

**Case Study 27.4: UNITAID and the Airline Levy - Innovative Financing for Global Health**

UNITAID was established in 2006 to pioneer innovative financing and market interventions for global health, with the airline solidarity levy as its foundational funding mechanism. France implemented the levy in 2006, adding a small tax to airline tickets (1-40 euros depending on class and destination), with revenues dedicated to UNITAID. Subsequently, Cameroon, Chile, Congo, Madagascar, Mali, Mauritius, Niger, and the Republic of Korea adopted similar levies, while additional countries provided voluntary contributions (76).

The airline levy represented an application of proposals for "innovative financing" that had gained attention following the 2002 Monterrey Consensus on Financing for Development. Proponents argued that such mechanisms could provide predictable, additional resources for global public goods, independent of annual budget processes and fluctuating political priorities (31).

The airline levy has generated over $3 billion since 2006, demonstrating feasibility of this innovative financing mechanism. UNITAID has invested these resources in market interventions that have substantially reduced prices and expanded access to essential health products. Investments in HIV viral load diagnostics contributed to price reductions exceeding 50%, while support for pediatric ARV formulations addressed critical treatment gaps (32). UNITAID's market intervention approach differs from traditional procurement or subsidy programs by targeting systemic barriers to access. By investing in demand creation, quality assurance, and supply diversification, UNITAID aims to create sustainable markets rather than temporary access through direct procurement (77).

## Conclusion

Global health partnerships have transformed the landscape of international health cooperation, mobilizing unprecedented resources and introducing governance innovations that have contributed to remarkable health achievements. Multilateral organizations including WHO, the World Bank, and UN agencies provide foundational functions of normative guidance, financing, and technical support. Major global health initiatives—GAVI, the Global Fund, PEPFAR, and UNITAID—have demonstrated capacity for rapid program scale-up while introducing innovations in multi-stakeholder governance, performance-based funding, and market shaping. Public-private partnerships and philanthropic foundations have addressed market failures in neglected disease R&D and contributed substantial resources to global health priorities.

Yet critical analysis reveals persistent challenges. Power imbalances between donors and recipients, fragmentation among multiple initiatives, and tensions between disease-specific programming and health system strengthening remain ongoing concerns. Aid effectiveness principles—ownership, alignment, harmonization, results, and mutual accountability—provide frameworks for improvement but have proven difficult to fully implement. South-South cooperation offers alternative partnership paradigms, though capacity constraints and sustainability questions persist.

Looking ahead, global health partnerships must adapt to evolving challenges including pandemic preparedness, climate and health, and the unfinished agenda of universal health coverage. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed limitations of existing architecture while also demonstrating remarkable capacity for resource mobilization and scientific collaboration. Meaningful progress toward health equity will require partnerships that genuinely center country leadership, strengthen health systems, and address the structural determinants of health inequities—moving beyond the predominant models of the past quarter century toward more equitable and sustainable approaches to international health cooperation.
