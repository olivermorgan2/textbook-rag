## Introduction

Occupational health and safety represents a fundamental component of public health that affects billions of workers worldwide and their families and communities. Work-related diseases and injuries constitute a significant portion of the global burden of disease, with profound implications for individual wellbeing, economic productivity, and sustainable development (1). The International Labour Organization (ILO) estimates that 2.78 million workers die annually from occupational accidents and work-related diseases, while 374 million workers suffer non-fatal occupational injuries (2).

The field of occupational health encompasses the promotion and maintenance of the highest degree of physical, mental, and social wellbeing of workers in all occupations through prevention of departures from health, control of risks, and adaptation of work to people and people to their jobs (3). This definition, established by the ILO and World Health Organization (WHO), emphasizes both the preventive and promotional aspects of worker health protection.

Occupational health challenges vary dramatically across global contexts, reflecting differences in economic development, regulatory frameworks, technological capabilities, and social protection systems. While high-income countries have generally achieved substantial reductions in traditional occupational hazards, they face emerging challenges related to work organization, psychosocial factors, and aging workforces (4). In contrast, low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) continue to experience high rates of preventable occupational diseases and injuries, complicated by large informal sectors, limited regulatory capacity, and competing health priorities (5).

The significance of occupational health extends beyond immediate workplace impacts. Work-related health conditions contribute to broader public health challenges, including non-communicable diseases, mental health disorders, and health inequities (6). Furthermore, globalization has created interconnected supply chains that link worker health across countries, making occupational health protection a shared global responsibility (7).

## Global Burden of Occupational Diseases and Injuries

### Magnitude and Distribution

The global burden of occupational diseases and injuries is substantial but unevenly distributed across regions, economic sectors, and worker populations. According to ILO estimates, work-related diseases account for approximately 2.4 million deaths annually, while occupational accidents cause an additional 380,000 fatalities (2). These figures likely underestimate the true burden due to under-recognition and under-reporting, particularly in LMICs and informal sectors (8).

The WHO Global Health Observatory reports significant regional variations in occupational health outcomes. African and Southeast Asian regions demonstrate the highest rates of work-related mortality, with rates exceeding 15 deaths per 100,000 workers in several countries (9). In contrast, European and North American countries typically report rates below 5 per 100,000 workers, reflecting better workplace safety standards, stronger regulatory enforcement, and more comprehensive surveillance systems (10).

Economic sectors show marked differences in occupational health risks. Agriculture remains the most hazardous sector globally, accounting for over 170,000 work-related deaths annually despite employing approximately 27% of the global workforce (11). Construction, mining, and manufacturing also demonstrate elevated risks, with injury rates 2-3 times higher than service sectors (12). However, these traditional high-risk sectors are increasingly complemented by emerging hazards in healthcare, transportation, and service industries (13).

### Disease Patterns and Trends

Occupational disease patterns reflect broader epidemiological and economic transitions occurring globally. Acute traumatic injuries continue to predominate in LMICs, particularly in agriculture, construction, and informal manufacturing (14). Common causes include machinery accidents, falls from height, struck-by incidents, and transportation-related injuries (15). These injury patterns are often associated with inadequate safety training, limited access to personal protective equipment, and poor enforcement of safety regulations (16).

Chronic occupational diseases demonstrate different geographical distributions. Pneumoconioses, including silicosis, asbestosis, and coal workers' pneumoconiosis, remain prevalent in countries with active mining, construction, and industrial activities (17). The WHO estimates that over 30 million workers globally are exposed to silica dust, with approximately 15,000 deaths annually from silicosis (18). Occupational cancers, while less visible than acute injuries, contribute significantly to the global burden, with lung cancer from asbestos exposure being the most common occupational malignancy (19).

Mental health conditions increasingly represent a growing component of occupational disease burden, particularly in high-income countries. Work-related stress, depression, and anxiety disorders are associated with job insecurity, excessive workload, workplace violence, and poor work-life balance (20). The COVID-19 pandemic has further highlighted occupational mental health challenges, particularly among healthcare workers and essential service providers (21).

### Economic Impact

The economic consequences of occupational diseases and injuries extend far beyond direct medical costs. The ILO estimates that poor occupational safety and health conditions cost the global economy approximately 4% of annual gross domestic product (GDP), equivalent to $2.8 trillion (22). This figure includes direct costs such as medical treatment and compensation payments, as well as indirect costs including productivity losses, training replacement workers, and administrative expenses (23).

Individual workers and families bear substantial economic burdens from work-related health conditions. In many LMICs, occupational injuries can lead to catastrophic health expenditures, pushing families into poverty (24). Women workers often face additional economic vulnerabilities due to limited access to formal employment benefits and social protection systems (25).

## Work-Related Health Hazards

### Chemical Hazards

Chemical exposures represent one of the most significant categories of occupational health risks, affecting millions of workers across diverse industries. The ILO estimates that workers are exposed to over 100,000 chemical substances, with approximately 1,000 new chemicals introduced annually (26). Chemical hazards can cause acute health effects such as poisoning, burns, and respiratory distress, as well as chronic conditions including cancer, reproductive disorders, and neurological diseases (27).

Heavy metals continue to pose significant risks in many occupational settings. Lead exposure affects workers in battery manufacturing, construction, mining, and informal recycling operations, causing neurological impairment, cardiovascular disease, and reproductive toxicity (28). Mercury exposure in small-scale gold mining operations affects over 10 million workers globally, predominantly in sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, and South America (29). These exposures often occur without adequate protective measures or health surveillance.

Pesticide exposure affects an estimated 250 million agricultural workers worldwide, with particularly high risks in LMICs where regulation and enforcement may be limited (30). The WHO estimates that pesticides cause 11,000 deaths annually among agricultural workers, with 30-40 million workers experiencing acute pesticide poisoning each year (31). Chronic pesticide exposure is associated with cancer, neurological disorders, and reproductive health problems (32).

Organic solvents used in manufacturing, printing, and service industries present risks of acute intoxication and chronic health effects. Workers in furniture manufacturing, automotive painting, and dry cleaning face elevated risks of cancer, reproductive disorders, and central nervous system effects from solvent exposure (33). The transition to water-based alternatives has reduced exposures in some high-income countries but implementation remains limited in many LMICs (34).

### Physical Hazards

Physical hazards encompass a broad range of environmental factors that can cause immediate injury or long-term health effects. Noise exposure affects an estimated 600 million workers globally, making it one of the most prevalent occupational hazards (35). Chronic noise exposure above 85 decibels can cause permanent hearing loss, while acute exposure to very high noise levels can cause immediate auditory damage (36). Beyond hearing effects, occupational noise exposure is associated with cardiovascular disease, stress responses, and decreased work performance (37).

Vibration exposure affects workers using powered hand tools, operating mobile equipment, and working in transportation. Hand-arm vibration syndrome affects an estimated 50 million workers globally, causing vascular, neurological, and musculoskeletal disorders (38). Whole-body vibration exposure in transportation and earth-moving equipment operation is associated with low back disorders and increased risk of spinal injuries (39).

Extreme temperatures pose significant risks in many occupational settings. Heat-related illness affects workers in agriculture, construction, manufacturing, and service industries, with climate change increasing the magnitude of these risks (40). The WHO estimates that heat stress could affect 175 million additional workers by 2030, with the greatest impacts in tropical and subtropical regions (41). Cold exposure affects workers in food processing, outdoor occupations, and cold storage facilities, causing frostbite, hypothermia, and increased injury risk (42).

Radiation exposure presents risks in healthcare, nuclear power, industrial radiography, and research settings. Ionizing radiation exposure can cause cancer, reproductive effects, and acute radiation syndrome, while non-ionizing radiation from lasers, radiofrequency equipment, and ultraviolet sources presents risks of eye damage, skin effects, and thermal burns (43).

### Biological Hazards

Biological hazards include exposure to viruses, bacteria, fungi, parasites, and other microorganisms that can cause infectious diseases. Healthcare workers face elevated risks of bloodborne pathogen transmission, including hepatitis B, hepatitis C, and HIV through needlestick injuries and exposure to infected blood and body fluids (44). The WHO estimates that healthcare workers experience 2-3 million needlestick injuries annually, resulting in thousands of infections with bloodborne pathogens (45).

Agricultural workers face diverse biological hazards, including zoonotic diseases transmitted from animals, fungal spores causing respiratory conditions, and vector-borne diseases prevalent in rural environments (46). Brucellosis, Q fever, and anthrax represent significant risks for workers handling livestock and animal products (47). Additionally, workers in crop production face risks from fungal allergens and bacterial pathogens associated with organic dusts (48).

Laboratory workers and research personnel encounter risks from exposure to pathogenic microorganisms, including emerging infectious diseases. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the vulnerability of laboratory workers to novel pathogens and the importance of appropriate containment measures and personal protective equipment (49). Previous incidents involving accidental exposures to dangerous pathogens in research settings underscore the need for robust biosafety protocols (50).

Workers in waste management and water treatment face exposure to diverse biological agents, including bacteria, viruses, and parasites that can cause gastrointestinal, respiratory, and systemic infections (51). Informal waste workers, particularly prevalent in LMICs, often lack access to protective equipment and face particularly high exposure risks (52).

### Ergonomic Hazards

Ergonomic hazards result from poor workplace design that forces workers to adapt to unsuitable working conditions rather than adapting work to human capabilities. Musculoskeletal disorders (MSDs) represent the most common category of work-related illness globally, affecting millions of workers across all economic sectors (53). The EU reports that MSDs account for over 50% of all work-related absences lasting three or more days (54).

Manual handling tasks involving lifting, carrying, pushing, and pulling heavy objects are major risk factors for back injuries and other MSDs. Construction workers, healthcare staff, warehouse employees, and agricultural workers face particularly high risks from manual handling activities (55). The global shift toward mechanization has reduced some manual handling risks in high-income countries, but many workers in LMICs continue to perform physically demanding tasks without adequate mechanical aids (56).

Repetitive motion injuries affect workers performing the same movements repeatedly, particularly in manufacturing, data entry, and assembly operations. Carpal tunnel syndrome, tendinitis, and other repetitive strain injuries are common among workers in electronics manufacturing, poultry processing, and clerical work (57). The rise of computer-based work has created new categories of repetitive motion risks affecting office workers globally (58).

Prolonged standing or sitting presents risks for circulatory disorders, back problems, and other health conditions. Retail workers, security guards, and machine operators often face prolonged standing, while office workers, professional drivers, and call center operators may experience health effects from prolonged sitting (59). Proper workplace design and work organization can significantly reduce these risks through job rotation, adjustable workstations, and regular movement breaks (60).

### Psychosocial Hazards

Psychosocial hazards encompass aspects of work organization, management practices, and workplace culture that can cause psychological and physiological stress responses. These hazards have gained increasing recognition as significant contributors to work-related ill health, particularly in service-oriented economies (61). The WHO estimates that work-related stress affects 20-30% of workers globally, with higher prevalence in certain occupational groups (62).

Job stress results from imbalances between job demands and worker resources, including control, support, and rewards. High-demand, low-control work environments are associated with increased risks of cardiovascular disease, mental health disorders, and musculoskeletal problems (63). Shift work and non-standard working hours disrupt circadian rhythms and are associated with sleep disorders, digestive problems, and increased accident risk (64).

Workplace violence affects workers across many sectors, with particularly high risks in healthcare, education, retail, and law enforcement. The ILO estimates that 8% of workers globally experience physical workplace violence annually, while psychological violence including bullying, harassment, and intimidation affects even larger numbers of workers (65). Women workers face additional risks of sexual harassment and gender-based violence in many workplace settings (66).

Job insecurity and precarious employment conditions have become increasingly common globally, contributing to chronic stress and poor mental health outcomes. Gig economy workers, temporary employees, and those in informal employment often lack job security, benefits, and social protection, creating chronic stress and financial insecurity (67).

## Occupational Disease Prevention

### Exposure Assessment Principles

Effective occupational disease prevention begins with comprehensive exposure assessment to identify, evaluate, and characterize workplace hazards. Exposure assessment involves systematic evaluation of worker contact with chemical, physical, biological, ergonomic, and psychosocial hazards (68). This process requires understanding exposure pathways, dose-response relationships, and vulnerable populations within the workforce (69).

Qualitative exposure assessment provides initial hazard identification through workplace surveys, material safety data sheet review, and basic exposure observations. This approach helps prioritize hazards for more detailed assessment and implement immediate control measures for obvious high-risk exposures (70). Industrial hygienists typically conduct workplace walkthroughs, interview workers and supervisors, and review work processes to identify potential exposure sources (71).

Quantitative exposure assessment involves measurement of specific exposures using standardized sampling and analytical methods. Air sampling for chemical vapors, noise level measurements, and biological monitoring represent common quantitative assessment approaches (72). These measurements must account for temporal and spatial variability in exposures, individual work practices, and effectiveness of existing control measures (73).

Exposure modeling provides cost-effective approaches for estimating exposures in situations where direct measurement is impractical or impossible. Mathematical models can predict exposures based on emission rates, ventilation characteristics, work practices, and environmental conditions (74). Newer approaches incorporating machine learning and artificial intelligence show promise for improving exposure predictions and identifying previously unrecognized exposure patterns (75).

### Hierarchy of Controls

The hierarchy of controls provides a systematic framework for reducing occupational exposures and preventing work-related diseases. This approach prioritizes interventions based on their effectiveness and reliability, with elimination representing the most effective control and personal protective equipment the least reliable (76).

Elimination involves completely removing hazards from the workplace through process changes, material substitution, or technology modification. Examples include replacing toxic solvents with water-based alternatives, automating dangerous tasks to remove worker exposure, and redesigning processes to eliminate hazardous steps (77). While elimination represents the most effective control approach, it may require significant capital investment and technical expertise that challenges implementation in some settings (78).

Substitution involves replacing hazardous materials, processes, or equipment with less dangerous alternatives. This approach has proven highly effective in reducing exposures to asbestos, lead-based paints, and certain organic solvents (79). However, substitution requires careful evaluation to ensure that replacement materials do not introduce new or unexpected risks (80).

Engineering controls modify the work environment to reduce exposures without relying on worker behavior. Ventilation systems, noise enclosures, machine guarding, and ergonomic workstation design represent common engineering approaches (81). Well-designed engineering controls provide reliable protection for multiple workers simultaneously and typically require minimal ongoing maintenance once properly installed (82).

Administrative controls involve changes in work practices, policies, and training to reduce exposures. Job rotation to limit individual exposure duration, development of safe work procedures, and training programs represent administrative control approaches (83). These controls rely heavily on consistent implementation and worker compliance, making them less reliable than elimination, substitution, or engineering controls (84).

Personal protective equipment (PPE) provides the last line of defense when other control measures are insufficient or impractical. Respirators, hearing protection, safety glasses, and protective clothing can be effective when properly selected, fitted, maintained, and used (85). However, PPE places the burden of protection on individual workers and may fail due to improper use, equipment failure, or inadequate maintenance (86).

### Surveillance Systems

Occupational health surveillance systems provide essential data for identifying work-related health problems, evaluating prevention program effectiveness, and guiding policy development. Effective surveillance requires systematic data collection, analysis, and dissemination to appropriate stakeholders (87). The WHO emphasizes that surveillance should be integrated with broader health information systems while maintaining focus on work-related exposures and outcomes (88).

Case-based surveillance involves systematic collection and investigation of individual cases of occupational diseases and injuries. This approach enables detailed characterization of specific health events, identification of contributing factors, and implementation of targeted prevention measures (89). Workers' compensation systems, poison control centers, and occupational disease reporting networks provide important data sources for case-based surveillance (90).

Exposure-based surveillance monitors worker exposures to specific hazards over time, enabling early intervention before health effects occur. Industrial hygiene monitoring programs, biological monitoring initiatives, and health screening programs represent exposure-based surveillance approaches (91). This type of surveillance is particularly valuable for identifying trends in exposure levels and evaluating the effectiveness of control measures (92).

Sentinel surveillance utilizes selected healthcare providers, employers, or institutions to report work-related health conditions. This approach can be cost-effective for rare conditions or in settings with limited surveillance infrastructure (93). Healthcare provider-based surveillance networks have proven effective for identifying occupational asthma, lead poisoning, and other conditions that may not be recognized without specific surveillance efforts (94).

Population-based surveillance examines work-related health outcomes in defined populations, typically through surveys, administrative databases, or population health registries. This approach enables estimation of disease burden, identification of high-risk groups, and evaluation of population-level trends (95). National health surveys increasingly include occupational health indicators, providing valuable population-level surveillance data (96).

## Workplace Injury Prevention

### Safety Management Systems

Comprehensive safety management systems provide structured approaches to preventing workplace injuries through systematic hazard identification, risk assessment, and control implementation. These systems emphasize proactive prevention rather than reactive responses to injuries after they occur (97). Effective safety management requires leadership commitment, worker participation, systematic hazard identification, and continuous improvement processes (98).

Safety management system components typically include policy development, hazard identification and risk assessment, incident investigation, training programs, and performance monitoring. Written safety policies establish organizational expectations and provide frameworks for decision-making about workplace safety (99). These policies must be supported by adequate resources, clear accountability structures, and consistent enforcement to be effective (100).

Hazard identification and risk assessment processes systematically evaluate workplace conditions to identify potential injury sources and assess their likelihood and severity. Job safety analysis, safety audits, and worker safety surveys provide structured approaches for identifying hazards that may not be obvious during casual workplace observations (101). Risk assessment matrices help prioritize hazards based on potential consequences and probability of occurrence (102).

Incident investigation systems examine workplace injuries and near-misses to identify contributing factors and prevent similar events. Effective investigation focuses on systems failures rather than individual blame, seeking to understand how workplace conditions, procedures, and management systems contributed to the incident (103). Root cause analysis techniques help identify underlying causes that may not be immediately apparent (104).

Training programs ensure that workers understand safety procedures, recognize hazards, and know how to protect themselves and others. Effective safety training is interactive, job-specific, and culturally appropriate for the workforce (105). Training evaluation should assess not only knowledge acquisition but also behavior change and injury reduction outcomes (106).

### Behavioral Safety Approaches

Behavioral safety approaches focus on influencing worker actions and decisions to reduce injury risk. These approaches recognize that individual behavior represents an important component of workplace safety while acknowledging that behavior occurs within organizational and environmental contexts (107). Successful behavioral safety programs address both individual factors and organizational systems that influence safety behavior (108).

Safety climate represents workers' shared perceptions about the importance of safety in their organization. Positive safety climate is associated with reduced injury rates and increased safety behavior compliance (109). Factors contributing to positive safety climate include management commitment to safety, supervisor support for safe practices, open communication about safety concerns, and fair treatment of workers who report safety problems (110).

Safety incentive programs attempt to motivate safe behavior through rewards and recognition. However, these programs must be carefully designed to avoid creating perverse incentives that discourage injury reporting or encourage unsafe shortcuts to achieve safety goals (111). Effective incentive programs focus on leading indicators such as hazard reporting and safety training participation rather than lagging indicators like injury rates (112).

Peer safety programs engage workers as safety observers and coaches for their colleagues. These programs can be effective because workers often have detailed knowledge of job-specific risks and may be more credible sources of safety information than supervisors or safety professionals (113). Successful peer programs require training in observation skills, feedback techniques, and conflict resolution (114).

Safety communication programs ensure that safety information is effectively shared throughout the organization. Multiple communication channels including toolbox talks, safety meetings, bulletin boards, and digital platforms help reinforce safety messages (115). Communication must be bidirectional, encouraging worker input and feedback about safety concerns and suggestions (116).

## Industrial Hygiene

### Exposure Monitoring Strategies

Industrial hygiene exposure monitoring provides quantitative assessment of worker exposures to chemical, physical, and biological hazards. Effective monitoring strategies balance the need for accurate exposure assessment with practical considerations including cost, technical feasibility, and worker acceptance (117). Strategic approaches to exposure monitoring help optimize resource allocation while ensuring adequate protection for workers (118).

Personal exposure monitoring involves placing sampling devices on individual workers to measure their actual exposures during work activities. This approach provides the most relevant exposure data for health risk assessment and regulatory compliance evaluation (119). Personal monitoring accounts for variations in work practices, locations, and tasks that may not be captured by area monitoring approaches (120).

Area monitoring measures contaminant levels at fixed locations in the workplace, providing information about emission sources and general workplace conditions. While area monitoring may not reflect individual worker exposures as accurately as personal monitoring, it provides valuable information for evaluating control system effectiveness and identifying emission sources (121). Area monitoring can also be more cost-effective for routine surveillance of workplace conditions (122).

Exposure modeling techniques predict worker exposures based on emission rates, workplace characteristics, and work practices. These approaches can be particularly valuable for evaluating proposed control measures, assessing exposures in situations where direct measurement is difficult, and prioritizing monitoring resources (123). Computational fluid dynamics models and statistical exposure models represent increasingly sophisticated approaches to exposure prediction (124).

Biological monitoring measures contaminants or their metabolites in biological samples such as blood, urine, or breath. This approach provides information about absorbed dose rather than external exposure, potentially providing more relevant data for health risk assessment (125). Biological monitoring can also identify exposures that occur through skin absorption or ingestion that might be missed by air monitoring (126).

### Ventilation Design and Control

Ventilation systems provide essential engineering controls for reducing airborne exposures in many workplace settings. Effective ventilation design requires understanding of contaminant sources, airflow patterns, and worker activities to optimize contaminant removal while maintaining acceptable working conditions (127). Proper ventilation can dramatically reduce exposures to dusts, vapors, gases, and biological aerosols (128).

Local exhaust ventilation captures contaminants at their source before they can disperse into the workplace atmosphere. Hood design, duct layout, and fan selection must be carefully matched to specific applications to achieve effective contaminant control (129). Capture velocity requirements vary based on contaminant properties, generation rate, and cross-drafts that may interfere with contaminant capture (130).

Dilution ventilation reduces contaminant concentrations by mixing contaminated air with clean air. This approach is typically used when contaminants are generated from multiple sources or when local exhaust ventilation is not practical (131). Dilution ventilation requires adequate air exchange rates and proper air distribution patterns to be effective (132).

Natural ventilation utilizes wind and temperature differences to provide air movement without mechanical systems. This approach can be effective in appropriate climates and building designs, particularly for reducing heat stress and providing general air quality improvements (133). However, natural ventilation may be inadequate for controlling specific chemical exposures or maintaining consistent environmental conditions (134).

Ventilation system maintenance is critical for maintaining effective contaminant control over time. Regular filter replacement, duct cleaning, and fan maintenance ensure that systems continue to perform as designed (135). Performance testing and monitoring verify that ventilation systems achieve design specifications and identify problems before they compromise worker protection (136).

### Personal Protective Equipment Selection and Use

Personal protective equipment serves as an essential component of comprehensive exposure control programs, particularly when engineering and administrative controls are insufficient to reduce exposures to acceptable levels. Effective PPE programs require proper hazard assessment, equipment selection, training, maintenance, and monitoring to ensure adequate worker protection (137).

Respiratory protection programs protect workers from inhalation of hazardous airborne contaminants including dusts, vapors, gases, and biological aerosols. Respirator selection must consider contaminant properties, exposure concentrations, work requirements, and worker characteristics (138). Air-purifying respirators filter contaminants from ambient air, while supplied-air respirators provide clean breathing air from an external source (139).

Skin and eye protection prevents contact with corrosive chemicals, infectious materials, and physical hazards. Chemical-resistant gloves, protective clothing, safety glasses, and face shields must be selected based on specific hazard characteristics and exposure conditions (140). Breakthrough time, permeation rate, and degradation resistance represent important selection criteria for chemical protective equipment (141).

Hearing protection prevents noise-induced hearing loss through attenuation of sound levels reaching the inner ear. Earplugs and earmuffs provide different attenuation characteristics and may be more or less suitable depending on noise characteristics, work requirements, and worker preferences (142). Noise reduction rating (NRR) values provide standardized measures of hearing protector effectiveness (143).

PPE training ensures that workers understand when protection is needed, how to properly use and maintain equipment, and what limitations apply to specific protective devices. Training must be job-specific and include hands-on practice with actual equipment under simulated work conditions (144). Refresher training and competency assessment help ensure continued effective use over time (145).

## Occupational Health Services

### Workplace Health Programs

Comprehensive workplace health programs integrate health promotion, disease prevention, and healthcare delivery to improve worker health outcomes and organizational performance. These programs address both occupational and non-occupational health risks, recognizing that worker health is influenced by multiple factors both inside and outside the workplace (146). Effective programs require leadership support, worker participation, and integration with broader organizational policies and practices (147).

Health promotion components focus on lifestyle factors including nutrition, physical activity, stress management, and substance use prevention. Workplace wellness programs can be particularly effective because they reach large populations of working adults who may have limited access to health promotion resources elsewhere (148). Successful programs offer multiple intervention modalities and address environmental factors that support healthy behaviors (149).

Preventive healthcare services include health screenings, immunizations, and early detection programs for chronic diseases. Workplace-based preventive services can improve access and convenience for workers while potentially reducing healthcare costs and improving productivity (150). However, these services must be carefully designed to protect worker privacy and prevent discrimination based on health status (151).

Occupational health surveillance monitors worker health in relation to workplace exposures and job demands. This includes pre-placement examinations, periodic health surveillance, and exit examinations designed to detect work-related health effects (152). Surveillance programs must be based on scientific evidence of exposure-health relationships and must protect worker confidentiality (153).

Return-to-work programs help workers recover from injuries and illnesses while minimizing disability duration and promoting successful job reintegration. Effective programs provide modified duties, graduated return schedules, and coordination between healthcare providers, supervisors, and workers (154). Early intervention and case management approaches can significantly reduce disability duration and associated costs (155).

### Integration with Healthcare Systems

Integration of occupational health services with broader healthcare systems can improve accessibility, efficiency, and quality of care for workers while reducing costs and administrative burden. This integration is particularly important in LMICs where specialized occupational health services may be limited and where primary healthcare systems serve as the main point of healthcare contact for workers (156).

Primary healthcare integration involves training primary care providers to recognize and manage work-related health conditions while incorporating occupational health screening into routine healthcare visits. This approach can significantly expand access to occupational health services, particularly in rural and underserved areas (157). However, primary care providers need appropriate training and support to effectively address occupational health issues (158).

Specialist referral networks ensure that workers with complex occupational health conditions receive appropriate specialized care when needed. These networks may include occupational medicine physicians, pulmonologists, dermatologists, and other specialists with expertise in work-related health conditions (159). Telemedicine approaches can help extend specialist expertise to remote areas where such services are not locally available (160).

Information system integration enables sharing of relevant health information between occupational health services and other healthcare providers while protecting worker privacy. Electronic health records can facilitate communication between providers and improve continuity of care (161). However, integration must address concerns about employer access to healthcare information and potential discrimination (162).

Quality assurance programs ensure that occupational health services meet professional standards and achieve desired health outcomes. Accreditation programs, clinical practice guidelines, and outcome monitoring help maintain service quality and identify opportunities for improvement (163). Professional development and continuing education support ongoing competency among occupational health service providers (164).

**Case Study 21.1: Brazil's National Program for Silicosis Elimination**

Brazil's approach to silicosis prevention demonstrates how developing countries can implement comprehensive occupational disease prevention programs despite resource constraints and competing health priorities. Silicosis, caused by crystalline silica exposure in mining, construction, and manufacturing, represented a significant occupational health problem in Brazil, with particularly high rates among sandblasting workers and miners (165).

The National Program for Silicosis Elimination, launched in 2001, established ambitious goals to eliminate silicosis as a public health problem through multi-sectoral collaboration between government agencies, employers, workers, and civil society organizations. The program combined regulatory approaches, technical assistance, worker education, and health surveillance to address silicosis comprehensively (166).

Regulatory initiatives included banning sandblasting with silica sand, establishing stricter exposure limits, and mandating health surveillance for exposed workers. The Ministry of Labor implemented enhanced enforcement activities with increased workplace inspections and significant penalties for non-compliance (167). These regulatory changes were supported by technical guidance documents and training programs for labor inspectors (168).

Technical assistance programs helped employers, particularly small and medium enterprises, implement effective exposure control measures. Government agencies provided guidance on alternative abrasive materials, improved ventilation systems, and respiratory protection programs (169). This support was critical for smaller employers who lacked technical expertise and resources to independently implement effective controls (170).

Worker education initiatives raised awareness about silicosis risks and promoted worker participation in prevention efforts. Trade unions played key roles in educating members about exposure risks and advocating for improved workplace conditions (171). Health education materials were developed in multiple languages and formats to reach diverse worker populations (172).

Health surveillance systems monitored silicosis trends and identified high-risk populations requiring intervention. The program established standardized protocols for medical examination, chest radiography, and case reporting (173). Surveillance data were used to target enforcement activities and evaluate program effectiveness over time (174).

Evaluation results demonstrated significant improvements in silica exposure control and reductions in silicosis incidence. Workplace exposure monitoring showed substantial decreases in silica concentrations in industries covered by the program (175). Medical surveillance data indicated reduced rates of new silicosis cases, particularly among younger workers entering high-risk occupations (176).

The program's success resulted from strong political commitment, adequate resource allocation, and effective collaboration among multiple stakeholders. Key factors included leadership from government agencies, active participation by worker and employer organizations, and integration with existing occupational health infrastructure (177). However, challenges remain in reaching informal sector workers and ensuring sustained compliance with preventive measures (178).

**Case Study 21.2: Singapore's Construction Safety Transformation**

Singapore's transformation of construction safety demonstrates how systematic approaches combining regulation, technology, and economic incentives can dramatically reduce workplace injury rates in high-risk industries. Construction historically represented one of the most dangerous occupations in Singapore, with fatality rates significantly higher than other economic sectors (179).

The national construction safety strategy launched in the early 2000s established ambitious targets for injury reduction and implemented comprehensive interventions addressing technical, organizational, and behavioral factors contributing to construction injuries. The program emphasized prevention through design, safety management system implementation, and performance-based incentives (180).

Regulatory reforms strengthened safety requirements for construction projects while streamlining compliance processes. The Building and Construction Authority implemented risk-based inspection approaches focusing resources on high-risk projects and contractors with poor safety performance (181). Enhanced penalties for safety violations provided strong economic incentives for compliance while technical support helped contractors improve safety management capabilities (182).

Technology integration played a crucial role in improving construction safety monitoring and hazard control. Digital surveillance systems, wearable sensors, and building information modeling enabled real-time monitoring of safety conditions and worker behavior (183). Automated fall protection systems, remote-controlled equipment, and prefabrication approaches reduced worker exposure to traditional construction hazards (184).

Safety management system requirements mandated systematic approaches to hazard identification, risk assessment, and control implementation. Contractors were required to demonstrate competency in safety management through certification programs and regular audits (185). Safety management systems emphasized worker participation, continuous improvement, and integration of safety considerations into all project phases (186).

Economic incentive programs provided financial rewards for superior safety performance while imposing costs for poor performance. Government contracts included safety performance criteria in contractor selection and payment systems (187). Insurance premium adjustments reflected individual contractor safety records, creating market-based incentives for safety improvement (188).

Worker training and certification programs ensured that construction workers possessed necessary safety knowledge and skills. Mandatory training requirements covered general construction safety as well as trade-specific hazards and control measures (189). Competency-based certification systems verified worker capabilities before allowing access to high-risk work activities (190).

Results demonstrated dramatic improvements in construction safety performance over the program implementation period. Fatal injury rates declined by over 70% while total recordable injury rates fell by more than 50% (191). Economic analyses indicated that safety improvements generated substantial returns on investment through reduced workers' compensation costs, project delays, and regulatory penalties (192).

Critical success factors included strong government leadership, industry collaboration, and sustained commitment to safety improvement over multiple years. The program benefited from Singapore's strong regulatory capacity, advanced technological infrastructure, and collaborative relationships between government and industry (193). However, maintaining improvements requires ongoing attention to emerging hazards and continuous adaptation of safety management approaches (194).

**Case Study 21.3: Ethiopia's Integrated Occupational Health Services**

Ethiopia's integration of occupational health services into primary healthcare demonstrates how LMICs can expand access to worker health protection despite limited specialist resources and competing health priorities. Ethiopia's largely agricultural economy, growing industrial sector, and significant informal employment create diverse occupational health challenges (195).

The integrated occupational health model developed through collaboration between the Ministry of Health, ILO, and international development partners. The approach built upon Ethiopia's existing Health Extension Program infrastructure while adding specialized occupational health capabilities (196). Primary healthcare facilities received training and resources to address basic occupational health needs while referral systems connected workers to specialized services when needed (197).

Health Extension Workers received additional training in occupational health risk assessment, basic exposure monitoring, and worker health education. These community-based health workers were particularly well-positioned to address agricultural worker health risks given their presence in rural communities and understanding of local working conditions (198). Training materials were developed in local languages and adapted to address region-specific occupational health priorities (199).

Primary healthcare facilities incorporated occupational health screening into routine healthcare visits while establishing specialized occupational health clinics for more complex cases. Healthcare providers received training in recognizing work-related health conditions, conducting exposure assessments, and providing appropriate treatment and referral (200). Medical record systems were modified to capture occupational information and track work-related health conditions (201).

Community-based interventions addressed occupational health risks that could not be effectively managed through healthcare facility-based approaches. These included environmental health assessments, workplace safety training, and promotion of improved agricultural practices (202). Community health committees were established to identify occupational health priorities and coordinate prevention activities (203).

Referral systems connected primary healthcare providers with specialized occupational health expertise when needed. Telemedicine approaches enabled remote consultation with occupational medicine specialists for complex cases (204). Mobile clinic programs brought specialized services to remote rural areas with limited access to facility-based care (205).

Health surveillance systems monitored occupational health trends and evaluated program effectiveness. Standardized data collection protocols enabled tracking of work-related health conditions while protecting patient confidentiality (206). Surveillance data informed resource allocation decisions and helped identify emerging occupational health problems (207).

Evaluation results showed improved recognition and management of work-related health conditions along with increased access to occupational health services. Healthcare providers demonstrated improved knowledge of occupational health principles and increased confidence in managing work-related health problems (208). Community members reported increased awareness of occupational health risks and improved adoption of protective behaviors (209).

Program sustainability required ongoing technical support, continued funding for training and supervision, and integration with broader health system strengthening efforts. Key challenges included competing demands on healthcare provider time, limited availability of occupational health experts, and need for ongoing resource mobilization (210). Success factors included strong government support, effective partnership coordination, and adaptation of interventions to local contexts (211).

**Case Study 21.4: India's Informal Sector Worker Health Initiative**

India's comprehensive approach to informal sector worker health addresses the unique challenges of protecting workers who lack traditional employment relationships and social protection coverage. With over 90% of India's workforce employed in the informal economy, traditional occupational health approaches focused on formal sector employers are insufficient to protect most workers (212).

The Self-Employed Women's Association (SEWA) model demonstrates how community-based organizations can provide occupational health services to informal sector workers. SEWA developed comprehensive programs addressing street vendors, domestic workers, agricultural laborers, and home-based workers across multiple Indian states (213). The approach combines health education, preventive services, treatment, and advocacy for improved working conditions (214).

Community health workers recruited from informal worker communities provide peer education, basic health screening, and linkages to healthcare services. These workers receive training in recognizing occupational health hazards, providing first aid, and promoting protective behaviors (215). Peer educator approaches are particularly effective because they build trust and understanding within worker communities (216).

Mobile health clinics bring healthcare services directly to worker communities, overcoming barriers related to location, time, and cost that limit access to facility-based care. These clinics provide health screening, treatment for common occupational health problems, and referral to specialized services when needed (217). Mobile unit scheduling is coordinated with worker availability and community gathering times (218).

Occupational safety training programs address specific hazards faced by different categories of informal workers. Street vendor programs focus on traffic safety, air pollution exposure, and ergonomic hazards from prolonged standing (219). Domestic worker training addresses chemical exposures, musculoskeletal risks, and workplace violence prevention (220). Training materials are developed in local languages and use participatory education methods (221).

Microinsurance programs provide financial protection for informal workers facing health problems and work-related injuries. These community-managed insurance schemes pool resources to provide healthcare coverage and income replacement during periods of illness or injury (222). Insurance programs are designed to be affordable and accessible to workers with irregular incomes and limited financial resources (223).

Advocacy efforts promote policy changes to improve working conditions and expand social protection coverage for informal workers. SEWA has successfully advocated for recognition of informal workers in national labor legislation, improved access to healthcare services, and development of social protection schemes (224). Advocacy combines grassroots organizing with policy research and engagement with government officials (225).

Multi-stakeholder partnerships bring together community organizations, healthcare providers, government agencies, and research institutions to address informal worker health comprehensively. These partnerships enable resource sharing, coordinate service delivery, and advocate for policy changes (226). Research partnerships help document health problems and evaluate intervention effectiveness (227).

Results demonstrate improved health outcomes among participating informal workers along with increased access to healthcare services and social protection. Participants showed improved knowledge of occupational health risks and increased adoption of protective behaviors (228). Healthcare utilization increased among program participants while catastrophic health expenditures decreased (229).

Scaling challenges include resource mobilization, capacity building for community organizations, and integration with government health and social protection systems. Successful scaling requires adaptation to local contexts while maintaining core program components (230). Government support and policy changes are essential for achieving population-level impact (231).

## Global Labor Standards and Worker Protection

### International Labour Organization Framework

The International Labour Organization provides the primary global framework for establishing and promoting labor standards related to occupational health and safety. Founded in 1919, the ILO operates on the principle that labor is not a commodity and that working conditions should ensure human dignity and social justice (232). ILO conventions and recommendations establish international standards that guide national labor legislation and workplace practices (233).

The ILO Constitution establishes the right to safe and healthy working conditions as a fundamental principle and right at work, alongside freedom of association, elimination of forced labor, abolition of child labor, and elimination of employment discrimination (234). This framework recognizes that worker safety and health are essential for human dignity and sustainable development (235).

Core ILO conventions related to occupational safety and health include the Occupational Safety and Health Convention (C155) and the Occupational Health Services Convention (C161). These conventions establish basic principles for national occupational safety and health policies, including employer responsibilities, worker rights, and government oversight functions (236). Additional conventions address specific hazards including chemicals, construction, mining, and agriculture (237).

The ILO Global Strategy on Occupational Safety and Health promotes a preventive safety and health culture through national programs, workplace-level implementation, and international cooperation. This strategy emphasizes the business case for occupational safety and health while recognizing the fundamental human right to safe work (238). Key elements include leadership commitment, worker participation, systematic approaches to hazard management, and continuous improvement (239).

Technical assistance programs help member countries develop and implement effective occupational safety and health systems. The ILO provides policy guidance, capacity building, and technical support for developing legislation, building institutional capacity, and improving workplace practices (240). South-South cooperation initiatives enable knowledge sharing between countries facing similar challenges (241).

### Supply Chain Responsibility

Global supply chains create complex relationships between multinational corporations and workers in developing countries, raising important questions about responsibility for worker health and safety across organizational and national boundaries. International buyers increasingly recognize that their purchasing practices and supplier requirements significantly influence working conditions in supplier facilities (242).

Corporate social responsibility approaches vary from voluntary initiatives to mandatory due diligence requirements. Some multinational corporations have implemented comprehensive supplier auditing programs, capacity building initiatives, and contractual requirements for occupational safety and health compliance (243). However, voluntary approaches may be insufficient to ensure consistent protection for all workers in global supply chains (244).

Multi-stakeholder initiatives bring together brands, suppliers, trade unions, and civil society organizations to address supply chain labor issues. The Bangladesh Accord on Fire and Building Safety, established following the Rana Plaza factory collapse, demonstrates how binding agreements can drive substantial improvements in workplace safety (245). Similar initiatives in other sectors and countries show promise for protecting worker rights and safety (246).

Due diligence legislation in some high-income countries requires companies to assess and address human rights risks, including occupational health and safety, in their global operations and supply chains. These laws create legal obligations for companies to identify, prevent, and remediate adverse impacts on workers (247). Implementation challenges include defining appropriate standards, ensuring effective monitoring, and providing meaningful remedies for affected workers (248).

Capacity building programs help suppliers in LMICs improve their occupational safety and health management capabilities. These programs may include technical assistance, training, financing for safety improvements, and long-term partnership arrangements (249). Effective programs recognize that sustainable improvements require addressing underlying capacity constraints rather than simply imposing compliance requirements (250).

Worker voice and participation represent essential components of effective supply chain responsibility initiatives. Programs that enable workers to raise safety concerns, participate in workplace safety committees, and access grievance mechanisms are more likely to achieve sustainable improvements (251). International trade union networks and labor rights organizations play important roles in supporting worker participation in global supply chains (252).

## Special Populations in Occupational Health

### Women Workers

Women workers face unique occupational health challenges related to biological differences, social roles, and workplace discrimination. Gender-based occupational health disparities reflect complex interactions between biological factors, work exposures, social determinants, and healthcare access (253). Addressing these disparities requires understanding how gender influences occupational health risks and outcomes (254).

Reproductive health represents a significant concern for women workers exposed to chemicals, physical agents, and work organization factors that may affect fertility, pregnancy outcomes, and child health. Lead exposure, organic solvents, pesticides, and ionizing radiation present particular risks for reproductive health (255). However, protective policies must avoid discrimination against women workers while ensuring appropriate protection for reproductive health (256).

Musculoskeletal disorders affect women workers disproportionately in many occupations, reflecting both occupational exposures and gender-based differences in work organization. Women are overrepresented in occupations involving repetitive motions, prolonged standing, and patient handling (257). Workplace design that accounts for anthropometric differences and provides adjustable equipment can reduce these risks (258).

Sexual harassment and workplace violence affect women workers across many occupations and countries. These experiences can cause immediate psychological trauma as well as long-term mental health effects (259). Prevention requires comprehensive policies, training programs, and enforcement mechanisms that create safe work environments for all workers (260).

Work-life balance challenges disproportionately affect women workers who often bear primary responsibility for childcare and elder care. Shift work, long hours, and inflexible scheduling can create conflicts between work and family responsibilities that affect both worker wellbeing and family health (261). Family-friendly workplace policies can help address these challenges while benefiting both workers and employers (262).

### Child Labor and Young Workers

Child labor affects an estimated 152 million children globally, with many working in hazardous conditions that pose immediate and long-term health risks. The worst forms of child labor, as defined by ILO Convention 182, include work that is likely to harm children's health, safety, or morals (263). Agriculture represents the largest sector employing child laborers, accounting for approximately 70% of all child labor (264).

Health effects of child labor include increased risks of injury, exposure to hazardous substances, musculoskeletal problems, and psychosocial stress. Children are particularly vulnerable to occupational hazards due to their physical and cognitive development, smaller body size, and limited understanding of risks (265). Long-term health effects may include stunted growth, educational deficits, and increased risks of adult chronic diseases (266).

Young worker protection requires age-appropriate work restrictions, enhanced safety measures, and comprehensive training programs. International standards prohibit employment of children under minimum age and restrict hazardous work for adolescents under 18 (267). Where young people are legally employed, additional protections including health surveillance, reduced working hours, and enhanced supervision are appropriate (268).

School-to-work transition programs help young people develop necessary skills while protecting their health and safety. These programs should include occupational safety and health training, career guidance, and support for continued education (269). Apprenticeship programs that combine education with supervised work experience can provide pathways to decent employment (270).

Enforcement challenges include identifying child labor in informal settings, providing alternative income sources for families dependent on children's earnings, and ensuring access to quality education. Effective approaches require coordination between labor inspection, education, and social protection systems (271). Community-based monitoring and social mobilization can help identify and address child labor in areas where formal enforcement is limited (272).

### Migrant Workers

Migrant workers face elevated occupational health risks due to language barriers, unfamiliarity with local safety regulations, precarious employment relationships, and limited access to healthcare and social protection. An estimated 164 million workers are employed outside their country of origin, with many working in high-risk occupations including construction, agriculture, and domestic work (273).

Language and cultural barriers limit migrant workers' ability to understand safety training, recognize workplace hazards, and communicate safety concerns to supervisors. Safety training and information materials must be provided in languages that workers understand and must account for different cultural backgrounds and educational levels (274). Peer support programs using workers from similar backgrounds can help overcome these barriers (275).

Employment relationships for migrant workers are often characterized by temporary contracts, limited job security, and dependence on employers for visa status. These factors may discourage workers from reporting safety problems or seeking healthcare for work-related injuries (276). Labor migration policies should ensure that migrant workers have the same occupational health and safety protections as domestic workers (277).

Access to healthcare represents a significant challenge for many migrant workers, particularly those in irregular employment situations. Barriers include cost, language, documentation requirements, and fear of deportation (278). Healthcare systems need culturally appropriate services and policies that ensure access regardless of immigration status (279).

Social protection coverage for migrant workers varies significantly across countries and may not provide adequate protection for work-related injuries and diseases. Portability of benefits, coordination between social protection systems, and inclusion of migrant workers in national programs represent important policy priorities (280). Bilateral and multilateral agreements can help ensure social protection coverage for migrant workers (281).

### Informal Sector Workers

Informal sector workers represent approximately 60% of the global workforce but typically lack access to traditional occupational health and safety protections. These workers are employed in small enterprises, self-employment, domestic work, and other arrangements that fall outside formal labor regulations (282). Occupational health approaches for informal workers require innovative strategies that address their unique circumstances and constraints (283).

Regulatory coverage for informal workers is often limited or non-existent, creating gaps in occupational health protection. Traditional regulatory approaches based on formal employer-employee relationships may not be appropriate for self-employed workers, family enterprises, and other informal arrangements (284). New regulatory frameworks should consider the diversity of informal work arrangements while providing appropriate protection (285).

Community-based approaches can provide occupational health services to informal workers who lack access to employer-based programs. Worker organizations, cooperatives, and community health programs can deliver training, health services, and advocacy for improved working conditions (286). These approaches build on existing social networks and community institutions (287).

Microfinance and social protection initiatives can help informal workers invest in safety improvements and protect against income loss from work-related injuries. Microinsurance schemes, savings programs, and social safety nets designed for informal workers can provide financial protection during health emergencies (288). These programs require careful design to be affordable and accessible to workers with irregular incomes (289).

Technology applications offer new opportunities for reaching informal workers with occupational health information and services. Mobile phone-based training programs, digital health platforms, and online safety resources can overcome geographic and time barriers (290). However, digital approaches must account for varying levels of technology access and digital literacy among informal workers (291).

## Future Directions and Emerging Challenges

The future of occupational health faces evolving challenges related to technological change, demographic transitions, climate change, and economic globalization. Artificial intelligence, automation, and digital technologies are transforming work organization while creating new categories of occupational health risks (292). Aging workforces in high-income countries present challenges related to age-related health conditions and workplace accommodation needs (293).

Climate change increasingly affects occupational health through heat stress, extreme weather events, air pollution, and vector-borne disease transmission. Outdoor workers in agriculture, construction, and other sectors face particular risks from rising temperatures and changing weather patterns (294). Adaptation strategies must address both immediate risks and long-term changes in working conditions (295).

Gig economy and platform work create new challenges for occupational health protection as traditional employment relationships evolve. These work arrangements often fall outside existing regulatory frameworks while creating new types of psychosocial and physical health risks (296). Policy responses must balance worker protection with economic flexibility and innovation (297).

Mental health and psychosocial factors are gaining recognition as major occupational health challenges requiring comprehensive prevention and intervention approaches. Work-related stress, depression, and anxiety represent growing components of occupational disease burden in many countries (298). Workplace mental health programs must address both individual factors and organizational conditions that contribute to mental health problems (299).

Global health security considerations increasingly recognize occupational health as essential for pandemic preparedness and response. Healthcare workers, essential service providers, and other occupational groups face elevated risks during health emergencies (300). Occupational health infrastructure and emergency preparedness must be strengthened to protect workers and maintain essential services during crises (301).

## Conclusion

Occupational health and safety represents a fundamental component of global public health that affects billions of workers worldwide and their families and communities. The global burden of work-related diseases and injuries remains substantial, with significant variations across regions, economic sectors, and worker populations. Effective prevention requires comprehensive approaches combining hazard identification, exposure assessment, control implementation, and health surveillance.

The hierarchy of controls provides a systematic framework for reducing occupational exposures, with elimination and substitution representing the most effective approaches. Engineering controls, administrative measures, and personal protective equipment serve important roles in comprehensive prevention strategies. Industrial hygiene principles guide exposure assessment and control selection, while occupational health services provide essential prevention, treatment, and rehabilitation services.

Global labor standards established by the ILO provide frameworks for national legislation and workplace practices, while supply chain responsibility initiatives extend protection to workers in global production networks. Special populations including women workers, young workers, migrant workers, and informal sector workers face unique challenges requiring targeted interventions and innovative service delivery approaches.

Future challenges related to technological change, demographic transitions, climate change, and evolving work arrangements will require adaptive and innovative responses from the occupational health community. Success in protecting worker health globally depends on sustained commitment from governments, employers, workers, and civil society organizations to create safe and healthy working conditions for all workers.

The case studies presented demonstrate that effective occupational health interventions are possible across diverse economic and social contexts. Brazil's silicosis prevention program shows how developing countries can address specific occupational diseases through comprehensive national programs. Singapore's construction safety transformation demonstrates the potential for dramatic safety improvements through systematic approaches combining technology, regulation, and economic incentives. Ethiopia's integrated occupational health services show how primary healthcare systems can be strengthened to address worker health needs. India's informal sector initiatives demonstrate innovative community-based approaches for protecting workers outside traditional employment relationships.

These examples provide valuable lessons for scaling effective occupational health interventions globally while adapting to local contexts and constraints. Success requires political commitment, adequate resources, multi-stakeholder collaboration, and sustained effort over time. The fundamental principle that all workers deserve safe and healthy working conditions must guide efforts to strengthen occupational health protection globally.
