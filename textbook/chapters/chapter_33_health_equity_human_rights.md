2. **Explain** state obligations under the right to health framework and interpret health equity indicators for identifying disparities

## Introduction

The intersection of health equity and human rights represents one of the most powerful conceptual frameworks in contemporary global public health. While the aspiration for health as a fundamental human entitlement has deep historical roots, the formal articulation of health as a human right emerged in the aftermath of World War II, fundamentally reshaping how the international community conceptualizes state obligations toward population health (1). The Constitution of the World Health Organization, adopted in 1946, proclaimed that "the enjoyment of the highest attainable standard of health is one of the fundamental rights of every human being without distinction of race, religion, political belief, economic or social condition" (2).

This chapter examines the theoretical foundations, practical applications, and contemporary challenges in integrating human rights frameworks with health equity approaches. The right to health provides a normative foundation for addressing health inequities, establishing clear obligations for states and accountability mechanisms for ensuring progress. Health equity, in turn, offers conceptual and measurement tools for identifying and addressing unjust, avoidable differences in health outcomes across population groups (3). Together, these frameworks provide both the moral imperative and practical guidance for transforming health systems to serve all populations fairly.

The significance of this integration cannot be overstated. Global health inequities remain profound, with life expectancy varying by more than 30 years between countries and substantial disparities persisting within countries along lines of income, education, gender, ethnicity, and geography (4). The COVID-19 pandemic starkly revealed these inequities, with marginalized populations experiencing disproportionate morbidity and mortality across all regions (5). Addressing these disparities requires moving beyond technical interventions to confront the structural, political, and economic factors that produce and perpetuate health inequities.

## The Right to Health Framework

### International Legal Foundations

The right to health is enshrined in numerous international and regional human rights instruments, creating a comprehensive legal framework for state obligations. The International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR), adopted in 1966 and ratified by 171 countries, provides the most authoritative articulation, recognizing "the right of everyone to the enjoyment of the highest attainable standard of physical and mental health" (6). Article 12 of the ICESCR specifies that states must take steps including reducing stillbirth and infant mortality, improving environmental and industrial hygiene, preventing and treating epidemic diseases, and creating conditions ensuring medical services for all (6).

The Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights, in General Comment No. 14 (2000), provided extensive interpretation of the right to health, clarifying that it encompasses not only timely and appropriate healthcare but also the underlying determinants of health, including safe water, adequate sanitation, nutrition, housing, healthy working conditions, and access to health-related education and information (7). This interpretation significantly expanded understanding of state obligations beyond healthcare services to encompass the broader conditions necessary for health.

Regional human rights systems have further developed health rights protections. The African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights (1981) recognizes the right to health and obligates states to take necessary measures to protect population health (8). The Additional Protocol to the American Convention on Human Rights in the Area of Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (Protocol of San Salvador, 1988) guarantees the right to health with specific provisions for primary healthcare and immunization (9). The European Social Charter includes provisions on health protection and healthcare access (10).

### Core Content and AAAQ Framework

General Comment No. 14 established the AAAQ framework, identifying four essential elements of the right to health: Availability, Accessibility, Acceptability, and Quality (7). Availability requires functioning public health and healthcare facilities, goods, services, and programs in sufficient quantity. This includes trained health personnel receiving competitive salaries, essential medicines as defined by the WHO Essential Medicines List, and adequate health infrastructure including hospitals, clinics, and community health services.

Accessibility encompasses four overlapping dimensions. Non-discrimination requires that health facilities, goods, and services be accessible to all, especially the most vulnerable and marginalized. Physical accessibility means services must be within safe physical reach for all population segments, including in rural areas. Economic accessibility (affordability) requires that payment for services be based on equity principles, ensuring services are affordable for all. Information accessibility ensures the right to seek, receive, and impart health-related information (7).

Acceptability requires that health facilities, goods, and services respect medical ethics, cultural appropriateness, and sensitivity to gender and life-cycle requirements. Quality demands that facilities, goods, and services be scientifically and medically appropriate and of good quality, including skilled health personnel, approved and unexpired medications, appropriate equipment, and safe water and sanitation (7).

### State Obligations

The right to health imposes three types of obligations on states: to respect, protect, and fulfill. The obligation to respect requires states to refrain from interfering directly or indirectly with the enjoyment of the right to health, including not denying or limiting equal access, not imposing discriminatory practices, and not marketing unsafe drugs (7). The obligation to protect requires states to prevent third parties from interfering with health rights, including regulating private healthcare providers, controlling marketing of harmful substances, and preventing environmental pollution (11).

The obligation to fulfill contains obligations to facilitate, provide, and promote. States must facilitate by taking positive measures enabling individuals and communities to enjoy the right to health. They must provide when individuals or groups cannot realize the right themselves. They must promote by undertaking actions creating, maintaining, and restoring population health, including research, health education, and information campaigns (7).

The principle of progressive realization acknowledges that full realization of economic and social rights cannot be achieved immediately but requires states to take deliberate, concrete, and targeted steps toward full realization using maximum available resources (12). However, certain obligations are immediate, including guaranteeing non-discrimination, taking deliberate steps toward full realization, and not taking retrogressive measures without compelling justification (13).

## Health Equity Concepts and Frameworks

### Equality versus Equity

Understanding the distinction between equality and equity is fundamental to health rights and justice. Equality implies sameness—treating everyone identically regardless of circumstance. Equity, by contrast, recognizes that different people require different resources and approaches to achieve similar outcomes, accounting for systemic barriers and historical disadvantages (14). In health, equality might mean providing identical services to all, while equity requires tailoring resources and interventions to address the specific barriers faced by disadvantaged groups (15).

Health equity has been defined as "the absence of unfair and avoidable or remediable differences in health among population groups defined socially, economically, demographically, or geographically" (3). This definition emphasizes that not all health differences constitute inequities—only those that are unjust and avoidable. Natural biological variation exists, but systematic differences in health outcomes linked to social position, wealth, ethnicity, gender, or geography represent inequities demanding action (16).

The social gradient in health—the phenomenon whereby health outcomes improve incrementally with each step up the socioeconomic ladder—demonstrates that health inequities are not simply a matter of the poor versus the rich but affect everyone below the top of the social hierarchy (17). Research across high-income countries has consistently demonstrated this gradient, with implications for policy extending beyond targeted interventions for the poorest to population-wide approaches addressing social determinants (18).

### Social Justice and Distributive Justice

Health equity is fundamentally a matter of social justice—the fair and just distribution of social goods, opportunities, and burdens across society. John Rawls' theory of justice, while not specifically addressing health, has influenced health equity thinking through principles of fair equality of opportunity and the difference principle, which permits inequalities only when they benefit the least advantaged members of society (19). Norman Daniels extended Rawlsian theory to health, arguing that healthcare and the social determinants of health are special goods because they protect fair equality of opportunity (20).

The capabilities approach, developed by Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum, provides another influential framework, focusing on individuals' capabilities to achieve valued functionings and lead flourishing lives (21). Health is central to capabilities—it enables people to work, learn, participate in social life, and exercise other freedoms. Equity from this perspective means ensuring all individuals have the capability to achieve good health, which may require unequal distribution of resources to those facing greater barriers (22).

Powers and Faden developed a theory of social justice specifically for public health, identifying six dimensions of well-being that justice requires: health, personal security, reasoning, respect, attachment, and self-determination (23). Their framework emphasizes that health is both instrumentally valuable for other dimensions and intrinsically important for well-being, and that systematic disadvantages across multiple dimensions compound to create profound injustice.

### Intersectionality and Cumulative Disadvantage

Intersectionality, a concept originating in Black feminist scholarship and developed by legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw, has become essential for understanding health inequities (24). Intersectionality recognizes that individuals hold multiple social identities simultaneously—such as race, gender, class, disability, and sexuality—and that these identities interact to produce unique experiences of privilege or disadvantage that cannot be understood by examining any single identity in isolation (25).

Applied to health equity, intersectionality reveals how multiple forms of discrimination and marginalization compound to create heightened vulnerability and worse health outcomes. For example, the health experiences of low-income indigenous women cannot be fully understood by separately examining the effects of poverty, indigeneity, and gender—the intersection produces distinct patterns of exposure to risk factors, barriers to care, and health outcomes (26). Research demonstrates that intersecting social positions create multiplicative rather than merely additive effects on health (27).

Cumulative disadvantage theory extends this understanding across the life course, showing how disadvantages accumulate over time and across generations. Early life disadvantages in nutrition, education, and social environment shape adult health, which in turn influences the next generation through biological, social, and economic pathways (28). Addressing health inequities therefore requires interventions at multiple life stages and attention to intergenerational transmission of disadvantage (29).

## Health Equity Measurement

### Health Equity Indicators and Metrics

Measuring health equity requires going beyond population averages to examine the distribution of health across population subgroups. Common metrics include the absolute and relative gap between best-off and worst-off groups, the slope and relative indices of inequality across ordered groups (such as income quintiles or education levels), and the concentration index measuring the extent to which health is concentrated among advantaged or disadvantaged groups (30).

The choice of metric matters substantively and politically. Relative measures (such as rate ratios) may remain stable or increase even as absolute differences narrow, while absolute measures capture actual differences in disease burden (31). The WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health recommended using multiple measures and disaggregating data by relevant equity stratifiers including economic status, education, occupation, gender, ethnicity, place of residence, and other locally relevant dimensions (32).

Composite indices such as the WHO's Health Inequality Monitoring Framework provide standardized approaches for tracking inequities across indicators and stratifiers (33). The framework emphasizes the importance of linking monitoring data to policy action through regular reporting, stakeholder engagement, and integration with health system performance assessment.

### Data Disaggregation Approaches

Effective equity monitoring requires data systems capable of disaggregation by relevant population characteristics. This poses significant challenges, as many health information systems were not designed for equity analysis and lack the necessary individual-level sociodemographic data (34). Civil registration and vital statistics systems, routine health facility data, and health surveys each have strengths and limitations for equity monitoring.

Population-based surveys such as Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS) and Multiple Indicator Cluster Surveys (MICS) have been instrumental in equity monitoring, providing standardized data disaggregated by wealth quintiles, education, urban/rural residence, and other characteristics (35). Asset-based wealth indices enable estimation of household economic status in settings where income data are unreliable. These surveys have enabled global tracking of equity in reproductive, maternal, newborn, and child health indicators.

Increasingly, countries are strengthening routine health information systems to enable equity analysis. This requires capturing relevant sociodemographic data at service delivery points while protecting confidentiality, linking data across sources, and building analytical capacity for equity-focused analysis (36). Digital health technologies offer new opportunities for more granular and timely equity monitoring, though concerns about digital divides and data privacy must be addressed (37).

### Intersectionality Analysis Methods

Analyzing health inequities through an intersectionality lens requires methods capable of examining how multiple social positions interact. Quantitative approaches include interaction analysis in regression models testing whether the effect of one social position differs across levels of another (38). However, interpreting interactions can be complex, and statistical power limitations may obscure significant intersectional effects.

Multilevel analysis of individual heterogeneity and discriminatory accuracy (MAIHDA) provides an approach specifically designed for intersectionality analysis, using random effects models to estimate the extent to which intersectional positions explain variation in health outcomes beyond additive effects of individual social positions (39). This approach has been applied to examine intersections of race, gender, and class in cardiovascular health, mental health, and access to care.

Qualitative and mixed methods research is essential for understanding the mechanisms through which intersecting identities shape health experiences. In-depth interviews, focus groups, and ethnographic research can illuminate how discrimination operates, how marginalized groups navigate health systems, and what interventions might be most effective (40). Community-based participatory research approaches engage affected communities in identifying priorities and interpreting findings.

## Social Determinants and Human Rights

### Underlying Determinants of Health

The right to health encompasses not only healthcare but the underlying determinants of health—the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age that shape health outcomes (7). General Comment No. 14 identifies these underlying determinants as including access to safe and potable water and adequate sanitation, safe and nutritious food, adequate housing, healthy occupational and environmental conditions, and access to health-related education and information (7).

This broad conception of health rights creates linkages with other human rights, including the right to adequate food, the right to housing, the right to education, and the right to work. The indivisibility and interdependence of human rights means that fulfilling the right to health requires attention to these interconnected rights (41). States cannot claim to be fulfilling health rights while neglecting food security, housing adequacy, or environmental protection.

The WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health documented the profound influence of social conditions on health across countries and emphasized that health inequities stem from "the unequal distribution of power, money, and resources" rather than individual behaviors or genetic predisposition (32). The Commission called for action on daily living conditions, structural drivers of inequity, and the political and economic forces shaping both.

### Structural Approaches to Health Equity

Addressing the underlying determinants requires structural approaches that go beyond healthcare interventions and individual behavior change. Structural interventions target the contexts within which individual risks operate—the policies, systems, and environments that shape exposure to health risks and access to protective factors (42). Examples include tobacco taxation, housing quality regulations, minimum wage policies, and urban planning interventions.

Health in All Policies (HiAP) provides a framework for ensuring that health equity considerations are incorporated across government sectors, recognizing that decisions in transportation, education, housing, employment, and other areas significantly affect population health (43). HiAP involves systematic consideration of health implications of policies, seeking synergies across sectors, and avoiding harmful health impacts of non-health policies. Implementation requires governance mechanisms enabling cross-sectoral collaboration and health impact assessment capacity.

Proportionate universalism offers a strategy for balancing universal and targeted approaches, providing universal services with intensity and resources proportionate to need (44). This approach acknowledges that targeting only the poorest may miss the broader social gradient and stigmatize beneficiaries, while purely universal approaches may not adequately address the needs of the most disadvantaged. The goal is to reduce the gradient across the entire population while ensuring adequate reach to those with greatest needs.

## Vulnerable Populations and Discrimination

### Marginalized and Disadvantaged Groups

Certain population groups face systematic marginalization and discrimination that produce profound health inequities. These include, but are not limited to, ethnic and racial minorities, indigenous peoples, migrants and refugees, persons with disabilities, LGBTQ+ individuals, people living in poverty, and those in particular geographic areas such as informal settlements or remote rural regions (45). The specifics of which groups are marginalized vary by context, but the pattern of systematic exclusion and worse health outcomes is consistent globally.

Indigenous peoples experience significant health disparities in virtually every country where data exist, with lower life expectancy, higher infant and maternal mortality, and elevated rates of both infectious and chronic diseases (46). These disparities reflect histories of colonization, dispossession, and ongoing discrimination, as well as disconnection from traditional lands, cultures, and food systems that sustained health for generations. Indigenous health frameworks emphasize self-determination, cultural continuity, and recognition of indigenous knowledge systems as essential for health improvement (47).

People living with disabilities face barriers to healthcare access, exclusion from health promotion, and discrimination within health systems. The Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) recognizes the right to the highest attainable standard of health without discrimination on the basis of disability, requiring accessible health services, disability-specific health services, and prevention of discriminatory denial of health services (48). Despite this framework, people with disabilities continue to experience shorter life expectancy and poorer health outcomes across conditions unrelated to their disability.

### Forms of Discrimination in Health

Discrimination in health operates at multiple levels. Interpersonal discrimination involves biased treatment by healthcare providers, manifesting as dismissal of symptoms, lower quality of care, or overt hostility (49). Research demonstrates that healthcare provider implicit bias affects clinical decision-making, pain management, and patient-provider communication across racial, ethnic, and other dimensions (50).

Institutional discrimination operates through policies, practices, and procedures that systematically disadvantage certain groups, even without individual prejudice. Examples include health facility locations that inadequately serve minority communities, intake procedures that create barriers for people with limited literacy or documentation, and treatment protocols developed without consideration of diverse populations (51). Addressing institutional discrimination requires systematic review of policies and practices through an equity lens.

Structural discrimination refers to the broader social, economic, and political forces that produce and perpetuate inequities. This includes residential segregation limiting access to resources and services, labor market discrimination affecting income and health insurance coverage, and political exclusion limiting voice in health policy decisions (52). Addressing structural discrimination requires interventions beyond the health sector, including anti-discrimination laws, affirmative action policies, and economic redistribution.

## Rights-Based Approaches to Health

### PANEL Principles

Rights-based approaches to health programming operationalize human rights principles in practical interventions. The PANEL framework identifies five core principles: Participation, Accountability, Non-discrimination and equality, Empowerment, and Legality (53). These principles provide guidance for designing, implementing, and evaluating health programs that respect and advance human rights.

Participation requires meaningful involvement of affected populations in decisions affecting their health, including priority-setting, program design, implementation, and evaluation. This goes beyond consultation to include substantive influence over decisions and resources for effective participation. Participation is both instrumentally valuable—programs designed with community input are more likely to be effective and acceptable—and intrinsically important as a matter of dignity and self-determination (54).

Accountability mechanisms ensure that duty-bearers can be held responsible for their obligations. This includes monitoring and transparency regarding health system performance, accessible complaints mechanisms, and remedies for rights violations. Accountability operates at multiple levels, from facility-based mechanisms for addressing service quality concerns to national human rights institutions examining systemic issues to international monitoring through treaty bodies and special procedures (55).

### Rights-Based Programming

Implementing rights-based approaches requires integrating human rights principles throughout the program cycle. In assessment and analysis, this means identifying rights-holders and their claims, duty-bearers and their obligations, and the patterns of discrimination and exclusion underlying health disparities. It requires going beyond needs assessments to rights assessments that examine the structural causes of problems (56).

In program design, rights-based approaches prioritize interventions addressing the most pressing rights claims and the needs of the most marginalized. Programs should strengthen both rights-holders' capacity to claim their rights and duty-bearers' capacity to fulfill their obligations. This dual focus distinguishes rights-based approaches from service delivery approaches that address only immediate needs without building longer-term capacity for rights realization (57).

Monitoring and evaluation in rights-based programming assess not only outcomes but also processes—whether participation was genuine, whether non-discrimination was ensured, whether accountability mechanisms functioned effectively. Human rights indicators may include structural indicators (legal and policy frameworks), process indicators (efforts and resources devoted to rights realization), and outcome indicators (actual enjoyment of rights) (58).

### Capacity Building for Rights-Holders and Duty-Bearers

Effective rights-based approaches require capacity building on both sides of the rights relationship. Rights-holders need awareness of their entitlements, understanding of how to access services and mechanisms for redress, and organizational capacity to advocate collectively. This may include health literacy programs, community organizing support, and leadership development among marginalized groups (59).

Duty-bearers, particularly health workers and managers, require training on human rights principles, understanding of their legal obligations, and skills for implementing rights-based approaches in practice. This includes respectful care, informed consent procedures, confidentiality protections, and cultural humility. Organizational cultures must support rights-respecting practice, with supervision and accountability systems reinforcing human rights principles (60).

## Health Advocacy and Social Movements

### Civil Society Roles and Strategies

Civil society organizations play essential roles in advancing health equity and human rights, including service provision, advocacy, monitoring, and accountability. Service-providing organizations may directly address gaps in public provision while also demonstrating effective models and building evidence for policy change. Advocacy organizations work to influence policy through research, public campaigns, coalition building, and engagement with decision-makers (61).

Effective health advocacy requires strategic analysis of the political context, identification of leverage points and potential allies, clear messaging, and sustained engagement. The advocacy coalition framework describes how coalitions of actors with shared beliefs work over time to influence policy, with policy change often requiring external shocks or shifts in the governing coalition (62). Successful health advocacy movements have typically combined insider strategies (technical engagement with policymakers) with outsider strategies (public mobilization and pressure).

Watchdog and monitoring organizations hold governments and other actors accountable for health commitments. This includes budget monitoring to track health expenditure and equity of resource allocation, service quality monitoring using community scorecards and other tools, and human rights documentation of violations and systemic failures. Such monitoring creates evidence for advocacy, enables communities to engage in accountability processes, and creates reputational incentives for improved performance (63).

### Social Movements for Health

Social movements—sustained collective efforts for social change through extra-institutional means—have been powerful forces for health equity and rights. The HIV/AIDS movement transformed global health governance, securing treatment access, research priorities, and meaningful involvement of affected communities in decision-making (64). The global health movement's success in establishing affordable access to antiretroviral therapy demonstrates the potential of combined strategies including activism, litigation, and policy engagement.

The reproductive rights movement has similarly combined grassroots organizing, legal advocacy, and policy engagement to advance access to contraception, safe abortion where legal, and comprehensive reproductive healthcare. Despite ongoing challenges and opposition, the movement has secured important legal and policy victories while building power among those directly affected (65).

Contemporary movements for health equity include campaigns for universal health coverage, access to medicines, environmental justice, and food sovereignty. The People's Health Movement, a global network of health activists, combines advocacy at international policy venues with grassroots organizing and capacity building, grounded in the principles of primary healthcare and the right to health (66).

### Health Litigation and Strategic Legal Action

Strategic litigation uses legal action to advance policy goals beyond the immediate case, establishing precedents, creating visibility for issues, and pressuring for systemic change. Health-related litigation has increased substantially globally, with courts addressing access to medicines, environmental health harms, health system failures, and discrimination in healthcare (67).

The advantages of litigation include the binding nature of court decisions, the requirement for government response, and the symbolic power of judicial recognition of rights. However, limitations include the focus on individual remedies rather than systemic change, accessibility barriers for marginalized populations, dependence on judicial capacity and independence, and potential backlash against judicial intervention in health policy (68).

Effective strategic litigation is typically embedded within broader advocacy strategies, with legal victories reinforcing and being reinforced by political organizing and public engagement. Litigation can shift the terms of political debate, establish new norms, and create implementation pressure that translates legal victories into practical change (69).

## Case Studies

**Case Study 33.1: Brazil's Constitutional Right to Health and Judicialization**

Brazil's 1988 Constitution established health as a fundamental right, declaring that "health is the right of all and the duty of the state" and creating the Unified Health System (Sistema Único de Saúde, SUS) to provide universal access to healthcare (70). This constitutional enshrinement of health rights has had profound implications for health policy and practice, including the phenomenon of "judicialization"—the increasing use of courts to claim access to health services and medicines.

The Brazilian Constitutional framework goes beyond many other countries in establishing health as an immediately enforceable right. Article 196 establishes health as a right of all and duty of the state, guaranteed through social and economic policies aimed at reducing disease risk and ensuring universal and equal access to health services (71). This has provided grounds for individuals to claim access to specific treatments through the courts.

Judicialization in Brazil has grown exponentially, with hundreds of thousands of health-related lawsuits filed annually seeking access to medicines, procedures, and services not provided through the public system. Studies show that the majority of litigation involves high-cost medicines, often for cancer and other serious conditions, and that while litigation has expanded access for some, it has also created concerns about equity, sustainability, and democratic deliberation over health priorities (72).

The equity implications of judicialization are contested. On one hand, courts have enabled access to life-saving treatments for individuals who would otherwise be denied. On the other hand, resources devoted to complying with court orders may divert from other priorities, and litigation benefits have disproportionately gone to those with greater resources and access to legal representation (73). Recent efforts have sought to balance rights enforcement with health system sustainability through specialized health courts, technical advisory committees, and enhanced administrative processes for addressing complaints.

Constitutional enshrinement creates strong legal foundations for health rights. Judicialization can expand access but raises questions about equity and allocation. Balancing individual claims with collective priorities requires institutional mechanisms beyond courts alone.

**Case Study 33.2: South Africa Treatment Action Campaign - HIV/AIDS Advocacy**

The Treatment Action Campaign (TAC), founded in 1998, represents one of the most successful health rights advocacy movements in global health history. Confronting a government that initially denied the link between HIV and AIDS and resisted providing antiretroviral treatment, TAC combined grassroots organizing, strategic litigation, and international solidarity to transform South Africa's AIDS response and contribute to global access to medicines (74).

TAC's organizing model emphasized building power among those directly affected. Starting in townships around Cape Town, the organization recruited and trained HIV-positive individuals as treatment literacy activists who could explain the science of HIV/AIDS, challenge stigma, and mobilize for treatment access. This ground-up approach created a base of informed, committed activists while building the leadership of people living with HIV (75).

The constitutional litigation strategy yielded landmark victories. In Minister of Health v Treatment Action Campaign (2002), the Constitutional Court ordered the government to develop and implement a plan for preventing mother-to-child transmission of HIV, finding that the government's restriction of nevirapine to pilot sites violated the constitutional right to healthcare (76). This decision established important precedents regarding justiciability of socioeconomic rights and reasonableness of government policies.

TAC combined domestic advocacy with international solidarity work, joining campaigns for access to affordable generic medicines and challenging pharmaceutical patent claims that blocked access to affordable treatment. The organization played key roles in the DOHA Declaration on TRIPS and Public Health (2001), which affirmed countries' rights to use flexibilities in intellectual property rules to protect public health (77).

Grassroots organizing among affected communities builds sustainable movements. Constitutional litigation can establish important precedents and shift government policy. Combining domestic and international strategies creates multiple pressure points. Treatment literacy and community empowerment are essential for sustained advocacy.

**Case Study 33.3: Mexico's Conditional Cash Transfers for Health Equity**

Mexico's conditional cash transfer program, known successively as PROGRESA (1997), Oportunidades (2002), and Prospera (2014-2019), became one of the world's most influential social protection interventions for health equity. The program provided cash transfers to poor households conditional on children's school attendance and family participation in health services, including prenatal care, growth monitoring, and immunizations (78).

The program's design was explicitly informed by equity concerns, targeting the poorest households in rural and subsequently urban areas through a rigorous proxy means test. Transfers were provided primarily to women, based on evidence of women's greater investment in child welfare. The health conditions were designed to address underutilization of preventive services among poor populations, while the cash transfers addressed immediate economic barriers (79).

Rigorous evaluation using randomized controlled trials and quasi-experimental methods demonstrated significant impacts on health outcomes. Evaluations found improvements in child growth, reduced anemia, increased immunization coverage, and improved utilization of prenatal care (80). Longer-term follow-up found sustained impacts on educational attainment and some evidence of intergenerational benefits.

The program also faced critiques and challenges. Conditionality has been questioned on rights grounds, with critics arguing that essential services should not be contingent on behavior and that conditions may exclude the most vulnerable (81). Implementation challenges included inadequate supply of quality health services in some areas, difficulty reaching the most marginalized populations, and potential for the conditions to disempower rather than empower beneficiaries. The program was discontinued in 2019 by the incoming government, which replaced it with unconditional transfers.

Conditional cash transfers can significantly improve health outcomes among poor populations when adequately resourced and implemented. Targeting and conditionality design have important equity implications. Supply-side investments in service quality must accompany demand-side interventions. Program sustainability requires continued political support across government transitions.

**Case Study 33.4: Thailand's Universal Health Coverage - Social Accountability Mechanisms**

Thailand's Universal Coverage Scheme (UCS), launched in 2002, achieved near-universal health coverage within a decade while incorporating innovative accountability mechanisms that enable citizen participation and responsiveness to concerns. The UCS extended coverage to approximately 47 million previously uninsured Thais, primarily informal sector workers and their dependents, providing comprehensive services with minimal copayments (82).

The governance structure of the UCS incorporated social accountability mechanisms from the outset. The National Health Security Board, which oversees the scheme, includes representatives from civil society and healthcare providers alongside government officials. This multi-stakeholder governance enables diverse perspectives in policy decisions and creates channels for raising concerns (83).

The UCS established a comprehensive complaints and grievance mechanism enabling beneficiaries to raise concerns about service quality, denials of care, and other issues. Complaints can be submitted through multiple channels including telephone hotlines, local health security offices, and online platforms. An independent investigation process assesses complaints, and the National Health Security Office publishes annual reports on complaints and resolutions, creating transparency and accountability (84).

Community participation mechanisms include the Area Health Boards at provincial level, which include civil society representatives and provide oversight of local implementation. Quality monitoring involves community scorecards and regular citizen surveys assessing satisfaction and experiences with services. These mechanisms create feedback loops enabling continuous quality improvement and responsiveness to community priorities (85).

Accountability mechanisms should be integrated into health system design from inception. Multiple channels for citizen feedback increase accessibility and reach. Transparent reporting on complaints and resolutions creates incentives for improvement. Multi-stakeholder governance enables diverse perspectives while maintaining governmental accountability.

## Universal Health Coverage and Human Rights

### UHC as Right to Health Operationalization

Universal health coverage has emerged as a primary framework for operationalizing the right to health, defined as ensuring that all people have access to needed health services of sufficient quality to be effective without suffering financial hardship (86). UHC encompasses the core elements of the right to health—availability, accessibility, acceptability, and quality—while providing a measurable framework for tracking progress.

The relationship between UHC and the right to health is complementary but not identical. UHC provides an operational goal and measurement framework, while human rights provide normative foundations, legal obligations, and accountability mechanisms. Rights-based UHC emphasizes not only coverage expansion but also equity, participation, and accountability in how coverage is achieved (87).

The SDG target for UHC (3.8) includes monitoring of both service coverage and financial protection, with a composite index tracking coverage of essential health services across reproductive, maternal, newborn and child health, infectious diseases, noncommunicable diseases, and service capacity (88). However, the indicator framework has been critiqued for inadequate attention to equity, quality, and the process dimensions emphasized in rights frameworks (89).

### Access, Quality, and Financial Protection

Meaningful UHC requires attention to all three dimensions—access, quality, and financial protection—and to equity within each. Access encompasses availability of services, geographic accessibility, and absence of discrimination in who can obtain care. Research demonstrates substantial gaps in effective coverage, with many nominally covered populations unable to access services due to geographic, financial, or other barriers (90).

Quality of care has received increasing attention as coverage expands, with recognition that poor quality care fails to advance health rights even when accessed. Studies estimate that poor quality care accounts for substantial proportions of amenable mortality in low- and middle-income countries, highlighting that access without quality fails to fulfill the right to health (91).

Financial protection remains a critical concern, with out-of-pocket health expenditures continuing to impoverish millions of households annually and deter others from seeking needed care (92). Progressive financing through general taxation, mandatory social health insurance, or mixed systems is essential for reducing financial barriers while ensuring sustainable funding.

### Participation and Accountability for UHC

Rights-based approaches to UHC emphasize participation and accountability as essential for equity and effectiveness. Participation involves meaningful community input into benefit package design, priority-setting, and governance of health financing and service delivery. This includes representation of marginalized groups whose needs might otherwise be overlooked and processes enabling genuine influence rather than tokenistic consultation (93).

Accountability for UHC includes monitoring coverage and financial protection disaggregated by equity-relevant stratifiers, grievance mechanisms for addressing service failures, and political accountability for UHC commitments. International monitoring through the UHC Partnership, WHO, and other bodies creates global accountability while complementing national mechanisms (94).

Civil society engagement in UHC monitoring and advocacy has been crucial for maintaining equity focus and holding governments to commitments. Organizations like the UHC Coalition bring together diverse stakeholders to advocate for rights-based approaches while the People's Health Movement and others provide critical perspectives emphasizing equity and participation (95).

## Emerging Challenges and Future Directions

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed and exacerbated health inequities globally, with marginalized populations experiencing higher infection rates, worse outcomes, and disproportionate social and economic impacts (96). Pandemic response measures including lockdowns and service disruptions often harmed those with fewer resources to cope, while vaccine access inequities between and within countries demonstrated ongoing failures to apply equity and rights principles (97).

Climate change represents an emerging challenge for health equity and rights, with climate impacts falling disproportionately on populations that have contributed least to emissions and have fewest resources for adaptation (98). Climate justice frameworks emphasize the rights dimensions of climate action, including procedural rights to participation in decisions and substantive rights to protection from harm. Health systems must adapt to increasing climate-related health threats while reducing their own environmental footprint.

Digital health technologies offer potential for extending access and improving quality but also risk exacerbating inequities if access to technology and digital literacy are unevenly distributed. Rights frameworks emphasize privacy protections, informed consent, and inclusive design as essential safeguards, while advocating for digital inclusion as a dimension of health equity (99).

Commercial determinants of health—the activities of corporations that affect health through products, practices, and policies—represent an increasingly recognized driver of health inequities requiring rights-based responses. Strategies include regulatory action, litigation, and strengthening conflict of interest policies to protect health policy from commercial influence (100).

## Conclusion

Health equity and human rights provide complementary and mutually reinforcing frameworks for understanding and addressing unjust health disparities. The right to health establishes clear state obligations and accountability mechanisms, while health equity concepts provide tools for measuring and analyzing disparities. Together, they provide both the normative foundation and practical guidance for transformative approaches to global health.

Key principles include attention to underlying determinants of health, not only healthcare services; focus on the most marginalized populations and intersecting forms of disadvantage; participation of affected communities in decisions affecting their health; and robust accountability mechanisms for ensuring progress. Rights-based approaches operationalize these principles through the PANEL framework and capacity building for both rights-holders and duty-bearers.

Progress toward health equity and realization of the right to health requires action across multiple domains: legal frameworks establishing enforceable health rights; policies addressing social determinants; health systems designed for equity and responsiveness; strong civil society advocacy; and international cooperation and accountability. The case studies examined demonstrate diverse strategies—constitutional rights, litigation, social protection, and accountability mechanisms—that have advanced health equity and rights in different contexts.

The challenges ahead are substantial, including widening inequities exposed by the COVID-19 pandemic, emerging threats from climate change, and ongoing gaps in coverage, quality, and protection. Yet the frameworks and experiences described in this chapter provide foundations for continued progress. Realizing health as a human right for all remains the central challenge and aspiration of global public health.
