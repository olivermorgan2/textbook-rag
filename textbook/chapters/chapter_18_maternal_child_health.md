# Chapter 18: Maternal and Child Health

## Introduction and Conceptual Framework

Maternal and child health (MCH) represents one of the most fundamental areas of public health practice, reflecting the health and well-being of populations across the life course. The health of mothers, newborns, and children serves as a sensitive indicator of health system performance, social equity, and overall development progress within nations (1). This chapter examines the complex determinants, interventions, and programmatic approaches that shape maternal and child health outcomes globally, with particular attention to persistent disparities and evidence-based strategies for improvement.

The conceptual framework for maternal and child health has evolved significantly from a narrow focus on clinical interventions to a comprehensive life course approach that recognizes the interconnected nature of health across developmental stages (2). This framework emphasizes that investments in maternal and child health yield benefits that extend far beyond immediate survival outcomes, influencing cognitive development, educational attainment, economic productivity, and intergenerational transmission of health and social advantage (3).

Contemporary MCH practice is grounded in several foundational principles. The continuum of care approach recognizes that optimal health outcomes depend on seamless integration of services from preconception through adolescence, spanning multiple care settings and providers (4). The life course perspective acknowledges that early experiences during pregnancy, infancy, and childhood establish biological and social foundations for health throughout life (5). Rights-based approaches emphasize that access to quality maternal and child health services constitutes a fundamental human right, requiring attention to equity, non-discrimination, and meaningful participation in health decision-making (6).

The global architecture for maternal and child health has been shaped by multiple international commitments and initiatives. The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) establish specific targets for maternal mortality (SDG 3.1), neonatal and under-5 mortality (SDG 3.2), and universal access to reproductive health services (SDG 3.7), creating accountability frameworks that drive policy and investment decisions (7). The Global Strategy for Women's, Children's and Adolescents' Health (2016-2030) provides a comprehensive roadmap for achieving these targets while addressing underlying determinants of health (8).

Understanding maternal and child health requires recognition of the complex interplay between biological, social, economic, and environmental factors that influence health outcomes. Social determinants including poverty, education, gender inequality, and social exclusion fundamentally shape access to and quality of health services (9). Environmental factors such as air pollution, water quality, and climate change increasingly influence maternal and child health outcomes, particularly among vulnerable populations (10). Health system factors including service availability, quality, and affordability determine whether interventions reach those most in need (11).

## Global Burden and Trends in Maternal and Child Health

### Maternal Mortality: Trends and Disparities

Maternal mortality represents one of the starkest health inequities globally, with dramatic disparities between and within countries reflecting broader patterns of social and economic inequality. The maternal mortality ratio (MMR), defined as the number of maternal deaths per 100,000 live births, serves as a key indicator of health system performance and women's status within society (12).

Global estimates indicate that approximately 287,000 women died from pregnancy-related causes in 2020, representing a decline from 451,000 deaths in 2000 but falling short of the progress needed to achieve SDG targets (13). The global MMR decreased from 342 per 100,000 live births in 2000 to 223 per 100,000 in 2020, representing a 35% reduction over two decades (13). However, this progress has been highly uneven across regions and countries.

Sub-Saharan Africa bears the highest burden of maternal mortality, accounting for approximately 70% of global maternal deaths despite representing only 29% of global births (13). The regional MMR in Sub-Saharan Africa was 545 per 100,000 live births in 2020, compared to 21 per 100,000 in Europe and Northern America (13). Within regions, disparities are equally stark. Nigeria alone accounts for nearly 20% of global maternal deaths, with an estimated MMR of 1,047 per 100,000 live births, while South Africa has achieved an MMR of 119 per 100,000 through sustained health system investments (13).

The leading causes of maternal mortality globally include severe bleeding (postpartum hemorrhage), infections, hypertensive disorders of pregnancy, delivery complications, and unsafe abortion (14). These direct obstetric causes account for approximately 75% of maternal deaths, while indirect causes such as pre-existing medical conditions exacerbated by pregnancy contribute to the remaining 25% (14). Importantly, the vast majority of maternal deaths are preventable with timely access to quality obstetric care and skilled birth attendance (15).

### Child Mortality: Patterns and Determinants

Child survival has improved dramatically over the past three decades, yet significant challenges remain in achieving universal child survival. The under-5 mortality rate (U5MR), representing deaths per 1,000 live births among children before their fifth birthday, declined from 93 per 1,000 live births in 1990 to 38 per 1,000 in 2021 (16). Despite this progress, an estimated 5.0 million children under age 5 died in 2021, with the majority of deaths concentrated in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia (16).

Neonatal mortality, defined as deaths during the first 28 days of life, accounts for approximately 47% of all under-5 deaths globally (16). The neonatal mortality rate decreased from 37 per 1,000 live births in 1990 to 17 per 1,000 in 2021, but progress has slowed in recent years, particularly in conflict-affected and fragile states (16). The leading causes of neonatal deaths include complications related to preterm birth, intrapartum complications (birth asphyxia), and infections, which together account for over 80% of neonatal deaths (17).

Geographic disparities in child mortality mirror those observed for maternal mortality. Sub-Saharan Africa has a U5MR of 74 per 1,000 live births, compared to 4 per 1,000 in Europe and Northern America (16). Within countries, rural-urban disparities are substantial, with rural children facing mortality risks 1.5-2 times higher than urban children in many low- and middle-income countries (18). Wealth-related inequities are equally pronounced, with children from the poorest household quintiles experiencing mortality rates 2-3 times higher than those from the wealthiest quintiles (19).

### Nutrition and Child Development Outcomes

Malnutrition remains a significant contributor to child morbidity and mortality globally, with both acute and chronic forms imposing substantial burdens on affected populations. Stunting, defined as height-for-age below -2 standard deviations from the median of reference populations, affected an estimated 149.2 million children under age 5 in 2020 (20). Wasting, defined as weight-for-height below -2 standard deviations, affected 45.4 million children globally in 2020 (20).

The distribution of malnutrition follows patterns similar to other MCH indicators, with the highest prevalence in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. Countries such as Niger, Madagascar, and Yemen have stunting prevalence exceeding 40%, while Nordic countries maintain rates below 2% (20). These disparities reflect complex interactions between food security, poverty, disease burden, and access to health and nutrition services.

Maternal nutrition plays a crucial role in child health outcomes, with maternal undernutrition increasing risks of low birth weight, preterm birth, and subsequent child malnutrition (21). Iron deficiency anemia affects approximately 37% of pregnant women globally, contributing to increased risks of maternal and perinatal mortality (22). Folic acid deficiency increases risks of neural tube defects, while iodine deficiency can cause severe cognitive impairments in children (23).

## Maternal Health: Mortality, Morbidity and Determinants

### Direct and Indirect Causes of Maternal Mortality

Understanding the causes of maternal mortality is essential for designing effective prevention strategies. Direct obstetric causes result from complications during pregnancy, delivery, or the postpartum period, while indirect causes involve pre-existing conditions or diseases that develop during pregnancy and are aggravated by physiological changes of pregnancy (14).

Severe bleeding (hemorrhage) represents the leading cause of maternal death globally, accounting for approximately 27% of maternal deaths (14). Postpartum hemorrhage, defined as blood loss exceeding 500ml following vaginal delivery or 1,000ml following cesarean section, can progress rapidly to shock and death without prompt intervention (24). Risk factors include previous cesarean section, multiple pregnancy, prolonged labor, and delivery by unskilled attendants in settings lacking access to blood transfusion and surgical intervention (24).

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy, including preeclampsia and eclampsia, account for approximately 14% of maternal deaths globally (14). These conditions are characterized by elevated blood pressure and proteinuria, progressing to seizures in cases of eclampsia (25). Early detection through antenatal care and timely management with antihypertensive medications and magnesium sulfate can prevent most deaths from hypertensive disorders (25).

Infection represents another major cause of maternal mortality, accounting for approximately 11% of deaths globally (14). Puerperal sepsis, occurring in the postpartum period, often results from unhygienic delivery practices, prolonged labor, or retained placental products (26). Proper infection prevention practices, including clean delivery techniques and prophylactic antibiotics for cesarean sections, can dramatically reduce infection-related mortality (26).

Unsafe abortion accounts for approximately 8% of maternal deaths globally, with higher proportions in regions with restrictive abortion laws and limited access to safe services (14). The World Health Organization estimates that 23,000 women die annually from complications of unsafe abortion, with millions more experiencing serious morbidity (27). Countries with liberal abortion laws and accessible safe abortion services demonstrate significantly lower rates of abortion-related mortality (27).

### Maternal Morbidity and Long-term Health Consequences

While maternal mortality captures the most severe outcome of pregnancy complications, maternal morbidity affects a much larger number of women and can have lasting impacts on health and quality of life. Severe acute maternal morbidity (SAMM), also known as "near miss" mortality, refers to cases where women nearly die but survive severe complications during pregnancy, delivery, or the postpartum period (28).

For every maternal death, an estimated 20-30 women experience severe maternal morbidity requiring emergency intervention (28). Common severe morbidities include severe hemorrhage requiring blood transfusion, hysterectomy, sepsis requiring intensive care, and eclampsia resulting in organ dysfunction (29). These conditions can lead to long-term health consequences including chronic pain, infertility, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder (29).

Obstetric fistula represents a particularly devastating form of maternal morbidity, affecting an estimated 2-3.5 million women globally (30). This condition, resulting from prolonged obstructed labor without timely intervention, causes continuous leaking of urine and/or feces through the vagina, leading to social isolation and economic hardship (30). Obstetric fistula is largely preventable through timely access to emergency obstetric care and is treatable through surgical repair when services are available (30).

### Social Determinants and Structural Factors

The social determinants of maternal health encompass the conditions in which women are born, grow, live, work, and age, as well as the broader systems that shape these conditions (31). Poverty represents the most fundamental determinant, influencing access to education, nutrition, healthcare, and social support systems that affect maternal health outcomes (31).

Women's education levels show strong associations with maternal and child health outcomes. Each additional year of maternal education reduces child mortality by approximately 9%, while secondary education completion reduces maternal mortality by nearly 50% (32). Education influences health through multiple pathways including increased knowledge of health practices, improved health-seeking behavior, enhanced economic opportunities, and greater autonomy in health decision-making (32).

Gender inequality and women's empowerment significantly influence maternal health outcomes. Women with greater decision-making autonomy, control over economic resources, and freedom of movement demonstrate better utilization of maternal health services and improved outcomes (33). Cultural practices that restrict women's mobility or decision-making authority can delay care-seeking during obstetric emergencies, contributing to maternal mortality (33).

Geographic factors, including distance to health facilities and transportation infrastructure, significantly influence access to maternal health services. Rural populations often face greater barriers to accessing skilled birth attendance and emergency obstetric care due to geographic isolation and limited transportation options (34). In many low-income countries, over 40% of women live more than 2 hours from the nearest facility providing basic emergency obstetric care (34).

## Child Health: Neonatal, Infant and Under-5 Mortality

### Neonatal Health and Survival

The neonatal period, encompassing the first 28 days of life, represents the most vulnerable time for child survival. Despite accounting for less than 4% of the under-5 period, the neonatal period accounts for 47% of all under-5 deaths globally (16). Early neonatal deaths (first 7 days) account for approximately 75% of neonatal deaths, highlighting the critical importance of immediate postnatal care (17).

The leading causes of neonatal mortality include complications of preterm birth (35%), intrapartum-related complications (24%), and neonatal infections (15%) (17). Preterm birth, defined as delivery before 37 completed weeks of gestation, affects approximately 15 million infants globally and is the leading cause of neonatal and child mortality (35). Complications of prematurity include respiratory distress syndrome, necrotizing enterocolitis, intraventricular hemorrhage, and increased susceptibility to infections (35).

Birth asphyxia and intrapartum complications result from inadequate oxygen supply during labor and delivery, often associated with prolonged or obstructed labor, breech presentation, or cord complications (36). These complications can be prevented through skilled birth attendance, appropriate monitoring during labor, and timely intervention when complications arise (36). Neonatal resuscitation training for birth attendants can significantly reduce mortality from birth asphyxia (36).

Neonatal infections include sepsis, pneumonia, meningitis, and tetanus, often resulting from poor hygiene practices during delivery or inadequate postnatal care (37). Risk factors include premature rupture of membranes, maternal infection, low birth weight, and delivery in unclean environments (37). Prevention strategies include clean delivery practices, immediate thermal care, early initiation of breastfeeding, and prompt recognition and treatment of danger signs (37).

### Essential Newborn Care Interventions

Evidence-based newborn care interventions can prevent the majority of neonatal deaths when implemented with high coverage and quality. The World Health Organization recommends a package of essential newborn care practices that can be implemented at home, in health posts, or in health facilities depending on the level of care required (38).

Immediate thermal care involves drying the newborn immediately after birth, providing skin-to-skin contact with the mother, and delaying bathing for at least 6 hours to prevent hypothermia (38). Hypothermia increases the risk of infection and mortality, particularly among preterm and low birth weight infants (39). Kangaroo mother care, involving continuous skin-to-skin contact, has been shown to reduce neonatal mortality by 36% among infants weighing less than 2000g at birth (40).

Early initiation of breastfeeding within the first hour after birth provides essential nutrients and antibodies while promoting mother-infant bonding (41). Exclusive breastfeeding for the first six months of life reduces the risk of diarrheal diseases, respiratory infections, and allergic diseases while providing optimal nutrition for growth and development (41). Breastfeeding practices are influenced by cultural beliefs, health facility practices, and social support systems (42).

Cord care practices significantly influence neonatal infection rates. The WHO recommends dry cord care for births in clean environments and chlorhexidine application in settings with high neonatal infection rates (43). Clean cord cutting using sterile instruments and keeping the cord stump dry and clean can prevent tetanus and other serious infections (43).

### Child Health Beyond the Neonatal Period

Infant and child mortality beyond the neonatal period is primarily caused by infectious diseases and nutritional disorders. The leading causes of death among children aged 1-59 months include pneumonia (18%), diarrheal diseases (9%), malaria (5%), and measles (1%), with malnutrition contributing to approximately 35% of all child deaths as an underlying factor (44).

Pneumonia remains the leading infectious cause of death among children under 5, causing approximately 740,000 deaths annually (45). Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae type b, and respiratory syncytial virus represent the most common pathogens causing severe pneumonia (45). Risk factors include malnutrition, indoor air pollution, crowded living conditions, and lack of breastfeeding (46). Prevention strategies include vaccination against pneumococcal disease and Haemophilus influenzae type b, improved nutrition, and reduced exposure to indoor air pollution (46).

Diarrheal diseases cause approximately 370,000 deaths among children under 5 annually, primarily due to dehydration and electrolyte imbalance (47). Rotavirus represents the leading cause of severe diarrhea among young children, while bacterial pathogens including Shigella, Salmonella, and enteropathogenic E. coli contribute to significant morbidity and mortality (47). Oral rehydration therapy (ORT) can prevent most deaths from diarrheal dehydration when provided promptly and appropriately (48).

Malaria causes approximately 260,000 deaths among children under 5 annually, with 95% of deaths occurring in Africa (49). Plasmodium falciparum causes the most severe form of malaria, progressing rapidly to cerebral malaria, severe anemia, and death without prompt treatment (49). Prevention strategies include insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor residual spraying, and seasonal malaria chemoprevention in high-transmission settings (50).

### Integrated Management of Childhood Illness

The Integrated Management of Childhood Illness (IMCI) strategy was developed by WHO and UNICEF to address the leading causes of child morbidity and mortality through a systematic approach to case management (51). IMCI aims to improve health worker skills, strengthen health systems, and promote appropriate care-seeking and home care practices (51).

The IMCI case management approach uses simple clinical algorithms to assess, classify, and treat common childhood illnesses including pneumonia, diarrhea, malaria, malnutrition, and ear infections (52). Health workers are trained to assess danger signs, classify illness severity, provide appropriate treatment, and counsel caregivers on home care and follow-up (52). This standardized approach has been shown to improve case management quality and reduce child mortality when implemented with adequate supervision and support (53).

Community IMCI extends the strategy beyond health facilities to include community health workers trained to assess and manage common childhood illnesses (54). Community case management of pneumonia, diarrhea, and malaria using simplified protocols and pre-packaged medications has demonstrated significant reductions in child mortality in multiple settings (54). The strategy emphasizes early recognition of danger signs, prompt treatment with appropriate antibiotics or antimalarials, and timely referral for severe cases (55).

## Reproductive Health and Family Planning

### Family Planning and Reproductive Rights

Family planning encompasses the knowledge, attitudes, and practices that enable individuals and couples to decide freely and responsibly the number, spacing, and timing of their children (56). Access to family planning services represents a fundamental human right while contributing significantly to maternal and child health outcomes through birth spacing, prevention of unintended pregnancies, and reduction of high-risk pregnancies (56).

An estimated 218 million women in developing countries who want to avoid pregnancy lack access to modern contraceptive methods, representing unmet need for family planning (57). This unmet need is highest in Sub-Saharan Africa (22%) and varies significantly by age, education level, wealth status, and geographic location (57). Addressing unmet need for family planning could prevent an estimated 67 million unintended pregnancies, 22 million unsafe abortions, and 25,000 maternal deaths annually (58).

Modern contraceptive methods include hormonal methods (oral contraceptive pills, injectables, implants, patches), barrier methods (male and female condoms), intrauterine devices (IUDs), and permanent methods (male and female sterilization) (59). Each method has distinct efficacy rates, side effect profiles, and acceptability considerations that influence individual choice and continuation rates (59). Long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs), including implants and IUDs, demonstrate high efficacy and continuation rates but require trained providers and appropriate service delivery systems (60).

Barriers to family planning access include limited availability of methods, inadequate provider training, restrictive policies, cultural opposition, and economic constraints (61). Quality of care factors including provider attitudes, counseling quality, and follow-up support significantly influence contraceptive adoption and continuation (61). Task-shifting strategies that enable lower-level health workers to provide certain family planning services can improve access while maintaining safety and quality (62).

### Adolescent Sexual and Reproductive Health

Adolescent sexual and reproductive health represents a critical component of MCH programming, given that approximately 16 million adolescent girls aged 15-19 give birth annually, with 95% of these births occurring in developing countries (63). Adolescent pregnancy carries increased risks of maternal mortality, preterm birth, low birth weight, and neonatal mortality compared to adult pregnancy (64).

Early marriage and childbearing limit educational opportunities and economic prospects for adolescent girls while perpetuating cycles of poverty and gender inequality (65). Girls who marry before age 18 are more likely to experience intimate partner violence, have limited decision-making autonomy, and face restrictions on mobility and social participation (65). Comprehensive sexuality education and access to youth-friendly reproductive health services can delay marriage and first birth while improving health outcomes (66).

Youth-friendly health services are designed to meet the specific needs and preferences of young people through accessible, acceptable, and appropriate service delivery approaches (67). Key characteristics include convenient locations and hours, trained staff who respect youth confidentiality and autonomy, affordable services, and comprehensive care that addresses multiple health needs (67). Evidence demonstrates that youth-friendly approaches increase service utilization and improve reproductive health outcomes among adolescents (68).

### Preconception Care and Birth Spacing

Preconception care encompasses health promotion, screening, and intervention activities designed to optimize women's health before pregnancy occurs (69). This approach recognizes that many pregnancy complications have origins before conception and that interventions during this period can significantly improve maternal and child health outcomes (69).

Key components of preconception care include folic acid supplementation to prevent neural tube defects, screening and management of chronic conditions such as diabetes and hypertension, immunization against rubella and other vaccine-preventable diseases, counseling on healthy lifestyle behaviors, and family planning to ensure pregnancy planning and appropriate birth spacing (70).

Birth spacing represents one of the most important factors influencing maternal and child health outcomes. Short birth intervals (less than 18 months between births) increase risks of maternal mortality, preterm birth, low birth weight, and child mortality (71). Optimal birth spacing of 2-3 years between pregnancies allows for maternal recovery from pregnancy and childbirth while providing adequate care and attention to existing children (71).

## Essential Interventions Across the Continuum of Care

### Antenatal Care and Pregnancy Management

Antenatal care (ANC) provides opportunities to identify and manage pregnancy complications, promote healthy behaviors, prepare for delivery, and strengthen linkages between women and the health system (72). The WHO recommends a minimum of eight ANC contacts during pregnancy, with the first contact occurring within the first 12 weeks of gestation (73).

Essential components of ANC include health assessment and promotion, fetal assessment, screening and prevention of diseases, and health education (73). Specific interventions include monitoring maternal weight gain and blood pressure, screening for anemia and other conditions, providing iron and folic acid supplementation, tetanus toxoid immunization, and counseling on danger signs, birth preparedness, and breastfeeding (74).

Despite the proven benefits of ANC, significant gaps remain in coverage and quality globally. While 86% of pregnant women receive at least one ANC visit, only 64% receive the recommended four or more visits, with substantial disparities by region, wealth status, and education level (75). Quality of ANC varies widely, with many women receiving incomplete packages of recommended interventions due to supply shortages, inadequate provider training, or system constraints (76).

### Skilled Birth Attendance and Emergency Obstetric Care

Skilled birth attendance represents one of the most effective interventions for preventing maternal and neonatal mortality. A skilled birth attendant is defined as "an accredited health professional such as a midwife, doctor, or nurse who has been educated and trained to proficiency in the skills needed to manage normal (uncomplicated) pregnancies, childbirth and the immediate postnatal period, and in the identification, management and referral of complications in women and newborns" (77).

Globally, 84% of births are attended by skilled personnel, ranging from 59% in Sub-Saharan Africa to 99% in developed regions (78). However, skilled attendance at birth does not guarantee quality care, as many births attended by skilled personnel occur in facilities lacking essential equipment, supplies, or backup support for emergency interventions (79). The presence of a skilled attendant must be coupled with an enabling environment that includes appropriate infrastructure, equipment, and referral systems (79).

Emergency obstetric care (EmOC) services are designed to manage life-threatening complications that can arise during pregnancy, childbirth, and the postpartum period. Basic EmOC includes parenteral antibiotics, oxytocic drugs, anticonvulsants, manual removal of placenta, removal of retained products of conception, and assisted vaginal delivery (80). Comprehensive EmOC includes all basic EmOC functions plus cesarean section and blood transfusion (80).

The UN Process Indicators for emergency obstetric care recommend that all women have access to basic EmOC within 2 hours and comprehensive EmOC within 12 hours of home (81). However, in many low-resource settings, these standards are not met due to insufficient numbers of facilities providing EmOC, inadequate transportation systems, and financial barriers to care (81).

### Postnatal Care and Family Planning Integration

Postnatal care provides critical opportunities to monitor maternal and newborn recovery, identify and manage complications, promote healthy behaviors, and provide family planning services (82). The WHO recommends postnatal contacts for all mothers and newborns at 6-12 hours, 6 days, 6 weeks, and 6 months after birth, with additional contacts as needed based on risk factors or complications (83).

Essential postnatal care includes assessment of maternal and newborn recovery, monitoring for danger signs, promotion of exclusive breastfeeding, immunization, family planning counseling and services, and support for maternal mental health (83). Early detection and management of postpartum complications such as infection, hemorrhage, and hypertensive disorders can prevent progression to life-threatening conditions (84).

Integration of family planning services into postnatal care represents an important opportunity to address unmet need for contraception and prevent closely spaced pregnancies (85). The postpartum period is characterized by high motivation for family planning, regular contact with health services, and clear understanding of pregnancy and childbirth experiences (85). Postpartum family planning programs have demonstrated significant increases in contraceptive adoption and improved birth spacing outcomes (86).

## Community-Based Approaches and Health System Strengthening

### Community Health Workers in MCH

Community health workers (CHWs) play increasingly important roles in extending MCH services to underserved populations and improving health outcomes at scale. CHWs are trained health service providers who share ethnicity, language, and life experiences with the communities they serve and are selected by the communities to provide basic health services (87).

Evidence from multiple settings demonstrates that CHWs can effectively provide a range of MCH services including health education, basic antenatal and postnatal care, immunization, management of childhood illnesses, and family planning services (88). A systematic review of CHW programs for child health found significant reductions in neonatal mortality (relative risk 0.76) and under-5 mortality (relative risk 0.87) when CHWs provided comprehensive care packages (89).

Successful CHW programs for MCH require adequate training, supervision, supplies, and community support. Training programs typically range from one week for basic preventive services to several months for more complex clinical tasks (90). Ongoing supervision and support are essential for maintaining service quality and CHW motivation (90). Supply chain management must ensure consistent availability of essential commodities including medications, rapid diagnostic tests, and educational materials (91).

### Performance-Based Financing and Incentives

Performance-based financing (PBF) involves direct payments to health facilities or providers based on achievement of predetermined service delivery targets and quality indicators (92). In MCH, PBF programs typically include indicators such as number of skilled births attended, immunization coverage rates, antenatal care visits, and family planning uptake (92).

Evidence from multiple countries demonstrates that PBF can increase utilization of MCH services, though impacts on health outcomes are more mixed (93). A systematic review of PBF programs found significant increases in skilled birth attendance (odds ratio 1.83), antenatal care utilization (odds ratio 1.25), and immunization coverage (odds ratio 1.37) (93). However, impacts on neonatal and maternal mortality have been inconsistent across studies (94).

Successful implementation of PBF requires robust monitoring and verification systems, clear payment mechanisms, and attention to potential unintended consequences such as gaming of indicators or neglect of non-incentivized services (95). Integration with existing health system structures and alignment with national health policies are essential for sustainability and system-wide impact (95).

### Health System Strengthening for MCH

Health system strengthening (HSS) encompasses efforts to improve health system performance across six core building blocks: service delivery, health workforce, health information systems, medical products and technologies, financing, and leadership and governance (96). MCH outcomes depend critically on well-functioning health systems that can deliver quality services consistently across the continuum of care (97).

Human resources for health represent a critical constraint in many low-resource settings, with shortages particularly acute for skilled birth attendants and specialists capable of providing emergency obstetric and newborn care (98). Task-shifting strategies that enable nurses and midwives to perform functions traditionally reserved for physicians can improve access to essential services while maintaining quality and safety (99). In-service training and continuing professional development programs are essential for maintaining and upgrading clinical skills (100).

Health information systems play crucial roles in monitoring MCH program performance, identifying gaps in coverage, and guiding resource allocation decisions (101). Key data sources include routine health information systems, population-based surveys, and vital registration systems (101). Strengthening data quality and utilization requires investments in infrastructure, training, and feedback mechanisms that enable data-driven decision making at all levels of the health system (102).

**Case Study 18.1: Rwanda's Maternal Mortality Reduction Through Community Health Cooperatives and Performance-Based Financing**

Rwanda's remarkable progress in reducing maternal mortality from 1,071 per 100,000 live births in 2000 to 210 per 100,000 in 2017 represents one of the most dramatic improvements in maternal health outcomes achieved by any country in recent decades (103). This transformation resulted from a comprehensive approach that combined health system strengthening, community mobilization, financial innovation, and strong political commitment to improving maternal health outcomes (104).

The foundation of Rwanda's approach was the development of community health cooperatives (CHCs) that integrated health service delivery with economic empowerment for rural communities (105). CHCs were established in each administrative cell (the smallest administrative unit in Rwanda) and consisted of trained community health workers who provided basic health services while operating income-generating activities such as crop cultivation or livestock rearing (105). This model ensured financial sustainability while strengthening community ownership of health programs (106).

Training and deployment of community health workers represented a cornerstone of the strategy. Rwanda trained over 45,000 community health workers to provide basic maternal and child health services including antenatal care, health education, identification of danger signs, and referral for complications (107). CHWs were equipped with basic supplies and communication technologies to maintain contact with health facilities and facilitate emergency referrals (107).

Performance-based financing (PBF) was introduced in 2005 to incentivize quality improvement and increase utilization of maternal health services (108). Health facilities received payments based on achievement of quantity and quality indicators including number of skilled births attended, antenatal care coverage, and facility-based delivery rates (108). Quality assessments evaluated structural elements (equipment, supplies, staff), clinical processes (adherence to protocols), and patient satisfaction (109).

The PBF program demonstrated significant impacts on maternal health service utilization. Skilled birth attendance increased from 39% in 2005 to 91% in 2014, while facility-based delivery rates increased from 28% to 84% over the same period (110). Antenatal care coverage improved substantially, with 98% of pregnant women receiving at least one ANC visit and 44% completing the recommended four visits by 2014 (110).

Complementary investments in health infrastructure and human resources strengthened the capacity of the health system to manage increased demand for services (111). Rwanda constructed new health facilities, upgraded existing facilities to provide basic and comprehensive emergency obstetric care, and implemented aggressive recruitment and training programs for nurses, midwives, and physicians (111). The number of health facilities providing basic EmOC increased from 10 in 2000 to 469 in 2012 (112).

Universal health insurance coverage through community-based health insurance (mutuelles de santé) ensured that financial barriers did not prevent women from accessing maternal health services (113). By 2014, over 90% of the population was covered by health insurance, with user fees for maternal health services eliminated through government subsidies (113).

Results from Rwanda's integrated approach extend beyond mortality reduction to include improvements in other maternal health indicators. The proportion of births attended by skilled personnel increased from 38% in 2000 to 91% in 2014, while cesarean section rates increased from less than 1% to 14%, indicating improved access to emergency obstetric care (114). Institutional delivery rates increased dramatically, particularly in rural areas where the majority of maternal deaths had previously occurred (115).

Key lessons from Rwanda's experience include the importance of political commitment and leadership, the effectiveness of integrated approaches that address multiple health system constraints simultaneously, and the value of community engagement in driving demand for and ownership of health programs (116). The combination of performance incentives with investments in health system capacity created a virtuous cycle of quality improvement and increased utilization (117). However, sustainability challenges remain, including dependence on external funding for PBF payments and the need for ongoing investments in human resources and infrastructure (118).

**Case Study 18.2: Brazil's Criança Feliz Program - Integrated Early Childhood Development**

Brazil's Criança Feliz (Happy Child) program, launched in 2016, represents a comprehensive approach to early childhood development that integrates maternal and child health services with social protection and educational interventions (119). The program targets vulnerable families with children from conception to six years of age, with priority given to families receiving conditional cash transfers through the Bolsa Família program (120).

The theoretical foundation of Criança Feliz is based on scientific evidence demonstrating the critical importance of early childhood experiences for lifelong health, learning, and social development (121). The program recognizes that the first 1,000 days from conception to age 2 represent a unique window of opportunity for interventions that can influence brain development, cognitive capacity, and social-emotional skills (121).

Home visiting represents the core service delivery strategy of Criança Feliz, with trained home visitors conducting weekly visits to eligible families (122). Home visitors are recruited from local communities and receive extensive training on child development, parenting practices, health promotion, and family support techniques (122). The curriculum emphasizes play-based learning, responsive caregiving, and strengthening of parent-child relationships (123).

Integration with existing social protection programs significantly enhances the program's reach and effectiveness. Families receiving Bolsa Família conditional cash transfers are automatically eligible for Criança Feliz services, creating natural linkages between income support and child development interventions (124). This integration enables comprehensive support for families addressing multiple dimensions of poverty and vulnerability (124).

The program's health components include promotion of antenatal care, skilled birth attendance, immunization, exclusive breastfeeding, and growth monitoring (125). Home visitors are trained to identify danger signs in pregnant women, newborns, and young children and to facilitate referrals to appropriate health services (125). Strong linkages with primary health care teams ensure continuity of care across service platforms (126).

Criança Feliz has achieved significant scale, reaching over 700,000 families across all Brazilian states by 2019 (127). Priority is given to families in situations of vulnerability including extreme poverty, families with children with disabilities, indigenous families, and families in rural and remote areas (127). The program operates in partnership with municipalities, which are responsible for implementing services according to national guidelines (128).

Evaluation results demonstrate positive impacts on multiple child development outcomes. A randomized controlled trial in the state of Ceará found significant improvements in child cognitive development, language skills, and social-emotional development among children in families receiving home visiting services (129). Mothers in intervention groups demonstrated improved knowledge of child development, more responsive parenting practices, and better mental health outcomes (129).

The program has also contributed to improved health outcomes including increased immunization coverage, improved nutritional status, and reduced hospitalization rates among participating children (130). Pregnant women in the program showed improved antenatal care utilization and better birth preparedness, contributing to improved maternal and newborn outcomes (130).

Key implementation factors contributing to the program's success include strong political commitment at federal and municipal levels, comprehensive training and supervision systems for home visitors, robust monitoring and evaluation frameworks, and effective coordination mechanisms between sectors (131). The integration with Bolsa Família provided an established targeting mechanism and reduced implementation costs (132).

Challenges encountered during implementation include high turnover rates among home visitors, difficulties reaching families in remote rural areas, and ensuring quality and fidelity of home visiting services across diverse contexts (133). Ongoing efforts focus on strengthening supervision systems, improving working conditions for home visitors, and enhancing training curricula based on implementation experience (133).

**Case Study 18.3: India's RMNCH+A Strategy - Scaling Reproductive, Maternal, Newborn, Child Health and Adolescent Programs**

India's Reproductive, Maternal, Newborn, Child Health and Adolescent (RMNCH+A) strategy represents one of the world's largest and most comprehensive maternal and child health programs, serving a population of over 1.4 billion people (134). Launched in 2013, RMNCH+A builds on previous maternal and child health initiatives while expanding coverage to include adolescent health and strengthening integration across the continuum of care (135).

The conceptual framework for RMNCH+A emphasizes a life course approach that recognizes the interconnected nature of health outcomes across developmental stages (136). The strategy encompasses interventions from adolescence through pregnancy, childbirth, the postnatal period, and childhood, with specific attention to vulnerable populations including tribal communities, urban poor, and families in conflict-affected areas (136).

Implementation of RMNCH+A relies on India's extensive network of Accredited Social Health Activists (ASHAs), who serve as the primary link between communities and the health system (137). Over 900,000 ASHAs have been trained and deployed across rural India to provide basic maternal and child health services, health education, and facilitation of access to health facilities (137). ASHAs receive performance-based incentives for specific activities including accompanying women for delivery, promoting immunization, and facilitating family planning services (138).

The Janani Suraksha Yojana (JSY) represents a key component of the strategy, providing conditional cash transfers to women who deliver in health facilities (139). JSY aims to increase institutional deliveries and reduce maternal and neonatal mortality by addressing financial barriers to care (139). Eligible women receive cash payments ranging from 600-1,400 rupees (approximately $8-19 USD) depending on geographic location and socioeconomic status (140).

Facility strengthening initiatives under RMNCH+A focus on ensuring availability of essential obstetric and newborn care services at appropriate levels of the health system (141). District hospitals are equipped to provide comprehensive emergency obstetric care including cesarean sections, blood transfusion, and intensive care for mothers and newborns (141). Community health centers provide basic emergency obstetric care, while primary health centers offer antenatal care, normal deliveries, and basic newborn care (142).

The strategy includes specific components targeting adolescent health, recognizing the importance of this life stage for preventing early pregnancy and promoting healthy transitions to adulthood (143). Adolescent-friendly health services are established in health facilities, while community-based interventions address issues such as nutrition, education, and life skills development (143). The Rashtriya Kishor Swasthya Karyakram (RKSK) provides the framework for adolescent health programming within the broader RMNCH+A strategy (144).

Family planning services are integrated throughout the continuum of care, with emphasis on spacing methods and quality counseling (145). The strategy promotes a range of contraceptive options including pills, condoms, injectables, intrauterine devices, and sterilization, with particular attention to addressing unmet need among young women and marginalized populations (145). Contraceptive security is ensured through robust supply chain management and quality assurance systems (146).

Results from RMNCH+A implementation demonstrate significant improvements in maternal and child health indicators. The maternal mortality ratio decreased from 556 per 100,000 live births in 1990 to 103 per 100,000 in 2017-19, representing an 82% reduction (147). Under-5 mortality declined from 126 per 1,000 live births in 1990 to 35 per 1,000 in 2019, while infant mortality decreased from 88 to 30 per 1,000 live births over the same period (147).

Institutional delivery rates increased dramatically under RMNCH+A, reaching 89% nationally by 2019-20 compared to 47% in 2007-08 (148). This increase was particularly pronounced in rural areas and among marginalized populations, indicating improved equity in access to skilled birth attendance (148). Antenatal care coverage also improved substantially, with 84% of women receiving at least four ANC visits by 2019-20 (149).

Challenges in RMNCH+A implementation include ensuring quality of care amid rapid scale-up, addressing persistent geographic and socioeconomic disparities, and maintaining adequate human resources in remote and underserved areas (150). Quality improvement initiatives focus on strengthening clinical protocols, enhancing provider training, and implementing supportive supervision systems (150).

The strategy's success factors include strong political commitment and financial investment, utilization of existing community health worker networks, integration of demand-side incentives with supply-side improvements, and robust monitoring and evaluation systems (151). The federal structure of India's health system allows for adaptation of strategies to local contexts while maintaining national standards and objectives (152).

**Case Study 18.4: Ghana's Community-Based Health Planning and Services (CHPS) - Mobile Technology for Pregnancy Monitoring**

Ghana's Community-based Health Planning and Services (CHPS) initiative represents an innovative approach to bringing health services closer to communities while leveraging mobile health technologies to improve maternal health outcomes (153). The CHPS model relocates trained community health officers to community-based compounds where they provide primary health care services including maternal and child health, family planning, and basic curative care (154).

The development of CHPS was informed by experiences from the Navrongo Health Research Centre's community health and family planning project, which demonstrated that community-based service delivery could significantly improve health outcomes in rural areas (155). The model emphasizes community engagement, trained health providers, and strategic use of technology to enhance service quality and coverage (155).

Mobile health (mHealth) technology has been integrated into CHPS to address challenges in pregnancy monitoring and maternal health service delivery (156). The Mobile Technology for Community Health (MOTECH) platform was developed in collaboration with Ghana Health Service to provide voice messages, text reminders, and decision support for pregnant women and community health workers (156).

The MOTECH system provides automated voice messages to pregnant women in local languages, delivering health education content on topics such as antenatal care, danger signs, birth preparedness, and newborn care (157). Women receive weekly messages tailored to their gestational age, with content designed to promote appropriate care-seeking behaviors and healthy practices during pregnancy (157). The system also sends reminders for scheduled antenatal care appointments and immunizations (158).

Community health officers receive mobile phone-based decision support tools that guide clinical assessment and management of pregnant women (159). The mobile application includes algorithms for risk assessment, standardized protocols for common conditions, and referral guidelines for complications requiring facility-based care (159). Real-time data collection through mobile devices enables continuous monitoring of service delivery and health outcomes (160).

Emergency referral systems are strengthened through mobile communication networks that connect community health officers with higher-level facilities (161). GPS-enabled emergency transport services can be activated through mobile phones, while telemedicine consultations provide access to specialist expertise in remote areas (161). These innovations address traditional barriers to accessing emergency obstetric care in rural communities (162).

Implementation of the CHPS-MOTECH model involved extensive community engagement and sensitization activities to build acceptance and ownership of the technology-enhanced services (163). Community leaders, women's groups, and traditional birth attendants were actively involved in program design and implementation to ensure cultural appropriateness and sustainability (163).

Training programs for community health officers emphasized both clinical skills and effective use of mobile health technologies (164). Comprehensive curricula covered pregnancy management, mobile phone operation, data collection protocols, and emergency response procedures (164). Ongoing supportive supervision ensured quality maintenance and continuous improvement of service delivery (165).

Results from pilot implementations of CHPS-MOTECH demonstrated significant improvements in maternal health service utilization and outcomes. Antenatal care attendance increased by 24% in intervention areas compared to control areas, while skilled birth attendance increased by 18% (166). Women in intervention areas demonstrated improved knowledge of danger signs and were more likely to seek care promptly when complications arose (166).

Pregnancy outcomes also improved in areas implementing the technology-enhanced CHPS model. The proportion of women completing four or more antenatal care visits increased from 42% to 67% over the implementation period (167). Emergency referrals for obstetric complications increased significantly, indicating improved recognition and management of high-risk pregnancies (167).

Key factors contributing to the success of the CHPS-MOTECH model include strong partnership between government, research institutions, and technology developers; extensive community engagement and ownership; comprehensive training and supervision systems; and integration with existing health system structures (168). The model demonstrates how technology can enhance rather than replace human-centered care delivery (169).

Challenges encountered during implementation include ensuring reliable mobile network coverage in remote areas, maintaining adequate power supply for charging mobile devices, addressing literacy constraints among some users, and ensuring long-term financial sustainability of technology platforms (170). Strategies to address these challenges include solar-powered charging stations, voice-based rather than text-based messaging, and integration of mHealth platforms with routine health system operations (170).

The Ghana experience provides important lessons for other countries considering mobile health interventions for maternal and child health. Success factors include careful attention to local context and user needs, robust training and support systems, integration with existing service delivery platforms, and sustained commitment from health system leadership (171). The model demonstrates potential for significant impact when technology is thoughtfully integrated with community-based health service delivery (172).

## Challenges and Future Directions

### Addressing Persistent Inequities

Despite significant progress in improving maternal and child health outcomes globally, persistent inequities continue to undermine efforts to achieve universal access to quality MCH services. Geographic disparities between rural and urban areas remain substantial, with rural populations often experiencing mortality rates 50-100% higher than urban populations (173). These disparities reflect complex interactions between poverty, education, infrastructure, and health service availability that require multisectoral approaches to address effectively (174).

Wealth-related inequities in MCH outcomes are equally pronounced, with children from the poorest household quintiles experiencing under-5 mortality rates 2-3 times higher than children from the wealthiest quintiles in many countries (175). These disparities extend beyond mortality to include differences in malnutrition, immunization coverage, and access to quality health services (175). Addressing wealth-based inequities requires not only health system strengthening but also broader social protection and economic development strategies (176).

Gender inequities significantly influence MCH outcomes, particularly in settings where women have limited decision-making autonomy, restricted mobility, or inadequate control over economic resources (177). Cultural practices that undervalue women and girls can result in preferential treatment for male children, delayed care-seeking for maternal complications, and limited investment in girls' education and health (177). Addressing gender inequities requires sustained efforts to change social norms while strengthening legal and policy frameworks that protect women's rights (178).

### Climate Change and Environmental Health

Climate change poses increasing threats to maternal and child health through direct impacts such as extreme weather events and indirect effects including food insecurity, water scarcity, and disease vector expansion (179). Pregnant women and young children are particularly vulnerable to heat stress, air pollution, and climate-related disasters due to their physiological characteristics and dependency on caregivers (179).

Rising temperatures and changing precipitation patterns affect food security and nutritional status, with implications for maternal nutrition during pregnancy and child growth and development (180). Drought conditions can reduce agricultural productivity and increase food prices, leading to increased rates of malnutrition among vulnerable populations (180). Flooding and extreme weather events can disrupt health services and limit access to essential MCH interventions (181).

Vector-borne diseases are expanding their geographic range due to climate change, exposing new populations to risks of malaria, dengue, and other diseases that significantly impact maternal and child health (182). Pregnant women are at increased risk of severe complications from malaria, while young children face elevated risks of death from these diseases (182). Climate adaptation strategies for health systems must include strengthening disease surveillance and response capacity for climate-sensitive diseases (183).

### Technological Innovation and Digital Health

Digital health technologies offer significant potential for improving MCH outcomes through enhanced service delivery, quality improvement, and system strengthening (184). Mobile health platforms can provide health education, appointment reminders, and clinical decision support while enabling real-time monitoring of health outcomes and service delivery performance (184).

Artificial intelligence and machine learning applications show promise for improving diagnostic accuracy, predicting pregnancy complications, and optimizing resource allocation within health systems (185). Digital platforms can also facilitate training and continuing education for health workers, particularly in remote areas with limited access to traditional educational resources (185).

However, digital health interventions must be carefully designed to avoid exacerbating existing inequities or creating new barriers to care (186). Issues of digital literacy, infrastructure constraints, and affordability must be addressed to ensure that technology benefits reach the most vulnerable populations (186). Privacy and data security concerns require robust governance frameworks and ethical guidelines for digital health implementation (187).

### Health System Resilience and Pandemic Preparedness

The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted significant vulnerabilities in health systems globally, with particular impacts on maternal and child health services (188). Disruptions to routine MCH services resulted in decreased immunization coverage, reduced antenatal care utilization, and delayed care-seeking for childhood illnesses (188). Modeling studies suggest that pandemic-related disruptions may have caused significant increases in maternal and child mortality (189).

Building resilient health systems requires strengthening core capacities including disease surveillance, emergency response, supply chain management, and workforce development (190). MCH programs must be designed with sufficient flexibility to maintain essential services during health emergencies while adapting delivery modalities as needed (190).

Community health systems play crucial roles in pandemic preparedness and response, serving as early warning systems for disease outbreaks while maintaining access to essential health services during emergencies (191). Investments in community health worker training, equipment, and support systems can enhance both routine service delivery and emergency response capacity (191).

### Financing and Sustainability

Achieving universal access to quality MCH services requires substantial and sustained financial investments from both domestic and international sources (192). Many low-income countries continue to face significant resource constraints that limit their ability to expand MCH program coverage and quality (192). External funding, while essential, must be designed to support rather than undermine domestic health system development (193).

Innovative financing mechanisms including results-based financing, social impact bonds, and blended finance approaches offer potential for mobilizing additional resources while incentivizing performance improvement (194). However, these mechanisms must be carefully designed to align with country priorities and health system strengthening objectives (194).

Domestic resource mobilization remains critical for long-term sustainability of MCH programs. Countries must strengthen their capacity to generate government revenues while prioritizing health sector investments within national budgets (195). Universal health coverage frameworks can provide sustainable financing mechanisms for MCH services while ensuring financial protection for vulnerable populations (195).

## Conclusion

Maternal and child health represents a foundational component of global public health that reflects broader patterns of social equity, health system performance, and development progress. While significant advances have been achieved in reducing maternal and child mortality globally, substantial challenges remain in addressing persistent inequities, ensuring quality of care, and building resilient health systems capable of delivering universal MCH coverage.

The evidence presented in this chapter demonstrates that effective MCH interventions exist and can be implemented successfully across diverse contexts when supported by adequate resources, political commitment, and well-functioning health systems. The case studies from Rwanda, Brazil, India, and Ghana illustrate different approaches to scaling MCH interventions while addressing local constraints and opportunities. Common success factors include strong government leadership, community engagement, integration across service delivery platforms, and sustained financing for program implementation.

Future progress in MCH will require continued innovation in service delivery approaches, technologies, and financing mechanisms while maintaining focus on equity and rights-based approaches to health programming. Climate change, demographic transitions, and emerging health threats will create new challenges that MCH programs must be prepared to address through adaptive and resilient health systems.

The life course approach to MCH emphasizes that investments in maternal and child health yield benefits that extend far beyond immediate survival outcomes, influencing educational attainment, economic productivity, and social development across generations. This perspective reinforces the importance of MCH as both a development imperative and a human rights obligation that requires sustained commitment from the global health community.

Achieving the Sustainable Development Goal targets for maternal and child mortality will require accelerated progress, particularly in countries and regions with the highest burden of preventable deaths. This acceleration must be grounded in evidence-based interventions, supported by strengthened health systems, and guided by principles of equity and social justice that ensure no woman or child is left behind in the pursuit of universal health coverage and optimal health outcomes for all.
