## Introduction

Health economics provides essential analytical frameworks for understanding how societies allocate scarce resources to improve population health. In a world where health needs invariably exceed available resources, economic principles guide decisions about which interventions to fund, how to organize health care delivery, and how to ensure that all people can access needed services without financial hardship. The discipline integrates concepts from economics, epidemiology, and health policy to inform evidence-based decision-making across diverse country contexts.

Global health financing presents both immense challenges and opportunities. Total global health expenditure reached approximately $9.0 trillion in 2020, representing about 10.8% of global gross domestic product (1). However, this spending is distributed highly inequitably, with high-income countries averaging over $3,000 per capita annually while low-income countries spend less than $50 per capita (2). These disparities translate directly into differences in health outcomes, with preventable deaths continuing at alarming rates in settings where health financing remains inadequate.

The pursuit of universal health coverage has elevated health financing to the forefront of global health policy. Sustainable Development Goal target 3.8 calls for achieving universal health coverage including financial risk protection, access to quality essential health care services, and access to safe, effective, quality, and affordable essential medicines and vaccines for all (3). Achieving this ambitious target requires not only increasing health spending but fundamentally transforming how resources are mobilized, pooled, and used to purchase health services.

This chapter examines the foundational concepts of health economics and their application to health financing policy. We explore different financing mechanisms and their implications for equity and financial protection, methods for economic evaluation of health interventions, and approaches to improving health system efficiency. Through case studies from Thailand, Bangladesh, India, and Rwanda, we illustrate how these concepts translate into policy and practice across diverse settings.

## Foundational Concepts in Health Economics

### Scarcity and Opportunity Cost

The fundamental economic problem confronting all health systems is scarcity: the reality that resources available to meet health needs will always be finite while potential uses for those resources are unlimited. This scarcity necessitates choice, and every choice involves opportunity cost—the value of the next best alternative foregone. When a ministry of health allocates budget to expand HIV treatment programs, those resources cannot simultaneously fund maternal health services or cancer screening initiatives. Understanding opportunity cost is essential for rational resource allocation in health (4).

Opportunity cost extends beyond financial resources to encompass human resources, time, and infrastructure. Training community health workers to deliver malaria prevention interventions means those workers are unavailable for other tasks. Hospital bed capacity used for elective surgical procedures cannot simultaneously accommodate emergency admissions. These trade-offs are inherent in all health systems and require careful consideration in planning and budgeting processes (5).

The concept of opportunity cost has particular relevance in resource-constrained settings where every dollar spent inefficiently represents a potentially preventable death or disability. Studies have estimated that improving allocative efficiency in health spending could avert millions of deaths annually in low- and middle-income countries without requiring additional resources (6). This finding underscores the critical importance of economic analysis in guiding health resource allocation decisions.

### Efficiency: Allocative and Technical

Efficiency in health economics encompasses two distinct but related concepts. Technical efficiency refers to producing maximum output from a given set of inputs, or equivalently, producing a given output with minimum inputs. A technically efficient health facility achieves the maximum number of patient consultations given its staffing levels, equipment, and supplies. Technical inefficiency represents waste—resources that could produce more health outcomes if used differently (7).

Allocative efficiency concerns the distribution of resources among different uses to maximize overall health outcomes or social welfare. Even if every health facility operates at maximum technical efficiency, the health system may still be allocatively inefficient if resources are directed toward interventions that produce less health gain than alternatives. For example, investing heavily in tertiary cardiac surgery while neglecting cost-effective primary prevention interventions for cardiovascular disease represents allocative inefficiency (8).

Measuring efficiency in health systems presents substantial methodological challenges. Data envelopment analysis and stochastic frontier analysis have been applied to compare efficiency across health facilities and countries, though results are sensitive to model specification and data quality (9). The World Health Organization's assessment of health system efficiency has evolved considerably, moving from controversial country rankings toward more nuanced analyses that recognize the complexity of efficiency measurement (10).

### Equity in Health Financing

Equity considerations are central to health financing policy. Horizontal equity requires that individuals with equal needs receive equal treatment, while vertical equity demands that those with greater needs receive proportionally more resources. In financing terms, horizontal equity suggests that people with similar ability to pay should contribute similarly, while vertical equity requires progressivity—those with greater resources contributing proportionally more (11).

Health financing systems vary substantially in their equity implications. General taxation is typically progressive, with wealthier individuals paying higher tax rates. Social health insurance contributions linked to income also tend toward progressivity, though caps on contributions can limit this effect. Out-of-pocket payments are inherently regressive, representing a larger share of income for poorer households and creating barriers to access for those least able to pay (12).

Benefit incidence analysis examines who receives health system benefits relative to who pays. Studies across countries consistently find that government health spending often benefits wealthier populations disproportionately, particularly when concentrated on hospital-based tertiary care in urban areas. Pro-poor benefit incidence requires deliberate policy attention to primary care, rural service delivery, and targeting mechanisms for vulnerable populations (13).

## Health Financing Functions and Mechanisms

### The Health Financing Functions Framework

Health financing systems perform three core functions: revenue collection, pooling, and purchasing. Revenue collection encompasses all mechanisms through which the health system receives money, including taxation, insurance contributions, out-of-pocket payments, and external assistance. Pooling refers to the accumulation and management of revenues to spread financial risk across the population. Purchasing involves the allocation of pooled funds to health service providers in exchange for services (14).

These functions can be organized in various ways across health systems. In the United Kingdom's National Health Service, all three functions are integrated within the public sector: taxes fund the system, risk is pooled nationally, and the government purchases services from public and private providers. In contrast, the United States fragments these functions across multiple public programs and private insurers, resulting in significant administrative complexity and coverage gaps (15).

The relationship between these functions has important implications for system performance. Fragmented pooling arrangements reduce the benefits of risk spreading and may create adverse selection problems. Passive purchasing—simply reimbursing whatever services providers deliver—forgoes opportunities to improve efficiency and quality through strategic purchasing. Countries seeking to advance toward universal coverage must attend to all three functions and their interconnections (16).

### Taxation-Based Health Financing

General taxation represents the predominant financing source in many health systems worldwide. Tax-funded systems draw on revenues from income taxes, corporate taxes, value-added taxes, and other fiscal instruments. This approach offers several advantages: the revenue base is broad, administrative costs are relatively low, and financing is delinked from individual health status, avoiding selection problems inherent in voluntary insurance (17).

Tax-funded health systems have achieved some of the world's best health outcomes. Countries including the United Kingdom, Sweden, Canada, and Thailand rely primarily on general taxation for health financing and have achieved near-universal coverage with strong financial protection (18). The approach is particularly suited to countries with robust tax systems and administrative capacity to collect revenues efficiently.

However, taxation-based financing faces challenges in contexts where tax administration is weak, informality in the economy is high, and fiscal space is constrained. Many low- and middle-income countries collect taxes equivalent to less than 15% of gross domestic product, limiting the potential for tax-funded health programs. Earmarked health taxes on products such as tobacco and alcohol can provide additional revenue while simultaneously addressing public health priorities (19).

### Social Health Insurance

Social health insurance systems collect mandatory contributions, typically linked to employment and wages, to finance health coverage for contributors and their dependents. Germany pioneered this approach in 1883 under Chancellor Bismarck, and variations now operate in countries across Europe, Latin America, and Asia. Contributions are usually shared between employers and employees, with rates determined by legislation (20).

The strengths of social health insurance include dedicated funding streams for health, explicit entitlements for contributors, and separation of health financing from annual government budget negotiations. However, employment-based contributions can exclude informal sector workers, the unemployed, and other populations outside formal employment relationships. Many social health insurance systems have evolved to incorporate subsidized coverage for these groups financed through general taxation (21).

Countries including Germany, France, Japan, and the Republic of Korea have achieved universal or near-universal coverage through social health insurance systems, though with varying degrees of fragmentation across multiple insurance funds. Recent reforms in many countries have sought to consolidate funds to improve risk pooling and administrative efficiency while maintaining the contributory character of social insurance (22).

### Community-Based Health Insurance

Community-based health insurance schemes emerged in many low- and middle-income countries as responses to inadequate public services and unaffordable private care. These voluntary, non-profit schemes pool contributions from community members to finance defined benefit packages, typically delivered through contracted health facilities. Rwanda's community-based health insurance program, known as Mutuelles de Santé, has achieved remarkable population coverage and serves as a prominent example (23).

Community-based health insurance offers advantages in contexts where formal employment is limited and government administrative capacity is constrained. Schemes can be adapted to local contexts, build on social capital and community solidarity, and complement other financing mechanisms. However, voluntary enrollment creates adverse selection risks, and the small size of many schemes limits risk pooling benefits and administrative efficiency (24).

Evidence on community-based health insurance impact is mixed. While schemes can improve access to care and provide some financial protection, coverage tends to plateau at modest levels without sustained subsidization. Rwanda's success has depended critically on government subsidies for the poor and substantial external support. Purely voluntary, community-financed schemes rarely achieve the population coverage or financial protection needed for universal health coverage (25).

### Out-of-Pocket Payments

Out-of-pocket payments remain the dominant health financing mechanism in many countries, particularly in low- and middle-income settings. These direct payments at the point of service include user fees at public facilities, copayments under insurance arrangements, and payments for services from private providers. Globally, out-of-pocket payments account for approximately 18% of current health expenditure, though this figure exceeds 40% in many countries (26).

High reliance on out-of-pocket payments creates significant barriers to accessing needed health services, particularly for poor and vulnerable populations. When faced with direct costs, individuals may delay or forgo care, use lower-quality services, or fail to complete treatment courses. These behavioral responses have well-documented negative consequences for health outcomes and contribute to the perpetuation of poverty through ill health (27).

The relationship between out-of-pocket payments and catastrophic health expenditure is direct: as the share of out-of-pocket financing increases, more households face health spending that strains or exceeds their capacity to pay. Reducing out-of-pocket payment shares represents a key policy objective in the pursuit of universal health coverage, requiring expansion of prepaid and pooled financing mechanisms (28).

### External Health Financing

Development assistance for health has played an important role in health financing in low-income countries, though its significance varies substantially across countries and health programs. External funding peaked at approximately $40 billion annually in recent years, representing a major resource for HIV/AIDS, malaria, tuberculosis, and immunization programs in particular. However, external funding accounts for less than 1% of global health expenditure (29).

External financing creates both opportunities and challenges for recipient countries. Donor resources have enabled expansion of essential services that would otherwise be unaffordable, including antiretroviral treatment for millions of people living with HIV. However, earmarking and fragmentation of external funding can distort national priorities, create parallel systems, and undermine domestic financing efforts. Concerns about sustainability have intensified as some donors reduce health commitments (30).

The transition from external to domestic financing has become a critical policy challenge as countries graduate from low-income status or as donor priorities shift. Effective transition planning requires gradual increases in domestic financing, maintained coverage of essential services, and attention to the most vulnerable populations who may lose coverage as external support declines (31).

## Cost-Effectiveness Analysis in Health

### Principles and Methods

Cost-effectiveness analysis compares the costs and health outcomes of alternative interventions to inform resource allocation decisions. The fundamental question addressed is: how much health improvement can be purchased with available resources, and which interventions offer the best value? This question is inherently comparative—cost-effectiveness is meaningful only in relation to alternatives, including the option of doing nothing (32).

The basic measure in cost-effectiveness analysis is the incremental cost-effectiveness ratio, calculated as the difference in costs between interventions divided by the difference in health outcomes. When comparing a new intervention to current practice, the incremental cost-effectiveness ratio indicates the additional cost per additional unit of health outcome achieved. Interventions with lower incremental cost-effectiveness ratios represent better value (33).

Health outcomes in cost-effectiveness analysis are typically measured using summary measures that combine mortality and morbidity impacts. The quality-adjusted life year weights life years by health-related quality of life, with one year in perfect health equivalent to one quality-adjusted life year. The disability-adjusted life year, developed for global burden of disease studies, measures disease burden as the sum of years of life lost to premature mortality and years lived with disability (34).

### Cost-Effectiveness Thresholds

Cost-effectiveness thresholds provide decision rules for determining whether an intervention represents good value for money in a given context. Early approaches suggested thresholds based on multiples of gross domestic product per capita, with interventions costing less than one gross domestic product per capita per disability-adjusted life year averted considered highly cost-effective and those below three times gross domestic product per capita considered cost-effective (35).

However, these gross domestic product-based thresholds have been increasingly questioned on both theoretical and empirical grounds. Studies examining actual coverage decisions and budget constraints suggest that many countries operate at effective thresholds well below one gross domestic product per capita. Furthermore, threshold values should in principle reflect the opportunity cost of resources—the health gains foregone when funds are allocated to one intervention rather than another (36).

Recent guidance from the World Health Organization recommends country-specific threshold estimation based on empirical analysis of health outcomes produced by marginal changes in health expenditure. This approach acknowledges that thresholds should differ across countries based on resource constraints and that explicit threshold values can support more transparent priority-setting processes (37).

### Applications in Global Health

Cost-effectiveness analysis has informed major resource allocation decisions in global health, from vaccine introduction to treatment program expansion. The Disease Control Priorities project has systematically assessed the cost-effectiveness of interventions across health conditions, identifying packages of highly cost-effective interventions that could address the majority of preventable mortality in low- and middle-income countries (38).

Vaccine introduction decisions illustrate the application of cost-effectiveness analysis in practice. Before introducing new vaccines into national immunization programs, countries typically conduct economic evaluations considering vaccine costs, disease burden, efficacy, and implementation requirements. These analyses have supported decisions on rotavirus, pneumococcal, and human papillomavirus vaccines across diverse country contexts (39).

However, cost-effectiveness analysis faces important limitations. Results are sensitive to methodological choices regarding discount rates, time horizons, and outcome measurement. Equity considerations may conflict with efficiency objectives if the most cost-effective interventions do not reach the most disadvantaged populations. Multi-criteria decision analysis and extended cost-effectiveness analysis have been proposed to incorporate equity and financial protection alongside efficiency (40).

## Financial Protection and Catastrophic Expenditure

### Measuring Financial Protection

Financial protection represents a core dimension of universal health coverage, ensuring that health care costs do not cause financial hardship or impoverishment. Measurement of financial protection centers on two related concepts: catastrophic health expenditure and impoverishment due to health spending. Both indicators capture household-level financial consequences of health care costs (41).

Catastrophic health expenditure is typically defined as out-of-pocket health spending exceeding a threshold percentage of household income or consumption. Common thresholds include 10% of total household consumption or 40% of non-food consumption. The World Bank and World Health Organization jointly track catastrophic health expenditure as part of universal health coverage monitoring, finding that over 900 million people globally face catastrophic spending annually (42).

Impoverishment due to health spending occurs when out-of-pocket payments push households below the poverty line or deepen poverty for those already poor. Approximately 100 million people are pushed into extreme poverty each year due to health costs. This impoverishment measure captures the most severe financial consequences of inadequate financial protection and links health financing to broader poverty reduction objectives (43).

### Determinants of Financial Protection

The extent of financial protection in a health system depends on multiple factors including the health financing mix, benefit package design, and health service organization. Countries with higher shares of prepaid and pooled financing—through taxation or insurance—consistently demonstrate lower catastrophic expenditure rates. The relationship between out-of-pocket payment shares and catastrophic expenditure is well established empirically across countries (44).

Benefit package comprehensiveness affects financial protection by determining which services are covered without direct charges. Packages that exclude expensive inpatient care, chronic disease medications, or certain diagnostic tests leave individuals exposed to potentially catastrophic costs for these services. Extending coverage to essential high-cost services represents a priority for financial protection even when it requires difficult trade-offs with other objectives (45).

Service delivery organization also matters for financial protection. When public services are unavailable, of poor quality, or geographically inaccessible, individuals may turn to private providers with higher costs and no financial protection. Strengthening public service delivery and regulating private sector pricing can complement financing reforms in reducing household financial burden (46).

### Policy Approaches to Strengthening Financial Protection

Policy approaches to strengthening financial protection address both the financing and delivery sides of health systems. On the financing side, expanding prepaid coverage through taxation or insurance mechanisms directly reduces out-of-pocket payment shares. Targeted subsidies for poor and vulnerable populations can extend coverage to those unable to afford contributions. Reducing or eliminating user fees at public facilities removes a direct barrier to access (47).

Design of cost-sharing arrangements significantly affects financial protection. While some copayments may be retained to manage demand or generate revenue, these should be designed to minimize barriers for essential services and protect vulnerable populations. Annual caps on out-of-pocket spending, income-related copayments, and exemptions for priority populations and services can preserve some cost-sharing while limiting financial exposure (48).

Monitoring financial protection outcomes enables evidence-based policy adjustment. Regular household surveys with detailed health expenditure modules provide data for tracking catastrophic expenditure and impoverishment. This monitoring should be disaggregated by income quintile, geographic area, and other equity-relevant dimensions to identify populations remaining at risk despite overall progress (49).

## Health Care Markets and Regulation

### Market Failures in Health Care

Health care markets differ fundamentally from markets for most other goods and services due to pervasive market failures. Information asymmetry between providers and patients means that patients cannot fully evaluate the quality or appropriateness of care they receive. This asymmetry gives providers substantial influence over demand for their services and undermines the assumption of informed consumer choice that underpins market efficiency (50).

Uncertainty regarding both the incidence and cost of illness creates demand for insurance, but insurance markets face their own failures. Adverse selection occurs when individuals with higher health risks are more likely to purchase insurance, potentially leading to unsustainable risk pools if insurers cannot differentiate pricing. Moral hazard describes the tendency for insured individuals to use more services than they would if paying the full cost directly (51).

Externalities—effects on third parties not involved in transactions—are significant in health care. Infectious disease prevention generates benefits beyond the individual receiving vaccination or treatment through reduced transmission. Mental health services can benefit families and communities beyond the individual patient. These positive externalities suggest that private markets will undersupply such services relative to the social optimum (52).

### Public-Private Mix in Health Systems

All health systems involve some combination of public and private financing and provision. The appropriate public-private mix has been debated extensively, with positions ranging from advocacy for predominantly public systems to support for market-based approaches with limited government roles. Evidence suggests that the specific arrangements matter more than the public-private balance per se (53).

Private health care provision is substantial in most countries, including many with strong public financing. Private providers deliver the majority of outpatient care in countries as diverse as India, Nigeria, and the United States. Engaging private providers effectively—through contracting, regulation, and strategic purchasing—represents an important policy challenge regardless of ideological preferences (54).

Public-private partnerships in health take various forms, from infrastructure financing arrangements to service delivery contracts. Evidence on partnership effectiveness is mixed, with success dependent on contract design, government capacity for oversight, and alignment of incentives with public health objectives. Partnership arrangements that transfer risk appropriately and maintain accountability can complement public service delivery (55).

### Regulatory Approaches

Regulation of health care markets aims to correct market failures and protect public interests including access, quality, and affordability. Regulatory tools include licensing and accreditation of providers, price regulation, quality standards and enforcement, and rules governing insurance markets. The appropriate regulatory intensity and approach depend on market structure and government capacity (56).

Provider regulation through licensing establishes minimum qualifications for health care practice, while accreditation systems assess facility quality against defined standards. However, regulation is only effective if enforced, and many countries lack the institutional capacity for meaningful oversight of large and fragmented provider markets. Regulatory capture—where regulated entities influence regulators to serve their interests—represents a persistent challenge (57).

Price regulation has been applied in various forms across health systems. Reference pricing for pharmaceuticals establishes maximum reimbursement levels based on prices in comparator countries or for similar products. Hospital rate regulation can control spending growth but may create access problems if rates are set too low. Evidence on price regulation effectiveness depends heavily on design details and implementation context (58).

## Health System Efficiency

### Measuring Health System Efficiency

Health system efficiency measurement seeks to identify waste and opportunities for improvement. Technical efficiency at the facility level can be assessed using data envelopment analysis, which identifies the production frontier representing best practice and measures how far individual facilities fall from this frontier. Stochastic frontier analysis offers an alternative approach that accounts for statistical noise in efficiency estimates (59).

At the system level, efficiency measurement is more challenging. The World Health Organization's 2000 World Health Report ranked countries by health system efficiency, generating substantial controversy over methodology and interpretation. Subsequent work has emphasized the difficulty of attributing health outcomes to health system inputs given the importance of social determinants and the complexity of disentangling efficiency from effectiveness (60).

Allocative efficiency assessment requires comparing actual resource allocation to an optimal distribution across interventions. The Disease Control Priorities project and related analyses have identified substantial potential health gains from reallocating resources toward more cost-effective interventions. However, implementing such reallocation faces political, institutional, and practical constraints (61).

### Sources of Inefficiency

Health system inefficiency arises from multiple sources at different levels. At the clinical level, inappropriate care—services that provide little or no benefit or that cause harm—represents waste. Studies estimate that 20-40% of health spending in high-income countries may be for services of questionable value. Overuse of diagnostics, unnecessary procedures, and antimicrobial misuse contribute to clinical inefficiency (62).

Administrative inefficiency encompasses excess costs of managing health systems, including billing, insurance processing, and regulatory compliance. The United States spends more than any other country on health administration, with estimates suggesting administrative costs account for over 30% of total health expenditure. Simplifying administrative processes through single-payer or streamlined multi-payer systems can reduce these costs (63).

Procurement inefficiency—paying more than necessary for pharmaceuticals, supplies, and equipment—is common across health systems. Price variation for identical products can exceed 100% across facilities or countries. Pooled procurement, reference pricing, and transparent tendering processes can achieve substantial savings. The GAVI Vaccine Alliance and Global Fund have demonstrated the potential of pooled procurement for donor-funded commodities (64).

### Strategies for Improving Efficiency

Improving health system efficiency requires interventions across multiple domains. Strategic purchasing—actively deciding what services to buy, from whom, and at what price—can improve both technical and allocative efficiency compared to passive reimbursement. Purchasers can use contracts to specify quality requirements, performance targets, and payment rates that incentivize efficient practice (65).

Provider payment reform affects incentives for efficient care delivery. Fee-for-service payment encourages volume over value, potentially driving unnecessary utilization. Alternative approaches including capitation, case-based payments, and pay-for-performance can better align provider incentives with efficiency and quality objectives, though each creates its own potential distortions (66).

Health technology assessment institutionalizes evidence-based coverage decisions. By systematically evaluating clinical effectiveness, cost-effectiveness, and budget impact of new technologies, health technology assessment bodies can ensure that health systems adopt technologies offering good value while avoiding wasteful spending on marginally beneficial innovations. Such bodies operate in countries including the United Kingdom, Thailand, and Brazil (67).

## Case Studies in Health Financing

**Case Study 30.1: Thailand's Universal Coverage Scheme**

Thailand's achievement of universal health coverage through the Universal Coverage Scheme launched in 2002 represents one of the most significant health financing reforms of recent decades. Prior to reform, approximately 30% of the population lacked health coverage. The Universal Coverage Scheme extended tax-financed coverage to this previously uninsured population, complementing existing civil servant and social security schemes (68).

The Universal Coverage Scheme operates through annual capitation payments to contracted provider networks, creating closed-end budgets that have controlled expenditure growth. Importantly, the scheme provides comprehensive benefits with minimal copayments—just 30 baht per visit initially, later eliminated—ensuring strong financial protection. Per capita government health expenditure increased substantially to support the expansion (69).

Evidence on scheme impact demonstrates significant improvements in access, utilization, and financial protection. Catastrophic health expenditure declined markedly following implementation, particularly among the poorest quintiles. Health outcomes including infant mortality continued improving, though attribution to the coverage expansion is complicated by concurrent economic and social development (70).

Thailand's experience illustrates several key lessons for health financing reform. Strong political commitment—the scheme was a signature policy of Prime Minister Thaksin Shinawatra—enabled rapid implementation. Tax-based financing proved feasible in a middle-income country with reasonable administrative capacity. And closed-end budgets with capitation payments have maintained financial sustainability despite comprehensive benefits (71).

**Case Study 30.2: Cost-Effectiveness of Rotavirus Vaccine in Bangladesh**

Bangladesh's decision to introduce rotavirus vaccine into the national immunization program followed rigorous cost-effectiveness analysis that illustrated evidence-based priority setting in practice. Rotavirus causes approximately 25% of diarrheal deaths among children under five globally, with particularly high mortality in low-income settings including Bangladesh (72).

The cost-effectiveness analysis modeled vaccine impact on rotavirus hospitalizations, outpatient visits, and deaths based on local disease burden data and clinical trial efficacy estimates. Costs included vaccine procurement, delivery through the existing immunization program, and medical care costs averted. Health outcomes were measured in disability-adjusted life years averted (73).

Results indicated that rotavirus vaccination would be highly cost-effective in Bangladesh at available vaccine prices, with incremental cost-effectiveness ratios well below threshold values. Sensitivity analyses explored uncertainty in key parameters, demonstrating robust cost-effectiveness across plausible ranges. The analysis supported the government's decision to introduce the vaccine with support from Gavi, the Vaccine Alliance (74).

This case illustrates the application of cost-effectiveness analysis to vaccine introduction decisions. Country-specific analysis using local data strengthens the evidence base for decision-making. Standardized methods enable comparison across interventions and countries. However, cost-effectiveness is one input among several in coverage decisions, alongside budget impact, equity, and implementation feasibility considerations (75).

**Case Study 30.3: India's Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY**

India launched Ayushman Bharat Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana (PM-JAY) in 2018 as the world's largest government-funded health insurance program, targeting approximately 500 million people in the bottom 40% of the income distribution. The program provides coverage up to 500,000 rupees (approximately $6,000) per family annually for secondary and tertiary care at empaneled public and private hospitals (76).

PM-JAY built on previous state-level insurance schemes while substantially expanding coverage and benefits. The program uses a trust-based model in most states, with the government acting as insurer rather than contracting with commercial insurers. A sophisticated information technology platform enables paperless enrollment based on socioeconomic census data and facilitates claims processing (77).

Early evaluations indicate rapid expansion of coverage and utilization, with millions of hospitalizations covered in the first years of implementation. However, concerns have been raised about benefit package adequacy for the highest-cost conditions, inclusion errors in targeting, and the continued burden of outpatient care and medication costs not covered by the scheme. Primary care strengthening through Ayushman Bharat Health and Wellness Centres complements the hospitalization coverage (78).

PM-JAY illustrates both the potential and limitations of large-scale insurance programs for financial protection. The program demonstrates government capacity for rapid scale-up using technology platforms. However, the focus on hospitalization leaves substantial out-of-pocket exposure for outpatient care. Comprehensive financial protection likely requires integration of hospitalization coverage with primary care and outpatient medicine benefits (79).

**Case Study 30.4: Results-Based Financing in Rwanda**

Rwanda implemented performance-based financing for health facilities beginning in 2002, becoming a prominent example of results-based financing in low-income settings. Under the program, health facilities receive payments based on verified delivery of priority services including immunization, institutional deliveries, and family planning. Payment rates are set to incentivize underutilized high-impact services (80).

The program design includes several critical elements. Service delivery is verified through community-based organizations and independent counter-verification to ensure payment integrity. Quality assessments affect payment amounts, linking incentives to both quantity and quality. Facility autonomy in using earned revenues allows local decision-making to improve services (81).

Rigorous evaluation using a randomized controlled design demonstrated significant impacts on targeted service utilization and quality. Institutional deliveries, preventive visits for children, and other incentivized services increased substantially in intervention facilities compared to controls. Some evidence suggested spillover improvements in non-incentivized services, though findings were mixed (82).

Rwanda's experience with results-based financing illustrates both potential benefits and implementation requirements. When carefully designed with verification systems and appropriate incentive levels, performance-based payments can improve service delivery. However, the approach requires substantial administrative capacity and may not be appropriate for all services or settings. Integration with community-based health insurance has strengthened Rwanda's overall health financing architecture (83).

## Emerging Issues in Health Financing

### Sustainable Financing for Universal Health Coverage

Achieving and sustaining universal health coverage requires addressing financing gaps in many countries. The Lancet Commission on Investing in Health estimated that low-income and middle-income countries would need to increase health spending by $274 billion annually by 2030 to achieve ambitious health targets. Mobilizing these resources domestically while reducing dependence on external financing presents a significant challenge (84).

Expanding fiscal space for health involves multiple strategies: economic growth that expands the revenue base; improving tax administration and reducing illicit financial flows; reprioritizing government budgets toward health; and introducing or increasing earmarked health taxes. Each strategy has different feasibility and yield across country contexts. Sustainable financing requires attention to all potential sources (85).

Efficiency improvements can stretch available resources further, but technical efficiency gains alone are unlikely to close financing gaps in the most resource-constrained settings. External financing will remain important for low-income countries for the foreseeable future, though with increasing emphasis on domestic co-financing, transition planning, and country ownership of financing strategies (86).

### Climate Change and Health Financing

Climate change poses profound challenges for health financing. Direct health costs from climate-related illness, injury, and mortality are projected to increase substantially as extreme weather events intensify and vector-borne disease ranges expand. These costs will fall disproportionately on lower-income countries least responsible for emissions and least equipped to respond (87).

Adaptation investments in health systems—including climate-resilient infrastructure, surveillance systems, and health workforce capacity—require substantial financing. Integrating health into climate finance mechanisms and national adaptation plans can mobilize additional resources. However, health has historically received only a small fraction of climate adaptation funding (88).

The intersection of climate and health financing highlights the need for cross-sectoral approaches. Investments in clean energy, sustainable transportation, and food systems can generate health co-benefits while reducing emissions. Quantifying these co-benefits through economic analysis can strengthen the case for climate action while improving resource allocation across health-affecting sectors (89).

### Digital Health and Financing Innovation

Digital health technologies are transforming both health service delivery and health financing. Electronic health records, mobile health applications, and telemedicine platforms can improve efficiency and extend access. Digital payment systems and mobile money have expanded options for collecting insurance contributions and processing claims in settings with limited banking infrastructure (90).

However, digital health investments require careful economic evaluation. Technology costs, including initial investment and ongoing maintenance, must be weighed against expected benefits. Many digital health interventions lack rigorous cost-effectiveness evidence. The potential for widening inequities through digital divides requires attention to ensure that innovations benefit rather than bypass disadvantaged populations (91).

Innovative financing mechanisms including health impact bonds, blended finance, and results-based financing for digital health are being explored. These approaches seek to mobilize private capital for health while linking payments to verified outcomes. Early experience suggests potential but also highlights the complexity of designing effective payment mechanisms for health innovations (92).

## Conclusion

Health economics provides essential frameworks for understanding and improving health resource allocation. Fundamental concepts including scarcity, opportunity cost, efficiency, and equity guide analysis of health financing policies and programs. These concepts have practical application across diverse country contexts as nations pursue universal health coverage.

Health financing systems mobilize resources through various mechanisms including taxation, social health insurance, community-based insurance, out-of-pocket payments, and external assistance. Each mechanism has distinct implications for revenue generation, risk pooling, and equity. Moving toward universal coverage generally requires expanding prepaid and pooled financing while reducing reliance on direct out-of-pocket payments.

Cost-effectiveness analysis informs evidence-based priority setting by comparing the value of alternative interventions. While methodological debates continue, economic evaluation has become integral to vaccine introduction decisions, treatment guideline development, and benefit package design. Appropriate use of cost-effectiveness evidence alongside equity and other considerations strengthens health system decision-making.

Financial protection—ensuring that health care costs do not cause financial hardship—represents a core dimension of universal health coverage. Measurement of catastrophic health expenditure and impoverishment provides evidence for tracking progress and identifying populations remaining at risk. Policy approaches to strengthening financial protection address both financing mechanisms and service delivery organization.

Health care market failures justify active government roles in financing, provision, and regulation. The appropriate public-private mix varies across contexts, but all systems require effective engagement of private providers and appropriate regulatory frameworks. Improving health system efficiency through strategic purchasing, payment reform, and health technology assessment can extend the reach of available resources.

Case studies from Thailand, Bangladesh, India, and Rwanda illustrate how health economics concepts translate into policy and practice. These experiences demonstrate that progress toward universal health coverage is achievable across income levels through sustained political commitment, appropriate financing mechanisms, and evidence-based decision-making. Continued learning from diverse country experiences will strengthen the global knowledge base for health financing reform.
