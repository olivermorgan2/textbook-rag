## Introduction

Global health policy and governance encompass the complex systems, institutions, and processes through which the international community addresses health challenges that transcend national boundaries. This chapter examines how global health policies are developed, negotiated, implemented, and evaluated, exploring the roles of diverse actors from intergovernmental organizations to civil society. Understanding global health governance is essential for public health practitioners who must navigate multilateral systems, advocate for health priorities in diplomatic forums, and translate international commitments into national action.

The architecture of global health governance has evolved substantially since the establishment of the World Health Organization in 1948, responding to new health challenges, shifting power dynamics, and changing expectations about the role of health in international relations. Recent crises, particularly the COVID-19 pandemic, have revealed both the strengths and limitations of existing governance mechanisms, spurring renewed debates about reform and the need for more effective collective action. This chapter provides frameworks for analyzing governance structures, understanding policy processes, and engaging effectively in global health diplomacy.

## 31.1 Introduction to Global Health Governance
### 31.1.1 Defining Global Health Governance
**Global health governance** refers to the use of formal and informal institutions, rules, and processes by states, intergovernmental organizations, and non-state actors to address health issues that transcend national boundaries (1). This concept extends beyond traditional notions of government to encompass the broader array of actors and mechanisms that shape health outcomes at the global level. Fidler (2) distinguishes governance from government, noting that governance involves steering and coordination rather than direct control, reflecting the reality that no single authority commands global health action.

The rationale for global health governance rests on the recognition that many health challenges require collective action that individual nations cannot achieve alone. Infectious disease transmission, pharmaceutical development, health workforce migration, and environmental health threats all exemplify problems where national boundaries constrain effective responses (3). **Collective action problems** in global health arise when individual countries acting in their own interest produce suboptimal outcomes for all, necessitating coordinated governance mechanisms to align incentives and facilitate cooperation.

Global health governance operates through multiple channels: binding international law, non-binding norms and standards, financial mechanisms, technical cooperation, and diplomatic engagement. The effectiveness of governance depends not only on formal institutional arrangements but also on the legitimacy, resources, and political will that actors bring to collective endeavors. Understanding these dynamics is essential for anyone seeking to influence global health policy or translate international commitments into national action.

### 31.1.2 Historical Evolution of Global Health Governance
The origins of international health cooperation can be traced to the **International Sanitary Conferences** beginning in 1851, convened to establish common approaches to quarantine and disease control that would not unduly restrict trade (4). These early efforts focused narrowly on preventing the international spread of cholera, plague, and yellow fever while minimizing disruption to commerce. The first International Sanitary Convention in 1892 established standardized reporting requirements and quarantine measures that presaged modern international health regulations.

The establishment of the **League of Nations Health Organization** in 1923 marked a significant expansion of international health cooperation, extending beyond epidemic control to address broader health concerns including standardization, statistics, and technical assistance (5). This organization laid groundwork for contemporary global health governance, establishing precedents for international technical cooperation and norm-setting that the World Health Organization would later build upon.

The creation of WHO in 1948 represented a transformative moment in global health governance. The WHO Constitution established health as a fundamental human right and mandated the organization to act as the directing and coordinating authority on international health (6). Over subsequent decades, WHO's role evolved from technical assistance and disease control toward broader health systems development, primary health care promotion, and global health leadership. The Declaration of Alma-Ata in 1978 and the Health for All movement exemplified WHO's expanded normative role in shaping global health policy.

The late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries witnessed significant changes in the global health governance landscape, including the proliferation of new actors, increased private sector involvement, and the emergence of major global health initiatives outside the UN system. The establishment of UNAIDS, the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, and Gavi, the Vaccine Alliance reflected both the mobilization of new resources and the fragmentation of governance across multiple institutions.

## 31.2 Global Health Governance Architecture
### 31.2.1 The World Health Organization
The **World Health Organization** remains the preeminent institution in global health governance, serving as the directing and coordinating authority for health within the United Nations system. Understanding WHO's structure, functions, and limitations is essential for effective engagement with global health policy processes.

The **World Health Assembly (WHA)**, composed of representatives from all 194 Member States, serves as the supreme decision-making body of WHO (7). Meeting annually in Geneva, the WHA determines policies, approves the programme budget, elects the Director-General, and adopts international health regulations and conventions. The principle of one country, one vote in the WHA provides formal equality among Member States, though in practice, negotiations often reflect underlying power disparities. WHA resolutions, while typically non-binding, establish important normative frameworks that shape national health policies.

The **Executive Board**, consisting of 34 members technically qualified in health and serving three-year terms, meets twice yearly to implement WHA decisions and provide governance between Assembly sessions (8). The Board prepares the agenda for WHA meetings, submits proposals, and oversees organizational performance. Regional balance in Board composition helps ensure geographic representation, though questions persist about whether this arrangement adequately reflects contemporary geopolitical realities.

The WHO **Secretariat**, led by the Director-General, implements programmes, provides technical support to Member States, and coordinates international health action. Six regional offices—for Africa, the Americas, Southeast Asia, Europe, the Eastern Mediterranean, and the Western Pacific—operate with substantial autonomy, including electing their own regional directors. This decentralized structure facilitates regional responsiveness but can complicate global coordination and create inconsistencies in policy implementation (9).

WHO's financing structure significantly shapes its governance capacity. **Assessed contributions** from Member States, based on economic capacity, provide predictable but limited resources, currently representing only about 16% of the total budget. **Voluntary contributions**, which account for the majority of WHO funding, come with varying degrees of earmarking that constrain the organization's flexibility (10). This funding model has generated concerns about donor influence over organizational priorities and the sustainability of essential functions. Recent reform efforts have sought to increase assessed contributions and provide more flexible funding to strengthen WHO's independence and effectiveness.

### 31.2.2 Other UN System Actors
Multiple United Nations agencies beyond WHO contribute to global health governance, each bringing distinct mandates and capacities. **UNICEF** plays a crucial role in child health, nutrition, and immunization, operating extensive field programmes and serving as a major vaccine procurement agency. UNICEF's operational capacity and country presence complement WHO's normative functions, though coordination challenges can arise from overlapping mandates (11).

**UNAIDS**, established in 1996 as a joint programme of multiple UN agencies, pioneered new governance approaches in global health, including meaningful engagement of civil society and affected communities in decision-making. UNAIDS coordinates HIV/AIDS responses across the UN system while advocating for resources and policy change. Its inclusive governance model has influenced broader discussions about civil society participation in global health governance (12).

The **United Nations Population Fund (UNFPA)** leads on sexual and reproductive health and rights, family planning, and maternal health. In an era of contested reproductive health policies, UNFPA's work demonstrates how global health governance intersects with broader political and ideological debates. The agency's experience navigating policy disagreements offers lessons for managing controversy in multilateral settings.

The **World Bank**, though not exclusively a health institution, has become a major player in global health governance through its health financing work, analytical capacity, and substantial lending for health systems strengthening. The Bank's convening power and influence over domestic economic policy give it leverage in health policy discussions that differs from WHO's technical authority (13). Tensions between economic and health perspectives sometimes emerge in Bank-supported programs, raising questions about appropriate governance arrangements for health within development institutions.

### 31.2.3 Non-State Actors in Global Health Governance
Contemporary global health governance involves extensive participation by non-state actors, including civil society organizations, private sector entities, and philanthropic foundations. This pluralization of governance has expanded resources and perspectives but also raised questions about legitimacy, accountability, and coherence.

**Civil society organizations (CSOs)** contribute to global health governance through advocacy, service delivery, community engagement, and accountability monitoring. CSO involvement varies from grassroots community groups to large international non-governmental organizations with significant resources and policy influence. The meaningful participation of affected communities, pioneered in HIV/AIDS responses, has become an expected feature of global health governance, though challenges remain in ensuring representative and effective engagement (14).

**Private sector** involvement in global health governance takes multiple forms, from pharmaceutical company participation in product development partnerships to food and beverage industry engagement in nutrition policy discussions. Private sector resources and expertise can accelerate health innovation and programme implementation, but conflicts of interest raise concerns about industry influence on policy. WHO's Framework of Engagement with Non-State Actors seeks to manage these relationships while safeguarding against inappropriate influence (15).

**Philanthropic foundations**, particularly the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, have become major actors in global health governance. The Gates Foundation's substantial funding—exceeding that of most WHO Member States—gives it significant influence over research priorities, programme approaches, and institutional agendas. While foundation resources have accelerated progress on priority diseases, questions persist about accountability, priority-setting legitimacy, and the sustainability of foundation-dependent initiatives (16).

**Public-private partnerships** such as Gavi and the Global Fund represent hybrid governance arrangements that blend public authority with private resources and expertise. These partnerships have mobilized substantial funding and achieved impressive results, but their governance structures—involving donors, recipient countries, civil society, and private sector—create complex accountability relationships that differ from traditional intergovernmental models.

### 31.2.4 Regional Health Governance
Regional organizations increasingly contribute to global health governance, addressing shared health challenges and coordinating responses across neighboring countries. Regional governance offers advantages of geographic proximity, shared contexts, and potentially stronger enforcement mechanisms, while complementing global frameworks.

The **Africa Centres for Disease Control and Prevention (Africa CDC)**, established in 2017 as a specialized agency of the African Union, has rapidly emerged as a significant regional health governance actor. Africa CDC's response to COVID-19 demonstrated the value of regional coordination, facilitating collective procurement, technical guidance adapted to regional contexts, and political mobilization across the continent (17). The institution's governance through the African Union provides political authority that WHO regional offices may lack.

The **European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC)** provides technical expertise and coordination for communicable disease surveillance and response across European Union Member States. While ECDC cannot mandate national action, its technical guidance and surveillance functions support regional health security. The European experience illustrates how regional governance can complement national systems while respecting subsidiarity principles (18).

Other regional bodies, including the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) and Mercosur, have developed health governance mechanisms addressing shared challenges from infectious disease control to pharmaceutical regulation. These arrangements vary in institutional development and effectiveness but collectively represent an important intermediate level between national and global governance (19).


## 31.3 International Health Law
### 31.3.1 Types of International Health Instruments
International health law encompasses a spectrum of instruments with varying degrees of legal bindingness and enforcement mechanisms. Understanding these distinctions is essential for assessing the tools available to address global health challenges and the constraints that limit their effectiveness.

**Binding international health law** creates legal obligations for states that consent to be bound, typically through treaty ratification. The WHO Constitution grants the World Health Assembly authority to adopt **conventions** requiring ratification and **regulations** that bind Member States unless they explicitly opt out. This regulatory authority distinguishes WHO from most international organizations and provides potentially powerful tools for collective action, though it has been exercised sparingly (20).

**Soft law** instruments, including WHO recommendations, guidelines, and declarations, establish norms and expectations without creating enforceable legal obligations. While lacking binding force, soft law can significantly influence state behavior by establishing best practices, creating normative pressure, and providing reference points for domestic policy development. The influence of WHO recommendations on national tobacco control policies before the FCTC illustrates soft law's potential impact (21).

The choice between binding and non-binding instruments involves tradeoffs. Binding agreements may achieve stronger commitments but require lengthy negotiation, face higher barriers to adoption, and risk limited ratification. Non-binding instruments can be developed more rapidly and adopted more broadly but may lack the compliance mechanisms needed to ensure implementation. Strategic selection among available instruments depends on the nature of the health challenge, political feasibility, and the specific objectives sought (22).

### 31.3.2 International Health Regulations (2005)
The **International Health Regulations (2005)** represent the primary binding international legal instrument for preventing and responding to the international spread of disease. The IHR establish obligations for Member States to develop core public health capacities, notify WHO of events that may constitute public health emergencies of international concern, and respond to WHO recommendations (23).

Central to the IHR is the concept of a **Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC)**, defined as an extraordinary event that constitutes a public health risk through international spread and may require coordinated international response. The WHO Director-General, advised by an Emergency Committee of experts, determines whether events meet PHEIC criteria. Since 2005, PHEICs have been declared for H1N1 influenza, polio, Ebola, Zika, and COVID-19, among others (24).

The IHR require Member States to develop and maintain minimum **core public health capacities** for surveillance, response, points of entry, and laboratory functions. However, assessment through Joint External Evaluations and State Party Self-Assessment Annual Reports has revealed significant capacity gaps, particularly in lower-income countries. By 2019, only one-third of countries reported meeting all IHR core capacity requirements (25).

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed significant limitations in IHR implementation. Delayed notification, inadequate information sharing, limited compliance with WHO recommendations, and insufficient core capacities undermined the regulations' effectiveness. These failures have prompted calls for IHR reform, including strengthened compliance mechanisms, enhanced financing for capacity building, and improved accountability arrangements. Negotiations for amendments to the IHR continue alongside discussions of a new pandemic treaty (26).

### 31.3.3 WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control
The **WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC)**, adopted in 2003 and entering into force in 2005, represents the first international treaty negotiated under WHO auspices and a landmark in international health law. The FCTC addresses tobacco as a global health threat, establishing evidence-based measures to reduce both demand for and supply of tobacco products (27).

The FCTC addresses **demand reduction** through measures including price and tax policies (Article 6), protection from tobacco smoke exposure (Article 8), regulation of tobacco product contents and disclosures (Articles 9-10), packaging and labeling requirements including health warnings (Article 11), education and public awareness (Article 12), advertising bans (Article 13), and cessation support (Article 14). Guidelines adopted by the Conference of the Parties provide detailed implementation guidance for each article (28).

**Supply reduction** measures address illicit trade (Article 15), sales to minors (Article 16), economically viable alternatives for tobacco workers and growers (Article 17), and environmental protection (Article 18). The Protocol to Eliminate Illicit Trade in Tobacco Products, adopted in 2012, establishes a comprehensive framework for addressing smuggling and counterfeiting.

The FCTC has achieved near-universal ratification, with 182 Parties representing over 90% of the world's population. Implementation has progressed substantially, with significant increases in countries adopting smoke-free legislation, graphic health warnings, and advertising restrictions. However, implementation gaps remain, particularly in lower-income countries facing resource constraints and industry interference (29). The FCTC experience demonstrates both the potential and limitations of international health law, highlighting the importance of sustained attention to implementation beyond treaty adoption (30).

### 31.3.4 Emerging Legal Frameworks
The global health law landscape continues to evolve, with negotiations underway for new instruments addressing contemporary challenges. The COVID-19 pandemic catalyzed intensified efforts to strengthen international legal frameworks for pandemic preparedness and response.

**Pandemic treaty negotiations**, mandated by a special session of the World Health Assembly in December 2021, seek to establish a comprehensive legal framework for pandemic prevention, preparedness, and response. Key issues under negotiation include pathogen access and benefit sharing, technology transfer, financing mechanisms, and governance arrangements. Negotiations have revealed tensions between countries seeking stronger international coordination and those emphasizing national sovereignty (31).

**Antimicrobial resistance** presents another frontier for international health law. While no binding agreement specifically addresses AMR, various soft law instruments including WHA resolutions and the Global Action Plan on AMR establish frameworks for national action. Discussions continue about whether binding legal mechanisms might accelerate progress on this slow-moving but profound threat to modern medicine (32).

The **Nagoya Protocol** on access to genetic resources and benefit sharing, though primarily an environmental agreement, has significant implications for pathogen sharing and pandemic response. Tensions between public health imperatives for rapid pathogen sharing and benefit-sharing principles have prompted discussions about specialized frameworks for pathogens, including within pandemic treaty negotiations (33).

## 31.4 Health Diplomacy
### 31.4.1 Conceptualizing Health Diplomacy
**Health diplomacy** encompasses the negotiation processes that shape global health policy environments and the use of health issues to advance broader diplomatic objectives. Kickbusch and colleagues (34) define health diplomacy as "political change activity that meets the dual goals of improving health while maintaining and improving international relations." This dual character distinguishes health diplomacy from purely technical cooperation, situating health within the broader context of international relations.

Health diplomacy operates at multiple levels. **Global health diplomacy** occurs in multilateral forums such as the World Health Assembly, where countries negotiate resolutions, regulations, and conventions. **Regional health diplomacy** addresses shared health challenges among neighboring countries, often through regional organizations. **Bilateral health diplomacy** involves direct negotiations between countries on health cooperation agreements, health worker migration, or health aspects of trade relationships (35).

The concept of **soft power**—the ability to influence others through attraction rather than coercion—has particular relevance for health diplomacy. Countries may build influence through health assistance, medical diplomacy, or leadership on global health issues. Cuba's medical internationalism, China's health silk road, and US global health programmes all represent soft power strategies with health dimensions. Understanding these dynamics helps explain state motivations in global health and the political context within which technical cooperation occurs (36).

### 31.4.2 Health in Foreign Policy
The integration of health into foreign policy has gained increasing recognition as health challenges affect core foreign policy interests including security, economic prosperity, and international standing. The **Oslo Declaration** of 2007, signed by foreign ministers of Brazil, France, Indonesia, Norway, Senegal, South Africa, and Thailand, marked a significant moment in recognizing health as a foreign policy priority requiring ministerial-level attention (37).

Countries pursuing health as foreign policy have developed various institutional mechanisms. Some have established **health attaché** positions within diplomatic missions, providing specialized expertise for health negotiations and bilateral cooperation. Foreign ministry training programmes increasingly include health content, recognizing that diplomats need familiarity with health issues beyond traditional diplomatic portfolios (38).

The foreign policy community's engagement with health reflects several considerations. Health security concerns, particularly around infectious disease threats, directly implicate national security interests. Economic interests in pharmaceutical and medical device industries create trade policy stakes in health regulations. Development assistance for health represents both humanitarian commitment and diplomatic tool for building relationships with recipient countries. These varied interests create opportunities for advancing health through diplomatic channels while requiring attention to potential conflicts between health and other policy objectives (39).

### 31.4.3 Multilateral Health Negotiations
Effective participation in multilateral health negotiations requires understanding the distinctive features of these processes and developing appropriate strategic approaches. World Health Assembly negotiations differ significantly from bilateral discussions, involving complex dynamics among nearly 200 Member States with diverse interests and capacities.

**Coalition formation** is essential in multilateral settings where individual country influence is limited. Regional groups, interest-based coalitions, and cross-regional alliances all feature in WHA negotiations. The African Group, European Union, Group of 77, and other coalitions coordinate positions and amplify member voices. Ad hoc coalitions form around specific issues, with countries joining different alignments depending on the topic (40).

**Consensus-building** traditions in WHO mean that most decisions are taken without formal votes, even though voting procedures exist. This consensus orientation encourages negotiation and compromise but can also result in weak language that papers over disagreement. Understanding when to seek consensus and when formal decision-making might advance health objectives requires strategic judgment (41).

**North-South dynamics** significantly shape many global health negotiations. Lower-income countries may prioritize technology transfer, financing, and capacity support, while higher-income countries emphasize intellectual property protection, market access, and voluntary approaches. Negotiations on access to medicines, pandemic preparedness, and health financing frequently reveal these tensions. Effective health diplomacy requires understanding these dynamics while seeking common ground that advances health for all (42).

## 31.5 Global Health Policy Processes
### 31.5.1 Agenda Setting in Global Health
Understanding how issues reach the global health policy agenda is essential for advocates seeking to advance health priorities. **Agenda setting** refers to the process by which problems come to attention of decision-makers and gain priority for policy action. Not all health problems, regardless of their magnitude, achieve agenda status; understanding the factors that enable or constrain agenda access helps explain policy attention patterns.

**Kingdon's multiple streams framework** provides a useful lens for analyzing global health agenda setting (43). The framework identifies three streams that flow largely independently: problem streams (conditions recognized as problems requiring attention), policy streams (solutions and proposals developed by specialists), and political streams (political conditions including government priorities and interest group pressures). Policy windows open when streams converge, creating opportunities for significant policy change.

**Problem definition** significantly shapes policy responses. How a health issue is framed—as a security threat, economic concern, human rights issue, or technical problem—affects which actors engage and what responses seem appropriate. The successful framing of HIV/AIDS as simultaneously a development crisis, security threat, and human rights issue helped mobilize unprecedented resources and political attention. Conversely, framing antimicrobial resistance primarily as a technical problem may have constrained the political mobilization needed for effective response (44).

**Policy entrepreneurs** play crucial roles in advancing issues onto the global agenda. These individuals or organizations invest resources in promoting particular problems and solutions, waiting for or creating opportunities to advance their priorities. Effective policy entrepreneurs combine technical expertise with political acumen, building coalitions and seizing opportunities when policy windows open (45).

### 31.5.2 Policy Formulation and Adoption
Once issues achieve agenda status, policy formulation processes develop specific proposals for collective action. In global health, formulation typically involves both technical deliberation and political negotiation, with varying balances depending on the issue and institutional context.

**Evidence-informed policymaking** represents an ideal in global health governance, with expectations that policies should reflect best available evidence about problems and effective interventions. WHO's guideline development processes exemplify attempts to systematically incorporate evidence, using methodologies such as GRADE to assess certainty and develop recommendations. However, evidence alone rarely determines policy; values, interests, and political considerations inevitably shape decisions (46).

**Epistemic communities**—networks of professionals with recognized expertise and shared normative commitments—influence policy formulation through their authority on technical matters. Expert committees, scientific advisory bodies, and professional networks help define problems and legitimate particular solutions. Understanding which communities shape specific policy domains helps explain policy content and identify opportunities for influence (47).

**Stakeholder engagement** in policy formulation has expanded significantly in global health, with increasing expectations for civil society participation and affected community involvement. Processes for non-state actor engagement vary across institutions and issues, from formal consultations to civil society representation in governance bodies. Meaningful engagement requires attention to power dynamics, resource disparities, and the risk that participation processes legitimate decisions without genuinely influencing them (48).

### 31.5.3 Implementation and Evaluation
The adoption of global health policies represents only the beginning of the policy process; implementation determines whether commitments translate into improved health outcomes. Implementation challenges at the global level involve both translating international agreements into national action and building capacities for effective domestic policy implementation.

**Policy implementation frameworks** identify factors affecting whether policies achieve intended objectives. Mazmanian and Sabatier (49) emphasize the importance of clear objectives, adequate causal theory, resources, supportive implementing structures, and favorable external conditions. In global health, implementation challenges multiply across diverse national contexts, varying capacities, and complex multi-actor systems.

**Policy transfer and lesson learning** occur as countries adapt approaches developed elsewhere to their own contexts. Mechanisms of transfer include coercion (conditionality on aid or trade), harmonization (coordinating policies for efficiency), competition (adopting successful policies to remain competitive), and learning (voluntarily adopting policies shown effective elsewhere). The FCTC illustrates policy transfer dynamics, with countries learning from early implementers while adapting provisions to national circumstances (50).

**Monitoring and evaluation** of global health policy implementation provides feedback for policy refinement and accountability for commitments. The SDG indicator framework exemplifies efforts to track progress across countries, while WHO monitoring of FCTC implementation and IHR capacity informs assessments of treaty effectiveness. However, evaluation in global health faces challenges including attribution difficulties, long time horizons for health impacts, and political sensitivities around comparative assessment (51).


## 31.6 Sustainable Development Goals and Health
### 31.6.1 Health in the SDG Framework
The **Sustainable Development Goals**, adopted in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, establish the overarching framework for international development cooperation, including health. **SDG 3** specifically addresses health with the goal to "ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages," encompassing 13 targets covering maternal and child health, communicable and non-communicable diseases, universal health coverage, and health system capacities (52).

Beyond SDG 3, health connects to numerous other goals through the social, economic, and environmental determinants of health. Targets on nutrition (SDG 2), water and sanitation (SDG 6), air quality (SDG 11), and climate action (SDG 13) all have direct health implications. The SDG framework's recognition of interconnections encourages **intersectoral collaboration** and "Health in All Policies" approaches, though implementation of these connections remains challenging (53).

**Universal health coverage (UHC)**, addressed in SDG target 3.8, serves as a unifying framework within SDG 3. UHC encompasses both service coverage—ensuring all people receive needed health services—and financial protection—ensuring health costs do not cause financial hardship. Progress toward UHC provides a synthetic measure of health system development and access to care. As discussed in Chapter 4, UHC's prominence in the SDGs reflects its emergence as the primary organizing concept for health systems strengthening globally (54).

### 31.6.2 SDG Accountability and Monitoring
The SDG framework includes mechanisms for monitoring progress and promoting accountability, though these remain substantially weaker than mechanisms for economic policy coordination. The **SDG indicator framework**, developed by the Inter-Agency and Expert Group on SDG Indicators, provides standardized metrics for assessing progress across all goals and targets. For health, indicators range from mortality rates and disease incidence to measures of service coverage and health system capacity (55).

The **High-Level Political Forum (HLPF)** on Sustainable Development serves as the central UN platform for follow-up and review of the 2030 Agenda. Annual HLPF meetings review progress, with rotating thematic focuses that periodically highlight health. However, the HLPF lacks authority to compel action, relying instead on peer pressure and voluntary commitments to encourage progress (56).

**Voluntary National Reviews (VNRs)** provide opportunities for countries to report on SDG implementation, share lessons, and identify challenges. VNRs vary considerably in quality and depth, and the voluntary nature limits their accountability function. Nevertheless, the VNR process has encouraged national SDG coordination mechanisms and provided platforms for civil society engagement in holding governments accountable for commitments (57).

The **"leave no one behind"** principle, central to the SDG framework, requires attention to inequalities within and among countries. Disaggregated data collection and analysis are essential for identifying who is being left behind and targeting interventions accordingly. For health, this principle connects to longstanding equity concerns and reinforces attention to reaching marginalized and underserved populations.

### 31.6.3 National SDG Adaptation
Translating global SDG commitments into national policy and action requires adaptation to country contexts, integration with existing planning processes, and mobilization of domestic resources. **SDG localization** involves setting national targets, aligning sectoral strategies, and establishing coordination mechanisms across government.

Countries have taken varied approaches to health sector SDG integration. Some have developed specific health sector SDG strategies, while others have incorporated SDG targets into broader national health strategic plans. Rwanda's approach, examined in Case Study 31.4, illustrates how a country can systematically integrate SDG health targets into national planning while maintaining alignment with overall development strategies (58).

**Financing** SDG health targets presents significant challenges, particularly for lower-income countries. Estimates suggest that achieving SDG 3 targets would require substantial increases in health spending in many countries. Domestic resource mobilization, external assistance, and innovative financing mechanisms all contribute to closing financing gaps, though progress has been insufficient, and the COVID-19 pandemic has created additional fiscal pressures (59).

Effective national SDG implementation requires **multisectoral governance mechanisms** that coordinate action across ministries and with non-governmental stakeholders. Health ministries alone cannot achieve SDG health targets; progress requires engagement of finance, education, agriculture, environment, and other sectors. Establishing and sustaining such coordination mechanisms remains a governance challenge in most countries (60).

## 31.7 Global Health Security
### 31.7.1 Health Security Frameworks
**Health security** has emerged as a prominent concept in global health governance, though its meaning and implications remain contested. At its core, health security concerns protection from health threats, but different frameworks emphasize different threats, referent objects, and response approaches.

The **securitization of health** involves framing health issues as security threats requiring exceptional measures. This framing can mobilize political attention and resources, as demonstrated by the treatment of HIV/AIDS as a security issue in UN Security Council discussions. However, securitization also raises concerns about militarizing health responses, prioritizing national interests over global solidarity, and focusing narrowly on threats to powerful countries while neglecting broader health needs (61).

Broader conceptions of health security, aligned with **human security** frameworks, emphasize protection of individuals and communities from diverse health threats including chronic diseases, environmental hazards, and health system failures. The WHO's health security framework encompasses this broader perspective while maintaining particular attention to acute public health emergencies with potential for international spread (62).

Tensions between national and global perspectives on health security became evident during the COVID-19 pandemic. National responses often prioritized domestic populations, restricting exports of medical supplies, implementing travel restrictions contrary to WHO guidance, and engaging in vaccine nationalism. These dynamics highlighted the challenges of achieving cooperative responses when countries perceive fundamental security interests at stake (63).

### 31.7.2 Pandemic Preparedness and Response
Pandemic preparedness has received renewed attention following COVID-19, building on frameworks developed after SARS, H5N1, and H1N1. Effective preparedness requires both national capacity development and international coordination mechanisms.

**National pandemic preparedness plans** establish frameworks for detecting, assessing, and responding to pandemic threats. WHO guidance recommends all-hazards approaches that build flexible capacities applicable across various threat scenarios. However, the existence of plans does not ensure adequate preparedness; many countries with ostensibly comprehensive plans struggled in COVID-19 response due to insufficient investment in implementation (64).

The **Joint External Evaluation (JEE)** provides a voluntary mechanism for assessing country capacities against IHR requirements and identifying gaps for targeted investment. JEE assessments, conducted collaboratively by national and international experts, have been completed in over 100 countries. However, assessments alone do not build capacity; follow-up investment in addressing identified gaps is essential but has often been inadequate (65).

**Emergency response mechanisms** at the global level include WHO's Health Emergencies Programme, which coordinates international response to acute events. The WHO Contingency Fund for Emergencies provides rapid financing for initial response, while longer-term response depends on donor mobilization. Experience from COVID-19 and earlier emergencies has revealed both the value of these mechanisms and their limitations in scale and speed of response (66).

### 31.7.3 Global Health Security Agenda
The **Global Health Security Agenda (GHSA)**, launched in 2014, represents a multilateral effort to accelerate progress toward a world safe from infectious disease threats. GHSA brings together over 70 countries, international organizations, and non-governmental stakeholders to advance health security through measurable targets and action packages (67).

GHSA's **action packages** address specific capacities including antimicrobial resistance, zoonotic disease, biosafety and biosecurity, immunization, national laboratory systems, surveillance, workforce development, and emergency operations. These action packages provide frameworks for assessing progress and targeting investments, complementing IHR core capacity requirements with more specific operational guidance (68).

The **One Health approach**, emphasized within GHSA, recognizes that human, animal, and environmental health are interconnected and that effective health security requires collaboration across these domains. Zoonotic disease emergence, antimicrobial resistance, and food safety all require One Health approaches. GHSA has promoted One Health coordination mechanisms and multisectoral collaboration, though implementation remains challenging (69).

GHSA's voluntary, partnership-based model differs from treaty-based governance, relying on peer pressure, technical support, and demonstration effects rather than legal obligation. This approach has mobilized significant resources and political attention for health security but faces questions about sustainability and effectiveness compared to binding commitments.

## 31.8 Trade and Health
### 31.8.1 Trade Agreements and Public Health
International trade agreements increasingly affect public health through diverse pathways including market access provisions, regulatory standards, investment protections, and intellectual property rules. Understanding these intersections is essential for protecting policy space for health while realizing benefits from trade and investment.

The **World Trade Organization (WTO)** agreements establish the primary multilateral framework for trade governance, with specific agreements addressing goods, services, and intellectual property. Several WTO agreements have direct health relevance: the Agreement on Technical Barriers to Trade affects health and safety regulations; the Agreement on the Application of Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures addresses food safety and animal/plant health measures; and the General Agreement on Trade in Services affects health services trade and regulation (70).

**Bilateral and regional trade agreements** have proliferated, often including provisions that exceed WTO requirements ("TRIPS-plus" provisions) or address issues not covered multilaterally. These agreements may constrain policy space for health in ways not anticipated during negotiation, requiring attention to health implications during trade agreement development. Some agreements include public health exceptions or carve-outs, but the scope and effectiveness of these provisions vary (71).

**Health impact assessment** of trade agreements provides a mechanism for systematically considering health implications during negotiation and implementation. However, such assessments are not routinely conducted, and health ministries often have limited involvement in trade negotiations despite significant health stakes. Strengthening health sector engagement in trade policy processes remains an ongoing challenge (72).

### 31.8.2 Intellectual Property and Access to Medicines
The relationship between intellectual property protection and access to medicines represents one of the most contentious intersections of trade and health policy. The **TRIPS Agreement** establishes minimum standards for patent protection that WTO members must implement, including for pharmaceuticals. While proponents argue patents incentivize innovation, critics contend that patent-based pricing restricts access to essential medicines in lower-income settings (73).

The **Doha Declaration on the TRIPS Agreement and Public Health** (2001) affirmed that TRIPS "can and should be interpreted and implemented in a manner supportive of WTO Members' right to protect public health and, in particular, to promote access to medicines for all." The Declaration recognized the importance of TRIPS flexibilities and explicitly supported countries' right to use compulsory licensing for public health purposes (74).

**TRIPS flexibilities** provide mechanisms for addressing access concerns while remaining within the international trade framework. **Compulsory licensing** allows governments to authorize generic production or importation without patent holder consent, subject to conditions including adequate remuneration. **Parallel importation** permits purchasing patented products from lowest-cost international markets. The 2003 WTO decision created a mechanism for countries with insufficient manufacturing capacity to import generics produced under compulsory license elsewhere (75).

Despite these flexibilities, their utilization remains limited and contested. Countries considering compulsory licensing have faced political and economic pressure, as Thailand's experience (Case Study 31.3) illustrates. Capacity constraints, legal uncertainties, and fear of retaliation deter many countries from exercising flexibilities. TRIPS-plus provisions in bilateral agreements can further restrict policy space by extending patent terms or limiting compulsory licensing grounds (76).

### 31.8.3 Investment Treaties and Health Policy
**Investment treaties**, including bilateral investment treaties and investment chapters in trade agreements, protect foreign investors from government actions that harm their investments. While such protections aim to encourage investment, they can constrain health policy when investors challenge regulations affecting their interests.

**Investor-state dispute settlement (ISDS)** mechanisms allow investors to bring claims directly against states before international arbitration tribunals. Health-related disputes have addressed tobacco packaging requirements, pharmaceutical pricing policies, and environmental regulations. The potential for ISDS challenges creates concerns about **regulatory chill**—governments avoiding legitimate health measures due to fear of investor claims and associated costs (77).

The tobacco industry's use of ISDS to challenge health regulations has drawn particular attention. Philip Morris challenged Australia's plain packaging law through investor-state arbitration (ultimately unsuccessful on jurisdictional grounds) and brought similar claims against Uruguay. These cases, while ultimately resolved in favor of health measures, imposed significant costs on defending governments and may have deterred other countries from adopting similar policies (78).

Protecting policy space for health within investment agreements requires attention to treaty design. Options include carving out health measures from ISDS coverage, requiring exhaustion of domestic remedies before international arbitration, and ensuring that public health exceptions are interpreted broadly. Recent treaty revisions by some countries reflect increased attention to these concerns, though the landscape of investment protection remains complex and contested (79).

## 31.9 Governance Challenges and Reform
### 31.9.1 Power Dynamics in Global Health Governance
Despite formal equality among WHO Member States, global health governance reflects significant power disparities. Understanding these dynamics is essential for effective engagement and for evaluating governance legitimacy and effectiveness.

**Donor influence** shapes global health priorities through control over resources. Major donor countries and foundations determine which diseases and interventions receive funding, potentially distorting priorities away from recipient country needs. The earmarking of voluntary contributions to WHO limits organizational flexibility, effectively giving donors veto power over programme priorities. While efforts to increase flexible funding have achieved some progress, power asymmetries embedded in financing structures persist (80).

**Governance fragmentation** across multiple institutions creates coordination challenges and accountability gaps. The proliferation of global health initiatives, each with distinct governance arrangements, has increased resources but also complicated efforts to ensure coherent, country-led responses. Competition among institutions for funding and influence can undermine effectiveness, while overlapping mandates create confusion about responsibilities (81).

**Democratic deficit** concerns arise when affected populations lack meaningful voice in governance decisions. While civil society participation has expanded, questions persist about whether participation mechanisms genuinely influence decisions or merely legitimate predetermined outcomes. The influence of unelected foundations and private sector actors raises additional accountability concerns. Strengthening accountability to affected communities remains an ongoing challenge in global health governance (82).

### 31.9.2 Reform Proposals and Future Directions
The COVID-19 pandemic has catalyzed renewed attention to global health governance reform, building on longstanding concerns about institutional effectiveness and equity. Various reform proposals seek to address identified weaknesses while navigating political constraints.

**WHO reform** has been a recurring theme, with the current Director-General pursuing ambitious changes including increased sustainable financing, enhanced emergency authorities, and improved accountability mechanisms. The Seventy-fourth World Health Assembly in 2021 adopted a resolution on strengthening WHO preparedness and response, and subsequent negotiations have advanced proposals for both IHR amendments and a new pandemic instrument. However, fundamental reforms face resistance from Member States protective of sovereignty and from stakeholders benefiting from current arrangements (83).

**Pandemic treaty** negotiations represent the most ambitious current reform effort, seeking to establish a comprehensive legal framework for pandemic prevention, preparedness, and response. Key issues under negotiation include mechanisms for pathogen sharing and benefit distribution, technology transfer requirements, financing commitments, and governance arrangements. The outcome will significantly shape global health governance for decades, though negotiations have revealed deep divisions that may limit treaty ambition (84).

Beyond specific institutional reforms, broader shifts in global health governance may emerge from changing geopolitical dynamics, technological developments, and evolving conceptions of global solidarity. The rise of regional health governance, new approaches to financing such as pandemic bonds, and the potential role of digital technologies in surveillance and coordination all represent frontier issues that will shape governance evolution. Ultimately, effective global health governance requires both institutional strengthening and the political will to prioritize health as a shared global interest (85).

## Case Studies

**Case Study 31.1: The WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control - A Model for Global Health Law**

The WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control represents a landmark achievement in international health law, demonstrating that the global community can negotiate and implement binding agreements to address major health threats. By the early 1990s, tobacco use was recognized as a leading cause of preventable death globally, with epidemiological projections warning of dramatic increases in tobacco-attributable mortality, particularly in low- and middle-income countries (86). Previous tobacco control efforts had relied primarily on soft law approaches and voluntary industry agreements that proved ineffective against sophisticated industry strategies. WHO Member States began considering a treaty approach in 1996, leading to negotiations from 2000 to 2003 involving over 190 countries (27).

The FCTC negotiations demonstrated several innovative governance approaches. Civil society organizations participated actively, providing technical expertise, maintaining pressure for ambitious provisions, and counterbalancing industry influence. The negotiations employed a principle of transparency unusual for international treaty processes, with sessions generally open to accredited observers (87). Key negotiating challenges included disagreements over advertising restrictions and taxation provisions.

The FCTC entered into force in February 2005 following ratification by 40 parties. By 2024, 182 parties had ratified, representing over 90% of the world population (28). Evidence indicates substantial implementation progress across multiple provisions, with significant increases in countries adopting smoke-free environments, graphic health warnings, advertising bans, and cessation services (29). However, implementation gaps remain significant, with industry interference, resource constraints, and weak enforcement undermining progress in many jurisdictions (30).

The FCTC experience offers several lessons for global health governance: binding international law can establish meaningful obligations that influence national policy, though implementation requires sustained attention beyond treaty adoption; civil society engagement strengthens both negotiation processes and implementation accountability; and capacity building is essential for effective implementation, particularly in lower-resource settings.

**Case Study 31.2: The Global Health Security Agenda - Building International Cooperation for Pandemic Preparedness**

The Global Health Security Agenda emerged from recognition that despite the International Health Regulations, global capacity to prevent, detect, and respond to infectious disease threats remained inadequate. The 2014 West African Ebola outbreak dramatically illustrated the consequences of capacity gaps and galvanized additional support for the initiative (67). GHSA was launched as a partnership among initially 29 countries, growing to over 70 member countries. Unlike treaty-based frameworks, GHSA operates through voluntary commitments, technical cooperation, and peer accountability (88).

GHSA organizes work around 11 action packages addressing specific technical areas including antimicrobial resistance, zoonotic diseases, biosafety and biosecurity, immunization, national laboratory systems, real-time surveillance, and workforce development. Each action package specifies measurable targets and implementation guidance. The Joint External Evaluation process became closely linked to GHSA objectives, providing standardized capacity assessment (68). GHSA emphasizes One Health approaches recognizing that most emerging infectious diseases originate in animals and that effective prevention requires collaboration across human health, animal health, and environmental sectors (69).

The COVID-19 pandemic provided an unprecedented test of GHSA investments. Countries with stronger health security capacities generally mounted more effective responses, though the relationship was not uniform (89). The pandemic revealed limitations in existing frameworks, including inadequate financing for surge capacity and political factors that overwhelmed technical preparedness. Post-COVID discussions have focused on strengthening financing mechanisms, improving governance, and addressing the political dimensions of pandemic response.

**Case Study 31.3: Thailand's Use of TRIPS Flexibilities - Compulsory Licensing for Access to Medicines**

Thailand's decision to issue compulsory licenses for antiretroviral and cardiovascular medicines represents a significant case in the intersection of trade and health policy. By 2006, Thailand's universal health coverage programme faced challenges in providing affordable access to newer antiretroviral medications and to clopidogrel, a cardiovascular drug (90). Thailand's legal framework permitted compulsory licensing under the Patent Act of 1979, and the government had technical capacity to assess pharmaceutical needs and implement licensing decisions.

In November 2006, Thailand issued a compulsory license for efavirenz, an antiretroviral medication under patent to Merck, allowing government use of generic versions with royalty payments. This was followed in January 2007 by compulsory licenses for lopinavir/ritonavir and clopidogrel (91). The decisions were grounded in domestic law and consistent with TRIPS Agreement provisions as clarified by the Doha Declaration.

Thailand's decisions generated significant international reaction. The pharmaceutical industry strongly criticized the decisions, and the United States placed Thailand on its Special 301 Watch List (92). However, WHO affirmed that the licenses were consistent with international law, and civil society organizations praised Thailand's leadership. Thailand successfully imported generic versions at substantially reduced prices, enabling expanded treatment coverage. Subsequent negotiations with patent holders resulted in price reductions for some medicines, arguably influenced by the demonstrated willingness to use compulsory licensing.

The case demonstrates that TRIPS flexibilities can be utilized but requires substantial technical capacity, political commitment, and willingness to withstand international pressure. Thailand's middle-income status and manufacturing capacity positioned it relatively well; countries with less capacity face greater challenges (73).

**Case Study 31.4: Rwanda's SDG Integration - Health Sector Contributions to National Development**

Rwanda's approach to SDG implementation illustrates how countries can systematically integrate global commitments into national planning while maintaining country-led development strategies. Rwanda adopted the SDGs within its existing long-term development framework, Vision 2050, integrating SDG targets into existing planning and monitoring systems rather than creating parallel structures (93, 94).

Rwanda's Fourth Health Sector Strategic Plan (2018-2024) explicitly aligned with SDG 3 targets while addressing country-specific priorities, including reducing maternal mortality to 150 per 100,000 live births and under-five mortality to 32 per 1,000 live births by 2024. Progress has been substantial: maternal mortality declined from 476 per 100,000 in 2010 to an estimated 210 per 100,000 by 2020, health insurance coverage reached over 85% of the population, and HIV treatment coverage expanded significantly (95).

Rwanda's implementation recognizes that health outcomes depend on action across multiple sectors. The National SDG Steering Committee coordinates cross-sectoral action, and district-level imihigo (performance contracts) include health indicators alongside targets from other sectors. Rwanda's strong statistical system supports SDG monitoring, with regular household surveys providing disaggregated data for tracking progress. Despite progress, challenges remain including maternal mortality above SDG targets, continued out-of-pocket health expenditure burdens, and health workforce density shortfalls.
