3. **Apply** harm reduction principles to design context-appropriate interventions for key populations at elevated risk

## Introduction

Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) and blood-borne pathogens represent a significant global health challenge, with profound implications for individual health, population health, and health system sustainability. The World Health Organization estimates that more than one million STIs are acquired daily worldwide, with the annual incidence of curable STIs (chlamydia, gonorrhea, syphilis, and trichomoniasis) exceeding 374 million cases (1). These infections contribute substantially to morbidity and mortality through direct effects, complications, and their role in facilitating HIV transmission.

The epidemiology of STIs and blood-borne pathogens reflects the complex interplay of biological, behavioral, social, and structural determinants. Biological factors include the efficiency of transmission for different pathogens, the duration of infectiousness, and the presence of co-infections that enhance susceptibility (2). Sexual behavior patterns, partner numbers, condom use, and healthcare-seeking behavior shape individual risk. However, these behaviors occur within broader social contexts characterized by gender norms, power dynamics, stigma, discrimination, and structural inequalities that fundamentally shape vulnerability and access to prevention and care.

The concept of syndemics is particularly relevant to understanding STI epidemiology. Syndemics occur when two or more diseases cluster within a population, interact synergistically, and are driven by social conditions that bring them together (3). HIV, viral hepatitis, and other STIs frequently co-occur in populations experiencing marginalization, including men who have sex with men, people who inject drugs, sex workers, transgender individuals, and people in prisons. These co-infections share transmission routes but also share common social determinants including stigma, criminalization, poverty, and limited healthcare access.

The global response to STIs has evolved considerably over the past four decades. The HIV/AIDS epidemic catalyzed unprecedented mobilization of resources, scientific innovation, and community engagement. Lessons from HIV have informed broader approaches to STI prevention and control, including the recognition that effective responses must address structural determinants and protect human rights (4). The current era is characterized by ambitious elimination targets for HIV, viral hepatitis, and congenital syphilis, alongside growing concern about antimicrobial resistance threatening the treatability of bacterial STIs.

## HIV/AIDS: Global Epidemiology and Response

### Global Epidemiology and Regional Variations

The human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) epidemic remains a defining global health challenge. As of 2022, approximately 39 million people were living with HIV globally, with 1.3 million new infections and 630,000 AIDS-related deaths occurring annually (5). While these figures represent substantial progress from the epidemic's peak, they fall short of targets and highlight persistent gaps in prevention and treatment coverage.

HIV epidemiology varies dramatically across regions and populations. Sub-Saharan Africa remains the most affected region, home to approximately two-thirds of people living with HIV globally. Within this region, Southern and Eastern Africa experience the highest burden, with countries like Eswatini (27.9%), Lesotho (22.8%), and Botswana (20.8%) having the highest adult prevalence rates worldwide (6). These generalized epidemics are driven primarily by heterosexual transmission and are characterized by higher prevalence among women than men, reflecting gender inequalities in HIV risk.

In contrast, concentrated epidemics predominate in most other regions, where HIV is concentrated among key populations while remaining relatively low in the general population. Key populations—men who have sex with men, people who inject drugs, sex workers, transgender people, and people in prisons—account for 70% of new HIV infections globally outside of Sub-Saharan Africa (7). In some settings, including much of Eastern Europe and Central Asia, HIV transmission among people who inject drugs drives the epidemic, with some countries experiencing sustained increases in new infections.

Understanding epidemic typology is essential for designing appropriate prevention responses. Generalized epidemics require population-wide interventions delivered through health systems and communities, while concentrated epidemics demand focused programming for key populations combined with enabling legal and policy environments (8).

### The HIV Prevention Cascade and Combination Prevention

Effective HIV prevention requires a combination approach integrating behavioral, biomedical, and structural interventions tailored to epidemiological context and population needs. The prevention cascade framework provides a useful model for understanding the continuum of prevention services and identifying gaps. This cascade spans from awareness of HIV status and risk through knowledge of prevention options, access to preferred prevention methods, effective use of prevention, and sustained prevention coverage (9).

Behavioral interventions aim to reduce risk through changes in sexual behavior, including reducing partner numbers, increasing condom use, and promoting mutual monogamy. Evidence supports the effectiveness of well-designed behavioral interventions, particularly those that are theory-based, include skills-building components, and address social norms (10). However, behavioral interventions alone have proven insufficient to control the epidemic, motivating the development and scale-up of biomedical prevention technologies.

### Biomedical Interventions: PrEP, PEP, and VMMC

Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP) represents a transformative biomedical prevention technology. Daily oral PrEP with tenofovir-based regimens reduces HIV acquisition risk by approximately 99% when taken consistently (11). Multiple randomized controlled trials demonstrated efficacy across diverse populations, and real-world implementation studies have confirmed effectiveness at scale. The WHO recommends PrEP as a prevention option for all individuals at substantial HIV risk.

Global PrEP uptake has accelerated substantially since 2016, reaching approximately 3.5 million users by 2022 (12). However, coverage remains far below need, with significant disparities across regions and populations. Sub-Saharan Africa has achieved the largest absolute numbers, driven by programs targeting young women and adolescent girls in high-burden settings. High-income countries, particularly the United States and Australia, have achieved high coverage among men who have sex with men. Implementation challenges include awareness, access, cost, adherence, and the need for ongoing HIV testing and clinical monitoring.

Long-acting PrEP formulations address some limitations of daily oral pills. Injectable cabotegravir, administered every two months, demonstrated superior efficacy to daily oral PrEP in clinical trials among men who have sex with men, transgender women, and cisgender women (13,14). The dapivirine vaginal ring, approved by WHO for women at substantial HIV risk, provides an additional woman-controlled prevention option (15). These innovations expand choices and may improve prevention coverage among populations who face barriers to daily oral PrEP.

Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) involves a 28-day course of antiretroviral medications initiated within 72 hours of potential HIV exposure. Originally developed for occupational exposures among healthcare workers, PEP is now recommended for non-occupational exposures including unprotected sex and sexual assault (16). While an important prevention tool, PEP's time-limited window and requirement for healthcare access limit its population-level impact.

Voluntary medical male circumcision (VMMC) reduces female-to-male HIV transmission risk by approximately 60%, as demonstrated in three randomized controlled trials in Kenya, South Africa, and Uganda (17,18,19). The WHO recommends VMMC scale-up in settings with high HIV prevalence and low circumcision coverage, particularly in Eastern and Southern Africa. By 2022, over 34 million men had received VMMC through prioritized programs in 15 countries (20). While VMMC provides durable partial protection for men, it does not directly protect female partners, necessitating combination with other prevention methods.

### Prevention of Mother-to-Child Transmission

Prevention of mother-to-child transmission (PMTCT) has been one of the most successful HIV prevention interventions globally. Without intervention, HIV transmission risk from mother to child ranges from 15-45%, occurring during pregnancy, labor, delivery, or breastfeeding. With optimal PMTCT interventions, this risk can be reduced to below 1% (21).

The evolution of PMTCT approaches illustrates programmatic learning and scientific advancement. Early programs used single-dose nevirapine during labor, providing partial protection. Current WHO guidelines recommend Option B+, which initiates lifelong antiretroviral therapy (ART) for all pregnant and breastfeeding women living with HIV, regardless of CD4 count (22). This approach simplifies programming, improves maternal health, reduces transmission risk, and has been associated with improved long-term maternal and infant outcomes.

Global PMTCT coverage has increased substantially, reaching 82% of pregnant women living with HIV in 2022 (23). Several countries have achieved validation of elimination of mother-to-child transmission, defined as reducing vertical transmission to very low levels through sustained high coverage of interventions. Botswana, Cuba, Thailand, Belarus, and Armenia are among countries achieving this milestone, demonstrating that elimination is achievable with sustained commitment and health system capacity.

### Treatment as Prevention and the 95-95-95 Targets

The recognition that effective antiretroviral therapy prevents HIV transmission has transformed the global response. The landmark HPTN 052 trial demonstrated that early ART initiation reduced transmission to HIV-negative partners by 96% (24). Subsequent studies confirmed that people living with HIV who achieve and maintain viral suppression have effectively zero risk of sexually transmitting HIV—the principle encapsulated in "Undetectable = Untransmittable" (U=U) (25).

This evidence underpins the global 95-95-95 targets: by 2030, 95% of people living with HIV should know their status, 95% of those diagnosed should receive sustained ART, and 95% of those on treatment should achieve viral suppression (26). Achieving these targets would result in 86% of all people living with HIV being virally suppressed, dramatically reducing onward transmission while ensuring optimal individual health outcomes.

Progress toward these targets has been substantial but uneven. Globally, approximately 86% of people living with HIV knew their status, 89% of those were on treatment, and 93% of those on treatment were virally suppressed in 2022 (27). However, these global figures mask significant disparities across regions, populations, and demographic groups. Men, young people, and key populations consistently have lower cascade outcomes than women and older adults.

## Viral Hepatitis B and C

### Hepatitis B: Epidemiology and Vaccination Strategies

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection represents a major global health burden, with an estimated 296 million people living with chronic infection and approximately 820,000 deaths annually, primarily from cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma (28). HBV is highly infectious, transmitting through blood, semen, and vaginal fluids, as well as from mother to child during birth.

The epidemiology of HBV varies substantially across regions. High-endemicity regions (≥8% HBsAg prevalence) include much of sub-Saharan Africa, East Asia, and the Pacific Islands, where perinatal and early childhood transmission have historically predominated (29). In these settings, the risk of developing chronic infection following exposure is strongly age-dependent: 90% of infants infected perinatally develop chronic infection, compared with 25-50% of children infected between ages 1-5 and less than 5% of adults.

Hepatitis B vaccination is safe, highly effective (90-95% protection), and provides long-lasting immunity. The WHO recommends universal infant vaccination, including a birth dose within 24 hours to prevent perinatal transmission (30). Global three-dose coverage reached 84% in 2022, but birth dose coverage lagged at 45%, with significant regional variation (31). In Africa, where perinatal transmission contributes substantially to chronic HBV burden, birth dose coverage remains critically low.

Taiwan's hepatitis B vaccination program, initiated in 1984, provides compelling evidence for the long-term impact of universal vaccination. As the first country to implement universal HBV vaccination, Taiwan achieved dramatic reductions in HBsAg prevalence among children (from 10% to below 1%), hepatocellular carcinoma incidence in young adults, and fulminant hepatitis mortality (32). This success has informed global vaccination strategies and demonstrates that HBV elimination is achievable through sustained immunization programs.

### Hepatitis C: Direct-Acting Antivirals and Elimination

Hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection affects an estimated 58 million people globally, with approximately 290,000 deaths annually from cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma (33). Unlike HBV, HCV transmission occurs primarily through blood exposure, with injection drug use representing the predominant transmission route in most regions. Sexual transmission occurs but is less efficient than for HBV or HIV, though risk is elevated among HIV-positive men who have sex with men.

The development of direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) has revolutionized HCV treatment. These oral regimens achieve cure rates exceeding 95% with 8-12 weeks of treatment, minimal side effects, and few drug interactions (34). This represents a dramatic improvement over previous interferon-based treatments, which had lower efficacy (40-50%), longer duration, and significant adverse effects. Pan-genotypic regimens that are effective across all HCV genotypes have simplified treatment, eliminating the need for genotype testing in many contexts.

Despite the availability of curative treatment, global HCV treatment coverage remains inadequate. Of the estimated 58 million people with chronic HCV, only 9.4 million had received curative treatment by 2019 (35). Barriers include limited diagnosis (only 21% of people with HCV know their status), high treatment costs in some settings, lack of treatment programs for key populations, and weak healthcare infrastructure. Elimination will require substantial scale-up of testing and treatment, particularly among people who inject drugs.

### WHO Elimination Targets and Progress

The WHO has established targets for eliminating viral hepatitis as a public health threat by 2030, defined as 90% reduction in new infections and 65% reduction in mortality compared with 2015 (36). Interim 2020 targets included 30% reduction in new HBV and HCV infections and 10% mortality reduction. Few countries are on track to achieve these goals, though notable progress has occurred.

Egypt has achieved remarkable success in HCV elimination efforts. Following the identification of extremely high HCV prevalence (estimated 10% of the adult population, largely resulting from historical parenteral antischistosomal therapy campaigns), Egypt launched an ambitious national program combining mass screening with subsidized DAA treatment (37). The program screened over 60 million people and treated more than 4 million, achieving substantial prevalence reductions and positioning Egypt to achieve elimination targets.

Georgia has similarly emerged as a leader in HCV elimination, launching a national program in 2015 that offers free screening and treatment. By leveraging harm reduction infrastructure and reaching people who inject drugs, Georgia has treated a substantial proportion of its infected population and demonstrated the feasibility of elimination in middle-income settings (38).

## Bacterial Sexually Transmitted Infections

### Syphilis: Resurgence and Congenital Syphilis Elimination

Syphilis, caused by the spirochete Treponema pallidum, has resurged globally after decades of decline in many settings. The WHO estimates 7.1 million new cases of syphilis annually among adults aged 15-49 years (39). Resurgence has been particularly marked among men who have sex with men in high-income countries, associated with changes in sexual behavior, reduced condom use, and potential interactions with PrEP use.

Congenital syphilis remains a persistent tragedy, causing an estimated 661,000 adverse pregnancy outcomes annually, including stillbirths, neonatal deaths, preterm births, and infected infants (40). This is despite the availability of effective screening tests and highly efficacious, low-cost treatment with benzathine penicillin. The global initiative to eliminate mother-to-child transmission of syphilis (and HIV) has achieved success in several countries but requires strengthened antenatal screening and treatment programs in high-burden settings, particularly in Africa.

### Gonorrhea: The Challenge of Antimicrobial Resistance

Neisseria gonorrhoeae infection represents one of the most pressing antimicrobial resistance challenges in global health. The WHO estimates 82 million new gonorrhea infections annually, with increasing resistance to successive classes of antibiotics threatening treatability (41). Gonorrhea has sequentially developed resistance to sulfonamides, penicillins, tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, and now emerging resistance to extended-spectrum cephalosporins, the last remaining first-line treatment option.

The Global Gonococcal Antimicrobial Surveillance Programme (WHO GASP) monitors resistance patterns across regions. Data demonstrate high-level fluoroquinolone resistance (>50%) in many settings, precluding their use as first-line therapy, and concerning increases in azithromycin resistance and decreased susceptibility to ceftriaxone (42). Extensively drug-resistant strains, resistant to both ceftriaxone and azithromycin, have been reported in multiple countries.

Current WHO guidelines recommend dual therapy with ceftriaxone plus azithromycin where available, though updated single-dose ceftriaxone is also recommended depending on local resistance patterns (43). Preserving treatment options requires antimicrobial stewardship, enhanced surveillance, rapid diagnostics enabling point-of-care resistance testing, and accelerated development of new antibiotics and alternative therapies.

### Chlamydia and Emerging Pathogens

Chlamydia trachomatis infection is the most common bacterial STI globally, with an estimated 129 million new cases annually (44). Often asymptomatic, particularly in women, untreated chlamydia can cause pelvic inflammatory disease, ectopic pregnancy, tubal factor infertility, and chronic pelvic pain. In men, complications include epididymitis and potential impacts on fertility.

The silent nature of chlamydia infection has complicated control efforts, as many infected individuals do not seek care. Screening programs have been implemented in several high-income countries, targeting young women and, in some settings, men who have sex with men. However, evidence for population-level impact of screening remains limited, and some research suggests that treatment may reduce the development of natural immunity, potentially sustaining transmission (45).

Mycoplasma genitalium has emerged as an increasingly recognized cause of urethritis, cervicitis, and pelvic inflammatory disease. Like gonorrhea, M. genitalium has developed extensive antimicrobial resistance, including high-level resistance to macrolides and emerging fluoroquinolone resistance (46). Limited diagnostic availability and evolving understanding of clinical significance have complicated management.

### Syndromic Management and Laboratory Diagnosis

Syndromic management, an approach where STIs are diagnosed and treated based on clinical syndromes rather than laboratory tests, has been the mainstay of STI management in resource-limited settings. The WHO promotes syndromic management algorithms for urethral discharge, vaginal discharge, genital ulcer disease, and lower abdominal pain (47). This approach enables treatment at the first healthcare visit without requiring laboratory infrastructure or return visits for results.

However, syndromic management has significant limitations. Sensitivity and specificity vary by syndrome and setting, with particularly poor performance for vaginal discharge syndrome. Many infections, especially in women, are asymptomatic and go undetected. The approach may lead to overtreatment of some conditions and undertreatment of others. Rising antimicrobial resistance further complicates syndromic approaches, as treatment choices must be made without knowing the causative organism or its susceptibility pattern.

Point-of-care diagnostics offer the potential to combine the advantages of syndromic management (immediate treatment) with the accuracy of etiological diagnosis. Rapid tests for syphilis and HIV are widely available and used. Molecular point-of-care tests for chlamydia, gonorrhea, and trichomonas have been developed and are being evaluated for programmatic use, though cost and operational requirements limit scale-up in many settings (48).

## Comprehensive Prevention Strategies

### Combination Prevention Frameworks

Effective STI and HIV prevention requires combination approaches that integrate behavioral, biomedical, and structural interventions at appropriate scale for the local epidemic context. The combination prevention framework recognizes that no single intervention is sufficient and that optimal impact requires synergies between complementary approaches (49).

Behavioral interventions address knowledge, attitudes, skills, and practices related to sexual behavior and healthcare seeking. Effective components include accurate information provision, skills building for condom negotiation and use, counseling and communication, and social norm change approaches. Behavioral interventions can be delivered through interpersonal channels (counseling, peer education), community mobilization, and mass media campaigns.

Biomedical interventions provide direct biological protection or reduce biological susceptibility. In addition to treatment-based approaches (ART for HIV, DAAs for HCV, antibiotics for bacterial STIs), biomedical prevention includes vaccines (hepatitis B, HPV), barrier methods (male and female condoms), and prophylactic medications (PrEP, PEP).

Structural interventions address the social, economic, legal, and political factors that shape risk and vulnerability. These include policy and legislative reform, economic empowerment programs, reduction of stigma and discrimination, gender equality promotion, and improvements in healthcare access and quality. While often more difficult to implement and evaluate, structural interventions are essential for creating enabling environments that support individual-level behavior change and service uptake.

### Prevention for Key Populations

Key populations—men who have sex with men, people who inject drugs, sex workers, transgender people, and people in prisons—face elevated STI and HIV risk due to biological, behavioral, and structural factors. Effective programming for key populations requires tailored, rights-based approaches that address specific needs while reducing stigma and discrimination.

For men who have sex with men, comprehensive prevention includes condom and lubricant promotion and distribution, regular STI and HIV testing, PrEP for those at substantial risk, and prompt treatment of infections. Anal sex carries higher per-act HIV transmission risk than vaginal sex, and STI prevalence is often high in this population. Structural barriers including criminalization, stigma, and discrimination impede access to services in many settings (50).

For people who inject drugs, prevention focuses on harm reduction approaches including needle and syringe programs, opioid substitution therapy, and access to HIV and viral hepatitis testing and treatment. Integration of services addressing the multiple health needs of this population is essential. Structural interventions addressing drug policy, housing, and poverty are complementary.

Sex workers require access to condoms, lubricants, regular STI screening and treatment, HIV testing and prevention (including PrEP), and peer education and support. Community empowerment approaches, where sex workers are involved in program design and delivery, have demonstrated effectiveness (51). Structural interventions addressing criminalization, violence, and discrimination are essential for creating safe working environments.

### Youth-Friendly Services and Comprehensive Sexuality Education

Adolescents and young people face particular vulnerabilities to STIs, including biological susceptibility (cervical ectopy in young women), behavioral factors (partner concurrency, inconsistent condom use), and structural barriers to services (legal restrictions, provider attitudes). Approximately 30% of new HIV infections occur among young people aged 15-24, and STI rates are typically highest in this age group (52).

Youth-friendly health services are designed to be accessible, acceptable, equitable, appropriate, and effective for young people. Key characteristics include convenient locations and hours, affordable services, confidentiality protection, non-judgmental providers trained in adolescent health, and involvement of young people in service design (53). Integration of STI services with other adolescent health services (contraception, mental health) can improve access and coverage.

Comprehensive sexuality education (CSE) provides young people with age-appropriate, scientifically accurate information about sexuality, reproduction, and relationships. Evidence supports the effectiveness of CSE programs in improving knowledge, delaying sexual debut, reducing partner numbers, and increasing condom use (54). Effective programs are rights-based, gender-transformative, and address power dynamics in relationships. However, implementation remains limited in many settings due to political opposition and misconceptions about program content.

## Harm Reduction Approaches

### Principles and Evidence Base

Harm reduction is a pragmatic public health approach that aims to reduce the negative consequences of drug use without necessarily requiring abstinence. The approach is grounded in respect for the rights and dignity of people who use drugs, recognition that drug use exists on a spectrum of risk, and evidence that punitive approaches have failed to reduce drug use while causing substantial harm (55).

The evidence base for harm reduction is extensive and compelling. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses demonstrate that needle and syringe programs reduce HIV transmission among people who inject drugs by approximately 50% without increasing drug use (56). Opioid substitution therapy similarly reduces HIV risk while also reducing illicit opioid use, criminal activity, and mortality (57). Safe injection facilities prevent overdose deaths and facilitate referral to treatment without increasing drug use or drug-related crime in surrounding areas (58).

### Needle and Syringe Programs

Needle and syringe programs (NSPs) provide sterile injecting equipment to people who inject drugs, reducing sharing of contaminated equipment and thereby reducing HIV and hepatitis C transmission. Programs may operate through fixed-site services, mobile outreach, pharmacy distribution, or vending machines. Beyond equipment distribution, NSPs often provide education, referral to treatment and other services, naloxone distribution for overdose prevention, and a point of contact with marginalized populations.

Global NSP coverage remains inadequate, with substantial geographic variation. While high coverage has been achieved in some Western European countries and Australia, coverage remains critically low in much of Eastern Europe, Central Asia, and sub-Saharan Africa. The WHO recommends distribution of at least 200-300 sterile needles/syringes per person who injects drugs per year (59). Barriers to scale-up include funding constraints, legal and policy restrictions, community opposition, and limited engagement of people who inject drugs in program design.

### Opioid Substitution Therapy

Opioid substitution therapy (OST), primarily methadone and buprenorphine maintenance treatment, involves prescribing long-acting opioid agonists to reduce illicit opioid use and associated harms. OST is among the most effective treatments for opioid use disorder, reducing mortality, illicit drug use, criminal activity, and HIV risk behavior (60). WHO includes methadone and buprenorphine on the Essential Medicines List.

OST access varies enormously across countries. While widely available in Western Europe, North America, and Australia, OST remains unavailable or severely restricted in many countries with substantial populations of people who inject drugs. Legal barriers, limited prescriber training, inadequate funding, and stigma toward both drug use and medication-assisted treatment impede scale-up (61).

### Drug Policy Reform and Public Health Outcomes

Portugal's 2001 decriminalization of personal drug use provides compelling evidence for the public health benefits of drug policy reform. Rather than eliminating penalties for drug use, decriminalization shifted the response from criminal justice to health systems, referring people found with personal-use quantities to Dissuasion Commissions that can mandate treatment or impose administrative sanctions.

In the two decades following decriminalization, Portugal experienced substantial reductions in HIV infections among people who inject drugs, drug-related deaths, and problematic drug use, alongside increases in people accessing treatment (62). While decriminalization was implemented alongside significant investment in treatment and harm reduction services, making attribution difficult, the Portuguese experience demonstrates that removing criminal penalties does not increase drug use and may facilitate engagement with health services.

## Sexual and Reproductive Health Integration

Integration of STI services with broader sexual and reproductive health (SRH) services offers potential benefits including improved access, efficiency, and comprehensiveness of care. Integration can occur at various levels: policy and planning, service delivery, provider training, and monitoring and evaluation (63).

Common integration models include STI screening and treatment within family planning services, HIV and STI services in antenatal care, and youth-friendly services combining contraception, STI testing, and counseling. Integration can reduce stigma associated with seeking STI services specifically, improve efficiency by reaching populations already accessing care, and address multiple health needs simultaneously.

However, integration presents challenges. Provider training requirements increase, service delivery may become more complex, and quality may suffer if integration is implemented without adequate resources (64). Disease-specific vertical programs may have stronger technical capacity and dedicated funding streams. Optimal integration strategies depend on health system structure, epidemic context, and population needs.

## Stigma, Discrimination, and Human Rights

### Mechanisms and Impacts of Stigma

Stigma related to STIs and HIV operates through multiple mechanisms. Enacted stigma involves discrimination and prejudice directed toward affected individuals. Anticipated stigma reflects expectations of discrimination that may lead to avoidance of healthcare or disclosure. Internalized stigma occurs when individuals accept negative societal views, potentially causing shame, depression, and reduced self-efficacy (65).

The impacts of stigma on health outcomes are profound and well-documented. Stigma reduces uptake of HIV testing, delays in treatment initiation, and poorer adherence to ART (66). Fear of disclosure and discrimination deters healthcare-seeking for STI symptoms. Stigmatization of key populations drives these groups away from services and increases risk behaviors practiced in hidden or unsafe contexts.

Stigma reduction requires multi-level interventions addressing individual attitudes, community norms, institutional practices, and structural factors. Effective approaches include contact with affected individuals, information provision to correct misconceptions, skills building to challenge discrimination, and structural interventions addressing discriminatory laws and policies (67).

### Criminalization and Health Outcomes

Criminalization of behaviors associated with STI/HIV transmission or risk—including same-sex sexual behavior, sex work, drug use, and HIV non-disclosure/exposure—creates significant barriers to health and undermines public health responses. Criminalization drives affected populations underground, impedes access to prevention and treatment services, and perpetuates stigma and discrimination (68).

Research demonstrates associations between criminalization and adverse health outcomes. Countries criminalizing same-sex behavior have higher HIV prevalence among men who have sex with men than countries without such laws. Sex workers in settings where sex work is criminalized report higher rates of violence, inconsistent condom use, and STIs than in decriminalized settings. People who inject drugs in highly punitive environments have higher HIV prevalence and less access to harm reduction services (69).

Human rights organizations and public health bodies including UNAIDS and WHO have called for decriminalization of same-sex behavior, sex work, drug use, and HIV non-disclosure as essential for effective HIV and STI responses (70). Progress has been uneven, with some countries reforming laws while others maintain or strengthen criminalization.

### Gender-Based Violence and STI/HIV Risk

Gender-based violence, particularly intimate partner violence and sexual violence, substantially increases women's risk of HIV and STI acquisition. Violence limits women's ability to negotiate safer sex, increases biological susceptibility through genital trauma, and is associated with partners who have higher HIV/STI prevalence (71). Experiences of violence are also associated with reduced HIV testing, disclosure, and treatment adherence.

Addressing gender-based violence requires multi-sectoral approaches integrating health, social services, justice, and community interventions. Health sector responses include screening for violence, first-line support, and referral to specialized services. Structural interventions addressing gender inequality, economic empowerment of women, and engaging men and boys in prevention are essential components (72).


**Case Study 10.1: Thailand's 100% Condom Program**

Thailand experienced rapid HIV epidemic growth during the late 1980s, with HIV prevalence among sex workers in some provinces exceeding 40% by 1989. The epidemic was concentrated among sex workers and their clients, threatening to generalize to the broader population. This crisis catalyzed an unprecedented government response that would become a model for structural HIV prevention.

The 100% Condom Program (100% CCP), initiated in 1991, mandated condom use in all commercial sex establishments. Rather than targeting individual behavior change, the program addressed the structural context in which commercial sex occurred. Provincial health officials worked with police, local government, and entertainment establishment owners to enforce a simple policy: every commercial sex act must involve a condom (73). The program included regular STI screening of sex workers, provision of free condoms, monitoring through STI surveillance and mystery client surveys, and graduated enforcement through warnings and potential closure for non-compliant establishments. Critically, the program avoided targeting sex workers individually for enforcement, instead holding establishment owners accountable for ensuring condom use. Provincial governors provided political leadership, creating multi-sectoral committees including health, police, and local government. Health workers conducted regular visits to establishments, provided condoms and health education, and monitored compliance. Police supported enforcement by following up on reports of non-compliance rather than arresting sex workers (74).

The program achieved remarkable results. Condom use in commercial sex increased from approximately 14% in 1989 to over 90% by 1994. Annual reported STI cases declined from over 200,000 to fewer than 20,000. HIV prevalence among military conscripts (a proxy for young male population) declined from 4% in 1993 to 0.5% by 2010 (75). The success of 100% CCP reflected several factors: high-level political commitment and leadership, multi-sectoral collaboration, structural rather than individual-level intervention, consistent enforcement without criminalization of sex workers, and sustained implementation over decades. The program demonstrated that policy and structural change could achieve population-level behavior change that individual-level interventions alone had failed to accomplish.

Thailand's experience has informed prevention programming globally, particularly the recognition that structural interventions addressing contexts rather than individuals can be highly effective. The 100% CCP model has been adapted in other settings, including Cambodia, which achieved similar success with a comparable approach. However, transferability requires consideration of context-specific factors including epidemic type, commercial sex structure, government capacity, and legal frameworks. The program's success also reflected pre-existing infrastructure, including Thailand's strong health system, established STI surveillance, and government authority over entertainment establishments. Settings with less formal commercial sex structures or weaker government capacity may require adapted approaches.


**Case Study 10.2: Taiwan's Hepatitis B Elimination Through Universal Vaccination**

Taiwan historically had one of the world's highest hepatitis B prevalence rates, with approximately 15-20% of the adult population chronically infected in the early 1980s. This burden resulted primarily from perinatal and early childhood transmission, given the extremely high chronicity risk (90%) when infection occurs during infancy. Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) was consequently a leading cause of death, claiming approximately 10,000 lives annually (76).

In 1984, Taiwan became the first country globally to implement universal hepatitis B vaccination for newborns. The program was designed based on understanding of HBV natural history and transmission dynamics, particularly the critical importance of preventing perinatal infection. The vaccination program included universal screening of pregnant women for HBsAg, hepatitis B immune globulin (HBIG) administration to newborns of HBsAg-positive mothers within 24 hours of birth, and three-dose hepatitis B vaccine series for all newborns regardless of maternal status. The program achieved high coverage rapidly, reaching over 90% within the first years of implementation (77). Implementation required substantial health system investments including expansion of vaccination infrastructure, training of healthcare workers, establishment of cold chain for vaccine storage, and development of surveillance systems to monitor coverage and impact. Political commitment and sustained funding enabled scale-up and maintenance over decades.

The program's impact has been dramatic and well-documented through long-term follow-up. HBsAg prevalence among children declined from approximately 10% pre-vaccination to below 1% among those born after program initiation. Hepatocellular carcinoma incidence among children aged 6-14 declined from 0.70 per 100,000 in 1981-1986 to 0.13 per 100,000 in 1990-1994, a greater than 80% reduction (78). Longer-term follow-up has demonstrated persistence of protection and continued mortality reductions. Studies of cohorts born after universal vaccination show sustained low chronic infection rates and emerging reductions in HCC among young adults. The economic benefits of prevention substantially exceed program costs when accounting for avoided treatment, lost productivity, and premature mortality (79).

Taiwan's experience demonstrates that hepatitis B elimination is achievable through sustained universal infant vaccination with timely birth dose. Key success factors included strong political commitment, adequate health system capacity, high coverage achievement and maintenance, and integration of maternal screening with newborn vaccination. The Taiwan model has informed global hepatitis B vaccination recommendations and implementation. WHO currently recommends universal infant vaccination with birth dose within 24 hours, based substantially on evidence from Taiwan and subsequent program experience elsewhere. Global birth dose coverage has increased but remains inadequate in many high-burden settings, particularly in Africa, where operational challenges including home births and cold chain requirements impede implementation.


**Case Study 10.3: Portugal's Drug Policy Reform**

By the late 1990s, Portugal faced one of Europe's most severe drug problems. Approximately 1% of the population was estimated to use heroin, HIV prevalence among people who inject drugs exceeded 50% in some areas, and drug-related deaths were increasing. Previous decades of prohibitionist policy had failed to stem drug use while generating substantial harm through criminalization and incarceration.

In 2001, Portugal implemented a radical policy shift: decriminalization of the acquisition, possession, and use of all drugs for personal consumption. Possession of up to a 10-day supply of any drug was reclassified from a criminal offense to an administrative violation. People found with drugs are referred to Dissuasion Commissions composed of legal, social work, and health professionals who can impose administrative sanctions or refer to treatment but cannot impose criminal penalties (80). Critically, decriminalization was accompanied by substantial investment in treatment and harm reduction services. Public spending on drug-related programs increased significantly, funding expanded methadone maintenance, therapeutic communities, outreach programs, and needle exchange services. The policy explicitly reframed drug use as a public health issue rather than a criminal matter.

Two decades of experience have demonstrated substantial positive outcomes. HIV infections among people who inject drugs declined dramatically, from approximately 1,000 annually before 2001 to fewer than 20 in recent years. Drug-related deaths decreased by more than 80%. The proportion of drug offenders in prison dropped from 44% to 24% (81). Contrary to predictions that decriminalization would increase drug use, overall drug use rates did not rise substantially and remain below European averages. Use of the Dissuasion Commission system has created opportunities for therapeutic intervention that would not exist under criminalization. Treatment uptake has increased significantly.

Portugal's success has generated international interest and influenced drug policy debates globally. However, transferability requires nuanced consideration of context-specific factors. Portugal's reform occurred alongside significant investment in treatment and harm reduction--decriminalization alone without service expansion would likely yield different outcomes. Cultural, political, and structural factors also shape implementation feasibility. Portugal's small size, unified legal system, and political consensus for reform facilitated implementation. Larger, more federalized systems face different implementation challenges. The Portuguese model demonstrates the feasibility and benefits of public health approaches to drug use but requires adaptation rather than simple replication.


## Future Directions and Emerging Challenges

### HIV Vaccine and Cure Research

Despite decades of effort, an effective HIV vaccine remains elusive. The virus's extraordinary genetic diversity, rapid mutation, and ability to establish latent reservoirs complicate vaccine development. The RV144 trial in Thailand demonstrated modest efficacy (31% reduction in infection) with a prime-boost regimen, providing proof of concept that vaccine-induced protection is possible (82). Subsequent trials of similar approaches have had mixed results, and current research explores novel strategies including broadly neutralizing antibodies and mRNA platforms.

HIV cure research has intensified following the cases of individuals achieving sustained viral remission after stem cell transplantation for hematologic malignancies. While transplantation is not scalable as a cure strategy, these cases demonstrate that sustained remission is biologically possible and have informed research into therapeutic approaches targeting the latent reservoir (83).

### Antimicrobial Resistance Threats

The emergence and spread of antimicrobial resistance in N. gonorrhoeae represents an urgent threat to STI control. Without new antibiotics or alternative approaches, untreatable gonorrhea could emerge within years. Research priorities include development of new antibiotics, therapeutic vaccines, antimicrobial peptides, and host-directed therapies (84). Enhanced surveillance, antimicrobial stewardship, and point-of-care resistance testing are essential for preserving existing treatment options.

### Long-Acting Prevention Technologies

Long-acting formulations promise to improve prevention coverage by reducing adherence burden. Cabotegravir long-acting injectable PrEP, lenacapavir (ultra-long-acting with six-month dosing), and the dapivirine vaginal ring expand the prevention toolkit with options suited to different preferences and circumstances (85). Implementation will require adaptation of service delivery models and sustainable financing mechanisms.

### Health Systems Strengthening for Sustainability

Sustainable responses to STIs and blood-borne pathogens require strong health systems capable of delivering quality services at scale. This includes adequately trained and motivated health workforces, reliable supply chains for commodities, functional health information systems, and integration of vertical disease programs with broader health services (86). Transition from donor funding to domestic resource mobilization is particularly critical in middle-income countries facing graduation from global health initiatives.


## Conclusion

Sexually transmitted infections and blood-borne pathogens remain significant global health challenges requiring comprehensive, evidence-based responses. HIV, viral hepatitis, and bacterial STIs together cause millions of new infections and hundreds of thousands of deaths annually, with disproportionate impact on key populations and individuals experiencing marginalization.

Effective responses require combination prevention approaches integrating behavioral, biomedical, and structural interventions. Biomedical advances including PrEP, treatment as prevention, direct-acting antivirals for hepatitis C, and hepatitis B vaccination have transformed the prevention and treatment landscape. However, these tools must be implemented within enabling environments that address stigma, discrimination, and the structural determinants of vulnerability.

Human rights-based approaches are essential for effective and ethical responses. Criminalization of key populations undermines public health goals by driving affected individuals away from services. Stigma reduction, decriminalization of same-sex behavior, sex work, and drug use, and protection of the right to health are both ethical imperatives and evidence-based public health strategies.

The ambitious targets for ending AIDS, eliminating viral hepatitis, and controlling bacterial STIs by 2030 remain achievable but will require accelerated action, sustained political commitment, adequate resources, and continued attention to reaching those furthest from services. Progress will depend on implementation of proven interventions at scale, research and development of new tools, and structural changes that create enabling environments for health.
