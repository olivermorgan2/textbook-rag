# Chapter 2: Health Determinants and Health Equity

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

1. **Define** key concepts including social determinants of health, health equity, and structural determinants
2. **Explain** the WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health conceptual framework and its components
3. **Apply** determinants of health frameworks to analyze health disparities in different global contexts
4. **Analyze** complex causal pathways between social, environmental, and economic determinants and health outcomes
5. **Evaluate** evidence for interventions addressing upstream social determinants of health
6. **Design** comprehensive strategies to address health inequities using multi-sectoral approaches

## Introduction

Health outcomes vary dramatically both within and between countries, reflecting complex interactions between social, economic, environmental, and political factors that extend far beyond individual behaviors and healthcare access. A child born in Japan can expect to live 84 years on average, while a child born in Sierra Leone has a life expectancy of just 54 years—a 30-year gap that cannot be explained by genetic differences alone (1). Even within countries, profound disparities exist: in the United States, life expectancy varies by more than 20 years between different neighborhoods, with these differences strongly correlated with socioeconomic status and race (2).

These health inequities—systematic differences in health that are avoidable, unfair, and unjust—represent one of the greatest challenges in global public health (3). Understanding why health outcomes vary requires examining the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age, collectively known as the social determinants of health (4). This chapter explores the complex web of factors that determine population health outcomes and examines frameworks for understanding and addressing health inequities globally.

The COVID-19 pandemic starkly illustrated how existing health inequities become amplified during crises, with marginalized populations experiencing disproportionately higher rates of infection, severe disease, and death (5). These disparities reflect longstanding structural inequalities that create differential exposure to risk, access to resources, and resilience to health threats. Addressing these fundamental drivers of health inequity is essential for achieving sustainable improvements in global health.

## Conceptual Frameworks for Understanding Health Determinants

### WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health Framework

The WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health (CSDH), established in 2005, developed a comprehensive conceptual framework that has become foundational to understanding health inequities (6). The CSDH framework distinguishes between structural determinants and intermediary determinants, both embedded within broader social, economic, and political contexts.

**Structural determinants** represent the deeper social, economic, and political mechanisms that generate stratification and social class divisions in society. These include governance arrangements, macroeconomic policies, social policies, and cultural and societal norms that together create differential access to power, prestige, and resources (7). Structural determinants ultimately determine people's socioeconomic position, which then influences their exposure to health-compromising conditions.

**Intermediary determinants** encompass the material circumstances, behavioral and biological factors, psychosocial circumstances, and health system factors that more directly influence health outcomes (8). These factors mediate the relationship between structural determinants and health, but are themselves shaped by the broader structural context.

The CSDH framework emphasizes that health inequities arise from the unequal distribution of social determinants, and that addressing inequities requires action on both structural and intermediary levels. The Commission's final report, "Closing the Gap in a Generation," called for three overarching principles: improve daily living conditions, tackle the inequitable distribution of power, money and resources, and measure and understand the problem (9).

### Life Course Approach

The life course approach recognizes that health and disease patterns reflect accumulative effects of exposures and experiences from conception through old age (10). This perspective emphasizes critical periods when exposures have particular significance, chains of risk where one adverse experience leads to another, and the accumulation of advantage or disadvantage over time.

Early life experiences, including maternal health, birth weight, nutrition, and early childhood development, establish foundations that influence health throughout life (11). Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs), including abuse, neglect, and household dysfunction, have been linked to increased risks of chronic diseases, mental health problems, and premature mortality in adulthood (12).

### Intersectionality Framework

Intersectionality, originally developed by legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw, provides a framework for understanding how multiple social identities and systems of oppression interact to shape individual and group experiences (13). In health contexts, intersectionality helps explain how factors such as race, gender, class, sexuality, disability status, and immigration status combine in complex ways to influence health outcomes.

For example, research in the United States has demonstrated that African American women experience higher rates of maternal mortality than white women across all education and income levels, suggesting that the intersection of race and gender creates unique health disadvantages that cannot be explained by socioeconomic status alone (14).

## Social Determinants of Health

Social determinants of health encompass the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age, shaped by the distribution of money, power, and resources at global, national, and local levels (15). These determinants operate at multiple levels, from individual and household factors to community and societal influences.

### Education

Education serves as a fundamental social determinant of health, influencing health outcomes through multiple pathways. Higher education levels are consistently associated with better health outcomes across diverse global contexts (16). Education affects health through its impact on employment opportunities, income, social networks, health knowledge and behaviors, and psychological resources such as sense of control and self-efficacy (17).

In low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), maternal education is particularly strongly associated with child health outcomes. Each additional year of maternal schooling is associated with reduced child mortality rates, with estimates suggesting that achieving universal primary education for women could prevent more than 1.8 million child deaths annually (18). Education also influences women's autonomy, family planning decisions, and healthcare-seeking behaviors.

### Income and Wealth

Income influences health through multiple mechanisms, including access to material resources necessary for health (nutritious food, safe housing, healthcare), reduced stress and increased sense of control, and access to social and cultural resources (19). The relationship between income and health follows a gradient pattern, where health improves with each step up the income ladder, rather than simply reflecting differences between poor and non-poor populations (20).

Wealth inequality, separate from absolute poverty, also influences population health. Countries with higher levels of income inequality tend to have worse population health outcomes, even after controlling for average income levels (21). Proposed mechanisms include reduced social cohesion, increased stress, underinvestment in public goods, and differential access to social and economic opportunities (22).

### Employment and Working Conditions

Employment provides income, social connections, purpose, and identity, while unemployment is associated with increased risks of mortality, mental health problems, and reduced wellbeing (23). However, the quality of employment matters significantly. Precarious employment characterized by job insecurity, low wages, lack of benefits, and poor working conditions can negatively impact health (24).

Occupational health hazards vary dramatically across industries and countries. In many LMICs, workers face elevated risks from inadequate safety protections, exposure to harmful substances, and dangerous working conditions. The International Labour Organization estimates that 2.78 million people die annually from work-related injuries and diseases (25).

### Social Support and Social Cohesion

Social relationships and community connections serve as important determinants of health through psychosocial pathways and practical support mechanisms. Strong social support networks are associated with reduced mortality risk, better mental health outcomes, and improved recovery from illness (26). Conversely, social isolation and loneliness are associated with health risks comparable to smoking or obesity (27).

Social cohesion at the community level—characterized by trust, reciprocity, and collective efficacy—influences health outcomes through its effects on social capital, collective action for health-promoting resources, and social norms around health behaviors (28). Communities with higher social cohesion tend to have better health outcomes and reduced health inequities.

## Structural Determinants

Structural determinants operate at the macro level to shape social stratification and create differential access to resources and opportunities. These foundational factors establish the context within which more proximal determinants operate.

### Governance and Political Systems

Governance arrangements, including political systems, rule of law, transparency, and accountability mechanisms, fundamentally shape population health by influencing resource allocation, policy development, and social protection systems (29). Democratic governance structures that enable citizen participation and hold leaders accountable tend to be associated with better health outcomes and reduced health inequities (30).

Corruption undermines health systems by diverting resources, reducing quality of services, and creating barriers to access for marginalized populations. Studies across multiple countries demonstrate negative associations between corruption levels and health outcomes, with effects particularly pronounced for vulnerable populations (31).

### Macroeconomic Policies

Economic policies, including trade agreements, taxation systems, and labor market regulations, shape the distribution of resources and opportunities within societies. Structural adjustment programs implemented in many LMICs during the 1980s and 1990s often included reductions in public spending on health and education, with documented negative effects on population health outcomes (32).

Trade liberalization can have complex effects on health determinants. While economic growth may improve material living standards, trade policies can also increase income inequality, disrupt traditional livelihoods, and expose populations to health risks through increased marketing of harmful products such as tobacco and processed foods (33).

### Social Policies

Social protection systems, including universal healthcare, education, unemployment insurance, and pension systems, serve as crucial structural determinants by reducing vulnerabilities and providing security across the life course (34). Countries with more comprehensive social protection systems tend to have better health outcomes and smaller health inequities.

Universal basic income programs, implemented in various forms across different contexts, show promising evidence for improving health and wellbeing by reducing poverty and providing economic security (35). Pilot programs in Kenya, Finland, and Stockton, California have demonstrated positive effects on mental health, child development, and healthcare utilization (36).

### Discrimination and Social Exclusion

Systematic discrimination based on race, ethnicity, gender, religion, sexuality, disability, or other characteristics represents a fundamental structural determinant that creates and maintains health inequities. Discrimination operates through multiple mechanisms, including differential access to resources and opportunities, chronic stress exposure, and internalized oppression (37).

Structural racism, defined as the macro-level systems, social forces, institutions, ideologies, and processes that interact to generate and reinforce inequities among racial groups, has been increasingly recognized as a fundamental cause of racial health disparities (38). Evidence from the United States, Brazil, South Africa, and other contexts demonstrates how historical and contemporary discriminatory policies in housing, education, employment, and healthcare create lasting health disadvantages for marginalized racial and ethnic groups (39).

## Intermediary Determinants

Intermediary determinants represent the more proximal factors through which structural determinants ultimately influence health outcomes. These factors can be grouped into several categories that interact in complex ways.

### Material Circumstances

Material living conditions, including housing quality, neighborhood environment, access to safe water and sanitation, and food security, directly influence health through exposure to physical and chemical hazards, infectious agents, and stress (40).

Housing conditions affect health through multiple pathways. Poor housing quality, including dampness, overcrowding, inadequate heating, and structural problems, is associated with increased risks of respiratory diseases, injuries, mental health problems, and infectious diseases (41). In many LMICs, rapid urbanization has led to the growth of informal settlements where residents lack access to basic services and live in substandard housing conditions (42).

Water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) represent fundamental material determinants of health. Globally, 2 billion people lack access to safely managed drinking water at home, and 3.6 billion lack access to safely managed sanitation (43). Inadequate WASH infrastructure contributes to diarrheal diseases, malnutrition, and other health problems, with effects particularly pronounced for children and marginalized populations.

### Behavioral Factors

Health-related behaviors, including tobacco use, alcohol consumption, dietary patterns, and physical activity, significantly influence health outcomes. However, these behaviors are strongly shaped by social and environmental contexts rather than simply reflecting individual choices (44).

Tobacco use exemplifies how structural factors influence behavioral determinants. Global tobacco marketing strategies deliberately target low-income populations and youth, while tobacco control policies vary dramatically across countries. As tobacco use has declined in high-income countries, tobacco companies have increasingly focused on LMICs, contributing to growing global inequities in tobacco-related diseases (45).

### Psychosocial Factors

Psychosocial factors, including stress, social support, control, and self-efficacy, influence health through both direct physiological pathways and indirect behavioral mechanisms (46). Chronic stress exposure activates biological stress response systems that can contribute to cardiovascular disease, immune dysfunction, and mental health problems (47).

The concept of allostatic load describes how repeated stress exposure leads to cumulative wear and tear on physiological systems (48). Marginalized populations often experience chronic stress from multiple sources, including discrimination, economic insecurity, and neighborhood disorder, contributing to accelerated aging and increased disease risk.

### Health System Factors

Healthcare systems serve as important intermediary determinants by providing preventive, curative, and rehabilitative services. However, health systems can also contribute to health inequities when access, quality, or responsiveness varies across population groups (49).

Universal Health Coverage (UHC) represents a key strategy for addressing health system-related inequities by ensuring that all people have access to needed health services without financial hardship. However, achieving UHC requires attention to both financial barriers and other access barriers, including geographic, cultural, and organizational factors (50).

## Environmental Determinants

Environmental factors represent a crucial category of health determinants that intersect with social and economic factors to create complex patterns of exposure and vulnerability.

### Physical Environment

The physical environment encompasses air and water quality, soil contamination, noise pollution, and radiation exposure. Environmental health risks are unequally distributed, with marginalized populations often experiencing disproportionate exposure to environmental hazards (51).

Air pollution represents a major global environmental health risk, contributing to approximately 7 million premature deaths annually (52). Exposure to air pollution follows clear social gradients, with low-income communities, racial minorities, and other marginalized groups often experiencing higher exposure levels due to residential proximity to pollution sources and reduced political power to prevent environmental hazards (53).

### Climate Change

Climate change represents an emerging and increasingly important environmental determinant of health that intersects with existing social inequities to create differential vulnerabilities (54). Climate change affects health through multiple pathways, including extreme weather events, changes in infectious disease patterns, food and water insecurity, and forced migration (55).

The health impacts of climate change are unevenly distributed, with marginalized populations facing the greatest risks despite contributing least to greenhouse gas emissions. Small island developing states, Arctic communities, and populations in arid regions face particular vulnerabilities, while within countries, low-income communities and racial minorities often experience disproportionate climate health impacts (56).

### Built Environment

The built environment, including urban design, transportation systems, and access to green spaces, influences health through its effects on physical activity, air quality, social interaction, and mental health (57). Neighborhoods designed to encourage walking and cycling are associated with higher levels of physical activity and lower rates of obesity and cardiovascular disease (58).

Access to green spaces provides multiple health benefits, including opportunities for physical activity, stress reduction, improved air quality, and social interaction. However, access to parks and green spaces is often unequally distributed, with low-income communities and communities of color having less access to quality green spaces (59).

## Economic Determinants

Economic factors operate at multiple levels, from global trade systems to household income, to shape health outcomes and health inequities.

### Global Economic Systems

Global economic systems, including international trade rules, financial markets, and development policies, create the context within which national and local economies operate (60). Debt burdens in many LMICs limit government capacity to invest in health and social protection systems, while global tax avoidance by multinational corporations reduces domestic resource mobilization (61).

The financialization of healthcare—including the growing role of private equity and financial markets in healthcare delivery—raises concerns about prioritizing profit over health outcomes and exacerbating health inequities (62). Studies in the United States have documented how private equity ownership of healthcare facilities is associated with reduced quality and increased costs (63).

### Labor Markets

Labor market structures, including employment protection, minimum wage policies, and collective bargaining rights, influence health through their effects on income security, working conditions, and access to benefits (64). Countries with stronger labor protections and higher minimum wages tend to have better population health outcomes and smaller health inequities (65).

The growth of platform economy and gig work has created new forms of employment that often lack traditional protections and benefits, potentially creating new health vulnerabilities (66). Gig workers often lack access to health insurance, paid sick leave, and other benefits that support health and economic security.

### Poverty and Inequality

Poverty represents both an outcome and a determinant of poor health, creating reinforcing cycles where poor health reduces economic opportunities while economic disadvantage increases health risks (67). Multidimensional poverty measures that capture deprivation across health, education, and living standards provide more comprehensive understanding of poverty's relationship with health than income-based measures alone (68).

Income inequality, distinct from absolute poverty, influences health through multiple mechanisms. The relative income hypothesis suggests that individuals' position in the income distribution affects health through psychosocial stress, while the absolute income hypothesis emphasizes the health effects of material deprivation (69). Both mechanisms likely operate simultaneously, with their relative importance varying across contexts.

## Intersectionality and Multiple Forms of Disadvantage

Intersectionality provides a framework for understanding how multiple social identities and systems of oppression interact to create unique experiences of advantage and disadvantage (70). In health contexts, intersectionality helps explain patterns of health outcomes that cannot be understood by examining single characteristics in isolation.

### Race, Gender, and Class Intersections

Research on intersectionality in health has demonstrated how race, gender, and class interact in complex ways to shape health experiences. For example, studies in the United States have shown that Black women experience higher rates of cardiovascular disease than can be explained by the additive effects of being Black and being female, suggesting synergistic effects of racism and sexism (71).

The weathering hypothesis, developed by Arline Geronimus, proposes that chronic exposure to social and economic stressors leads to accelerated physiological aging among Black women in the United States, contributing to earlier onset of age-related diseases and health disparities across the life course (72).

### Sexual and Gender Minorities

Sexual and gender minorities face unique health challenges related to discrimination, stigma, and exclusion from healthcare systems (73). Transgender individuals experience particularly high levels of healthcare discrimination and barriers to accessing appropriate care, contributing to significant health disparities (74).

In many countries, criminalization of homosexuality and gender non-conformity creates additional barriers to health access and contributes to mental health problems among sexual and gender minorities (75). Even in countries with legal protections, social stigma and discrimination continue to affect health outcomes.

### Disability and Health Equity

People with disabilities face systematic barriers to healthcare access, including physical barriers, communication barriers, and provider bias, contributing to significant health disparities (76). The social model of disability emphasizes how environmental and social barriers, rather than individual impairments, create disability and health disadvantage (77).

Research demonstrates that people with disabilities experience higher rates of preventable conditions, suggesting that health disparities result from systemic barriers rather than inherent aspects of disability (78). Universal design principles and disability rights approaches can help address these inequities by creating inclusive healthcare systems and environments.

## Health Equity Measurement and Monitoring

Measuring and monitoring health equity requires systematic approaches to identify, quantify, and track health disparities across population groups and over time (79). Effective equity monitoring systems are essential for informing policy responses and tracking progress toward health equity goals.

### Equity Stratifiers

Equity stratifiers are the dimensions along which health inequities are assessed. The WHO recommends monitoring health inequities across five dimensions: economic status, education, place of residence, race/ethnicity, and sex (80). Additional stratifiers may be relevant in specific contexts, including disability status, sexual orientation, immigration status, and occupation.

Selection of appropriate stratifiers requires consideration of local contexts and data availability. In many settings, data collection on sensitive characteristics such as race, ethnicity, or sexual orientation may face political or ethical challenges, requiring careful consideration of benefits and risks (81).

### Measurement Approaches

Multiple approaches exist for measuring health inequities, each with distinct advantages and limitations. Simple measures compare health outcomes between specific groups, such as comparing infant mortality rates between urban and rural populations. Summary measures attempt to capture the overall level of inequality across an entire population (82).

The slope index of inequality (SII) measures the absolute difference in health outcomes between the most and least advantaged groups, while the relative index of inequality (RII) measures the relative difference. Concentration indices measure the degree to which health outcomes are concentrated among advantaged or disadvantaged groups (83).

### Global Monitoring Systems

Several global initiatives have developed systems for monitoring health equity. The WHO Health Equity Monitor provides data on health inequities within countries, while the Global Health Observatory includes equity-stratified indicators for key health outcomes (84).

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) include explicit commitments to reducing inequities, with indicators disaggregated by relevant stratifiers. SDG 10 specifically calls for reducing inequality within and among countries, while other health-related goals include equity considerations (85).

**Case Study 2.1: United Kingdom's Health Inequalities Strategy**

The United Kingdom has implemented one of the most comprehensive national strategies to address health inequalities among high-income countries, providing valuable lessons for addressing health equity at the national level (86).

Health inequalities in the UK are substantial and persistent. Life expectancy varies by more than 10 years between the most and least deprived areas, while healthy life expectancy shows even larger gaps of approximately 19 years (87). These inequalities follow clear social gradients, with health outcomes progressively worse at each step down the social ladder.

The UK's approach to health inequalities has evolved over several decades, with particular momentum following the 1998 Acheson Report and the 2010 Marmot Review, "Fair Society, Healthy Lives" (88). These reports emphasized the need for action on social determinants and proportionate universalism—policies that are universal but with scale and intensity proportionate to need.

The UK's health inequalities strategy has included multiple components addressing both upstream determinants and healthcare access. Key elements include:

**Sure Start** children's centers provided integrated early childhood services in disadvantaged areas, including healthcare, education, and family support. Evaluation studies demonstrated positive effects on child development and family wellbeing, particularly for the most disadvantaged families (89).

**Minimum wage and tax credit policies** addressed income inequalities through regulatory and redistribution mechanisms. The introduction of the national minimum wage in 1999 and subsequent increases, combined with tax credits for working families, helped reduce poverty and income inequality (90).

**Smoke-free legislation** implemented in 2007 reduced exposure to secondhand smoke and contributed to declining smoking rates, with benefits observed across all social groups but particularly pronounced effects in some disadvantaged communities (91).

**Health inequalities targets** set specific goals for reducing gaps in life expectancy and infant mortality between areas. While progress was mixed, the targets helped maintain political attention on health equity issues (92).

Despite comprehensive policies, implementation faced several challenges. Political priorities shifted with changes in government, affecting sustained commitment to health equity goals. Economic austerity measures following the 2008 financial crisis led to reduced spending on social programs, potentially undermining previous progress (93).

Local implementation varied significantly across areas, reflecting differences in capacity, leadership, and political commitment. Effective local implementation required strong partnerships across sectors, sustained leadership, and adequate resources (94).

Evaluation of the UK's health inequalities strategy shows mixed results. Some specific interventions demonstrated clear benefits, while overall health inequalities remained persistent. Life expectancy gaps between areas showed little change, though progress might have been worse without intervention (95).

Key lessons from the UK experience include the importance of political commitment across government, the need for sustained long-term investment, and the value of addressing multiple determinants simultaneously. The UK experience also highlights the challenge of addressing structural determinants that operate at larger scales than national policy can easily influence (96).

**Case Study 2.2: Brazil's Bolsa Familia Program**

Brazil's Bolsa Familia conditional cash transfer program represents one of the world's largest social protection programs, providing valuable insights into addressing health equity through comprehensive social policy (97).

Launched in 2003, Bolsa Familia provided monthly cash transfers to poor families conditional on children's school attendance and family participation in health services. At its peak, the program reached approximately 14 million families, representing about 25% of Brazil's population (98).

The program targeted families with monthly income below specified thresholds, with benefit amounts varying based on family composition and income level. Conditionalities required children to maintain 85% school attendance and families to participate in preventive health services, including vaccinations and prenatal care (99).

Implementation relied on coordinated systems across multiple government levels, including targeting and registration systems, payment mechanisms, and monitoring of conditionalities. The program required substantial institutional capacity building and coordination across sectors (100).

Rigorous evaluations demonstrated significant health benefits from Bolsa Família. Child mortality declined by approximately 17% in municipalities with higher program coverage, with effects particularly pronounced for deaths from poverty-related causes such as malnutrition and diarrhea (101).

Nutritional outcomes improved substantially, with reductions in child malnutrition and stunting. The program also contributed to increased healthcare utilization, including prenatal care and vaccinations, helping improve population health coverage (102).

Food security improved among participating families, with reduced prevalence of household food insecurity and improved dietary diversity. These effects resulted from both increased purchasing power and nutrition education components of the program (103).

Bolsa Familia addressed multiple social determinants simultaneously. Income effects reduced absolute poverty and provided economic security for vulnerable families. Educational conditionalities contributed to increased school enrollment and completion rates (104).

Health conditionalities strengthened linkages between families and primary healthcare systems, improving preventive care access. The program also had broader social effects, including women's empowerment through targeting transfers to female household heads (105).

Implementation challenges included reaching remote rural populations, ensuring adequate health and education service capacity, and managing program exit strategies. Geographic barriers and limited service availability in some areas constrained program effectiveness (106).

Political sustainability proved challenging, with significant program modifications following changes in government. The program was replaced in 2021 with a new cash transfer program with different design features, highlighting the importance of political consensus for sustained social protection (107).

Bolsa Familia's experience provides several lessons for addressing health equity through social protection. Conditional cash transfers can effectively address multiple determinants when combined with adequate service provision. Program design must consider local contexts and implementation capacity (108).

The importance of political commitment and institutional capacity for sustained implementation is highlighted by Brazil's experience. Rigorous evaluation and evidence generation helped build support for the program and inform design improvements (109).

**Case Study 2.3: Urban-Rural Health Disparities in Sub-Saharan Africa**

Urban-rural health disparities represent a persistent challenge across Sub-Saharan Africa, reflecting complex interactions between geographic, economic, and social factors (110).

Health outcomes generally favor urban populations across most Sub-Saharan African countries, though patterns are complex and vary by health indicator and country context. Child mortality rates are typically higher in rural areas, with differences ranging from modest to substantial across countries (111).

Access to healthcare services shows marked urban-rural differences. Rural populations often face greater distances to health facilities, reduced availability of skilled health workers, and limited access to emergency and specialist care. These access barriers contribute to lower utilization of preventive and curative services (112).

Maternal health indicators demonstrate particularly large urban-rural gaps. Skilled birth attendance rates are consistently higher in urban areas, contributing to higher maternal mortality in rural settings. Access to emergency obstetric care is often severely limited in rural areas (113).

Infrastructure differences represent fundamental drivers of urban-rural health disparities. Rural areas often lack reliable electricity, transportation networks, and communication systems that support health service delivery. These infrastructure gaps affect both service availability and quality (114).

Human resources for health are unevenly distributed, with most health workers concentrated in urban areas. Rural postings are often viewed as less desirable due to limited professional opportunities, inadequate housing and amenities, and social isolation. This maldistribution contributes to reduced service availability and quality in rural areas (115).

Economic factors also contribute to urban-rural disparities. Rural populations often have lower incomes and greater reliance on agriculture, which can be subject to climate and market volatility. Out-of-pocket healthcare costs represent a greater financial burden for rural families (116).

Several strategies have been implemented to address urban-rural health disparities across the region. Task-shifting approaches train community health workers and mid-level providers to deliver services traditionally provided by physicians, helping address human resource constraints (117).

Mobile health services use vehicles or temporary clinics to reach remote populations with preventive and curative services. These approaches can help overcome geographic barriers, though they require careful planning and coordination to ensure effectiveness (118).

Digital health technologies, including telemedicine and mobile health applications, offer potential to reduce geographic barriers to care. However, implementation requires addressing infrastructure and connectivity challenges in rural areas (119).

Addressing urban-rural health disparities requires comprehensive approaches that go beyond health sector interventions. Investment in rural infrastructure, including transportation, electricity, and communications, supports both economic development and health system strengthening (120).

Incentive systems for health workers, including financial incentives, career development opportunities, and improved working conditions, can help address geographic maldistribution. Some countries have implemented mandatory rural service requirements for newly graduated health workers (121).

Universal Health Coverage initiatives must pay particular attention to rural populations to avoid exacerbating existing disparities. Benefit packages, payment systems, and delivery mechanisms should be designed to ensure equitable access across geographic areas (122).

**Case Study 2.4: Indigenous Health Determinants in High-Income Countries**

Indigenous populations in high-income countries, including Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and the United States, experience substantial health disparities that reflect historical and ongoing colonization processes (123).

Indigenous populations in these countries experience significantly worse health outcomes across multiple indicators. Life expectancy gaps range from 8-10 years in New Zealand and Canada to approximately 17 years in Australia. These disparities have shown little improvement in recent decades despite overall health improvements (124).

Mental health disparities are particularly pronounced, with Indigenous populations experiencing higher rates of suicide, depression, and substance use disorders. These patterns reflect complex interactions between historical trauma, ongoing discrimination, and cultural disruption (125).

Chronic disease rates, including diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and kidney disease, are substantially higher among Indigenous populations. These diseases often occur at younger ages and with greater severity, contributing to premature mortality (126).

Contemporary Indigenous health disparities must be understood within the context of colonization, which disrupted traditional societies, economies, and cultures while imposing discriminatory policies and practices. Forced removal from traditional lands, residential school systems, and other assimilationist policies created lasting trauma and disadvantage (127).

Ongoing structural racism continues to affect Indigenous populations through discriminatory treatment in healthcare, education, employment, and criminal justice systems. Studies document bias and discrimination in healthcare settings that contributes to reduced access and quality of care (128).

Land rights and self-determination represent crucial determinants of Indigenous health. Connection to traditional lands provides not only economic opportunities but also cultural and spiritual resources that support wellbeing. Legal recognition of land rights has been associated with improved health outcomes in some contexts (129).

Indigenous concepts of health often differ from Western biomedical models, emphasizing holistic wellbeing that encompasses physical, mental, spiritual, and cultural dimensions. Traditional knowledge systems, languages, and cultural practices serve as protective factors for health (130).

Cultural continuity—the preservation and transmission of traditional knowledge, practices, and beliefs—has been identified as a key protective factor against suicide and other mental health problems among Indigenous youth. Communities with stronger cultural continuity show better mental health outcomes (131).

Access to traditional foods and medicines represents another important cultural determinant. Environmental degradation and restricted access to traditional territories can disrupt food systems and medicinal practices that have supported Indigenous health for generations (132).

Community-controlled health services have demonstrated effectiveness in improving Indigenous health outcomes. These services combine cultural safety with clinical care, providing culturally appropriate services while building community capacity and self-determination (133).

Cultural safety approaches in mainstream health services focus on addressing power imbalances and discrimination rather than simply cultural competency. Cultural safety requires healthcare providers to examine their own cultural biases and work to create services that are safe and effective for Indigenous patients (134).

Traditional healing integration programs combine traditional Indigenous healing practices with Western medicine, recognizing the value of both systems for promoting health. These approaches require careful collaboration between traditional healers and healthcare providers (135).

Addressing Indigenous health disparities requires recognition of Indigenous rights to self-determination and control over health services. Top-down approaches that do not involve Indigenous communities in leadership roles are unlikely to be effective (136).

Addressing historical trauma and ongoing discrimination requires systemic changes beyond the health sector. Truth and reconciliation processes, land rights recognition, and anti-racism initiatives are necessary for addressing underlying determinants (137).

Long-term commitment and sustained investment are essential for addressing Indigenous health disparities. Short-term program funding and frequent policy changes undermine community capacity building and trust development (138).

## Interventions and Strategies for Addressing Health Determinants

Addressing health determinants requires comprehensive strategies that operate at multiple levels, from individual and community interventions to policy and structural changes. Effective interventions often combine approaches targeting different determinants simultaneously.

### Policy Interventions

**Health in All Policies (HiAP)** represents a systematic approach to integrating health considerations into policy decisions across sectors (139). HiAP recognizes that many policy decisions outside the health sector have significant health implications and seeks to maximize positive health impacts while minimizing negative effects.

Implementation of HiAP requires institutional mechanisms for intersectoral collaboration, tools for health impact assessment, and political commitment across government sectors. Finland's North Karelia Project pioneered comprehensive community-based prevention that integrated health promotion into multiple sectors, achieving substantial reductions in cardiovascular disease mortality (140).

**Universal social protection systems** provide security across the life course and reduce vulnerabilities that contribute to poor health. Countries with comprehensive social protection systems, including universal healthcare, education, unemployment insurance, and pensions, tend to have better health outcomes and smaller health inequities (141).

### Community-Based Interventions

Community health worker programs have demonstrated effectiveness in addressing multiple health determinants, particularly in low-resource settings. These programs train community members to provide health education, basic clinical services, and linkages to formal health systems (142).

Ethiopia's Health Extension Program deploys female community health workers to provide preventive and basic curative services, with documented improvements in child health outcomes and healthcare access. The program's success reflects comprehensive training, regular supervision, and integration with the formal health system (143).

**Community-led total sanitation** approaches engage entire communities in eliminating open defecation and improving hygiene practices. These programs address environmental determinants while building community capacity and social cohesion (144).

### Economic Interventions

Conditional and unconditional cash transfer programs have demonstrated effectiveness in improving health outcomes by addressing income poverty and enabling access to health-promoting resources. Evidence from multiple countries shows that cash transfers can improve nutrition, healthcare utilization, and health outcomes (145).

**Microfinance and livelihood interventions** address economic determinants by providing access to credit and supporting income-generating activities. However, evidence for health impacts is mixed, with some studies showing benefits while others demonstrate limited or negative effects (146).

**Universal basic income** pilots in various settings have shown promising results for health and wellbeing, including reduced stress, improved mental health, and increased healthcare utilization. However, long-term effects and scalability remain under investigation (147).

### Environmental Interventions

**Air pollution control measures** can achieve significant health benefits, particularly for vulnerable populations. Mexico City's comprehensive air pollution control program, including vehicle emission standards, fuel quality improvements, and industrial controls, resulted in substantial improvements in air quality and child health outcomes (148).

**Urban planning interventions** that promote active transportation, increase green space access, and improve housing quality can address multiple environmental determinants simultaneously. Complete streets designs that accommodate pedestrians, cyclists, and public transit promote physical activity while reducing air pollution (149).

## Measuring Progress and Monitoring Health Equity

Effective strategies for addressing health determinants require robust systems for measuring progress and monitoring health equity. These systems must capture both health outcomes and the determinants that influence them.

### Indicator Development

Comprehensive health equity monitoring requires indicators that capture multiple dimensions of health and its determinants. The WHO Health Equity Monitor includes indicators covering reproductive, maternal, newborn and child health stratified by economic status, education, residence, and other equity dimensions (150).

The SDGs include explicit commitments to reducing inequalities, with health-related targets disaggregated by relevant stratifiers. Monitoring SDG progress requires sustained investment in data collection systems and analytical capacity (151).

### Data Collection Systems

Regular household surveys, such as Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS) and Multiple Indicator Cluster Surveys (MICS), provide comparable data on health outcomes and determinants across countries and over time. These surveys enable monitoring of health inequities and tracking progress toward equity goals (152).

Civil registration and vital statistics (CRVS) systems provide essential data for monitoring mortality patterns and trends. However, many LMICs have incomplete CRVS systems that may underrepresent marginalized populations, potentially obscuring health inequities (153).

### Participatory Monitoring

Community-based monitoring approaches engage affected communities in data collection and analysis, ensuring that monitoring systems capture community perspectives and priorities. Participatory approaches can identify issues that formal monitoring systems might miss (154).

Digital technologies offer new opportunities for community-based monitoring, including mobile data collection, real-time reporting, and participatory mapping. However, digital approaches must address issues of access, privacy, and data quality (155).

## Future Directions and Emerging Challenges

Several emerging challenges and opportunities will shape future efforts to address health determinants and health equity.

### Climate Change and Health Equity

Climate change represents an emerging health equity challenge that intersects with existing social determinants to create new patterns of vulnerability (156). Climate impacts affect health through multiple pathways, including extreme weather events, changes in infectious disease patterns, food and water security, and forced migration.

Adaptation strategies must address differential vulnerabilities and ensure that climate responses do not exacerbate existing inequities. Climate-resilient health systems require investment in infrastructure, emergency preparedness, and community capacity building (157).

### Digital Health and Health Equity

Digital health technologies offer opportunities to address some health determinants while potentially creating new forms of inequality. Telemedicine can improve access to care for rural and marginalized populations, while digital health records can improve care coordination (158).

However, digital divides based on access, affordability, and digital literacy may exacerbate existing health inequities. Ensuring equitable benefits from digital health requires attention to access barriers and user needs across diverse populations (159).

### Migration and Health Equity

Global migration patterns create new challenges for addressing health determinants and health equity. Migrants often face unique vulnerabilities related to documentation status, language barriers, discrimination, and limited access to social protection systems (160).

Health systems must adapt to serve increasingly diverse populations while addressing the specific vulnerabilities that migration can create. Portable social protection and health insurance systems can help maintain coverage across borders (161).

### Planetary Health

The planetary health framework recognizes the interconnections between human health and environmental sustainability, emphasizing that long-term health improvement requires environmental stewardship (162). This perspective highlights how environmental degradation particularly affects vulnerable populations and exacerbates health inequities.

Sustainable development approaches must integrate health equity considerations into environmental and economic policies. Green economic transitions can provide opportunities to address health determinants while promoting environmental sustainability (163).

## Conclusion

Health determinants operate through complex, interconnected pathways that span from individual and household factors to global economic and political systems. Understanding these relationships is essential for developing effective strategies to improve population health and reduce health inequities.

The evidence demonstrates that addressing health determinants requires action at multiple levels, from strengthening health systems to implementing comprehensive social policies. Successful interventions often combine approaches that address structural determinants with those targeting more proximal factors.

Health equity represents both a moral imperative and a practical necessity for sustainable development. Reducing health inequities requires sustained political commitment, adequate resources, and coordinated action across sectors. The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted how health inequities undermine collective health security and economic stability.

Future efforts to address health determinants must adapt to emerging challenges including climate change, technological change, and evolving patterns of global mobility. Success will require innovative approaches that combine traditional public health strategies with new technologies and partnerships.

The social determinants framework provides a roadmap for action, but implementation requires tailoring strategies to local contexts while addressing global forces that shape health inequities. Achieving health equity within a generation, as envisioned by the WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health, remains an ambitious but achievable goal with sustained commitment and comprehensive action.
