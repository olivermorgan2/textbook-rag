## Introduction

Armed conflicts, natural disasters, and complex emergencies represent some of the most severe threats to population health in the contemporary world. The intersection of conflict, displacement, and health creates a complex web of challenges that require specialized approaches distinct from traditional public health practice (1). **Humanitarian emergencies** affect millions of people globally, with over 100 million people currently displaced by conflict and persecution according to UNHCR estimates (2).

The concept of **complex emergencies** encompasses situations where multiple factors—including conflict, population displacement, food insecurity, and breakdown of health systems—interact to create severe threats to survival and wellbeing (3). These emergencies are characterized by their multifaceted nature, the breakdown of governance structures, and the need for coordinated international response.

This chapter adopts a comprehensive framework that recognizes the **humanitarian-development nexus**, understanding that effective responses must bridge emergency relief with longer-term development goals. The framework emphasizes protection principles, rights-based approaches, and the building of resilient health systems that can withstand and recover from crises.

This chapter examines the complex relationships between conflict, displacement, and health outcomes across the humanitarian continuum. We explore direct and indirect health impacts of emergencies, analyze health needs of displaced populations, and review evidence-based approaches to humanitarian health response. Special attention is given to mental health and psychosocial support, nutrition in emergencies, epidemic prevention, and health system recovery.

## Health Impacts of Armed Conflict and Complex Emergencies
### Direct Health Effects of Violence

Armed conflict produces immediate and devastating health impacts through direct violence. **War trauma** includes both physical injuries from weapons and psychological trauma from exposure to violence. Studies from contemporary conflicts demonstrate that civilian casualties comprise an increasing proportion of conflict-related deaths, with estimates suggesting civilians account for over 90% of casualties in modern wars (4).

Direct health effects include gunshot wounds, blast injuries, burns, and traumatic amputations. The pattern of injuries varies by conflict type and weapon systems used. Urban warfare produces different injury patterns compared to rural conflicts, with higher rates of multi-trauma cases requiring complex surgical interventions (5). Women and children face particular risks, including sexual violence, which has been documented as a weapon of war in multiple conflicts globally.

### Indirect Health Consequences

The indirect health consequences of conflict often exceed direct violence in their impact on population health. **Health system collapse** occurs through targeted attacks on healthcare facilities, displacement of health workers, disruption of supply chains, and breakdown of governance structures (6). WHO documentation shows that attacks on healthcare have occurred in over 20 countries experiencing conflict, with Syria experiencing over 600 documented attacks on health facilities between 2011 and 2021 (7).

Food insecurity and malnutrition increase dramatically during conflicts. Agricultural production declines due to displacement of farmers, destruction of crops and livestock, and disruption of food distribution systems. Market failures lead to increased food prices, making nutritious foods inaccessible to vulnerable populations. Studies from Yemen demonstrate how conflict-induced famine affects child nutrition, with acute malnutrition rates exceeding emergency thresholds in multiple governorates (8).

Water and sanitation systems suffer severe damage during conflicts, leading to increased risks of waterborne diseases. The destruction of water infrastructure, combined with overcrowding in displacement sites, creates conditions conducive to cholera, typhoid, and other enteric diseases. Analysis of the Syrian conflict shows how deliberate targeting of water infrastructure contributed to multiple disease outbreaks (9).

### Long-term Health Impacts

The health consequences of conflict extend well beyond the immediate emergency phase. **Excess mortality** continues in post-conflict periods due to weakened health systems, economic disruption, and social fragmentation. Longitudinal studies from various post-conflict settings demonstrate elevated mortality rates persisting for years after active conflict ends (10).

Chronic diseases receive inadequate attention during conflicts, leading to increased mortality among people with diabetes, hypertension, cancer, and other conditions requiring ongoing care. Mental health impacts persist across generations, with evidence of intergenerational transmission of trauma in post-conflict populations.

### Differential Population Impacts

**Vulnerable populations** experience disproportionate health impacts during conflicts. Children under five face the highest mortality rates, particularly from preventable diseases and malnutrition. Pregnant women experience increased maternal mortality due to lack of skilled birth attendance and emergency obstetric care. Elderly populations suffer from neglect of chronic conditions and social isolation.

## Forced Displacement and Population Health
### Refugee Health Needs and Vulnerabilities

**Refugee health** encompasses the unique health challenges faced by people who have crossed international borders fleeing conflict or persecution. Refugees experience multiple health vulnerabilities throughout their displacement journey, from pre-flight conditions in origin countries through transit risks to challenges in destination countries (11).

The health status of refugee populations is influenced by conditions in origin countries before displacement, experiences during flight including trauma exposure, and living conditions in displacement settings. Refugees often arrive in destination countries with untreated health conditions, malnutrition, and psychosocial distress. Language barriers, cultural differences, and legal status issues further complicate health service access.

Communicable diseases pose significant risks in refugee populations due to overcrowding, poor sanitation, and limited healthcare access. Vaccination coverage gaps increase risks of vaccine-preventable diseases, while respiratory infections spread rapidly in crowded conditions. Tuberculosis rates are consistently elevated in refugee populations globally, requiring specialized screening and treatment programs (12).

### Internally Displaced Persons

**Internally displaced persons (IDPs)** face distinct health challenges from international refugees, often experiencing greater vulnerability due to ongoing conflict exposure and limited international protection. IDPs remain within their country's borders but may face government hostility or neglect, limiting access to basic services including healthcare (13).

IDP populations experience higher mortality rates compared to non-displaced populations in the same countries. A systematic review of mortality in displacement settings found crude mortality rates 2-4 times higher than baseline populations, with children under five experiencing the greatest excess mortality (14).

**IDP vulnerabilities** include continued exposure to conflict, frequent movement between locations, limited legal protections, and competing priorities for limited government resources. Unlike refugees, IDPs may not qualify for specialized international assistance, creating gaps in humanitarian response.

### Urban Displacement Challenges

**Urban displacement** represents a growing phenomenon with distinct health implications. Unlike camp-based displacement, urban refugees and IDPs often live among host communities in informal settlements or urban slums. This dispersed settlement pattern creates challenges for health service delivery and humanitarian assistance (15).

Urban displaced populations face unique health risks including air pollution, overcrowding, poor housing conditions, and limited access to clean water and sanitation. Competition for employment and social services with host communities can create tensions affecting access to healthcare. Urban health systems may be overwhelmed by sudden population increases without proportional resource increases.

### Protracted Displacement Situations

**Protracted crises** involve displacement lasting more than five years, affecting millions globally. These situations present evolving health needs as acute emergency health risks give way to chronic disease burdens and long-term health system challenges. The average duration of displacement has increased globally, with many refugee situations lasting decades (16).

Long-term displacement creates specific health challenges including mental health deterioration, development of chronic diseases, and loss of traditional coping mechanisms. Children born in displacement may experience developmental delays and educational gaps affecting lifelong health outcomes. Host communities may experience strain on health systems and social services during prolonged displacement situations.

## Humanitarian Health Response Systems
### Emergency Preparedness Frameworks

**Emergency preparedness** forms the foundation of effective humanitarian health response, requiring systematic planning before crises occur. The WHO Health Emergency and Disaster Risk Management Framework provides a comprehensive approach to emergency preparedness, emphasizing the importance of multi-hazard planning and health system strengthening (17).

Preparedness frameworks emphasize risk assessment, early warning systems, and emergency response capacity development. Countries must assess their vulnerability to different types of emergencies and develop context-specific preparedness plans. This includes pre-positioning emergency supplies, training health workers in emergency response, and establishing coordination mechanisms.

Community-based preparedness involves training community members in basic emergency response skills and establishing local early warning networks. Evidence from multiple countries demonstrates that community preparedness significantly improves emergency response effectiveness and reduces mortality during crises (18).

### Rapid Response Mechanisms

**Rapid response** capabilities determine the effectiveness of humanitarian health interventions during the critical initial weeks of emergencies. The concept of the "golden hours" in emergency response emphasizes that rapid intervention can significantly reduce excess mortality (19).

International rapid response mechanisms include WHO's Health Cluster approach, which coordinates health sector response in humanitarian emergencies. The cluster system provides leadership, coordination, and standard-setting for health response, ensuring comprehensive coverage and avoiding duplication of efforts.

Emergency Medical Teams (EMTs) provide specialized clinical care during the acute phase of emergencies. WHO classification and verification of EMTs ensures quality standards and interoperability between international teams. Type 1 EMTs provide basic emergency care, while Type 2 and 3 teams offer advanced surgical and intensive care capabilities (20).

### Humanitarian Coordination Systems

**Humanitarian coordination** mechanisms ensure coherent and effective response across multiple actors and sectors. The Inter-Agency Standing Committee (IASC) provides the framework for humanitarian coordination, bringing together UN agencies, NGOs, and government partners.

The **cluster approach** organizes humanitarian response around specific technical sectors including health, nutrition, water and sanitation, and protection. Each cluster has clear leadership responsibilities and technical standards. The Health Cluster specifically coordinates health sector response, ensuring gaps are identified and addressed systematically.

Coordination challenges include competing organizational priorities, funding constraints, and coordination fatigue among field workers. Effective coordination requires clear leadership, established communication channels, and shared technical standards. Regular coordination meetings and information sharing systems are essential components (21).

### Resource Mobilization and Funding

Humanitarian funding mechanisms significantly influence the scope and quality of health response. The humanitarian funding gap continues to grow, with only 65% of requested funding received globally in 2022 according to UNOCHA Financial Tracking Service data.

Pooled funding mechanisms like the Central Emergency Response Fund (CERF) and Country-Based Pooled Funds provide flexible financing for rapid response. These mechanisms allow for immediate funding disbursement without lengthy proposal processes, critical during the acute phase of emergencies.

## Health Service Delivery in Humanitarian Emergencies
### Sphere Standards and Humanitarian Charter

The **Sphere Standards** represent the foundational framework for humanitarian response, establishing minimum standards for humanitarian action based on the humanitarian charter and principles of humanity, impartiality, neutrality, and independence (22). The health standards within Sphere provide evidence-based guidance for health service delivery in emergencies.

Sphere health standards cover six key areas: health system coordination, health service delivery, essential health services, health workforce, essential medicines and medical devices, and health information. Each standard includes key actions, key indicators, and guidance notes providing practical implementation advice.

The humanitarian charter establishes the moral and legal foundations for humanitarian action, asserting the right of affected populations to assistance and protection. The charter emphasizes accountability to affected populations and the principle of "do no harm" in humanitarian programming.

### Minimum Initial Service Package

The **Minimum Initial Service Package (MISP) for Sexual and Reproductive Health** provides specific guidance for reproductive health services during the acute phase of humanitarian emergencies. The MISP includes priority activities to be implemented immediately when a crisis occurs, before comprehensive reproductive health programs can be established (23).

MISP priority activities include: ensuring the health cluster/sector identifies an organization to lead implementation; preventing sexual violence and responding to survivors; preventing excess maternal and newborn mortality and morbidity; preventing the transmission of HIV and treating survivors of sexual violence; and planning for comprehensive reproductive health services.

Implementation of the MISP requires pre-positioning of reproductive health kits, training of health workers, and establishment of referral systems for emergency obstetric care. Studies from multiple emergency settings demonstrate that MISP implementation significantly reduces maternal mortality and morbidity (24).

### Essential Health Services Delivery

**Essential health services** in emergencies prioritize interventions addressing the leading causes of preventable mortality and morbidity. Priority interventions include management of acute respiratory infections, diarrheal diseases, malaria, measles, and acute malnutrition.

Service delivery models in emergencies must adapt to disrupted health systems and displaced populations. Mobile health teams provide services to remote or insecure areas where fixed facilities cannot operate. Community health worker programs extend service reach and provide culturally appropriate care.

Health service quality in emergencies faces multiple challenges including supply constraints, workforce limitations, and security restrictions. Quality improvement approaches in emergency settings emphasize standardized protocols, supervision systems, and continuous monitoring and evaluation (25).

### Health Facility Protection

**Health facility protection** has become increasingly critical as attacks on healthcare have escalated globally. The "Health Care in Danger" initiative led by the International Committee of the Red Cross works to ensure safe access to healthcare in armed conflict and other emergencies.

Protection measures include clearly marking health facilities with protected symbols, ensuring facilities maintain neutrality and impartiality, and advocating with all parties to conflict regarding obligations under international humanitarian law. Health facility protection requires both physical security measures and diplomatic engagement.

## Mental Health and Psychosocial Support in Emergencies
### Trauma and Mental Health in Conflict

**Trauma-informed care** recognizes the pervasive impact of traumatic experiences on individuals and communities affected by conflict and displacement. Understanding trauma's effects on mental health, behavior, and physical health is essential for effective humanitarian health programming (26).

Conflict-related trauma includes exposure to violence, forced displacement, family separation, sexual violence, and loss of livelihoods and social networks. The prevalence of mental health conditions increases dramatically in conflict-affected populations, with studies showing PTSD rates of 15-30% and depression rates of 20-40% in refugee populations (27).

Complex trauma results from repeated exposure to multiple traumatic events, common in protracted conflict and displacement situations. Complex trauma affects individual functioning, family dynamics, and community social structures. Children are particularly vulnerable to complex trauma, with potential lifelong impacts on development and wellbeing.

### MHPSS Intervention Framework

The Inter-Agency Standing Committee **Mental Health and Psychosocial Support (MHPSS)** guidelines provide a comprehensive framework for mental health programming in humanitarian settings. The MHPSS framework adopts a multi-layered approach recognizing that people have different levels of mental health needs requiring different types of support (28).

The MHPSS intervention pyramid includes four levels: basic services and security (bottom layer), community and family supports, focused non-specialized supports, and specialized services (top layer). Most people affected by emergencies need support at the bottom two layers, while smaller numbers require specialized clinical services.

Basic services include ensuring physical safety, adequate nutrition, healthcare, education, and livelihood opportunities. These foundational needs must be met before higher-level psychological interventions can be effective. Community-level interventions strengthen social connections, cultural practices, and community leadership capacity.

### Community-Based Approaches

**Community resilience** approaches recognize that communities possess inherent strengths and coping mechanisms that can be supported and strengthened during crises. Community-based MHPSS programming builds on local resources rather than imposing external models of mental health care (29).

Community interventions include support groups, cultural and religious activities, community mobilization, and traditional healing practices. These interventions are typically provided by trained community members rather than mental health professionals, making them more accessible and culturally appropriate.

Peer support programs train individuals with lived experience of displacement or trauma to provide support to others facing similar challenges. Peer support has shown effectiveness in reducing isolation, improving coping skills, and facilitating access to services in refugee and IDP populations.

### Cultural Considerations and Local Capacity

**Cultural adaptation** of mental health interventions is essential for effectiveness and acceptability in diverse humanitarian contexts. Western models of mental health may not align with local understandings of psychological distress and healing. Effective MHPSS programming requires understanding local concepts of mental health, help-seeking behaviors, and traditional healing practices (30).

Local capacity building involves training local health workers, traditional healers, religious leaders, and community members in basic psychological first aid and supportive counseling skills. This approach builds sustainable mental health capacity while respecting cultural values and practices.

Integration with traditional healing systems requires careful negotiation to identify complementary approaches while maintaining quality standards. Some traditional practices may be harmful or stigmatizing, requiring sensitization and alternative approaches.

## Nutrition in Humanitarian Emergencies
### Acute Malnutrition in Emergencies

**Acute malnutrition** represents one of the most severe and immediate threats to survival in humanitarian emergencies. Global acute malnutrition (GAM) rates above 15% or severe acute malnutrition (SAM) rates above 2% indicate emergency conditions requiring immediate intervention according to WHO standards (31).

Acute malnutrition results from inadequate food intake, poor diet quality, and increased morbidity from infectious diseases. The interaction between malnutrition and infection creates a vicious cycle where malnutrition increases susceptibility to infection, while infections worsen nutritional status. This cycle is particularly devastating for children under five years of age.

Emergency nutrition assessments use anthropometric measurements including weight-for-height Z-scores, mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC), and presence of bilateral pitting edema to identify acute malnutrition. MUAC screening is particularly useful in emergency settings due to its simplicity and reliability for identifying children requiring immediate treatment.

### Feeding Programs and Management

**Therapeutic feeding** programs provide specialized treatment for children with severe acute malnutrition (SAM). Community-based management of acute malnutrition (CMAM) has revolutionized SAM treatment by providing outpatient care using ready-to-use therapeutic foods (RUTF) for children without medical complications (32).

CMAM programs include three components: community outreach for case finding and referral, outpatient therapeutic programs (OTP) for uncomplicated SAM cases, and inpatient stabilization centers for SAM cases with medical complications. This approach has significantly improved treatment coverage and outcomes while reducing costs compared to facility-based programs.

Supplementary feeding programs target moderate acute malnutrition (MAM) and at-risk populations including pregnant and lactating women. Targeted supplementary feeding provides specialized nutritious foods to individuals identified as malnourished, while blanket supplementary feeding provides additional food to all members of vulnerable groups regardless of nutritional status.

### Micronutrient Supplementation

**Micronutrient deficiency** is common in emergency-affected populations due to limited food diversity and poor diet quality. Key micronutrient deficiencies include vitamin A, iron, zinc, folate, and vitamin B12. These deficiencies increase mortality risk and impair immune function, growth, and cognitive development (33).

Emergency micronutrient interventions include high-dose vitamin A supplementation for children 6-59 months, iron and folate supplementation for pregnant women, and multi-micronutrient powders for young children. Mass supplementation programs may be implemented for entire populations in settings where deficiencies are widespread.

Fortified foods provide an effective delivery mechanism for micronutrients in emergency settings. Fortified blended foods used in feeding programs provide multiple micronutrients in standardized amounts. Ready-to-use supplementary foods (RUSF) combine macronutrients with essential micronutrients for both treatment and prevention of malnutrition.

### Infant and Young Child Feeding

**Infant and Young Child Feeding in Emergencies (IYCF-E)** requires special consideration due to the unique vulnerability of infants and young children. Breastfeeding provides critical nutrition and immune protection during emergencies, but may be compromised by maternal stress, malnutrition, and separation (34).

IYCF-E interventions include protection and support of breastfeeding through counseling, establishment of mother-friendly spaces, and addressing barriers to breastfeeding. For non-breastfed infants, safe preparation of breast milk substitutes requires access to clean water, fuel, and appropriate feeding equipment.

Complementary feeding for children 6-24 months requires special attention to food safety, nutrient density, and feeding practices. Specialized complementary foods may be needed when local foods are inadequate or unavailable. IYCF-E programming requires coordination between nutrition, health, water and sanitation, and protection sectors (35).

## Epidemic Prevention and Response in Humanitarian Settings
### Disease Outbreak Risks in Emergencies

**Epidemic risk** increases dramatically during humanitarian emergencies due to multiple factors including population displacement, overcrowding, poor sanitation, disrupted health services, and weakened surveillance systems. Displaced populations often have low vaccination coverage and limited immunity to endemic diseases in their destination areas (36).

High-risk diseases in emergency settings include measles, cholera, meningitis, hepatitis A and E, typhoid, and respiratory tract infections. Vector-borne diseases including malaria, dengue, and leishmaniasis may emerge due to changed environmental conditions and vector breeding sites. COVID-19 has highlighted the particular vulnerability of humanitarian settings to respiratory disease outbreaks.

Risk factors for epidemics include overcrowding with more than 20 square meters per person in settlements, poor access to safe water with less than 15 liters per person per day, inadequate sanitation facilities, and poor hygiene practices. These conditions are common in displacement camps and informal settlements hosting displaced populations.

### Surveillance and Early Warning Systems

**Surveillance systems** in emergency settings must be rapidly established to detect disease outbreaks before they become widespread. Early warning surveillance focuses on priority diseases with epidemic potential and uses simplified case definitions for rapid identification and reporting.

Syndromic surveillance monitors symptom patterns rather than laboratory-confirmed diagnoses, allowing for rapid detection and response. Common syndromes monitored include acute watery diarrhea, bloody diarrhea, acute jaundice syndrome, and acute respiratory illness. Community-based surveillance trains community members to identify and report suspected cases.

Event-based surveillance captures reports of unusual health events that may not be detected through routine surveillance systems. This includes reports of deaths, illness clusters, or rumors of disease outbreaks from community members, health workers, or other sources. Social media monitoring has become increasingly important for early outbreak detection (37).

### Vaccination Campaigns in Emergencies

**Emergency vaccination** campaigns target priority diseases based on epidemic risk assessment and population immunity levels. Measles vaccination is the highest priority due to high case fatality rates in malnourished populations and rapid spread in crowded conditions. WHO recommends measles vaccination for all children 6 months to 15 years in emergency settings.

Mass vaccination campaigns require careful planning including vaccine procurement, cold chain management, target population estimation, and social mobilization. Campaigns typically use fixed posts in health facilities, outreach to settlements and camps, and mobile teams for hard-to-reach populations.

Routine immunization services must be maintained or rapidly re-established during emergencies to prevent accumulation of susceptible individuals. Integration of emergency and routine vaccination services optimizes resource utilization and ensures comprehensive coverage (38).

### Outbreak Investigation and Response

**Outbreak response** requires rapid deployment of investigation teams, implementation of control measures, and coordination with relevant authorities. Outbreak investigation follows standard epidemiological methods adapted for emergency settings with simplified case definitions and rapid diagnostic methods.

Control measures vary by disease but typically include case isolation, contact tracing, ring vaccination, improved sanitation, health education, and vector control. Implementation requires coordination between health, water and sanitation, shelter, and protection sectors.

Response monitoring tracks epidemic curves, attack rates, case fatality rates, and coverage of control measures. Real-time monitoring allows for adaptation of response strategies and resource reallocation as the outbreak evolves (39).

## Health System Recovery and Reconstruction
### Post-Conflict Health System Assessment

**Health system resilience** refers to the capacity of health systems to prepare for, respond to, and recover from shocks and stresses while maintaining essential functions. Post-conflict health system assessment provides the foundation for reconstruction planning by identifying remaining capacities, priority needs, and opportunities for building back better (40).

Comprehensive health system assessments examine six WHO health system building blocks: service delivery, health workforce, health information systems, essential medical products and technologies, financing, and leadership and governance. Assessment methodologies must be adapted to post-conflict contexts with limited data availability and security constraints.

Rapid assessment tools focus on essential indicators including health facility functionality, health worker availability, essential medicine availability, and basic service delivery capacity. These assessments inform immediate humanitarian programming while comprehensive assessments support longer-term reconstruction planning.

### Rebuilding Health Infrastructure

**Post-conflict reconstruction** provides opportunities to strengthen health systems beyond pre-conflict levels through improved design, technology, and governance structures. Health infrastructure reconstruction includes physical facilities, equipment, information systems, and organizational structures.

Reconstruction principles include community participation in planning, integration with development planning processes, adoption of resilient design standards, and incorporation of lessons learned from the conflict period. Decentralized health systems may be more resilient to future shocks but require stronger governance and management capacity (41).

Public-private partnerships can accelerate reconstruction while building sustainable financing mechanisms. Private sector engagement requires clear regulatory frameworks, quality standards, and mechanisms to ensure access for poor and vulnerable populations.

### Health Workforce Development

**Capacity building** for health workers is critical for sustainable health system recovery. Conflict often results in significant health worker losses through death, displacement, and emigration. Rebuilding health workforce capacity requires both immediate training for available workers and longer-term education and retention strategies.

Emergency training programs focus on essential clinical skills, public health competencies, and trauma-informed care. Standardized curricula and competency-based assessment ensure quality while accelerating training timelines. Task-sharing approaches maximize available human resources by training lower-level cadres for specific functions.

Long-term workforce development includes re-establishing medical and nursing education institutions, developing specialized training programs, and creating career advancement opportunities. Rural retention strategies address geographic maldistribution of health workers common in post-conflict settings (42).

### Transition to Development Programming

The **development transition** involves shifting from humanitarian relief to longer-term development programming while maintaining essential health services. This transition requires careful planning to avoid service disruptions and ensure continuity of care for vulnerable populations.

Transition planning addresses sustainability of funding mechanisms, handover of service delivery responsibilities, and integration with national health systems. Early recovery programming bridges humanitarian and development phases by establishing systems and capacity for long-term sustainability.

Development partner coordination ensures complementarity between different funding mechanisms and technical approaches. The "humanitarian-development nexus" emphasizes the need for coherent programming across the relief-recovery-development continuum (43).

## Rights-Based and Protection Approaches
### Right to Health in Humanitarian Settings

The **right to health** is established in international human rights law as a fundamental entitlement that applies in all circumstances, including humanitarian emergencies. The International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights recognizes the right to the highest attainable standard of physical and mental health (44).

During emergencies, states retain obligations to ensure access to healthcare for all people within their jurisdiction, including refugees, internally displaced persons, and other affected populations. When states lack capacity to fulfill these obligations, the international community has a responsibility to provide assistance.

Rights-based programming emphasizes participation of affected populations in program design, accountability mechanisms, non-discrimination in service provision, and attention to vulnerable groups. Health programs must ensure equal access regardless of gender, ethnicity, religion, or legal status.

### Protection of Healthcare Facilities

**Healthcare protection** has emerged as a critical concern due to increasing attacks on health facilities, health workers, and patients in conflict settings. Such attacks violate international humanitarian law and constitute war crimes under the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court.

Protection measures include physical security for health facilities and workers, advocacy for respect of international humanitarian law, documentation of attacks, and legal accountability mechanisms. The Health Care in Danger project provides guidance on protective measures for healthcare in conflict (45).

Operational protection strategies include facility marking with protected symbols, neutrality and impartiality in service provision, and communication with all parties to conflict about health facility locations and protective status. Security incidents must be systematically documented and reported to appropriate authorities.

### Gender-Based Violence Prevention

**Gender-based violence (GBV)** increases dramatically during humanitarian emergencies, with women and girls facing elevated risks of sexual violence, intimate partner violence, and harmful traditional practices. GBV has severe immediate and long-term health consequences requiring comprehensive response services (46).

GBV prevention strategies include ensuring safe and accessible service delivery environments, women-only spaces, adequate lighting and privacy in health facilities, and female health workers for gender-sensitive services. Community mobilization engages men and boys in prevention efforts and challenges harmful gender norms.

Clinical care for GBV survivors includes post-exposure prophylaxis for HIV and other sexually transmitted infections, emergency contraception, treatment of injuries, documentation of evidence, and psychological support. Referral systems connect health services with protection, legal, and psychosocial support services.

### Child Protection and Health

**Child protection** in humanitarian settings addresses multiple health-related risks including family separation, recruitment into armed forces, child labor, sexual exploitation, and violence. These experiences have severe immediate and long-term health consequences requiring specialized interventions (47).

Health systems play critical roles in child protection through identification of at-risk children, treatment of violence-related injuries, mental health support, and referral to protection services. Healthcare workers require training in child protection principles and referral procedures.

Family reunification programs work to reconnect separated children with their families while providing interim care arrangements. These programs require coordination between health, protection, and social service sectors to ensure children's safety and wellbeing during the reunification process.

**Case Study 25.1: Syrian Refugee Health Response in Jordan**

Jordan hosts the second-largest number of Syrian refugees globally, with over 1.3 million registered refugees and an estimated additional 300,000 unregistered refugees. This massive influx represents approximately 15% of Jordan's total population and has created unprecedented demands on the country's health system. The refugee population includes diverse groups with varying health needs, from recent arrivals to families who have been displaced for over a decade (48).

The health needs of Syrian refugees are complex, reflecting pre-existing health conditions from Syria, trauma and health impacts experienced during displacement, and ongoing health challenges related to living conditions in Jordan. Key health priorities include maternal and child health, management of chronic diseases, mental health and psychosocial support, and preventing communicable disease outbreaks.

The response involved multiple intervention strategies including health system strengthening through capacity building of Jordanian health facilities and workforce, establishment of dedicated health centers in refugee camps and host communities, multi-stakeholder coordination platforms bringing together government, UN agencies, and NGO partners, and efforts to integrate refugee health services with the national health system (49).

Results demonstrate significant achievements in maintaining health service access despite massive population increases. Vaccination coverage rates have remained above 90% for most antigens. Maternal mortality among Syrian refugees has remained lower than regional averages due to comprehensive reproductive health programming. Early collaboration with Jordan's Ministry of Health was critical for successful integration of refugee health services with national systems, while programs benefiting both refugees and Jordanian host communities reduced tensions and improved political sustainability of the response (50).

**Case Study 25.2: Post-Ebola Health System Recovery in Sierra Leone**

Sierra Leone experienced one of the most devastating Ebola virus disease outbreaks in history from 2014-2016, with over 14,000 cases and 4,000 deaths. The outbreak exposed severe weaknesses in the country's health system and led to complete collapse of routine health services in many areas. Beyond the direct mortality from Ebola, the outbreak caused significant excess mortality from preventable causes due to health system breakdown (51).

The post-Ebola recovery period provided an opportunity to "build back better" by addressing underlying health system weaknesses that contributed to the outbreak's severity. Recovery planning emphasized strengthening epidemic preparedness capacity, improving health service delivery, and building a more resilient health workforce. Post-Ebola health system recovery involved comprehensive interventions including rebuilding health facilities, developing national epidemic preparedness capacity with surveillance systems and laboratory capacity, training and deploying community health workers, and re-establishing routine health services with improved quality standards and community engagement.

Results showed gradual improvement across most indicators, with routine health service utilization returning to pre-outbreak levels by 2018. Community health worker programs expanded significantly, improving access to basic services in rural areas. Surveillance capacity strengthened substantially, enabling rapid detection and response to subsequent outbreaks including COVID-19. Restoring community confidence in the health system required transparent communication, community engagement, and demonstrable improvements in service quality. The experience demonstrated that combining epidemic preparedness with routine health service strengthening created synergies that strengthened overall health system capacity (52).

**Case Study 25.3: Mental Health Response for Rohingya Refugees in Bangladesh**

Over one million Rohingya refugees live in Cox's Bazar district in Bangladesh, making it one of the world's largest refugee settlements. The Rohingya population has experienced severe persecution, violence, and trauma in Myanmar, followed by difficult living conditions in overcrowded camps with limited livelihood opportunities. Mental health and psychosocial needs are enormous, with high prevalence of depression, anxiety, PTSD, and other trauma-related conditions (53).

The mental health response has emphasized community-based approaches that build on Rohingya cultural strengths and existing social support systems. Programming evolved from initial emergency response focused on psychological first aid to comprehensive programs addressing community, family, and individual needs across the MHPSS intervention pyramid. The response included training of health workers in psychological first aid and basic counseling, community-based psychosocial activities including support groups and cultural activities, training of community volunteers, and integration of mental health considerations into protection programming including GBV response and child protection.

Evaluation results show improvements in community social cohesion and reductions in psychological distress over time. Community-based interventions reached larger numbers of beneficiaries compared to individual clinical services, demonstrating the importance of the multi-layered approach. Mental health interventions required substantial adaptation to align with Rohingya cultural concepts of distress, healing, and help-seeking behaviors, while Rohingya communities possessed significant strengths and coping mechanisms that could be supported and strengthened rather than replaced with external models (54).

**Case Study 25.4: Health System Resilience During Afghanistan Crisis**

Afghanistan's health system faced unprecedented challenges following political transition in August 2021, including suspension of international development funding, restrictions on women's participation in health programming, and severe economic crisis. The health system, which had been heavily dependent on external support, faced imminent collapse threatening health services for 39 million people (55).

The crisis occurred amid multiple overlapping emergencies including drought, food insecurity, and economic collapse. Healthcare workers went unpaid for months, essential medicines ran out, and many health facilities closed. The situation was compounded by restrictions on women's education and work, affecting both service delivery and access, as women comprised 20% of the health workforce and were primary healthcare seekers for families. Emergency health system support involved direct funding of health facilities, establishment of humanitarian funding channels separate from development assistance, advocacy to ensure women's access to health services, and mobile health teams to reach areas with closed facilities.

Results showed significant deterioration in most indicators during the initial crisis period, followed by gradual stabilization as emergency support mechanisms took effect. Many facilities remained functional but with reduced service scope. Maternal health services were particularly affected due to restrictions on women's mobility and female health workers. The experience demonstrated that rapid establishment of humanitarian funding mechanisms was essential for preventing complete health system collapse, while health system functionality was severely compromised by restrictions on women's participation as both providers and users of health services (56).

## Conclusion

This chapter has examined the complex relationships between conflict, displacement, and health outcomes across the humanitarian continuum. Armed conflicts and complex emergencies create both direct health impacts through violence and indirect impacts through health system disruption, economic damage, and social breakdown. The effects disproportionately affect vulnerable populations including children, women, elderly persons, and those with chronic diseases.

Forced displacement, whether as refugees crossing international borders or internally displaced persons remaining within their countries, creates specific health vulnerabilities requiring specialized responses. Urban displacement and protracted displacement situations present distinct challenges for health service delivery and humanitarian assistance.

Effective humanitarian health response requires comprehensive emergency preparedness, rapid response mechanisms, and coordinated action across multiple sectors. International standards including Sphere standards and the Minimum Initial Service Package provide evidence-based guidance for health service delivery in emergencies.

Mental health and psychosocial support interventions must adopt multi-layered approaches that build on community strengths while providing specialized services for those with severe needs. Cultural adaptation and community engagement are essential for effectiveness and sustainability.

Nutrition programming in emergencies focuses on treating acute malnutrition through therapeutic feeding while preventing malnutrition through general food distribution and micronutrient supplementation. Infant and young child feeding requires special attention to protect breastfeeding while ensuring safe alternatives when needed.

Epidemic prevention and response capabilities are critical in humanitarian settings due to increased disease risks from overcrowding, poor sanitation, and weakened surveillance systems. Early warning systems and rapid response capacity can prevent localized outbreaks from becoming widespread epidemics.

Health system recovery and reconstruction provide opportunities to build back better by addressing pre-existing weaknesses and incorporating lessons learned from crisis response. Sustainable recovery requires strong government leadership, community participation, and coordinated international support that strengthens rather than undermines local capacity.

Rights-based and protection approaches ensure that health programming upholds human dignity and promotes protection of affected populations. This includes protecting healthcare facilities and workers, preventing and responding to gender-based violence, and ensuring children's safety and wellbeing.

The case studies demonstrate diverse approaches to humanitarian health programming across different contexts, populations, and phases of emergencies. Common themes include the importance of coordination, community engagement, cultural adaptation, and building sustainable local capacity while addressing immediate health needs.
