# Chapter 17: Substance Use Disorders

## Introduction

Substance use disorders represent one of the most complex and persistent public health challenges of the 21st century, affecting hundreds of millions of people worldwide and imposing enormous costs on individuals, families, communities, and health systems. The Global Burden of Disease Study 2019 estimated that substance use disorders accounted for over 40 million disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) globally, with alcohol use disorders contributing 18.4 million DALYs, drug use disorders 17.4 million DALYs, and tobacco use an additional 176 million DALYs when including all tobacco-related diseases (1). These figures represent not only significant individual suffering but also substantial economic burdens, with the global economic cost of substance use estimated at over $1.4 trillion annually (2).

The challenge of substance use disorders extends far beyond individual health consequences to encompass broader social, economic, and human rights considerations. Traditional approaches emphasizing criminalization and punishment have increasingly been challenged by evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of public health approaches that prioritize treatment, harm reduction, and human rights (3). The evolution from viewing substance use as a moral failing to understanding it as a complex health condition requiring comprehensive, evidence-based interventions represents a fundamental paradigm shift in global public health.

Contemporary understanding of substance use disorders recognizes them as chronic, relapsing brain diseases characterized by compulsive substance seeking and use despite harmful consequences (4). This neurobiological perspective has important implications for prevention, treatment, and policy approaches, emphasizing the need for long-term management strategies rather than acute interventions. Moreover, the recognition that substance use disorders often co-occur with other mental health conditions and are influenced by social determinants of health has led to more integrated and holistic approaches to addressing these complex conditions.

The global landscape of substance use is characterized by significant regional variations in patterns of use, availability of interventions, and policy approaches. While high-income countries have generally moved toward evidence-based treatment and harm reduction approaches, many low- and middle-income countries continue to rely primarily on criminal justice responses, often lacking adequate treatment infrastructure and evidence-based interventions (5). These disparities reflect broader inequities in global health and highlight the need for sustained efforts to improve access to effective interventions worldwide.

## Global Patterns of Substance Use

### Alcohol Use Patterns and Trends

Alcohol consumption patterns vary dramatically across global regions, influenced by cultural, religious, economic, and policy factors. The World Health Organization's Global Information System on Alcohol and Health reports that global per capita alcohol consumption among adults aged 15 years and older was 6.18 liters of pure alcohol in 2016, with significant regional variations (6). The European Region recorded the highest consumption at 9.8 liters per capita, followed by the Region of the Americas at 8.2 liters, while the Eastern Mediterranean Region had the lowest consumption at 0.6 liters per capita, largely due to religious and cultural factors.

Patterns of alcohol consumption are equally important as overall volume, with binge drinking and heavy episodic drinking contributing disproportionately to alcohol-related harms. The WHO defines heavy episodic drinking as consuming 60 grams or more of pure alcohol (approximately six standard drinks) on at least one occasion in the past 30 days (7). Globally, 18.2% of adults engaged in heavy episodic drinking in 2016, with higher rates among men (29.5%) compared to women (7.5%). These patterns are particularly concerning given the strong association between binge drinking and acute harms including injuries, violence, and risky sexual behavior.

The burden of alcohol use disorders varies significantly across income levels and regions. High-income countries generally have higher overall alcohol consumption but lower rates of alcohol use disorders relative to consumption, suggesting better access to treatment and support services (8). Conversely, many low- and middle-income countries experience disproportionately high rates of alcohol-related harms relative to consumption levels, reflecting limited access to treatment, greater exposure to unrecorded alcohol consumption, and higher rates of hazardous drinking patterns.

Economic transitions and globalization have significantly influenced alcohol consumption patterns, particularly in rapidly developing economies. The expansion of multinational alcohol companies into emerging markets, combined with economic development and urbanization, has contributed to increasing alcohol consumption in many Asian, African, and Latin American countries (9). These trends are particularly concerning given the often limited regulatory frameworks and treatment infrastructure in these settings.

### Tobacco Use Epidemiology

Tobacco use remains the leading preventable cause of death globally, causing more than 8 million deaths annually, including approximately 1.2 million deaths from exposure to secondhand smoke (10). The WHO estimates that there are currently 1.1 billion tobacco users worldwide, with 80% living in low- and middle-income countries where the burden of tobacco-related disease and death is heaviest.

Global tobacco use patterns reflect significant gender, regional, and socioeconomic disparities. Tobacco use among men remains highest in the Western Pacific Region (47%) and European Region (43%), while rates among women are highest in the European Region (18%) and Region of the Americas (13%) (11). These patterns reflect both cultural factors and the historical targeting of tobacco marketing, with tobacco companies increasingly focusing on women and youth in low- and middle-income countries as markets in high-income countries decline.

The tobacco epidemic follows a predictable pattern across countries and regions, beginning with adoption among high-status groups, spreading to the general population, and eventually concentrating among marginalized and disadvantaged populations as awareness of health risks increases and tobacco control policies are implemented (12). This pattern is evident in the current concentration of tobacco use among lower-income populations in many high-income countries, while in many low- and middle-income countries, tobacco use remains more prevalent among higher-income groups.

Electronic cigarettes and other novel tobacco products have emerged as significant challenges for tobacco control globally. While some evidence suggests that e-cigarettes may be less harmful than combustible cigarettes for individual users, their population-level impact remains uncertain, particularly regarding their potential to renormalize smoking and their uptake among youth (13). The regulatory response to these products varies dramatically across countries, from complete prohibitions to relatively permissive frameworks that allow marketing and sale with minimal restrictions.

### Illicit Drug Use Trends

The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime estimates that 275 million people worldwide used drugs at least once in 2019, representing 5.6% of the global population aged 15-64 years (14). Cannabis remains the most widely used illicit substance globally, with an estimated 192 million users, followed by opioids (58 million users), amphetamines (27 million users), and cocaine (20 million users). These figures likely underestimate the true prevalence of drug use, particularly in regions with limited surveillance systems and high levels of criminalization.

Regional variations in drug use patterns reflect factors including geographic proximity to production areas, trafficking routes, cultural factors, and policy approaches. Opioid use is most prevalent in Asia and Europe, reflecting both the proximity to opium production areas in Afghanistan and the availability of pharmaceutical opioids (15). Cocaine use remains concentrated in North and South America, while amphetamine use is highest in Asia and Oceania. Cannabis use is relatively widespread globally but shows particularly high prevalence in Oceania, North America, and Western and Central Europe.

The illicit drug market has undergone significant changes in recent decades, including the emergence of synthetic drugs, the expansion of online drug markets, and changes in production and trafficking patterns. The proliferation of new psychoactive substances (NPS) has created particular challenges for monitoring and response systems, with over 950 NPS reported to the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime early warning system as of 2019 (16). These substances often have unknown health effects and may not be detected by standard drug testing, complicating both clinical management and epidemiological surveillance.

### Prescription Drug Misuse

The misuse of prescription drugs, particularly opioid analgesics, has emerged as a significant global health challenge, though with marked regional variations. The opioid crisis in North America, characterized by dramatic increases in prescription opioid use, overdose deaths, and transitions to illicit opioids including fentanyl, represents the most severe manifestation of this problem (17). In the United States, prescription opioids were involved in approximately 40% of the 70,000 overdose deaths in 2019, with synthetic opioids including fentanyl contributing to an additional 50% of deaths.

The factors contributing to the prescription opioid crisis include aggressive marketing by pharmaceutical companies, changes in pain management practices, regulatory failures, and broader social and economic factors (18). The role of pharmaceutical marketing in promoting opioid prescribing has been extensively documented, including misleading claims about addiction risks and inappropriate targeting of high-risk populations. Regulatory agencies in many countries have implemented stronger oversight of opioid prescribing and marketing, though often after substantial harm had already occurred.

While the North American opioid crisis represents the most severe example of prescription drug misuse, similar patterns are emerging in other regions as access to prescription opioids increases. Australia, several European countries, and parts of the Middle East have reported increasing rates of prescription opioid misuse and related harms (19). These trends highlight the need for balanced approaches that ensure access to pain management for those who need it while preventing misuse and diversion.

## Alcohol Use Disorders

### Epidemiology and Health Consequences

Alcohol use disorders affect an estimated 283 million people globally, representing 5.1% of the adult population, with significantly higher rates among men (7.5%) compared to women (2.5%) (20). The prevalence of alcohol use disorders varies substantially across regions and income levels, with the highest rates generally observed in Eastern Europe, parts of sub-Saharan Africa, and some high-income countries with liberal alcohol policies. These disorders encompass a spectrum of conditions from harmful use to severe dependence, characterized by loss of control over drinking, tolerance, withdrawal symptoms, and continued use despite harmful consequences.

The health consequences of alcohol use disorders are extensive and affect virtually every organ system. Acute effects include increased risk of injuries, violence, risky sexual behavior, and alcohol poisoning. Chronic alcohol use is associated with over 200 disease conditions, including liver cirrhosis, cardiovascular disease, various cancers, neurological disorders, and mental health conditions (21). The Global Burden of Disease Study 2019 attributed 2.4 million deaths globally to alcohol use disorders, with alcohol use being the seventh leading risk factor for death and disability combined.

Alcohol-related liver disease represents one of the most significant health consequences of alcohol use disorders, accounting for approximately half of all liver disease deaths globally. The progression from fatty liver to alcoholic hepatitis to cirrhosis is well-established, though individual susceptibility varies based on genetic, nutritional, and co-morbidity factors (22). The increasing prevalence of alcohol-related liver disease among young adults in many countries reflects changing drinking patterns and highlights the need for early intervention and prevention strategies.

The relationship between alcohol use disorders and mental health is complex and bidirectional. Alcohol use disorders co-occur with depression in approximately 30-40% of cases and with anxiety disorders in 20-30% of cases (23). This comorbidity complicates treatment and is associated with worse outcomes, higher healthcare utilization, and increased suicide risk. The temporal relationship between alcohol use and mental health symptoms varies, with some individuals developing mental health problems as a consequence of alcohol use and others using alcohol to self-medicate pre-existing mental health conditions.

### Treatment Approaches and Evidence

Evidence-based treatments for alcohol use disorders include pharmacological interventions, psychosocial treatments, and combined approaches. The World Health Organization recommends a stepped-care approach that begins with brief interventions for hazardous and harmful drinking and progresses to more intensive treatments for alcohol dependence (24). This approach recognizes that different levels of intervention are appropriate for different levels of alcohol-related problems and allows for efficient allocation of resources.

Pharmacological treatments for alcohol use disorders include medications for acute withdrawal management and long-term relapse prevention. Benzodiazepines remain the gold standard for managing acute alcohol withdrawal, with chlordiazepoxide and diazepam being the most commonly used agents (25). For relapse prevention, three medications have strong evidence of effectiveness: naltrexone (which blocks opioid receptors and reduces alcohol's rewarding effects), acamprosate (which modulates glutamate neurotransmission), and disulfiram (which causes unpleasant reactions when alcohol is consumed). More recently, topiramate and gabapentin have shown promise as off-label treatments for alcohol use disorders.

Psychosocial interventions for alcohol use disorders include a range of approaches with varying levels of evidence support. Motivational interviewing, a client-centered counseling approach that enhances motivation for change, has strong evidence for effectiveness in alcohol use disorders (26). Cognitive-behavioral therapy helps individuals identify and modify thoughts and behaviors that contribute to drinking, while contingency management provides tangible rewards for achieving treatment goals. Twelve-step facilitation therapy, based on the principles of Alcoholics Anonymous, also has evidence of effectiveness, particularly for individuals with strong social support networks.

Brief interventions represent a particularly important component of alcohol use disorder treatment, given their potential for population-level impact and cost-effectiveness. These interventions, typically lasting 5-30 minutes and involving screening, feedback, advice, and goal-setting, have been shown to reduce alcohol consumption by 10-15% in primary care settings (27). The WHO recommends implementation of brief interventions in healthcare settings as part of comprehensive alcohol policy approaches, though implementation remains limited in many low- and middle-income countries.

### Population-Level Prevention and Policy

Population-level approaches to preventing alcohol use disorders focus on reducing overall alcohol availability, accessibility, and attractiveness through policy interventions. The WHO has identified a package of evidence-based policy interventions known as "best buys" due to their high impact and cost-effectiveness (28). These include: increasing alcohol taxes, restricting alcohol advertising and sponsorship, restricting alcohol availability through licensing systems and hours of sale, implementing minimum legal drinking ages, and facilitating access to treatment.

Alcohol taxation represents one of the most effective and cost-effective interventions for reducing alcohol consumption and related harms. A 10% increase in alcohol prices typically reduces consumption by 5-10%, with larger effects among heavy drinkers and young people (29). The effectiveness of taxation depends on the tax structure, with specific taxes (based on alcohol content) generally being more effective than ad valorem taxes (based on price). Minimum unit pricing, implemented in Scotland and several other jurisdictions, represents an innovative approach that specifically targets cheap, high-strength alcohol products often consumed by heavy drinkers.

Restrictions on alcohol availability through licensing systems, outlet density controls, and limitations on hours and days of sale have consistently been shown to reduce alcohol consumption and related harms. Systematic reviews demonstrate that increasing outlet density is associated with increased alcohol consumption, violence, and other alcohol-related problems (30). Restrictions on hours of sale, particularly late-night sales, have been shown to reduce alcohol-related violence and hospital admissions. These interventions are particularly important in low- and middle-income countries where informal alcohol markets may be prevalent.

## Tobacco Control

### Framework Convention on Tobacco Control Implementation

The WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC), which entered into force in 2005, represents the first global health treaty and provides a comprehensive framework for tobacco control policy implementation. With 182 Parties to the Convention, the FCTC covers over 90% of the world's population and has established international legal obligations for tobacco control (31). The Convention includes provisions for demand reduction measures (including price and tax measures, protection from exposure to tobacco smoke, regulation of tobacco product contents, packaging and labeling, education and awareness, and advertising and sponsorship bans) and supply reduction measures (including illicit trade and sales to minors).

Implementation of FCTC provisions has been uneven globally, with high-income countries generally showing greater progress than low- and middle-income countries. The WHO's biennial global reports on the tobacco epidemic track progress on six evidence-based tobacco control measures (MPOWER): Monitor tobacco use and prevention policies, Protect people from tobacco smoke, Offer help to quit tobacco use, Warn about the dangers of tobacco, Enforce bans on tobacco advertising promotion and sponsorship, and Raise taxes on tobacco (32). As of 2019, only 23 countries (representing 15% of the global population) had implemented all six MPOWER measures at the highest level.

Smoke-free policies represent one of the most widely implemented tobacco control measures, with 74 countries having comprehensive national smoke-free laws covering all public places and workplaces by 2019 (33). These policies protect non-smokers from exposure to secondhand smoke while also reducing tobacco consumption among smokers by reducing opportunities to smoke and changing social norms around smoking. The evidence for effectiveness of comprehensive smoke-free policies is overwhelming, with studies consistently demonstrating reductions in secondhand smoke exposure, acute myocardial infarctions, and respiratory symptoms.

Health warning labels on tobacco packages have been implemented in most countries, though with significant variation in size, design, and effectiveness. Plain packaging, which removes all branding and marketing elements except for brand names in standardized fonts and colors, represents the most advanced form of tobacco packaging regulation. Australia was the first country to implement plain packaging in 2012, and evidence from Australia and subsequent implementing countries demonstrates significant impacts on smoking behavior and tobacco industry marketing (34).

### MPOWER Strategy Implementation

The MPOWER package represents the WHO's evidence-based approach to implementing the most cost-effective tobacco control interventions. Each component of MPOWER has strong evidence of effectiveness, and countries implementing multiple components simultaneously achieve greater impact than those implementing individual measures (35). However, implementation remains challenging in many settings due to resource constraints, tobacco industry interference, and competing health priorities.

Tobacco taxation is consistently identified as the most effective and cost-effective tobacco control intervention. The WHO recommends that taxes should account for at least 75% of the retail price of tobacco products, though only 38 countries had achieved this level by 2019 (36). The effectiveness of tobacco taxation depends on the tax structure, with specific taxes generally being more effective than ad valorem taxes, and regular tax increases being necessary to maintain real price increases over time. Tax avoidance and evasion can undermine the effectiveness of tobacco taxation, particularly in countries with weak governance systems or porous borders.

Tobacco advertising, promotion, and sponsorship bans are required under Article 13 of the FCTC and have been shown to reduce tobacco consumption, particularly among youth. Comprehensive bans that cover all forms of advertising, promotion, and sponsorship are more effective than partial bans, as the tobacco industry often shifts resources to unrestricted channels when partial bans are implemented (37). The emergence of digital marketing channels and social media has created new challenges for implementing comprehensive advertising bans, requiring updated regulatory frameworks and enforcement mechanisms.

Cessation support services are essential for helping current tobacco users quit and include both clinical interventions (such as nicotine replacement therapy, prescription medications, and counseling) and population-level interventions (such as quitlines and mass media campaigns). The WHO recommends that cessation support be available through healthcare systems and that the costs be covered by insurance or government funding (38). Brief interventions by healthcare providers, even as short as 3 minutes, can increase quit rates significantly, while more intensive interventions can achieve quit rates of 20-25%.

### Emerging Challenges and Novel Products

The tobacco industry's development and marketing of novel tobacco and nicotine products, including electronic cigarettes, heated tobacco products, and oral tobacco products, represents a significant challenge for tobacco control globally. These products are often marketed as "reduced risk" alternatives to combustible cigarettes, though the evidence on their population-level health impacts remains limited and contested (39). Regulatory responses to these products vary dramatically across countries, from complete prohibitions to relatively permissive frameworks that allow marketing with minimal restrictions.

Electronic cigarettes have generated particular controversy within the tobacco control community, with some advocates arguing that they represent a harm reduction opportunity for current smokers while others worry about their potential to renormalize smoking and attract youth. The evidence on e-cigarettes' effectiveness for smoking cessation is mixed, with some randomized controlled trials showing modest benefits compared to other cessation aids while observational studies often show lower quit rates among e-cigarette users (40). The dual use of e-cigarettes and combustible cigarettes, which is common among adult users, may reduce the potential health benefits.

Youth uptake of e-cigarettes has become a major concern in many countries, particularly the United States, where the prevalence of e-cigarette use among high school students increased from 1.5% in 2011 to 27.5% in 2019 before declining to 19.6% in 2020 (41). The rapid increase was attributed to the introduction of pod-based devices with high nicotine concentrations and appealing flavors, along with aggressive marketing on social media platforms. The potential for e-cigarettes to serve as gateway products to conventional smoking remains controversial, with some studies suggesting increased risk while others find no association after controlling for confounding factors.

## Illicit Drug Use and Health Consequences

### Epidemiology of Drug Use Disorders

Drug use disorders affect an estimated 35.6 million people globally, representing 13% of all people who use drugs (42). The prevalence of drug use disorders varies significantly by substance, with opioid use disorders being the most common (21.6 million people), followed by cannabis use disorders (22.1 million people), amphetamine use disorders (4.9 million people), and cocaine use disorders (4.1 million people). These figures likely underestimate the true prevalence due to underreporting, particularly in regions with criminalized drug policies and limited healthcare access.

Opioid use disorders represent the most severe form of drug use disorders in terms of health consequences and mortality risk. People with opioid use disorders have a mortality risk 10-20 times higher than age-matched controls, with overdose being the leading cause of death (43). The global burden of opioid use disorders has increased substantially over the past two decades, driven primarily by increases in prescription opioid misuse in North America and continued heroin use in Asia and Europe. The introduction of synthetic opioids, particularly illicitly manufactured fentanyl, has dramatically increased overdose mortality in several regions.

Cannabis use disorders affect a smaller proportion of cannabis users compared to other substances, with approximately 10% of regular users developing dependence compared to 15-20% for cocaine and 25-30% for tobacco (44). However, the increasing potency of cannabis products and the emergence of high-concentration THC products has raised concerns about increasing rates of cannabis use disorders and related mental health consequences. The relationship between cannabis use and mental health, particularly psychosis and schizophrenia, remains an area of active research and debate.

Injection drug use represents a particularly high-risk form of drug use associated with increased mortality and morbidity, primarily due to the risk of blood-borne virus transmission, overdose, and injection-related injuries and infections. An estimated 11.2 million people inject drugs globally, with approximately 1.4 million living with HIV and 5.6 million living with hepatitis C (45). The concentration of people who inject drugs in marginalized populations and their often poor access to healthcare and harm reduction services exacerbates these health risks.

### Blood-Borne Virus Transmission

The transmission of blood-borne viruses, particularly HIV and hepatitis C, among people who inject drugs represents one of the most significant health consequences of illicit drug use. Injection drug use accounts for approximately 10% of global HIV infections, though this proportion varies dramatically by region, from less than 5% in sub-Saharan Africa to over 40% in Eastern Europe and Central Asia (46). The risk of HIV transmission through injection drug use is influenced by factors including sharing of injection equipment, frequency of injection, network characteristics, and access to prevention services.

Hepatitis C virus (HCV) transmission is even more efficient than HIV transmission through injection drug use, with seroprevalence rates among people who inject drugs typically ranging from 40-80% globally (47). The rapid transmission of HCV among people who inject drugs reflects the virus's ability to survive outside the body for extended periods and its efficient transmission through minimal blood contact. The development of highly effective direct-acting antiviral treatments for hepatitis C has created opportunities for treatment as prevention strategies, though access remains limited in many settings.

The prevention of blood-borne virus transmission among people who inject drugs requires comprehensive approaches that include needle and syringe exchange programs, opioid substitution therapy, HIV and HCV testing and treatment, and overdose prevention interventions. The WHO, UNODC, and UNAIDS have identified a package of nine evidence-based interventions for preventing HIV among people who inject drugs, though implementation of these interventions remains insufficient in most settings (48). Only 1% of people who inject drugs globally have access to the full package of harm reduction interventions.

### Overdose Prevention and Response

Drug overdose deaths have increased dramatically globally, driven primarily by the opioid epidemic in North America but with increasing trends in other regions as well. In 2019, there were an estimated 585,000 deaths worldwide due to drug use disorders, with overdose accounting for the majority of these deaths (49). The United States alone accounted for approximately 70,000 overdose deaths in 2019, representing a four-fold increase since 1999. The introduction of illicitly manufactured fentanyl and its analogues has been the primary driver of increasing overdose mortality, as these substances are significantly more potent than heroin and more likely to cause fatal overdoses.

Naloxone (also known as Narcan) represents the most important intervention for preventing overdose deaths from opioids. This opioid antagonist can rapidly reverse opioid overdose and has been shown to be safe and effective when administered by trained laypeople, including people who use drugs, family members, and community members (50). The WHO has added naloxone to its Model List of Essential Medicines and recommends that it be made widely available to people likely to witness opioid overdose. Many countries and jurisdictions have implemented naloxone distribution programs, though access remains limited in many settings.

Supervised consumption sites, also known as drug consumption rooms or safe injection facilities, provide a controlled environment where people can use pre-obtained drugs under medical supervision. These facilities have been shown to reduce overdose deaths, blood-borne virus transmission, and public drug use while connecting people to treatment and other health services (51). Despite strong evidence of effectiveness, supervised consumption sites remain controversial and are legal in only a limited number of countries, primarily in Europe, Australia, and Canada.

## Substance Use Treatment

### Detoxification and Acute Management

Medical detoxification represents the initial phase of treatment for many substance use disorders, involving the safe management of withdrawal symptoms under medical supervision. Withdrawal symptoms vary significantly depending on the substance used, duration and intensity of use, individual physiology, and co-occurring medical and mental health conditions. Alcohol withdrawal can be life-threatening due to the risk of seizures and delirium tremens, requiring careful medical monitoring and pharmacological management (52). Opioid withdrawal, while rarely life-threatening, can be extremely uncomfortable and is associated with high rates of treatment dropout without appropriate management.

Evidence-based protocols for managing alcohol withdrawal emphasize the use of benzodiazepines to prevent seizures and reduce anxiety, with chlordiazepoxide and diazepam being the most commonly used agents. The Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol (CIWA-Ar) is widely used to assess withdrawal severity and guide medication dosing (53). Thiamine supplementation is essential to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy, a potentially fatal neurological condition associated with chronic alcohol use and nutritional deficiency.

Opioid withdrawal management has been revolutionized by the development of evidence-based pharmacological approaches. Methadone and buprenorphine can be used for both acute withdrawal management and long-term maintenance therapy, providing superior outcomes compared to symptom-based management with non-opioid medications (54). Alpha-2 adrenergic agonists such as clonidine can help manage autonomic symptoms of opioid withdrawal, while comfort medications address specific symptoms such as diarrhea, nausea, and insomnia.

The integration of psychosocial support during detoxification improves outcomes and increases engagement with ongoing treatment. Motivational interviewing techniques can help individuals develop motivation for continued treatment, while cognitive-behavioral strategies can help develop coping skills for managing cravings and triggers (55). Family involvement and peer support can also enhance the effectiveness of detoxification programs, though care must be taken to address potential family dynamics that may contribute to substance use.

### Medication-Assisted Treatment

Medication-assisted treatment (MAT), also known as opioid substitution therapy (OST), represents the gold standard for treating opioid use disorders. Three medications are approved for opioid use disorders: methadone, buprenorphine, and extended-release naltrexone. Methadone and buprenorphine are opioid agonists or partial agonists that prevent withdrawal symptoms and reduce cravings while blocking the effects of other opioids (56). Extended-release naltrexone is an opioid antagonist that blocks the euphoric effects of opioids and is most appropriate for individuals who have already completed detoxification.

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses consistently demonstrate that methadone and buprenorphine maintenance therapy reduces illicit opioid use, criminal activity, HIV risk behaviors, and mortality compared to drug-free treatments (57). Treatment retention is significantly higher with MAT compared to behavioral interventions alone, with retention rates of 60-80% at 12 months not uncommon in well-implemented programs. The optimal duration of MAT is indefinite for most individuals, as discontinuation is associated with high rates of relapse and increased overdose risk.

Access to MAT remains limited globally, with an estimated treatment gap of over 80% for people with opioid use disorders. Barriers to MAT implementation include regulatory restrictions, provider attitudes and training, cost and reimbursement issues, and stigma from healthcare providers and communities (58). Many countries maintain restrictive regulations that limit MAT prescribing to specialized clinics or require daily observed dosing, creating significant access barriers particularly in rural and resource-limited settings.

The integration of psychosocial interventions with MAT can enhance outcomes, though medication alone is often sufficient for stabilization and improved functioning. Cognitive-behavioral therapy, contingency management, and motivational interviewing have evidence of effectiveness when combined with MAT (59). However, the effectiveness of MAT does not depend on the provision of psychosocial interventions, and programs should prioritize medication access over comprehensive services when resources are limited.

### Psychosocial Interventions

Psychosocial interventions form the cornerstone of treatment for many substance use disorders, particularly those for which effective medications are not available or suitable. Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) is the most widely studied and implemented psychosocial intervention for substance use disorders, with strong evidence of effectiveness across multiple substances (60). CBT focuses on identifying and modifying thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that contribute to substance use, while developing coping strategies for managing triggers and high-risk situations.

Motivational interviewing (MI) is a client-centered counseling approach that has demonstrated effectiveness for enhancing motivation for change and treatment engagement across diverse populations and settings. MI principles include expressing empathy, developing discrepancy between current behavior and personal goals, rolling with resistance rather than confronting it directly, and supporting self-efficacy for change (61). The effectiveness of MI has been demonstrated in brief interventions lasting as few as one or two sessions, making it particularly suitable for implementation in primary care and other non-specialty settings.

Contingency management (CM) provides tangible rewards for achieving specific treatment goals, such as providing drug-free urine samples or attending counseling sessions. CM is based on operant conditioning principles and has strong evidence of effectiveness, particularly for stimulant use disorders where effective medications are not available (62). The effectiveness of CM appears to depend on the immediacy, magnitude, and consistency of rewards, with voucher-based systems and prize-based systems being the most commonly implemented approaches.

Twelve-step facilitation therapy (TSF) is based on the principles of Alcoholics Anonymous and other mutual help groups and has evidence of effectiveness, particularly for alcohol use disorders. TSF differs from simply referring individuals to mutual help groups by actively facilitating engagement through structured sessions that introduce twelve-step concepts and encourage participation (63). The effectiveness of TSF may be mediated by increased social support for recovery and spiritual or existential changes, though the specific mechanisms remain unclear.

## Harm Reduction

### Principles and Evidence Base

Harm reduction is a set of practical strategies and ideas aimed at reducing negative health, social, and legal impacts associated with drug use. Rather than focusing solely on eliminating drug use, harm reduction prioritizes reducing the most severe consequences of drug use while respecting the rights and dignity of people who use drugs (64). Core principles of harm reduction include pragmatism (accepting that drug use occurs and working to minimize harm), human rights (treating people who use drugs with respect and dignity), focus on harms rather than use (prioritizing interventions that reduce the most severe consequences), and evidence-based practice (using interventions with demonstrated effectiveness).

The evidence base for harm reduction interventions is extensive and demonstrates significant benefits for individual and population health. Systematic reviews of needle and syringe exchange programs consistently show reductions in HIV and hepatitis C transmission among people who inject drugs, with greater effects observed in programs that provide adequate coverage and operate in combination with other interventions (65). Opioid substitution therapy reduces mortality by approximately 50%, HIV transmission by 50-80%, and criminal activity by 40-50% compared to no treatment or drug-free treatments.

The cost-effectiveness of harm reduction interventions has been demonstrated in numerous economic evaluations. Needle and syringe exchange programs typically save $3-7 in healthcare costs for every dollar invested, primarily through prevention of HIV and hepatitis C infections (66). Opioid substitution therapy generates even greater returns on investment, with some studies estimating savings of $12-15 for every dollar invested when considering healthcare, criminal justice, and productivity benefits.

The implementation of harm reduction interventions requires careful attention to local context, community acceptance, and integration with existing health and social services. Successful programs typically involve extensive community consultation and engagement, training and support for staff, integration with primary healthcare and mental health services, and ongoing monitoring and evaluation (67). The involvement of people who use drugs in program design, implementation, and evaluation is essential for ensuring that services meet client needs and reduce barriers to access.

### Needle and Syringe Exchange Programs

Needle and syringe exchange programs (NSP) provide sterile injection equipment to people who inject drugs and safely dispose of used equipment. These programs were first established in the 1980s in response to HIV transmission among people who inject drugs and have since been implemented in over 90 countries worldwide (68). NSP are typically provided through fixed sites, mobile services, or pharmacy-based programs, with the optimal model depending on local context, client needs, and regulatory environment.

The effectiveness of NSP in reducing blood-borne virus transmission has been demonstrated in numerous studies using diverse methodologies. A systematic review of 12 studies found that NSP were associated with a 50% reduction in HIV incidence among people who inject drugs, with greater effects observed in programs with higher coverage (69). Similar effects have been demonstrated for hepatitis C transmission, though the evidence is somewhat less consistent due to the higher efficiency of HCV transmission and the need for very high coverage to achieve population-level impact.

Coverage of NSP remains inadequate in most settings, with the WHO, UNODC, and UNAIDS recommending the distribution of at least 200 sterile needles and syringes per person who injects drugs per year. However, global coverage averages only 34 needles and syringes per person who injects drugs per year, with significant regional variations (70). Eastern Europe and Central Asia have particularly low coverage, which helps explain the continued high rates of HIV transmission among people who inject drugs in these regions.

The integration of NSP with other health and social services can enhance their effectiveness and improve client outcomes. Many programs provide HIV and hepatitis C testing and treatment, wound care, mental health services, social support, and referrals to drug treatment (71). The co-location or integration of NSP with opioid substitution therapy programs has been shown to improve outcomes for both interventions, as clients who are stable on OST are more likely to consistently use sterile injection equipment.

### Opioid Substitution Therapy

Opioid substitution therapy involves the provision of long-acting opioid medications (typically methadone or buprenorphine) to people with opioid use disorders as a long-term treatment strategy. OST differs from traditional abstinence-based treatments by maintaining individuals on opioid medications while helping them achieve stability in other areas of their lives (72). This approach recognizes that opioid dependence is a chronic medical condition requiring long-term management, similar to other chronic diseases such as diabetes or hypertension.

The evidence base for OST is extensive and consistently demonstrates significant benefits across multiple domains. Systematic reviews show that OST reduces all-cause mortality by approximately 50%, reduces HIV transmission by 50-80%, reduces hepatitis C transmission by 50%, and reduces criminal activity by 40-60% compared to no treatment (73). Treatment retention is significantly higher for OST compared to drug-free treatments, with 60-80% of clients remaining in treatment at 12 months in well-implemented programs.

Access to OST remains severely limited globally, with fewer than 50% of countries providing these services and coverage rates below 10% in most regions. Barriers to OST implementation include restrictive drug laws and regulations, lack of trained healthcare providers, stigma from healthcare workers and communities, and inadequate funding (74). Many countries that do provide OST maintain unnecessary restrictions such as daily observed dosing, limited prescribing authority, or caps on patient numbers that significantly limit access and effectiveness.

The quality of OST programs varies significantly and affects both effectiveness and client satisfaction. Key quality indicators include adequate medication dosing (typically 80-120mg daily for methadone), flexible take-home policies for stable clients, integration with medical and mental health services, and respectful treatment by staff (75). Programs that operate more like medical clinics than drug treatment centers typically achieve better outcomes and higher client satisfaction, reflecting the importance of addressing stigma and discrimination within treatment settings.

### Supervised Consumption Sites

Supervised consumption sites (SCS), also known as drug consumption rooms or safe injection facilities, provide hygienic, supervised environments where people can use pre-obtained drugs. These facilities are staffed by trained healthcare workers who can respond to overdoses and other medical emergencies while providing access to sterile injection equipment, wound care, and referrals to treatment and other services (76). SCS operate under the principle that providing a safe environment for drug use can reduce the most severe consequences while creating opportunities for engagement with healthcare and social services.

The evidence for SCS effectiveness comes primarily from observational studies, as randomized controlled trials are generally not feasible for ethical and practical reasons. Studies from Vancouver's InSite facility, the most extensively evaluated SCS, demonstrate reductions in fatal overdoses, HIV transmission, and public drug use in the surrounding neighborhood (77). A systematic review of 75 studies found consistent evidence for reduced high-risk injection behaviors, increased access to healthcare services, and reduced public disorder, with no evidence of increased drug use or crime in surrounding areas.

Despite strong evidence of effectiveness, SCS remain controversial and are currently operating in only 14 countries, primarily in Europe, along with Canada and Australia. Opposition to SCS often reflects concerns about normalizing drug use, enabling continued drug use rather than promoting treatment, and potential negative impacts on surrounding communities (78). However, research consistently demonstrates that SCS do not increase drug use or crime and actually reduce public drug use and associated nuisances.

The integration of SCS with other health and social services can enhance their effectiveness and improve client outcomes. Many SCS provide wound care, mental health services, case management, and direct referrals to drug treatment (79). Some facilities have integrated OSD programs on-site, allowing clients to receive maintenance therapy and inject illicitly obtained drugs in the same location. The provision of comprehensive services within SCS can address the multiple needs of people who inject drugs while reducing barriers to access.

**Case Study 17.1: Australia's Plain Packaging Tobacco Control Policy**

Australia implemented world-first legislation requiring plain packaging for all tobacco products on December 1, 2012, following several years of development, consultation, and legal challenges from the tobacco industry. The Tobacco Plain Packaging Act 2011 required all tobacco products to be sold in standardized dark brown packages with large graphic health warnings, removing all branding elements except for brand names displayed in standardized fonts (80). This policy represented a significant escalation in tobacco control efforts and was closely watched globally as a test case for advanced packaging restrictions.

The development of Australia's plain packaging policy involved extensive research, consultation, and political negotiation spanning nearly a decade. Initial research commissioned by the Australian government in the early 2000s demonstrated that packaging was a key component of tobacco marketing, particularly important for attracting youth and maintaining brand loyalty among adult smokers (81). Consumer research showed that plain packaging reduced the appeal of smoking, increased the noticeability and effectiveness of health warnings, and reduced misperceptions about the relative harmfulness of different tobacco products.

The tobacco industry mounted significant opposition to plain packaging through multiple channels, including legal challenges, trade disputes, intensive lobbying, and public relations campaigns designed to generate opposition from retailers and the general public. The industry argued that plain packaging would increase illicit trade, reduce product quality, violate intellectual property rights, and be ineffective in reducing smoking (82). However, tobacco companies' internal documents revealed that they viewed packaging as crucial for marketing and were concerned that plain packaging would significantly impact their business.

Implementation of plain packaging required extensive coordination across government agencies, retailers, wholesalers, and enforcement bodies. The government provided detailed guidance on packaging requirements, implemented a comprehensive compliance monitoring system, and imposed significant penalties for non-compliance (83). Special attention was given to preventing stockpiling of branded products before the implementation date and ensuring rapid transition to compliant packaging across the entire distribution system.

The evaluation of Australia's plain packaging policy has been comprehensive and demonstrates significant impacts on smoking behavior and tobacco industry marketing. Population-level studies show a 13% reduction in smoking prevalence between 2012 and 2018, with the steepest declines occurring immediately after plain packaging implementation (84). Studies of specific populations show particularly strong effects among youth and young adults, consistent with evidence that packaging is most important for initiating smoking and maintaining occasional smoking patterns.

Plain packaging has also changed smoking behaviors among continuing smokers, with studies showing reduced enjoyment of smoking, increased thoughts about quitting, and greater noticeability of health warnings. The policy has been particularly effective in reducing misperceptions about the relative harmfulness of different tobacco products, as consumers can no longer rely on packaging cues to distinguish between "light" and "regular" products (85). These effects appear to be sustained over time, contrary to tobacco industry predictions that consumers would adapt to the new packaging.

The international impact of Australia's plain packaging policy has been substantial, with 15 countries having implemented similar policies by 2021 and many others considering implementation. The successful defeat of tobacco industry legal challenges in multiple international forums has been crucial for encouraging other countries to proceed with plain packaging implementation (86). Research and evaluation evidence from Australia has been cited extensively in policy development processes in other countries and has contributed to updated WHO guidelines on tobacco packaging restrictions.

Key lessons from Australia's experience include the importance of comprehensive pre-implementation research, extensive consultation with stakeholders, careful attention to implementation logistics, and robust post-implementation evaluation. The policy's success demonstrates that innovative tobacco control measures can be successfully implemented even in the face of intensive industry opposition, provided there is strong political leadership and community support (87). The Australian experience also highlights the importance of implementing plain packaging as part of comprehensive tobacco control strategies rather than as isolated interventions.

**Case Study 17.2: Portugal's Drug Decriminalization and Treatment Expansion**

Portugal implemented a comprehensive drug policy reform in 2001 that decriminalized personal use and possession of all illicit drugs while simultaneously expanding treatment and harm reduction services. This policy shift represented a fundamental change from criminalization-focused approaches to public health-centered strategies and has become one of the most studied examples of drug policy reform globally (88). The Portuguese approach emphasizes treatment, harm reduction, and social reintegration while maintaining criminal penalties for drug trafficking and dealing.

The context for Portugal's drug policy reform was a severe drug crisis in the 1990s characterized by high rates of heroin use, HIV transmission among people who inject drugs, and drug-related crime. By 1999, Portugal had the highest rate of drug-related HIV infections in Europe and one of the highest rates of heroin use globally (89). Traditional criminal justice approaches had proven ineffective and costly, while treatment services were fragmented and inadequate to meet the scale of the problem. A national commission recommended comprehensive policy reform based on evidence from other countries and extensive consultation with experts and civil society.

The decriminalization policy removed criminal penalties for personal use and possession of drugs in quantities up to 10 days of personal use, as defined by technical committees for each substance. Individuals found in possession of drugs are referred to Dissuasion Committees comprising a lawyer, a physician, and a social worker who assess the individual and may recommend treatment, counseling, fines, or no action depending on the circumstances (90). This approach treats drug use as primarily a health issue while maintaining the illegal status of drugs and criminal penalties for trafficking.

Simultaneously with decriminalization, Portugal invested heavily in expanding treatment and harm reduction services. The number of treatment slots increased from fewer than 6,000 in 1998 to over 25,000 by 2008, with particular emphasis on opioid substitution therapy and psychosocial interventions (91). Needle and syringe exchange programs were expanded from 2 sites in 1993 to over 50 sites by 2008, providing comprehensive coverage across the country. Mobile outreach teams were established to reach marginalized populations and provide referrals to treatment and other services.

The outcomes of Portugal's drug policy reform have been extensively studied and demonstrate significant improvements across multiple indicators. Drug-related deaths decreased from 369 in 1999 to 27 in 2015, representing a 95% reduction and one of the lowest rates in Europe (92). New HIV infections among people who inject drugs decreased from 1,575 in 2000 to 18 in 2015, a 99% reduction. Treatment uptake increased substantially, with the number of people receiving opioid substitution therapy increasing from 6,040 in 1999 to 14,877 in 2008.

Drug use patterns also changed following the policy reform, though the interpretation of these changes remains debated. Lifetime prevalence of drug use increased among adults, consistent with trends in other European countries, while problematic drug use (particularly heroin injection) decreased substantially (93). Youth drug use rates remained stable or decreased for most substances, contrary to predictions that decriminalization would lead to increased youth experimentation. The quality of life for people who use drugs improved significantly, with better housing stability, employment outcomes, and social integration.

The sustainability of Portugal's drug policy reforms has been tested through economic crises and political changes but has remained largely intact for over two decades. The policy enjoys broad public and political support, with polling consistently showing majority support for the current approach (94). Cost-benefit analyses demonstrate that the reforms generate significant economic returns through reduced criminal justice costs, healthcare savings, and increased productivity, with estimated savings of €12 for every €1 invested in treatment and harm reduction services.

International interest in Portugal's model has been substantial, with delegations from dozens of countries visiting to study the Portuguese approach. However, implementation of similar policies in other countries has been limited, reflecting the complexity of policy transfer and the importance of local context (95). Countries that have implemented aspects of the Portuguese model, such as Switzerland and the Netherlands, have generally achieved positive results, though none have replicated the comprehensive approach exactly.

Key lessons from Portugal's experience include the importance of comprehensive rather than piecemeal reform, the need for substantial investment in treatment and harm reduction services, the value of evidence-based policy development, and the crucial role of political leadership in sustaining reform efforts. The Portuguese case demonstrates that treating drug use as primarily a health issue rather than a criminal justice issue can achieve significant improvements in both individual and population health outcomes (96).

**Case Study 17.3: Scotland's Minimum Unit Pricing for Alcohol**

Scotland implemented minimum unit pricing (MUP) for alcohol on May 1, 2018, becoming the first country to implement a comprehensive minimum price per unit of alcohol across all retail settings. The policy set a minimum price of £0.50 per unit of alcohol (equivalent to 10ml of pure alcohol), affecting approximately 60% of alcohol products sold in Scotland, particularly cheap, high-strength products favored by heavy drinkers (97). This policy represented a novel approach to alcohol control that directly targeted the relationship between price and alcohol-related harms.

The development of Scotland's MUP policy involved nearly a decade of research, policy development, and legal challenges. The Scottish government commissioned extensive modeling studies that projected significant health benefits from MUP implementation, particularly among heavy drinkers and lower-income populations who consume disproportionate amounts of cheap alcohol (98). Economic modeling suggested that MUP would be more effective than general tax increases because it would specifically target cheap alcohol products without affecting moderate consumers of more expensive products.

Legal challenges from the alcohol industry delayed implementation of MUP by several years, with the case ultimately reaching the UK Supreme Court. The industry argued that MUP would violate European Union trade law by restricting competition and creating barriers to trade (99). However, the Supreme Court ruled in 2017 that MUP was a proportionate response to the public health evidence and that less restrictive measures (such as taxation) would not achieve equivalent effects. This legal precedent has been important for other countries considering similar policies.

The implementation of MUP required extensive preparation and coordination across retailers, wholesalers, and enforcement agencies. The Scottish government conducted extensive stakeholder engagement and provided detailed guidance on pricing calculations, compliance requirements, and enforcement procedures (100). Point-of-sale systems were updated to automatically calculate minimum prices based on alcohol content, and monitoring systems were established to track price changes and compliance across different retail channels.

Early evaluation results demonstrate significant impacts on alcohol sales and purchasing patterns within the first two years of implementation. Overall alcohol sales decreased by 7.6% in the first year, with particularly large decreases for the cheapest products most affected by the policy (101). Off-trade beer sales decreased by 8.4%, spirits by 6.9%, and wine by 8.2%. The largest decreases were observed among households that purchased the most alcohol before implementation, consistent with predictions that MUP would have the greatest impact on heavy drinkers.

Health impacts from Scotland's MUP are beginning to emerge, though the full effects will take several years to become apparent. Hospital admissions for alcohol-specific conditions decreased in the months following implementation, particularly for conditions most associated with chronic heavy drinking (102). Emergency department attendances for alcohol-related conditions also showed early decreases, though these trends must be interpreted carefully given broader changes in healthcare utilization and the timing of policy implementation.

The distributional effects of MUP have been an important focus of evaluation, given concerns that the policy might disproportionately affect lower-income households. Research shows that while lower-income households experience larger absolute price increases due to their preference for cheaper products, the policy appears to reduce rather than exacerbate health inequalities because harmful drinking is more concentrated among lower-income groups (103). Households with dependent drinkers experienced the largest price increases but also the largest reductions in alcohol purchases.

Economic impacts of MUP on the alcohol industry have been mixed, with off-trade retailers generally experiencing increased revenues due to higher prices, while some manufacturers of cheap alcohol products have seen reduced volumes. The policy has led to product reformulation, with some manufacturers reducing alcohol content to lower the minimum price of their products (104). Cross-border purchasing appears to have increased slightly but represents a small proportion of overall alcohol consumption in Scotland.

International interest in Scotland's MUP policy has been substantial, with Wales implementing an identical policy in 2020 and numerous other countries and jurisdictions considering similar measures. The Irish government has passed legislation to implement MUP but has delayed implementation pending further evaluation of the Scottish experience (105). Research and evaluation evidence from Scotland is being closely monitored by policymakers, researchers, and public health advocates globally as a test case for this innovative policy approach.

Key lessons from Scotland's experience include the importance of robust pre-implementation modeling and research, the need for comprehensive stakeholder engagement, the value of strong legal frameworks for defending innovative policies, and the necessity of rigorous post-implementation evaluation. The Scottish case demonstrates that innovative alcohol policies can be successfully implemented despite industry opposition, provided there is strong evidence base and political commitment (106).

**Case Study 17.4: Vietnam's Methadone Maintenance Therapy Scale-Up**

Vietnam implemented one of the most successful methadone maintenance therapy (MMT) scale-up programs globally, expanding from a pilot program with 400 clients in 2008 to a national program serving over 48,000 clients by 2018. This expansion occurred in a country with limited healthcare infrastructure and significant stigma toward people who use drugs, demonstrating the potential for rapid scale-up of evidence-based treatment in challenging contexts (107). The program has been recognized as a model for other low- and middle-income countries seeking to address opioid use disorders and related HIV transmission.

The context for Vietnam's MMT program was a significant HIV epidemic among people who inject drugs, with HIV prevalence exceeding 30% in this population by 2005. Vietnam's HIV epidemic was concentrated among people who inject drugs, sex workers, and men who have sex with men, with injection drug use accounting for approximately 60% of new HIV infections (108). Traditional approaches emphasizing detoxification and abstinence-based treatment had proven ineffective, with high dropout rates and continued HIV transmission. International evidence on MMT effectiveness, combined with WHO and UNAIDS advocacy, led to Vietnamese government support for pilot MMT programs.

The initial pilot phase from 2008-2009 involved three MMT clinics in Ho Chi Minh City and Ha Noi serving 400 clients. These pilots were implemented through collaboration between the Ministry of Health, international technical assistance providers, and local healthcare facilities (109). The pilots demonstrated feasibility and effectiveness of MMT in the Vietnamese context, with high retention rates, reduced heroin use, and improved quality of life among participants. Successful pilot implementation provided evidence for political decision-makers and helped address initial skepticism from healthcare providers and law enforcement agencies.

Scale-up of MMT began in 2010 with support from international donors, particularly the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, and technical assistance from international organizations. The expansion strategy emphasized integration of MMT into existing healthcare facilities rather than creating standalone treatment centers (110). This approach leveraged existing infrastructure and human resources while reducing costs and improving sustainability. District-level hospitals and commune health centers were trained and equipped to provide MMT services, ensuring geographic accessibility across the country.

Training and capacity building represented crucial components of the scale-up strategy. Over 2,000 healthcare workers received training on MMT provision, including physicians, nurses, pharmacists, and counselors. Training curricula covered clinical aspects of MMT, psychosocial counseling, record-keeping and monitoring, and addressing stigma and discrimination (111). Ongoing supervision and mentoring systems were established to support newly trained providers and ensure quality of care. Regular refresher training and updates on best practices were provided to maintain clinical competency across the expanding network.

Community engagement and addressing stigma were essential for program success and sustainability. Extensive advocacy efforts were conducted with local government officials, law enforcement agencies, healthcare providers, and community leaders to build support for MMT programs (112). People who use drugs and their family members were involved in advocacy efforts and program design, helping to address misconceptions and reduce stigma. Peer support programs were established to provide ongoing support for MMT clients and facilitate connections with healthcare and social services.

Integration with HIV prevention and treatment services enhanced the effectiveness of the MMT program and improved outcomes for clients. MMT sites provided HIV testing and counseling, antiretroviral therapy for HIV-positive clients, and prevention services including condom distribution and risk reduction counseling (113). This integration was particularly important given the high prevalence of HIV among people who inject drugs and the synergistic benefits of combining MMT with HIV treatment. Co-located services reduced barriers to access and improved retention in both MMT and HIV treatment.

Outcomes from Vietnam's MMT program have been impressive across multiple indicators. Treatment retention rates consistently exceed 80% at 12 months, comparing favorably with international standards and significantly higher than previous abstinence-based programs (114). HIV incidence among people who inject drugs decreased from an estimated 16% in 2009 to 4% in 2014, with the decline attributed primarily to increased MMT coverage and other harm reduction interventions. Self-reported illicit heroin use decreased by over 90% among MMT clients, while employment rates and family relationships improved substantially.

The sustainability of Vietnam's MMT program has been supported by increasing government funding and integration into routine healthcare financing mechanisms. While initial scale-up relied heavily on international donor funding, the Vietnamese government has gradually increased its financial contribution and committed to sustaining the program through domestic resources (115). User fees have been implemented in some settings to support program sustainability, though subsidies are maintained for the poorest clients. The integration of MMT into social health insurance schemes has improved long-term financial sustainability.

International recognition of Vietnam's MMT program has led to extensive knowledge sharing and technical cooperation with other countries in the region and globally. Vietnamese experts have provided technical assistance to MMT programs in other Southeast Asian countries, while the program has been featured as a best practice example in international forums (116). The success of Vietnam's program has contributed to regional commitments to scale up MMT and other evidence-based interventions for drug use disorders.

Key lessons from Vietnam's experience include the importance of political commitment and government leadership, the value of pilot programs for demonstrating feasibility and building support, the necessity of comprehensive training and capacity building, and the benefits of integrating MMT with other health services. The Vietnamese case demonstrates that rapid scale-up of complex treatment interventions is possible in resource-constrained settings with appropriate planning, implementation support, and stakeholder engagement (117).

## Prevention Approaches

### Universal Prevention Strategies

Universal prevention strategies target entire populations or broad demographic groups without regard to individual risk levels, aiming to reduce the overall incidence of substance use initiation and progression to problematic use. These approaches are based on the premise that substance use disorders are influenced by environmental, social, and policy factors that affect entire populations, making population-wide interventions potentially cost-effective despite lower effect sizes per individual (118). Universal prevention strategies include policy interventions (such as taxation and availability restrictions), educational programs, and community-wide initiatives aimed at changing social norms and environmental factors.

School-based substance use prevention programs represent the most widely implemented form of universal prevention, reaching millions of students globally through curricula designed to prevent or delay substance use initiation. Effective school-based programs typically include components addressing social resistance skills, normative beliefs about substance use, and broader life skills development (119). The Life Skills Training program, which teaches general personal and social skills along with substance-specific information and resistance skills, has demonstrated effectiveness in reducing tobacco, alcohol, and marijuana use in numerous randomized controlled trials across diverse populations.

Mass media campaigns represent another important universal prevention strategy, using television, radio, print, and digital media to change knowledge, attitudes, and behaviors related to substance use. Effective media campaigns typically feature high-quality, emotionally engaging messages that are broadcast with sufficient intensity and duration to achieve population-level exposure (120). The Truth campaign in the United States, which uses hard-hitting anti-tobacco industry messages rather than health-focused appeals, has been associated with significant reductions in youth smoking rates and has been replicated in other countries with similar effects.

Community-based prevention approaches aim to change environmental and social factors that influence substance use at the community level. These strategies may include community mobilization efforts, environmental policy changes, enforcement activities, and coalition building among diverse stakeholders (121). The Communities That Care prevention system, which uses epidemiological data to identify community-specific risk and protective factors and then implements evidence-based programs targeting those factors, has demonstrated effectiveness in reducing youth substance use across multiple communities.

### Selective Prevention for High-Risk Groups

Selective prevention strategies target subgroups of the population that are at elevated risk for substance use disorders due to biological, psychological, social, or environmental factors. These approaches recognize that risk for substance use disorders is not equally distributed across populations and that targeting high-risk groups may achieve greater prevention effects with more efficient resource allocation (122). High-risk groups may be defined by demographic characteristics (such as age, gender, or socioeconomic status), family factors (such as parental substance use or family conflict), peer influences, or community characteristics.

Family-based prevention programs represent one of the most effective forms of selective prevention, particularly for children and adolescents from families affected by parental substance use or other risk factors. The Strengthening Families Program, which provides parallel skills training for parents and children along with family activities, has demonstrated effectiveness in reducing substance use initiation and other problem behaviors among high-risk youth (123). Similar programs focusing on family communication, parenting skills, and family bonding have shown consistent effects across diverse populations and cultural contexts.

Workplace prevention programs target employed populations who may be at elevated risk for substance use problems due to occupational factors, stress, or workplace culture. Effective workplace programs typically combine policy components (such as substance use policies and employee assistance programs) with educational and screening components (124). Industries with particularly high rates of substance use, such as construction, mining, and hospitality, may benefit from intensive prevention efforts that address industry-specific risk factors and cultural norms.

Prevention programs for specific cultural or ethnic groups may be necessary to address unique risk factors and to ensure cultural appropriateness of prevention messages and strategies. Indigenous populations in many countries experience disproportionately high rates of substance use disorders due to historical trauma, cultural disruption, and ongoing marginalization (125). Culturally adapted prevention programs that incorporate traditional values, practices, and healing approaches while addressing specific risk factors have shown promise in several settings, though more research is needed to establish best practices.

### Indicated Prevention and Early Intervention

Indicated prevention strategies target individuals who are already showing early signs of substance use problems or who have initiated substance use but have not yet developed substance use disorders. These approaches represent the most intensive form of prevention and often blur the boundaries between prevention and treatment (126). Indicated prevention may be appropriate for individuals who have been identified through screening in healthcare, educational, or social service settings, or who have come to attention due to substance-related problems such as legal issues or academic difficulties.

Brief intervention approaches, originally developed for alcohol problems in healthcare settings, have been adapted for use with various substances and populations as indicated prevention strategies. These interventions typically involve one to four sessions focusing on providing feedback about substance use and related risks, enhancing motivation for change, and developing specific goals for reducing or eliminating substance use (127). Brief interventions delivered in emergency departments, primary care settings, and colleges have demonstrated effectiveness in reducing substance use among individuals with hazardous or harmful use patterns.

Motivational interviewing techniques are frequently used in indicated prevention to address ambivalence about changing substance use behaviors and to enhance intrinsic motivation for change. These approaches avoid confrontational or directive styles that may increase resistance and instead focus on exploring the individual's own reasons for change (128). Motivational interviewing can be delivered in individual or group formats and has been shown to be effective across diverse populations and substances.

Cognitive-behavioral approaches may be used in indicated prevention to help individuals develop skills for managing triggers, coping with stress, and avoiding high-risk situations. These interventions teach specific skills such as identifying and challenging thoughts that lead to substance use, developing alternative coping strategies, and planning for high-risk situations (129). While more intensive than typical prevention approaches, cognitive-behavioral interventions may be cost-effective for high-risk individuals who are likely to progress to more severe substance use disorders without intervention.

## Policy Responses and Human Rights

### Criminalization Versus Public Health Approaches

The global landscape of drug policy reflects a fundamental tension between criminalization and public health approaches, with most countries maintaining some combination of both paradigms. Traditional criminalization approaches emphasize law enforcement, criminal penalties, and incarceration as primary responses to drug use and possession, based on assumptions that punitive measures will deter drug use and related crimes (130). However, mounting evidence demonstrates that criminalization approaches often exacerbate rather than reduce drug-related harms while imposing significant human and economic costs on individuals, families, and communities.

The limitations of criminalization approaches are well-documented and include high rates of recidivism, criminalization of health conditions, disproportionate impacts on marginalized populations, barriers to accessing healthcare and social services, and enormous costs to criminal justice systems (131). In the United States, over 1.5 million people are arrested annually for drug-related offenses, with the majority being for simple possession rather than trafficking or dealing. Despite this massive enforcement effort, drug availability and use rates have remained stable or increased, while drug-related harms have escalated, particularly overdose deaths.

Public health approaches to drug policy emphasize treating substance use disorders as health conditions requiring medical and social interventions rather than criminal sanctions. These approaches prioritize harm reduction, treatment access, human rights protection, and evidence-based interventions over punishment and deterrence (132). Countries implementing public health approaches have generally achieved better outcomes in terms of drug-related mortality, infectious disease transmission, crime rates, and social integration compared to countries maintaining primarily criminalization approaches.

The evidence base for public health approaches has grown substantially over the past two decades, with systematic reviews and meta-analyses consistently demonstrating superior outcomes compared to criminalization approaches. Depenalization or decriminalization of drug possession has not been associated with increased drug use in most settings while reducing incarceration rates and improving access to health services (133). Countries such as Portugal, Switzerland, and the Netherlands have demonstrated that treating drug use as primarily a health issue can achieve significant reductions in drug-related harms while maintaining public order and safety.

### Human Rights and Substance Use

International human rights law establishes clear obligations for governments to respect, protect, and fulfill the human rights of people who use drugs, including the rights to health, life, liberty, security of person, and freedom from torture and cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment. However, people who use drugs face widespread human rights violations globally, including arbitrary detention, forced treatment, denial of healthcare, violence by law enforcement, and discrimination in employment, housing, and education (134). These violations are often perpetuated or enabled by drug laws and policies that criminalize drug use and stigmatize people who use drugs.

The right to health, as defined in international human rights law, includes access to timely, acceptable, and affordable healthcare of appropriate quality, including prevention, treatment, and care for substance use disorders. This right requires governments to ensure availability of evidence-based treatment options, remove barriers to healthcare access, and address discrimination in healthcare settings (135). Many countries violate this right by restricting access to opioid substitution therapy, criminalizing healthcare providers who serve people who use drugs, or maintaining policies that deter people from seeking treatment due to fear of legal consequences.

Compulsory drug treatment, practiced in many countries particularly in Asia and Latin America, represents a severe violation of human rights and contradicts evidence-based principles of effective treatment. These programs, which typically involve detention in custodial facilities without due process, have no evidence of effectiveness and often involve forced labor, physical abuse, and denial of medical care (136). The UN system has consistently called for the closure of compulsory treatment centers and their replacement with voluntary, evidence-based treatment services that respect human rights and dignity.

The death penalty for drug-related offenses, maintained by approximately 30 countries primarily in Asia and the Middle East, represents the most extreme violation of human rights in drug policy. International human rights law restricts the death penalty to the "most serious crimes" and drug-related offenses do not meet this threshold according to UN human rights bodies (137). The application of the death penalty for drug offenses is often characterized by racial, ethnic, and socioeconomic discrimination, with poor and marginalized populations being disproportionately affected.

### Criminal Justice and Health System Integration

The integration of criminal justice and health systems represents a promising approach for addressing substance use disorders among individuals involved in the criminal justice system, who experience disproportionately high rates of substance use disorders and related health problems. Approximately 60-80% of individuals in criminal justice settings meet criteria for substance use disorders, compared to 10-15% in the general population (138). This concentration of substance use disorders in criminal justice settings creates both challenges and opportunities for intervention.

Drug courts represent one of the most widely implemented models for integrating criminal justice and health responses to drug-related offenses. These specialized courts divert individuals with substance use disorders from traditional criminal justice processing to intensive supervision combined with mandated treatment and other services (139). Systematic reviews demonstrate that drug courts reduce recidivism by approximately 10-15% compared to traditional court processing, though effects vary significantly across programs and populations. The most effective drug courts combine intensive supervision with high-quality treatment services and use graduated sanctions and incentives to promote compliance.

Treatment in correctional settings represents another important opportunity for integrating criminal justice and health responses. Correctional facilities can provide access to healthcare and treatment services for populations that may have limited contact with community healthcare systems (140). However, the quality of substance use disorder treatment in correctional settings is often poor, with most facilities providing only abstinence-based programs despite strong evidence for medication-assisted treatment. The provision of opioid substitution therapy in correctional settings reduces post-release mortality and recidivism but remains controversial and limited in many jurisdictions.

Pre-arrest diversion programs allow law enforcement officers to refer individuals with substance use disorders to treatment services instead of arresting them for low-level drug-related offenses. These programs recognize that traditional arrest and prosecution may be counterproductive for individuals whose primary need is treatment rather than punishment (141). Successful diversion programs typically involve collaboration between law enforcement, healthcare providers, and social service agencies, with clear protocols for identifying appropriate individuals and ensuring access to services.

## Conclusion

Substance use disorders represent one of the most complex and persistent challenges in global public health, requiring comprehensive, evidence-based responses that address individual, community, and societal factors. The evidence reviewed in this chapter demonstrates that effective interventions exist across the spectrum from prevention to treatment to harm reduction, but implementation remains inadequate in most settings globally. The continued dominance of criminalization approaches in many countries, despite overwhelming evidence of their ineffectiveness and harmful consequences, represents a fundamental barrier to improving outcomes for people affected by substance use disorders.

The case studies presented illustrate the potential for achieving significant population-level improvements in substance use-related outcomes through comprehensive, evidence-based policy and program interventions. Australia's plain packaging policy demonstrates that innovative tobacco control measures can overcome industry opposition and achieve meaningful impacts on smoking behavior. Portugal's drug policy reform shows that treating drug use as primarily a health issue rather than a criminal justice issue can dramatically reduce drug-related harms while improving individual and social outcomes. Scotland's minimum unit pricing policy provides a model for addressing alcohol-related harms through targeted pricing interventions. Vietnam's methadone program scale-up demonstrates that effective treatment can be rapidly expanded even in resource-constrained settings with appropriate planning and implementation support.

The transition from criminalization to public health approaches represents a fundamental paradigm shift that requires sustained political leadership, evidence-based advocacy, and comprehensive system change. This transition involves not only changing laws and policies but also transforming attitudes and practices among healthcare providers, criminal justice personnel, policymakers, and the general public (142). The involvement of people with lived experience of substance use disorders in policy development and program implementation is essential for ensuring that interventions are acceptable, accessible, and effective.

The integration of substance use disorder interventions with broader health and social systems represents both an opportunity and a necessity for improving outcomes and efficiency. The high rates of comorbidity between substance use disorders and other mental health conditions, infectious diseases, and chronic diseases require coordinated, integrated approaches that address multiple needs simultaneously (143). Similarly, the concentration of substance use disorders among marginalized and disadvantaged populations requires attention to broader social determinants of health including poverty, discrimination, trauma, and social exclusion.

Looking forward, several key priorities emerge for advancing global responses to substance use disorders. First, the scale-up of evidence-based treatment and harm reduction interventions remains urgently needed, particularly in low- and middle-income countries where access is most limited. This scale-up requires sustained financial investment, capacity building, and technical assistance, along with policy and regulatory changes that enable implementation of effective interventions.

Second, the development and implementation of comprehensive prevention strategies that address substance use disorders across the life course and across different populations is essential for reducing the future burden of these conditions. This includes strengthening universal prevention approaches such as tobacco and alcohol policy interventions while also developing targeted prevention strategies for high-risk populations.

Third, the transformation of drug policies from criminalization to public health approaches represents a critical priority for improving both individual and population outcomes. This transformation requires evidence-based advocacy, political leadership, and sustained efforts to change attitudes and practices across multiple sectors and institutions.

Finally, the integration of human rights principles into all aspects of substance use disorder policy and programming is essential for ensuring that interventions are effective, ethical, and sustainable. This includes addressing stigma and discrimination, ensuring meaningful participation of people who use drugs in policy and program development, and protecting the rights and dignity of all people affected by substance use disorders.
