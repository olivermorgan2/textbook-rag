# Chapter 28: Health Technology and Innovation

## 28.1 Introduction to Health Technology and Innovation

Health technology encompasses the application of organized knowledge and skills in the form of devices, medicines, vaccines, procedures, and systems developed to solve health problems and improve quality of life (1). In the contemporary global health landscape, technological innovation has emerged as a transformative force with potential to accelerate progress toward Universal Health Coverage and the Sustainable Development Goals (2). From mobile phones enabling community health workers in remote villages to artificial intelligence algorithms diagnosing diseases from medical images, health technology is reshaping how healthcare is delivered, accessed, and experienced across diverse settings (3).

The World Health Organization defines health technology broadly to include any intervention used to promote health, prevent, diagnose, or treat disease, or for rehabilitation and long-term care (1). This encompasses not only sophisticated medical devices and digital platforms but also organizational and supportive systems within which healthcare is delivered. Understanding this breadth is essential, as effective health technology deployment requires attention not just to the technology itself but to the ecosystem in which it operates.

The evolution of health technology in global health has accelerated dramatically in recent decades. While high-income countries have long benefited from technological advances, the digital revolution has created unprecedented opportunities for low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) to leapfrog traditional development pathways (4). Mobile phone penetration exceeding 90% in many LMICs, combined with decreasing costs of computing and connectivity, has democratized access to technologies that were previously available only to wealthy populations (5). However, this technological promise must be tempered by recognition of persistent inequities in technology access, the importance of context-appropriate solutions, and the need for robust governance frameworks to ensure technologies serve public health goals.

This chapter examines the landscape of health technology and innovation through a global health lens, emphasizing principles of appropriate technology, equitable access, and evidence-based implementation. We explore digital health applications, health information systems, medical devices for low-resource settings, artificial intelligence, and the frameworks needed to assess, regulate, and implement health technologies effectively across diverse contexts.

## 28.2 Digital Health: Foundations and Applications

Digital health represents the convergence of digital technologies with health, healthcare, living, and society to enhance the efficiency of healthcare delivery and make medicine more personalized and precise (6). The World Health Organization's Global Strategy on Digital Health 2020-2025 defines digital health as encompassing eHealth, as well as developing areas such as the use of advanced computing sciences, big data, genomics, and artificial intelligence (7).

### Telemedicine and Telehealth

Telemedicine involves the delivery of healthcare services using telecommunications technology to connect patients with providers across distances (8). The distinction between telemedicine (clinical services) and telehealth (broader health services including education and administration) reflects the expanding scope of remote health delivery. Synchronous telemedicine involves real-time interactions through video consultations, while asynchronous approaches use store-and-forward technologies for specialist consultations.

Evidence supporting telemedicine effectiveness has grown substantially. A systematic review of 93 randomized controlled trials found telemedicine interventions were as effective as face-to-face care for chronic disease management, with particular benefits for diabetes and cardiovascular conditions (9). The COVID-19 pandemic catalyzed unprecedented telemedicine expansion globally. In the United States, telehealth visits increased from approximately 1% to 43.5% of Medicare primary care visits between February and April 2020 (10). Similarly, India's eSanjeevani telemedicine platform recorded over 100 million consultations by 2023, demonstrating the scalability of digital health solutions when supported by policy frameworks (11).

However, telemedicine implementation faces significant challenges in resource-limited settings. A multi-country study across Africa identified infrastructure limitations, regulatory uncertainty, and health worker capacity as primary barriers (12). Successful programs have addressed these through hybrid models combining in-person and remote consultations, investment in connectivity infrastructure, and task-shifting approaches that leverage community health workers as telemedicine facilitators.

### Mobile Health (mHealth)

Mobile health harnesses the ubiquity of mobile devices to deliver health interventions, collect data, and facilitate communication between patients and providers (13). The mHealth landscape encompasses diverse applications including SMS-based reminder systems for medication adherence, mobile applications for health education, and sophisticated diagnostic support tools.

Evidence on mHealth effectiveness continues to mature. A Cochrane systematic review of mobile phone messaging for preventive healthcare found moderate-certainty evidence that text messaging interventions improve immunization rates and clinic attendance (14). Another systematic review of mHealth interventions for maternal and child health in LMICs identified positive effects on antenatal care attendance, skilled birth attendance, and childhood immunization coverage, though effect sizes varied considerably across contexts (15).

The success of Kenya's M-Tiba platform illustrates mHealth's potential to address health financing challenges. By enabling mobile money payments for healthcare and creating digital health savings wallets, M-Tiba has facilitated over 5 million outpatient visits while generating data to inform health system planning (16). Similarly, Bangladesh's Aponjon mHealth service delivers targeted health information to pregnant women and new mothers via mobile phones, reaching over 2 million registered users and demonstrating improvements in health-seeking behaviors (17).

### Electronic Health Records

Electronic health records (EHRs) serve as foundational infrastructure for digital health systems, enabling documentation, information sharing, and clinical decision support. The benefits of well-implemented EHR systems include improved care coordination, reduced medical errors, and enhanced population health management capabilities (18). However, EHR implementation in LMICs presents distinct challenges related to infrastructure, interoperability, and sustainability.

The OpenMRS community exemplifies successful open-source approaches to EHR implementation in resource-limited settings. Originally developed for HIV treatment programs, OpenMRS now serves as the backbone for health information systems in over 40 countries, supporting care for millions of patients (19). The platform's open-source architecture enables local adaptation while maintaining interoperability standards, and its implementation in Rwanda has been integral to that country's digital health transformation.

Interoperability remains a critical challenge globally. The adoption of health data standards such as HL7 FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) offers promise for enabling data exchange across systems, though implementation requires sustained investment in technical capacity and governance frameworks (20).

## 28.3 Health Information Systems

Health information systems (HIS) provide the foundation for evidence-based decision-making in health systems, encompassing the collection, processing, reporting, and use of information needed for improving health service effectiveness and efficiency (21). Strong health information systems are essential for disease surveillance, resource allocation, performance monitoring, and accountability.

### Health Management Information Systems

Routine health management information systems (HMIS) collect data from health facilities on services delivered, resources used, and health outcomes. The quality of HMIS data varies substantially across countries, with many LMICs facing challenges including incomplete reporting, data entry errors, parallel reporting systems, and limited data use culture (22).

The District Health Information Software 2 (DHIS2) has emerged as the dominant platform for routine health information management globally. Developed initially in South Africa through the Health Information Systems Program (HISP), DHIS2 is now the national HMIS platform in over 100 countries, covering an estimated 2.4 billion people (23). The platform's success reflects several key features: open-source licensing that reduces costs and dependency, flexibility enabling adaptation to local needs, and a global community of implementers providing technical support and innovation.

A multi-country evaluation of DHIS2 implementation identified critical success factors including strong governmental ownership, sustained capacity building, attention to data quality improvement, and cultivation of data use culture (24). Countries that have achieved mature DHIS2 implementations demonstrate substantially improved reporting completeness and timeliness, though translating improved data availability into better decision-making requires complementary investments in analytical capacity and organizational culture change.

### Disease Surveillance Systems

Effective disease surveillance is essential for detecting outbreaks, monitoring disease trends, and informing public health response. Traditional surveillance approaches based on passive reporting from health facilities are increasingly supplemented by digital approaches enabling more timely and comprehensive disease intelligence (25).

Event-based surveillance systems using digital technologies can dramatically reduce time to outbreak detection. ProMED-mail and HealthMap pioneered approaches to aggregate and analyze diverse information sources for early warning, while more recent systems incorporate machine learning for automated signal detection (26). During the COVID-19 pandemic, countries with strong digital surveillance infrastructure were better positioned to detect and respond to emerging variants and transmission patterns.

Integrated Disease Surveillance and Response (IDSR) strategies promoted by WHO Africa region exemplify efforts to strengthen surveillance capacity comprehensively. A systematic review of IDSR implementation across Africa found improvements in surveillance indicators but persistent challenges in laboratory confirmation, data quality, and response capacity (27).

### Clinical Decision Support Systems

Clinical decision support systems (CDSS) use patient data and clinical knowledge to generate case-specific advice supporting clinical decision-making. These systems range from simple alert systems for drug interactions to sophisticated diagnostic support tools incorporating artificial intelligence (28).

Evidence on CDSS effectiveness in LMICs is growing. A systematic review of CDSS in sub-Saharan Africa found that most evaluated systems demonstrated improvements in adherence to clinical guidelines and health worker performance, though effects on patient outcomes were less consistently demonstrated (29). Implementation challenges include integration with clinical workflows, maintenance of current clinical content, and ensuring systems enhance rather than burden healthcare workers.

## 28.4 Medical Devices for Low-Resource Settings

Medical devices appropriate for low-resource settings must be designed considering the unique constraints and requirements of these environments. The concept of appropriate technology, originally articulated by economist E.F. Schumacher, emphasizes technology suitable to the social, economic, and environmental conditions where it is used (30). In global health, this translates to devices that are affordable, durable, easy to use and maintain, and adapted to local infrastructure limitations.

### Principles of Appropriate Technology

Design principles for low-resource settings include simplicity of use requiring minimal training, robustness to environmental conditions including heat, humidity, and dust, minimal infrastructure requirements for power and water, affordability considering total cost of ownership, and local serviceability (31). The WHO Compendium of Innovative Health Technologies for Low-Resource Settings provides examples and guidance for appropriate medical devices (32).

Frugal innovation describes approaches that achieve more with less, creating products and services that are affordable, robust, and focused on core functionality (33). Distinguished from simply low-cost versions of existing products, frugal innovation often involves fundamental rethinking of design approaches. The GE Vscan pocket ultrasound exemplifies frugal innovation, providing essential ultrasound capability at a fraction of traditional system costs while being portable and battery-operated (34).

Reverse innovation occurs when innovations developed for resource-limited settings are subsequently adopted in high-income countries, recognizing that constraints can drive creativity with broader applicability (35). Examples include low-cost ECG devices developed in India subsequently finding markets in rural America, and portable diagnostic devices designed for African settings being deployed in emergency departments globally.

### Point-of-Care Diagnostics

Point-of-care testing (POCT) enables diagnostic evaluation at or near the site of patient care, reducing turnaround time and enabling same-visit clinical decision-making (36). For many conditions in resource-limited settings, POCT represents the only feasible diagnostic approach given limited laboratory infrastructure.

Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) for malaria transformed malaria case management globally. Prior to RDT availability, presumptive treatment based on clinical symptoms was standard practice, leading to substantial antimalarial overuse. RDT introduction enabled parasitological confirmation at point of care, improving diagnostic accuracy and enabling more rational use of antimalarials (37). By 2021, over 400 million malaria RDTs were distributed globally through public sector programs.

The GeneXpert MTB/RIF platform represents a more sophisticated POCT technology that has transformed tuberculosis diagnosis. This automated molecular diagnostic system detects tuberculosis bacteria and rifampicin resistance within two hours using a cartridge-based format suitable for decentralized settings (38). Negotiated pricing through the Stop TB Partnership has progressively reduced cartridge costs for high-burden countries, though challenges remain regarding instrument placement, cartridge supply chains, and integration with clinical management pathways.

### Design Considerations and Challenges

The design of medical devices for global health must consider the entire lifecycle including procurement, deployment, operation, maintenance, and eventual disposal. Studies of medical device donation programs have documented high rates of equipment non-functionality, often due to lack of spare parts, consumables, maintenance expertise, or appropriate infrastructure (39).

Human-centered design approaches engage end users throughout the development process to ensure devices meet actual needs and can be effectively used in context. The PATH organization has pioneered application of human-centered design to global health products, demonstrating improved adoption and effectiveness when user perspectives are systematically incorporated (40).

Supply chain considerations are critical for devices requiring consumables. Ensuring reliable, affordable supply of test cartridges, reagents, and other consumables is often more challenging than initial device procurement. Successful programs have addressed this through pooled procurement mechanisms, supply chain strengthening, and selection of devices with minimal consumable requirements.

## 28.5 Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning in Health

Artificial intelligence (AI) encompasses computer systems able to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence, while machine learning (ML) refers to systems that improve through experience without explicit programming (41). Healthcare applications of AI/ML have proliferated across diagnostic imaging, clinical decision support, drug discovery, and health system optimization.

### Diagnostic AI Applications

Medical imaging represents the most mature domain for AI diagnostic applications. Deep learning algorithms have demonstrated performance comparable to or exceeding human experts across multiple imaging modalities including radiology, pathology, dermatology, and ophthalmology (42). A landmark 2016 study demonstrated that deep learning algorithms could detect diabetic retinopathy from retinal photographs with sensitivity and specificity exceeding 90%, comparable to ophthalmologist performance (43).

The regulatory landscape for AI diagnostics has evolved rapidly. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration has authorized hundreds of AI-enabled medical devices, including the first autonomous AI diagnostic system approved for marketing without physician oversight (44). This device, designed for diabetic retinopathy screening in primary care settings, illustrates the potential for AI to enable task-shifting and extend diagnostic capacity.

Applications in LMICs are expanding. AI-powered chest X-ray analysis for tuberculosis screening has shown promise for increasing screening coverage and enabling community-based detection (45). Similarly, smartphone-based AI applications for skin lesion analysis could extend dermatology expertise to settings without specialist availability. However, algorithm performance can vary across populations due to differences in disease presentation, imaging quality, and representation in training datasets.

### Predictive Analytics and Risk Stratification

Machine learning approaches enable prediction of patient outcomes, disease progression, and resource utilization using patterns in clinical and administrative data (46). Applications include early warning systems for clinical deterioration, prediction of readmission risk, and identification of patients likely to benefit from intensive interventions.

While predictive analytics applications have proliferated, evidence of impact on patient outcomes remains limited. A systematic review of prediction models for clinical deterioration found that while numerous models existed with reasonable discrimination, few had been evaluated for impact on patient outcomes when implemented in clinical practice (47). Implementation requires attention not just to algorithm performance but to integration with clinical workflows, interpretability for clinicians, and appropriate action pathways for identified patients.

### Natural Language Processing

Natural language processing (NLP) enables extraction of structured information from clinical text, supporting applications including automated coding, clinical documentation, and identification of adverse events from medical records (48). In global health contexts, NLP applications include extraction of surveillance signals from news and social media, and chatbots providing health information and triage support.

### Algorithm Development and Validation

Rigorous development and validation of AI/ML algorithms is essential for safe and effective deployment. Best practices include clear problem definition, representative training data, appropriate performance metrics, external validation in diverse populations, and prospective evaluation in clinical settings (49). The TRIPOD-ML guidelines provide standards for transparent reporting of prediction model development and validation studies.

Concerns about algorithmic bias have grown as AI applications expand. Algorithms trained on data predominantly from certain populations may perform poorly in underrepresented groups. A prominent example involved a commercial algorithm used by U.S. health systems to identify patients needing care management, which was found to systematically underestimate illness severity in Black patients due to use of healthcare costs as a proxy for health needs (50). Addressing algorithmic bias requires diverse training data, explicit evaluation across subgroups, and ongoing monitoring of real-world performance.

**Case Study 28.1: Rwanda's Digital Health Transformation**

Rwanda exemplifies how strong political commitment combined with open-source technology can enable comprehensive digital health transformation in a low-income setting (51). Following the 1994 genocide, Rwanda rebuilt its health system with deliberate integration of technology to extend reach and improve quality.

The government's digital health strategy encompasses multiple interconnected elements. RapidSMS enables community health workers to report pregnancies, births, and deaths via mobile phone, creating a real-time monitoring system for maternal and child health (52). When a community health worker sends a message reporting a home birth, the system automatically generates alerts for postnatal visit follow-up. Analysis of Rwanda's community health data system demonstrated significant associations between program implementation and increased facility delivery rates.

OpenMRS provides the electronic medical records backbone for Rwanda's health facilities, initially deployed for HIV treatment programs and progressively expanded to primary care services (53). The system enables tracking of patients across facilities, clinical decision support for HIV treatment, and generation of reports for program management. By 2020, over 900 health facilities were using electronic medical records systems.

Critical success factors included sustained government commitment with digital health embedded in national strategy, investment in infrastructure including connectivity and power, progressive capacity building of health workforce, and deliberate selection of open-source platforms enabling local adaptation (54). Challenges remain, including ensuring interoperability across multiple platforms, sustaining systems beyond donor funding, and translating data availability into improved decision-making.

**Case Study 28.2: GeneXpert for Tuberculosis Diagnosis**

The Xpert MTB/RIF diagnostic system developed by Cepheid represents a paradigm shift in tuberculosis diagnosis, enabling same-day molecular diagnosis at point of care (55). The platform uses an automated real-time PCR system in a self-contained cartridge format requiring minimal technical expertise to operate.

Following WHO endorsement in 2010, global scale-up proceeded rapidly, facilitated by negotiated concessional pricing for high-burden countries (56). The Stop TB Partnership and UNITAID negotiated prices that progressively decreased from initial levels, though costs remain substantially higher than smear microscopy. By 2023, the technology was deployed in over 130 countries with over 28,000 instruments installed globally.

South Africa led initial national scale-up, replacing smear microscopy as the primary diagnostic test and deploying instruments across all districts (38). Evaluation studies demonstrated dramatic reductions in time to diagnosis and treatment initiation, improved detection of rifampicin resistance, and increased case detection among HIV-positive patients with paucibacillary disease. However, translation to improved patient outcomes proved more challenging, with implementation studies revealing bottlenecks in subsequent steps of the care cascade.

Key lessons include the importance of health system integration beyond the diagnostic test itself, requiring attention to specimen transport, results communication, and treatment initiation pathways. The technology's success also demonstrated the power of advocacy for access pricing, enabling scale-up that would have been impossible at initial commercial pricing. Ongoing innovation has continued to improve the platform, with the Xpert Ultra version offering improved sensitivity particularly for HIV-associated TB and paucibacillary disease.

## 28.6 Health Technology Assessment

Health technology assessment (HTA) provides a systematic approach to evaluating health technologies, examining their properties, effects, and impacts to inform decision-making on coverage, reimbursement, and clinical guidelines (57). As health system resources are finite, HTA enables evidence-informed prioritization of technologies offering greatest value.

### HTA Frameworks and Methodology

Comprehensive HTA examines multiple domains including clinical effectiveness, safety, cost-effectiveness, ethical considerations, organizational implications, and social impact (58). The assessment process typically begins with horizon scanning to identify emerging technologies, followed by topic selection based on policy relevance and potential impact.

Clinical effectiveness assessment draws on systematic review methodology to synthesize available evidence on health outcomes. Where direct evidence is limited, network meta-analysis and modeling approaches may be employed. Safety assessment examines adverse effects and risks, drawing on clinical trial data, observational studies, and post-market surveillance.

Economic evaluation, typically cost-effectiveness or cost-utility analysis, examines whether the health gains from a technology justify its costs compared to alternatives. Quality-adjusted life years (QALYs) provide a standardized outcome measure enabling comparison across different technologies and conditions (59). Cost-effectiveness thresholds, while controversial, provide benchmarks against which technologies can be assessed, with common thresholds ranging from 1-3 times GDP per capita in LMICs to fixed thresholds such as £20,000-30,000 per QALY in the UK.

### HTA in Low- and Middle-Income Countries

HTA capacity in LMICs has grown substantially, driven by recognition that constrained resources make evidence-informed prioritization especially critical (60). The International Network of Agencies for Health Technology Assessment (INAHTA) and HTAsiaLink bring together HTA agencies for knowledge sharing and capacity development.

Adaptation of HTA methods for LMIC contexts requires attention to several considerations. Evidence from high-income country populations may have limited transferability. Local cost data may be unavailable or unreliable. Implementation feasibility and health system capacity considerations may be paramount. Equity implications warrant explicit assessment given marked health disparities (61).

Thailand's Health Intervention and Technology Assessment Program (HITAP) demonstrates mature HTA integration into health policy. HITAP assessments inform the National List of Essential Medicines and the Universal Coverage benefit package, with demonstrated influence on pricing negotiations and coverage decisions (62). The program has also pioneered methods for incorporating equity considerations and stakeholder engagement into HTA processes.

**Case Study 28.3: AI-Powered Diabetic Retinopathy Screening**

The development and deployment of AI systems for diabetic retinopathy screening illustrates both the promise and challenges of translating AI research into clinical practice (43). Diabetic retinopathy is a leading cause of preventable blindness, yet screening coverage remains inadequate globally due to limited specialist capacity.

Deep learning algorithms trained on large datasets of retinal photographs have demonstrated diagnostic accuracy comparable to ophthalmologists for detecting referable diabetic retinopathy (63). Google Health's algorithm, trained on over 100,000 images, achieved 90% sensitivity and 98% specificity in validation studies across US and Indian populations. The IDx-DR system became the first autonomous AI diagnostic approved by FDA, authorized for diabetic retinopathy screening in primary care settings without ophthalmologist oversight (44).

Implementation experience reveals important lessons. In Thailand's national diabetic retinopathy screening program, AI-assisted screening increased screening coverage substantially while maintaining diagnostic accuracy (64). However, integration into clinical workflows required careful attention to camera image quality, referral pathway development, and patient communication about AI-assisted diagnosis.

Algorithm performance can vary across populations due to differences in retinal pigmentation, camera types, and disease presentation. Studies in African populations have demonstrated algorithm performance degradation, highlighting the importance of diverse training data and local validation (65). Ethical frameworks for AI-assisted diagnosis must address accountability when errors occur, patient autonomy and informed consent, and ensuring AI benefits reach underserved populations.

## 28.7 Innovation Ecosystems and Technology Transfer

Effective health technology innovation requires functioning ecosystems encompassing research institutions, industry, government, and civil society working in concert from discovery through development to delivery (66). Understanding these ecosystems and the mechanisms of technology transfer is essential for ensuring innovations reach those who need them.

### Research and Development Landscape

Global health R&D investment has historically neglected diseases primarily affecting poor populations. The "10/90 gap" terminology highlighted that only 10% of health research funding addressed conditions accounting for 90% of the global disease burden (67). While product development partnerships and global funding initiatives have partially addressed this market failure, significant gaps remain for neglected tropical diseases and conditions primarily affecting LMICs.

Product development partnerships (PDPs) have emerged as effective mechanisms for neglected disease R&D, combining public funding with pharmaceutical industry capabilities (68). Organizations such as DNDi (Drugs for Neglected Diseases initiative), FIND (Foundation for Innovative New Diagnostics), and PATH have successfully developed and delivered products including antimalarials, diagnostics, and vaccines for LMIC markets.

### Technology Transfer and Access

Technology transfer involves the movement of knowledge and technology from one context to another, enabling local production and adaptation (69). For health technologies, transfer mechanisms include licensing agreements, joint ventures, capacity building partnerships, and open-source approaches.

The Medicines Patent Pool exemplifies innovative approaches to facilitating technology transfer for essential medicines. By negotiating voluntary licenses from patent holders and sublicensing to generic manufacturers, the pool has enabled production of affordable antiretrovirals and hepatitis C treatments in LMICs (70).

### Intellectual Property Considerations

Intellectual property protection, primarily through patents, provides incentives for innovation by enabling innovators to recoup R&D investments. However, patent protection can also impede access to essential technologies in resource-limited settings (71). Flexibilities within the TRIPS Agreement, including compulsory licensing provisions, provide mechanisms to address access barriers, though their use remains politically challenging.

Open-source approaches offer alternatives to traditional proprietary models. Open-source software such as OpenMRS and DHIS2 have demonstrated that community-based development can produce robust, adaptable health technologies at scale. Extending open principles to hardware and diagnostics presents greater challenges but holds promise for increasing local production capacity (72).

## 28.8 Digital Divides and Equity in Health Technology

Despite remarkable progress in technology access globally, significant divides persist along geographic, socioeconomic, and demographic lines (73). These digital divides risk exacerbating existing health inequities if technologies primarily benefit already-advantaged populations.

### Dimensions of the Digital Divide

Infrastructure disparities remain foundational. While mobile phone penetration exceeds 90% in most countries, meaningful connectivity enabling health applications requires reliable networks and affordable data (5). Rural populations, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, face persistent connectivity gaps. Electricity access constrains deployment of technologies requiring reliable power.

Affordability extends beyond device costs to encompass data costs, which in many African countries consume substantial portions of monthly income. Studies demonstrate that high data costs constrain digital health utilization even when platforms are technically accessible (74).

Digital literacy encompasses the skills needed to effectively use digital technologies. Health literacy intersects with digital literacy in health applications, as users must navigate both technological interfaces and health information. Gender disparities in digital literacy are pronounced in many settings, with women facing barriers including lower education levels, cultural restrictions on technology use, and less disposable income for devices and data (75).

### Strategies for Equitable Access

Technology leapfrogging describes the potential for LMICs to bypass intermediate technological stages, adopting advanced solutions directly (4). Mobile banking in Kenya, where M-Pesa enabled financial inclusion without traditional banking infrastructure, exemplifies this potential. In health, similar leapfrogging opportunities exist through mobile-first approaches, cloud-based systems, and solutions designed specifically for resource-limited contexts.

Design for equity requires deliberate attention throughout the technology development process. This includes engaging underserved populations in design research, ensuring interfaces accommodate varying literacy levels, and testing explicitly in marginalized populations. The concept of "digital health equity" frameworks has emerged to guide systematic consideration of equity throughout digital health programs (76).

Policy interventions can address structural barriers to equitable access. Universal service obligations requiring telecommunications providers to extend coverage to underserved areas, subsidized connectivity for health facilities, and digital literacy programs integrated with health services represent complementary approaches.

**Case Study 28.4: DHIS2 and Global Health Information Infrastructure**

The District Health Information Software 2 (DHIS2) demonstrates how open-source platforms and global communities of practice can build health information infrastructure at scale (23). Originating from the Health Information Systems Program (HISP) at the University of Oslo, DHIS2 has become the world's largest health management information system, serving as the national platform in over 100 countries.

DHIS2's design principles prioritize flexibility, enabling adaptation to diverse country contexts while maintaining core functionality (77). Countries can configure data elements, indicators, organizational structures, and reporting formats to match local requirements. This configurability, combined with open-source licensing that eliminates licensing costs, has driven widespread adoption.

The platform's evolution illustrates iterative development responding to user needs. Originally focused on aggregate routine data, DHIS2 has expanded to encompass individual-level tracker data, enabling patient-level longitudinal tracking for programs including immunization, HIV treatment, and tuberculosis (78). During the COVID-19 pandemic, DHIS2 was rapidly adapted to support case-based surveillance, vaccine tracking, and laboratory management, with COVID-19 packages deployed in over 60 countries within months.

Critical success factors include the global HISP network providing implementation support and capacity building, deliberate cultivation of communities of practice for knowledge sharing, and WHO endorsement strengthening government adoption (24). Sustainability remains an ongoing challenge, as countries must develop local capacity for system administration, adaptation, and data use rather than remaining dependent on external technical assistance.

## 28.9 Regulatory Frameworks and Governance

Effective governance of health technologies requires regulatory frameworks that ensure safety and effectiveness while enabling innovation and access (79). The regulatory landscape is evolving rapidly to address novel technologies including software as a medical device, AI-based diagnostics, and digital therapeutics.

### Medical Device Regulation

Medical device regulatory systems vary substantially across countries in structure, capacity, and stringency. Mature regulatory systems including the US FDA, European Medicines Agency, and regulatory authorities in Japan, Canada, and Australia conduct rigorous premarket assessment. Many LMICs lack robust medical device regulation, creating reliance on approvals from reference authorities (80).

The WHO Global Model Regulatory Framework for Medical Devices provides guidance for countries developing regulatory capacity. Regional harmonization initiatives including the African Medicines Regulatory Harmonisation program and ASEAN medical device harmonization seek to pool regulatory capacity and reduce duplication.

### Digital Health Regulation

Digital health technologies challenge traditional regulatory approaches designed for physical medical devices. The boundary between consumer wellness applications and regulated medical devices is often unclear. Software updates can change device functionality after initial approval. AI-based technologies may evolve continuously through learning from new data (81).

Regulatory approaches are adapting to these challenges. The FDA's Digital Health Software Precertification Program explores approaches that evaluate organizational capabilities rather than individual products. The EU Medical Device Regulation 2017/745 brings software as a medical device more clearly within regulatory scope while introducing provisions for post-market surveillance.

### AI-Specific Regulatory Considerations

AI-based health technologies present distinct regulatory challenges including the "black box" nature of many algorithms, potential for performance drift, and need for ongoing monitoring (82). Regulatory guidance increasingly requires documentation of training data characteristics, algorithm validation across subpopulations, and post-deployment monitoring plans.

The FDA's action plan for AI/ML-based Software as a Medical Device outlines a total product lifecycle approach recognizing that AI systems may continuously learn and adapt. The EU's proposed AI Act would establish risk-based requirements for AI systems including those in healthcare, with high-risk applications subject to conformity assessment requirements.

## 28.10 Implementation and Scale-Up

Translating health technology innovations from pilot projects to sustainable scale requires deliberate attention to implementation processes and health system integration. Implementation science provides frameworks for understanding and facilitating this translation (83).

### Implementation Science Frameworks

The Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research (CFIR) identifies constructs across five domains influencing implementation: intervention characteristics, outer setting, inner setting, individual characteristics, and implementation process (84). Systematic attention to each domain improves prospects for successful implementation.

The RE-AIM framework (Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, Implementation, Maintenance) provides a structure for evaluating technology programs comprehensively, attending not just to efficacy but to real-world reach and sustainability (85). Programs with high efficacy but limited reach or sustainability will have minimal population impact.

### Technology Adoption

The Technology Acceptance Model identifies perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use as primary determinants of user acceptance (86). Health worker acceptance is frequently the critical bottleneck in health technology implementation, as technologies that increase workload or do not integrate with existing practices face resistance regardless of technical capability.

Successful implementation strategies address user acceptance through participatory design processes, adequate training and support, attention to workflow integration, and demonstration of clear benefits to users. Change management approaches including identifying champions, providing ongoing support, and celebrating early wins facilitate organizational adoption.

### Sustainability and Institutionalization

Moving from project-based implementation to institutionalized systems requires embedding technologies within organizational structures and financing mechanisms. This includes transitioning from external to domestic funding, developing local technical capacity, and integrating technology governance within health system management structures (87).

## 28.11 Ethics in Health Technology

Ethical considerations permeate health technology from design through implementation. As technologies become more sophisticated and pervasive, attending to ethical dimensions is increasingly critical for ensuring technologies serve human wellbeing.

### Algorithmic Bias and Fairness

AI/ML algorithms can perpetuate or amplify existing biases if training data reflect historical inequities or if performance varies across population subgroups (88). Addressing algorithmic bias requires diverse training data, explicit fairness criteria in algorithm development, and disaggregated performance evaluation. Ongoing monitoring of deployed algorithms is essential as population characteristics and data quality may shift over time.

### Data Privacy and Security

Digital health systems collect sensitive personal information requiring robust privacy protections. Data governance frameworks must address consent, data minimization, security measures, and individual rights of access and correction (89). The EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) has influenced global approaches to health data protection, though implementation in resource-limited settings faces capacity constraints.

### Clinical Accountability

When AI systems contribute to clinical decisions, questions arise regarding liability when errors occur. Frameworks for AI accountability in healthcare must clarify the respective responsibilities of developers, deploying institutions, and clinicians (90). Transparency regarding AI system capabilities and limitations supports appropriate human oversight.

### Technology and Equity

Ensuring health technologies advance rather than undermine equity requires deliberate attention throughout development and deployment. Equity-focused approaches assess who benefits from technologies, who may be excluded or harmed, and what interventions can address disparities. Universal design principles that create technologies accessible to all users, including those with disabilities and limited literacy, support equitable access (91).

## 28.12 Future Directions

The health technology landscape continues to evolve rapidly, with emerging technologies presenting both opportunities and challenges for global health.

### Emerging Technologies

Advances in genomics enable increasingly personalized approaches to prevention and treatment, though equitable access to precision medicine remains a significant challenge given cost and infrastructure requirements (92). Wearable devices and remote monitoring technologies create possibilities for continuous health surveillance, though privacy implications require careful attention.

Three-dimensional printing enables local production of medical devices and prosthetics, potentially transforming supply chains for resource-limited settings. Blockchain technologies offer potential for secure health records and supply chain management, though applications remain largely experimental.

### Preparing for Technological Change

Health systems must develop adaptive capacity to absorb beneficial technologies while managing associated risks. This requires investment in human capital, including training health workers for evolving technological environments. Governance frameworks must evolve to address emerging technologies while avoiding regulatory approaches that stifle innovation.

The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated both the potential and limitations of health technology. Digital platforms enabled continuity of some services during lockdowns, while surveillance technologies contributed to pandemic response. Yet the pandemic also revealed persistent digital divides, as populations without connectivity were further marginalized. Learning from this experience should inform investments in health technology infrastructure that promote resilience and equity.

## Conclusion

Health technology and innovation offer tremendous potential to accelerate progress toward global health goals, extending the reach of health services, improving quality of care, and enabling more efficient resource utilization. Digital health applications including telemedicine, mHealth, and electronic health records are transforming healthcare delivery across diverse settings. Health information systems provide the foundation for evidence-based decision-making, with platforms such as DHIS2 demonstrating the potential of open-source approaches to build infrastructure at scale. Appropriate technology principles and frugal innovation enable development of medical devices suited to resource-limited settings, while artificial intelligence offers new capabilities for diagnosis, prediction, and decision support.

Realizing this potential requires attention to implementation processes, equity considerations, and governance frameworks. Health technology assessment provides systematic approaches for evaluating technologies to inform evidence-based decisions. Regulatory frameworks must evolve to address novel technologies while ensuring safety and effectiveness. Implementation science frameworks guide translation from innovation to impact at scale. Throughout, ethical considerations including algorithmic bias, data privacy, and clinical accountability require deliberate attention.

The digital divide represents both a challenge and an opportunity. While disparities in technology access risk exacerbating health inequities, technology leapfrogging and deliberate design for equity can ensure innovations reach marginalized populations. Building equitable health technology ecosystems requires investment in infrastructure, capacity, and governance alongside the technologies themselves.

As health technologies continue to evolve, health systems must develop adaptive capacity to absorb beneficial innovations while managing risks. The ultimate measure of success is not technological sophistication but contribution to improved health outcomes and reduced health inequities across all populations.
