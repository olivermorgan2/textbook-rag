## Introduction

Global health research encompasses the systematic generation, synthesis, and translation of evidence to improve health outcomes across diverse populations and settings. This chapter examines the methodological, ethical, and practical foundations of research in global health contexts, emphasizing the distinctive challenges and opportunities that arise when conducting research across varied economic, cultural, and health system environments. From observational studies that characterize disease burden to implementation research that bridges efficacy and real-world effectiveness, global health research requires methodological rigor adapted to complex settings. The chapter addresses knowledge translation mechanisms that move evidence into policy and practice, ethical considerations specific to research involving vulnerable populations across international partnerships, and strategies for strengthening research capacity in low- and middle-income countries. Throughout, emphasis is placed on equitable research partnerships, community engagement, and decolonizing approaches that ensure research serves the populations it studies.

## 29.1 Global Health Research Methodologies

Global health research employs diverse methodological approaches tailored to research questions ranging from disease burden estimation to intervention effectiveness. The selection of appropriate methods depends on the research question, available resources, ethical considerations, and the intended use of findings (1). Understanding the strengths and limitations of different approaches enables researchers to match methods to questions while acknowledging the evidence each design can provide.

### 29.1.1 Observational Studies in Global Health
Observational studies form the backbone of global health epidemiology, characterizing disease patterns, identifying risk factors, and generating hypotheses for intervention development. **Cohort studies** follow defined populations over time to assess exposures and outcomes, providing estimates of incidence and relative risk while minimizing recall bias (2). The Nurses' Health Study and Framingham Heart Study demonstrated the power of cohort designs, while global health applications include birth cohorts examining early life exposures and adult health, and occupational cohorts assessing workplace hazards in diverse settings.

**Case-control studies** efficiently investigate rare diseases or outbreaks by comparing exposure histories between cases and controls, though they are susceptible to recall and selection bias. These designs have been particularly valuable in outbreak investigations where rapid evidence is needed to inform response. **Cross-sectional surveys** provide prevalence estimates and associations at a single time point, forming the basis for demographic and health surveys that characterize population health across countries. The Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS) program has conducted over 400 surveys in more than 90 countries since 1984, providing comparable data on fertility, family planning, maternal and child health, and increasingly, non-communicable diseases (3).

**Health and Demographic Surveillance Systems (HDSS)** represent a distinctive contribution to global health research infrastructure. These longitudinal population-based systems continuously monitor demographic events and health outcomes in defined geographic areas, providing platforms for multiple nested studies (4). The INDEPTH Network coordinates over 40 HDSS sites across Africa, Asia, and Oceania, enabling cross-site comparisons and pooled analyses while building local research capacity.

### 29.1.2 Experimental and Quasi-Experimental Designs
**Randomized controlled trials (RCTs)** remain the gold standard for establishing intervention efficacy, minimizing confounding through random allocation. However, individual randomization is often impractical in global health where interventions target communities, health facilities, or systems (5). **Cluster randomized trials** address this by randomizing groups rather than individuals, appropriate for interventions that cannot be individually assigned or where contamination between treatment arms is a concern. The design requires attention to intracluster correlation, adequate cluster numbers, and often larger sample sizes than individual randomization.

**Stepped-wedge designs** sequentially roll out interventions across clusters over time, with each cluster serving as its own control before receiving the intervention (6). This approach is particularly suitable when interventions are expected to be beneficial, making a pure control group ethically challenging, or when logistical constraints prevent simultaneous implementation. Stepped-wedge designs have been used to evaluate health system interventions, quality improvement initiatives, and policy changes across diverse settings.

**Natural experiments** exploit naturally occurring variation in exposure to assess intervention effects when randomization is infeasible. Policy changes, geographic boundaries, and administrative thresholds create opportunities for quasi-experimental analyses including difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, and instrumental variable approaches (7). These designs have been valuable for evaluating health financing reforms, tobacco control policies, and the health impacts of social programs.

**Pragmatic trials** prioritize external validity by testing interventions under real-world conditions with broad eligibility criteria, routine care delivery, and clinically relevant outcomes (8). The PRECIS-2 tool helps researchers design trials along a continuum from explanatory (testing under ideal conditions) to pragmatic (testing under typical conditions), with global health research often favoring pragmatic approaches that generate evidence directly applicable to implementation decisions.

### 29.1.3 Mixed Methods and Qualitative Research
Mixed methods research systematically integrates quantitative and qualitative approaches to address research questions that neither approach could answer alone (9). In global health, mixed methods are particularly valuable for understanding not just whether interventions work but how and why they work in specific contexts. **Convergent designs** collect quantitative and qualitative data simultaneously and merge findings to provide complementary perspectives. **Explanatory sequential designs** use qualitative methods to explain or elaborate quantitative findings, while **exploratory sequential designs** use qualitative findings to inform subsequent quantitative inquiry.

Qualitative research methods including **ethnography**, **phenomenology**, and **grounded theory** provide deep understanding of health behaviors, experiences, and social contexts (10). Ethnographic approaches have illuminated cultural factors affecting treatment adherence, health-seeking behavior, and the social dimensions of diseases from HIV to tuberculosis. Phenomenological studies explore lived experiences of illness, disability, and healthcare encounters, informing patient-centered care approaches. Grounded theory generates theoretical frameworks from data, useful when existing theories inadequately explain observed phenomena.

**Process evaluations** embedded within trials combine quantitative implementation measures with qualitative exploration of context, mechanisms, and implementation processes (11). The Medical Research Council guidance on process evaluation emphasizes understanding how context shapes implementation and outcomes, essential for interpreting trial results and informing scale-up decisions.

### 29.1.4 Participatory Research Approaches
**Community-based participatory research (CBPR)** represents a paradigm shift from research conducted on communities to research conducted with and by communities (12). CBPR principles include community partnership throughout the research process, co-learning between researchers and community members, local capacity building, and commitment to translating findings into action. This approach is particularly relevant in global health where research has historically been extractive, generating publications and careers for external researchers while providing limited benefit to studied populations.

**Action research** cycles through planning, action, observation, and reflection, with research directly informing ongoing practice improvement (13). Participatory action research extends this by involving community members as co-researchers throughout the cycle. These approaches have been applied to health system strengthening, community health worker programs, and health promotion initiatives.

**Indigenous research methodologies** challenge Western research paradigms by centering indigenous worldviews, knowledge systems, and research priorities (14). Kaupapa Māori research in Aotearoa New Zealand exemplifies this approach, grounding research in Māori philosophy and ensuring research serves Māori aspirations. Similar movements in Canada, Australia, and globally assert indigenous peoples' rights to govern research concerning them and their data.

| Design Type | Strengths | Limitations | Global Health Applications |
|------------|-----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| Cohort Studies | Temporal sequence, multiple outcomes, incidence estimation | Resource intensive, loss to follow-up, long duration | Birth cohorts, occupational health, HDSS platforms |
| Case-Control | Efficient for rare outcomes, rapid results | Recall bias, selection bias, cannot estimate incidence | Outbreak investigation, rare disease etiology |
| Cross-Sectional | Population representativeness, prevalence estimation | Cannot establish causation, temporal ambiguity | DHS surveys, STEPS surveys, population health assessment |
| Cluster RCT | Controls confounding, evaluates community interventions | Intracluster correlation, needs more clusters | Vaccine trials, health system interventions, behavioral programs |
| Stepped-Wedge | All clusters receive intervention, controls secular trends | Complex analysis, longer duration, attrition | Policy implementation, quality improvement, health reforms |
| Mixed Methods | Comprehensive understanding, triangulation | Resource intensive, methodological expertise needed | Implementation research, process evaluation, complex interventions |
| CBPR | Community relevance, local capacity, sustainable impact | Time intensive, power dynamics, generalizability | Health promotion, community health, indigenous health |


## 29.2 Implementation Science in Global Health
Implementation science addresses the critical gap between research evidence and practice, examining how evidence-based interventions can be effectively implemented in real-world settings (15). This field has particular relevance for global health, where interventions with proven efficacy often fail to achieve their potential impact due to implementation challenges. Implementation science provides frameworks, methods, and strategies for understanding and improving how interventions are delivered at scale.

### 29.2.1 Conceptual Frameworks for Implementation Research
Multiple frameworks guide implementation research, each offering different perspectives on implementation determinants, processes, and outcomes (16). The **Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research (CFIR)** organizes implementation determinants across five domains: intervention characteristics, outer setting, inner setting, characteristics of individuals, and process (17). CFIR has been widely applied in global health to systematically assess barriers and facilitators to implementation, though critics note its comprehensiveness can overwhelm practical application.

The **RE-AIM framework** (Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, Implementation, Maintenance) provides a structure for evaluating public health interventions across multiple dimensions (18). Originally developed for health promotion, RE-AIM has been adapted for global health applications where understanding reach into underserved populations and sustainability are critical concerns.

**Proctor's implementation outcomes** distinguish implementation outcomes (acceptability, adoption, appropriateness, cost, feasibility, fidelity, penetration, sustainability) from service outcomes and client outcomes, clarifying what implementation research should measure (19). This taxonomy helps researchers specify which outcomes their studies address and how implementation success or failure may affect ultimate health impacts.

The **Theoretical Domains Framework (TDF)** synthesizes psychological and organizational theories into 14 domains that influence behavior change, useful for understanding provider and patient behaviors that affect implementation (20). TDF has been applied to identify barriers to guideline adherence, design implementation interventions, and understand why implementations succeed or fail.

### 29.2.2 Effectiveness-Implementation Hybrid Designs
**Hybrid designs** simultaneously test clinical interventions and implementation strategies, accelerating the translation of evidence into practice (21). Type 1 hybrids primarily test clinical intervention effectiveness while observing and gathering information on implementation. Type 2 hybrids equally emphasize testing both the clinical intervention and the implementation strategy. Type 3 hybrids primarily test implementation strategies while observing clinical outcomes.

The choice among hybrid types depends on the evidence base for the intervention and implementation strategy. When intervention effectiveness is established, Type 3 designs test different approaches to implementation. When effectiveness evidence comes primarily from controlled settings, Type 2 designs can simultaneously assess real-world effectiveness and implementation (22). These designs are particularly valuable in global health where the urgency of health problems makes prolonged sequential research problematic.

Hybrid designs require careful attention to sample size calculations that account for both effectiveness and implementation aims, clear specification of implementation strategies being tested, and measurement approaches that capture both types of outcomes (23). Multi-site designs can examine how context moderates both effectiveness and implementation, informing adaptation decisions for different settings.

### 29.2.3 Adaptation and Scale-Up Research
Successful implementation rarely involves importing interventions unchanged from development settings to new contexts. **Adaptation science** examines how interventions should be modified to fit new contexts while preserving core functions that drive effectiveness (24). The tension between fidelity and adaptation requires understanding which intervention elements are essential and which can be modified without compromising outcomes.

The **ADAPT guidance** provides a systematic approach to planned adaptation, including assessment of contextual factors necessitating adaptation, decision-making about modifications, pilot testing of adapted interventions, and evaluation of adapted implementation (25). This contrasts with reactive adaptations made during implementation without systematic planning or evaluation.

**Scale-up research** addresses the processes through which effective interventions expand from pilot projects to broader implementation. The WHO/ExpandNet framework distinguishes between vertical scale-up (institutionalization through policy and systems) and horizontal scale-up (geographic expansion) while identifying attributes of scalable innovations, organizational capacities needed for scale-up, and the resource teams that drive expansion (26). Scale-up research examines how implementation changes as interventions expand, whether positive outcomes are maintained at scale, and what organizational and system changes enable or constrain scaling.

**Sustainment** represents a critical but often neglected phase, examining whether implementation persists after initial support ends (27). Research on sustainment examines factors at intervention, provider, organization, and system levels that predict whether evidence-based practices continue, adapt, or disappear after implementation funding ends. In global health, where external funding for implementation is often time-limited, understanding sustainment determinants is essential for achieving lasting health impact.

### 29.2.4 Context and Implementation
Context profoundly shapes implementation, yet has historically been treated as noise to be controlled rather than a central focus of inquiry (28). Contemporary implementation science increasingly emphasizes understanding how context influences implementation processes and outcomes, and how implementation strategies should be tailored to contextual conditions.

The **Context and Implementation of Complex Interventions (CICI) framework** provides a comprehensive approach to characterizing context across geographic, epidemiological, socio-cultural, socio-economic, ethical, legal, and political dimensions, along with the setting where implementation occurs (29). Systematic assessment of context enables researchers to understand implementation variation across sites, predict implementation challenges in new settings, and design context-appropriate implementation strategies.

**Process evaluation** methods illuminate how interventions interact with context during implementation, examining mechanisms of impact and contextual moderators (30). The integration of theory-based evaluation approaches with process evaluation enables understanding not just whether implementation succeeded but why, informing both scientific understanding and practical adaptation.


## 29.3 Knowledge Translation and Evidence-Informed Policy
The generation of research evidence is necessary but insufficient for improving health outcomes; evidence must be translated into policy and practice to have impact. Knowledge translation (KT) encompasses the synthesis, exchange, and ethically sound application of knowledge to accelerate benefits from research (31). In global health, KT processes must navigate diverse political systems, resource constraints, and cultural contexts while ensuring evidence reaches and influences decision-makers.

### 29.3.1 Knowledge Translation Frameworks
The **Knowledge-to-Action (KTA) framework** conceptualizes knowledge translation as iterative cycles of knowledge creation (including synthesis) and action (including adaptation to local context, barrier assessment, intervention selection, monitoring, and sustainability) (32). This framework acknowledges that knowledge translation is not linear but involves continuous cycles of learning and adaptation.

**Push and pull models** describe different mechanisms for knowledge exchange (33). Push approaches actively disseminate research findings to potential users through publications, presentations, and media. Pull approaches respond to decision-maker requests for evidence on specific questions. Interactive approaches facilitate ongoing dialogue between researchers and decision-makers, building relationships and mutual understanding. Integrated knowledge translation embeds knowledge users in the research process from inception, increasing relevance and facilitating uptake.

**Knowledge brokering** refers to intermediary functions that facilitate connections between researchers and knowledge users (34). Knowledge brokers may work within organizations, between sectors, or as independent intermediaries, performing functions including finding and interpreting relevant research, building relationships, facilitating dialogue, and building capacity for evidence use. Evidence suggests knowledge brokers can enhance research uptake, though their effectiveness depends on organizational context and individual capabilities.

### 29.3.2 Research-to-Policy Mechanisms
Understanding the policy process is essential for effective knowledge translation. **Policy windows** open when problems, policies, and politics align, creating opportunities for evidence-informed change (35). Researchers can prepare for these windows by having evidence synthesized and accessible, maintaining relationships with policymakers, and framing evidence in terms relevant to policy debates.

**Evidence briefs** for policy present research evidence in formats accessible to decision-makers, typically including key messages, context, evidence synthesis, policy options with their benefits and drawbacks, and implementation considerations (36). Effective briefs are concise, visually clear, address questions decision-makers are asking, and acknowledge uncertainty and contextual factors.

**Deliberative dialogues** bring together decision-makers, stakeholders, and researchers to discuss evidence and its policy implications (37). Unlike debates aimed at winning arguments, deliberative dialogues seek mutual understanding and collective problem-solving. Evidence suggests these dialogues can influence participants' views and subsequent actions, particularly when carefully designed and facilitated.

**Rapid response mechanisms** provide timely evidence syntheses in response to urgent policy questions. Services like the McMaster Health Forum's Rapid Response Program and the Africa Evidence Network's rapid evidence services demonstrate growing demand for timely evidence support. These mechanisms require pre-existing infrastructure for rapid synthesis and relationships that enable policymakers to access services.

### 29.3.3 Evidence-Informed Decision Making
Evidence-informed decision making recognizes that research evidence is one input among many, alongside values, preferences, resources, and contextual factors. The **GRADE approach** (Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation) provides systematic methods for rating evidence certainty and moving from evidence to recommendations (38). GRADE assesses evidence across domains including risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias, with explicit consideration of effect magnitude and dose-response relationships.

**Evidence-to-decision frameworks** structure the process of moving from evidence synthesis to recommendations (39). The GRADE EtD framework addresses problem priority, benefits and harms, certainty of evidence, values and preferences, resource requirements, equity, acceptability, and feasibility. These frameworks make explicit the factors considered in recommendations, enabling transparent and contestable decision-making.

Implementation of evidence-informed approaches requires organizational and system capacity for evidence synthesis and interpretation, cultures that value evidence use, and accountability mechanisms that reinforce evidence-informed practice (40). Capacity building for evidence-informed decision making is increasingly recognized as essential for health system strengthening.

### 29.3.4 Barriers and Facilitators to Knowledge Use
Multiple barriers impede research use in policy and practice. **Supply-side barriers** include research that is inaccessible, irrelevant to decision-maker needs, or poorly communicated. **Demand-side barriers** include limited capacity for evidence interpretation, organizational cultures that do not value evidence, and political contexts where evidence conflicts with interests (41). Structural barriers include weak linkages between research and policy systems, limited funding for knowledge translation activities, and short policy timelines that conflict with research timeframes.

Facilitators include sustained relationships between researchers and policymakers, organizational infrastructure for evidence use, alignment between research questions and policy priorities, and policy entrepreneurs who champion evidence-informed approaches (42). Research on what works to enhance evidence use suggests multi-component strategies addressing multiple barriers are more effective than single interventions, though evidence remains limited particularly in low- and middle-income country contexts (43).

## 29.4 Research Ethics in Global Health
Research ethics in global health addresses distinctive challenges arising from inequalities between researchers and participants, between sponsoring and host countries, and between research benefits and burdens (44). While universal ethical principles apply, their interpretation and application require attention to context, power dynamics, and the historical legacy of exploitative research.

### 29.4.1 Ethical Principles and International Guidelines
The **Declaration of Helsinki** provides foundational principles for medical research involving human subjects, emphasizing informed consent, independent ethics review, protection of vulnerable populations, and the primacy of participant welfare over scientific interests (45). While originated in response to Nazi atrocities, the Declaration has evolved to address contemporary challenges including research in developing countries and the use of placebos.

The **CIOMS International Ethical Guidelines** provide detailed guidance on research ethics with specific attention to research in low-resource settings (46). The 2016 revision addresses community engagement, obligations to provide ancillary care, post-research access to beneficial interventions, collection and use of biological samples, and research in disasters and disease outbreaks. CIOMS explicitly addresses the ethics of research where sponsors and host communities differ in resources and research capacity.

The **Common Rule** (US federal policy for human subjects protection) and similar national regulations establish requirements for institutional review, informed consent, and ongoing monitoring (47). International collaborative research must typically satisfy ethical requirements in both sponsoring and host countries, navigating differences in regulatory frameworks, cultural expectations, and capacity for ethical oversight.

### 29.4.2 Community Engagement in Research
Community engagement has evolved from an ethical add-on to a fundamental component of research integrity (48). The **spectrum of community engagement** ranges from minimal consultation through active involvement to community control of research. Different research questions and contexts call for different engagement levels, but meaningful engagement requires genuine influence over research decisions rather than tokenistic participation.

**Community advisory boards (CABs)** provide ongoing community input throughout the research process (49). Effective CABs have clear mandates, adequate resources, diverse representation, meaningful roles in decision-making, and mechanisms for two-way communication between community and researchers. Challenges include ensuring CAB members represent community perspectives rather than elite interests, managing expectations about CAB influence, and sustaining engagement over long research timelines.

**Community consent** supplements individual informed consent in research affecting communities, acknowledging that individual consent alone is insufficient when research has collective implications (50). Community consent processes vary by context but typically involve consultation with community leaders, discussion in community forums, and documented community agreement to host research. These processes must balance community authority with protection of individual rights, ensuring community consent does not substitute for individual voluntary informed consent.

### 29.4.3 Benefit Sharing and Post-Trial Access
Research involving communities that will not benefit from their participation raises fundamental justice concerns. The **fair benefits framework** argues that research in developing countries is ethical only when participants and communities receive a fair share of benefits (51). Benefits may include health services during research, capacity building, post-trial access to interventions proven effective, technology transfer, and contributions to local research infrastructure.

**Post-trial access** to beneficial interventions remains contentious (52). Arguments for post-trial access emphasize obligations arising from research relationships and the injustice of withdrawing beneficial interventions. Counter-arguments note practical challenges, potential distortion of research priorities toward conditions affecting research participants, and the primary responsibility of health systems rather than researchers for ongoing care. The 2016 CIOMS guidelines require that researchers and sponsors make reasonable provisions for post-trial access while acknowledging context-specific considerations.

**Ancillary care obligations** address researcher responsibilities for health needs discovered during research that are beyond the study scope (53). These obligations arise from the researcher-participant relationship and vary with the depth of that relationship, participant vulnerability, and researcher capacity to provide care. In settings with limited healthcare access, ancillary care questions become particularly salient and complex.

### 29.4.4 Decolonizing Research Ethics
Conventional research ethics, while essential, may inadequately address power imbalances and historical injustices embedded in global health research (54). **Decolonizing approaches** call for fundamental transformation of research relationships, epistemologies, and practices rather than incremental reforms within existing paradigms.

**Epistemic justice** demands recognition of diverse knowledge systems rather than privileging Western scientific knowledge (55). This includes acknowledging indigenous and local knowledge, questioning assumptions embedded in research questions and methods, and enabling communities to define research priorities and methodologies. Decolonizing research ethics extends beyond harm prevention to actively promote community benefit and self-determination.

**Data sovereignty** asserts community rights to govern data about them, challenging assumptions that data should be freely shared for scientific progress (56). Indigenous data sovereignty movements globally have articulated principles including community ownership of data, community consent for data use, and community benefit from data applications. These principles have implications for data sharing requirements, secondary analysis, and the application of open science principles in indigenous contexts.

**Research as reciprocity** reframes research relationships from extraction to exchange, emphasizing mutual benefit, ongoing relationships, and researcher obligations extending beyond publication (57). Implementing reciprocity requires structural changes including funding for capacity building, time for relationship development, and incentives aligned with community benefit rather than solely scientific productivity.


## 29.5 Research Capacity Strengthening
Sustainable improvement in global health requires building research capacity within low- and middle-income countries rather than relying on external expertise (58). Research capacity strengthening (RCS) encompasses efforts to develop individual researchers, institutions, and enabling environments for research. Effective RCS creates self-sustaining research ecosystems that generate locally relevant evidence and inform local decision-making.

### 29.5.1 Individual Capacity Building
**Research training programs** range from short courses building specific skills to doctoral and postdoctoral programs developing independent researchers. The **NIH Fogarty International Center** has supported research training for over 7,000 scientists from more than 130 countries through programs emphasizing mentored research experience, often combining time in US institutions with research in home countries (59). Evaluations demonstrate sustained research productivity among trainees, with many achieving leadership positions in research and policy.

**Mentorship** is critical for research career development, providing guidance, support, and access to networks (60). Effective mentorship in global health requires attention to power dynamics, cultural differences, and the distinct challenges facing researchers in low-resource settings. Mentorship chains, where senior trainees mentor junior researchers, extend mentorship reach and build mentorship capacity.

**Career pathways** for researchers in low- and middle-income countries often face structural barriers including limited positions, inadequate salaries, heavy teaching and clinical loads, and brain drain to better-resourced settings. Retention of trained researchers requires institutional changes including protected research time, competitive compensation, research infrastructure, and professional recognition. Regional career development programs and networks help create alternative pathways for researchers who might otherwise leave research or emigrate.

### 29.5.2 Institutional Strengthening
Individual capacity without institutional support dissipates as trained researchers leave or cannot apply their skills. **Institutional strengthening** addresses research infrastructure including laboratories, libraries, and computing facilities; research management including grants administration, financial systems, and human resources; and governance including strategic planning, quality assurance, and ethical oversight (61).

**Institutional review boards (IRBs)** in low- and middle-income countries often face challenges including limited expertise, inadequate resources, and tension between enabling research and protecting participants (62). IRB strengthening includes training for committee members, development of standard operating procedures, and establishment of review systems appropriate to research volume and complexity. Regional ethics review collaboratives provide peer support and quality improvement opportunities.

**Research management systems** support the full research lifecycle from proposal development through grant management to dissemination (63). Many institutions lack specialized research administration, leaving researchers to manage complex international grants while maintaining scientific productivity. Building research management capacity through training, staffing, and systems development enables institutions to host and manage increasingly complex research programs.

### 29.5.3 Systems and Enabling Environment
Individual and institutional capacity exists within broader systems that enable or constrain research. **National research systems** encompass policies governing research, funding mechanisms, research institutions, and research-policy linkages (64). Strengthening national research systems requires policy frameworks that prioritize research, domestic research funding, career structures for researchers, and mechanisms linking research to national priorities.

**Research funding** in most low- and middle-income countries remains dominated by external sources, creating dependency and directing research toward donor priorities (65). Building domestic research funding requires advocacy for research investment, development of competitive funding mechanisms, and demonstration of research value for national priorities. Regional funding mechanisms such as the African Academy of Sciences' Alliance for Accelerating Excellence in Science in Africa (AESA) provide alternatives to traditional North-South funding.

**Research culture** refers to organizational and societal values affecting research conduct and use (66). Building research culture includes promoting research integrity, valuing evidence in decision-making, and creating environments where researchers can pursue questions without political interference. Research culture develops through education, incentives, and leadership modeling evidence-informed approaches.

### 29.5.4 Evaluating Capacity Strengthening Impact
Evaluating research capacity strengthening impact remains challenging. **Traditional metrics** including publications, citations, and grants capture research outputs but may inadequately reflect broader capacity gains (67). Alternative indicators include researcher retention, institutional sustainability, research influence on policy, and capacity to train subsequent researcher generations.

The **ESSENCE framework** provides principles and good practice for research capacity strengthening, emphasizing country ownership, alignment with national priorities, harmonization among funders, and long-term commitment (68). ESSENCE guidance on M&E includes indicators across individual, institutional, and system levels while acknowledging context-specific definitions of success.

**Return on investment** analyses attempt to quantify capacity strengthening value, though attribution challenges and long time horizons complicate assessment (69). Beyond economic return, capacity strengthening evaluation should address equity considerations including whether investments reach those most in need of capacity development.

| Individual | Knowledge, skills, attitudes, career development | Training programs, mentorship, networking, career support | Publications, grants, leadership positions, retention |
| Institutional | Infrastructure, management, governance, culture | Infrastructure investment, systems development, leadership development | Grant management capacity, IRB functionality, strategic planning |

## 29.6 Systematic Reviews and Evidence Synthesis
Systematic reviews synthesize research evidence using rigorous, transparent methods to minimize bias and enable evidence-informed decisions (70). In global health, systematic reviews inform clinical guidelines, public health policies, and research priorities. The proliferation of reviews has led to concerns about quality, duplication, and accessibility, prompting efforts to improve review methods and infrastructure.

### 29.6.1 Systematic Review Methodology
**Systematic review methodology** has developed substantially since Archie Cochrane's call for systematic summaries of evidence. The **PRISMA statement** (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) provides guidance for transparent reporting, including flow diagrams documenting study selection and detailed description of methods (71). Extensions address specific review types including network meta-analyses, individual participant data analyses, and scoping reviews.

**Search strategies** must balance sensitivity (finding all relevant studies) with specificity (avoiding excessive irrelevant results). Global health reviews face particular challenges including literature in multiple languages, publication in local journals not indexed in major databases, and grey literature from governments and international organizations (72). Comprehensive searching may require regional databases, hand-searching relevant journals, and contact with experts.

**Risk of bias assessment** evaluates threats to validity in included studies. Tools including the Cochrane Risk of Bias tool and ROBINS-I (for non-randomized studies) provide structured approaches to assessing selection, performance, detection, attrition, and reporting biases. Assessment of non-randomized studies, common in global health reviews, requires particular attention to confounding and selection bias.

**Meta-analysis** statistically combines results across studies, increasing precision and enabling exploration of heterogeneity. Not all reviews warrant meta-analysis; statistical combination requires sufficiently similar studies addressing the same question. When heterogeneity is substantial, exploring sources of heterogeneity through subgroup analysis and meta-regression may be more informative than pooled estimates.

### 29.6.2 Evidence Synthesis for Global Health
The **Cochrane Collaboration** produces high-quality systematic reviews of healthcare interventions, with Cochrane Review Groups covering topics from infectious diseases to public health (73). Cochrane reviews follow standardized methods, undergo rigorous peer review, and are regularly updated. The **Campbell Collaboration** applies similar methods to social interventions, with relevance for health determinants and health system interventions.

**Living systematic reviews** continuously incorporate new evidence as it emerges, particularly valuable for rapidly evolving fields (74). During the COVID-19 pandemic, living reviews enabled timely evidence synthesis on treatments, diagnostics, and public health interventions. Living reviews require infrastructure for continuous searching, screening, and analysis, as well as governance for deciding when updates warrant recommendation changes.

**Rapid reviews** sacrifice some methodological rigor for timeliness, appropriate when decisions cannot wait for full systematic reviews (75). Rapid reviews may limit databases searched, use single reviewers, forgo quality assessment, or rely on narrative synthesis. Best practice includes transparent reporting of methodological shortcuts and their potential impact on findings.

**Evidence maps** provide visual overviews of evidence across a field, identifying gaps and clusters of evidence. Evidence gap maps have been developed for global health topics including nutrition, WASH, and health systems, informing research priority setting and helping decision-makers understand the evidence landscape.

### 29.6.3 From Evidence to Guidelines
Systematic reviews form the evidence base for clinical and public health guidelines, but moving from evidence synthesis to recommendations requires additional steps (76). **WHO guideline development** follows the GRADE approach, with guideline development groups formulating recommendations based on evidence certainty, balance of benefits and harms, values and preferences, resource requirements, equity, acceptability, and feasibility (77).

**Guideline adaptation** enables use of international guidelines in specific contexts without developing guidelines de novo (78). The ADAPTE process and subsequent tools provide structured approaches to assessing applicability, identifying modification needs, and adapting recommendations while maintaining evidence linkage. Adaptation is particularly relevant in global health where WHO guidelines may require modification for specific country contexts.

**Implementation guidance** accompanying guidelines addresses how recommendations can be operationalized (79). Implementation considerations include resource requirements, training needs, monitoring approaches, and strategies for addressing barriers. Increasingly, guidelines include implementation sections, though the evidence base for implementation guidance is often weaker than for clinical recommendations.

### 29.6.4 Addressing Evidence Gaps
When evidence is insufficient for confident recommendations, guideline developers must decide whether to make conditional recommendations, recommend research, or abstain from recommendations (80). **Conditional recommendations** indicate that the intervention may be appropriate for most patients but decision-makers should consider individual circumstances and preferences.

**Research recommendations** identify priority questions for future research (81). Well-formulated research recommendations specify the population, intervention, comparison, outcomes, and study design needed to address evidence gaps. These recommendations can inform research priority setting and funding decisions.

**Expert opinion** may inform recommendations when evidence is absent, though guidelines should clearly distinguish evidence-based from expert-based recommendations (82). Structured approaches to eliciting expert opinion, including Delphi methods and nominal group techniques, can improve transparency and reduce individual biases in expert consensus.

## 29.7 Research Priority Setting
With research resources inevitably limited relative to needs, systematic approaches to research priority setting ensure investments address the most important questions (83). Priority setting processes vary in their methods, stakeholder involvement, and criteria for prioritization, but share goals of transparency, legitimacy, and alignment of research with health needs.

### 29.7.1 Priority Setting Approaches
The **Child Health and Nutrition Research Initiative (CHNRI) method** provides a systematic approach to research priority setting that has been applied across global health domains (84). CHNRI involves scoring research questions against criteria including answerability, effectiveness, deliverability, maximum potential for disease burden reduction, and equity. The method enables comparison across diverse questions and explicit consideration of equity alongside scientific and practical criteria.

The **James Lind Alliance** brings patients, carers, and clinicians together to identify research priorities, explicitly excluding researchers and others with vested interests in particular questions (85). Priority setting partnerships develop questions, survey stakeholders, and convene workshops to achieve consensus on top priorities. This approach ensures patient-centered priorities but may underemphasize implementation and health system questions.

**Delphi methods** use iterative expert consultation to achieve consensus on priorities (86). Multiple rounds of questionnaires, with feedback on group responses between rounds, enable convergence while maintaining anonymity. Delphi approaches can efficiently engage global experts but depend heavily on expert selection and question framing.

**Burden-based approaches** prioritize research on conditions with greatest disease burden, measured through disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) or similar metrics. While appealing in their objectivity, burden-based approaches may neglect conditions that disproportionately affect disadvantaged populations, questions about existing interventions versus innovation, and research types beyond intervention evaluation.

### 29.7.2 Stakeholder Engagement in Priority Setting
**Patient and public involvement** in research priority setting ensures research addresses questions that matter to those affected (87). Methods for involvement include surveys, focus groups, consensus conferences, and representation on priority-setting committees. Meaningful involvement requires accessible processes, support for participation, and genuine influence over priorities.

**Community priorities** may differ from those identified through burden-based or expert-driven approaches (88). Participatory priority-setting processes reveal community perspectives, which may emphasize implementation of known interventions, social determinants, or conditions affecting daily life that do not necessarily dominate burden statistics.

Balancing **researcher, funder, and policy perspectives** requires transparent processes for weighing different views (89). Researchers may prioritize scientifically interesting questions; funders may emphasize alignment with strategic objectives; policymakers may focus on decision-relevant questions. Legitimate priority-setting processes acknowledge these different perspectives while maintaining focus on health impact and equity.

### 29.7.3 Global Research Priority Initiatives
**Disease Control Priorities (DCP)** provides economic analysis of health interventions to guide resource allocation, with implications for research on cost-effective interventions (90). Now in its fourth edition, DCP informs global health investment decisions while highlighting areas where research could strengthen the evidence base.

**Lancet Commissions** convene experts to address major health challenges, often generating research agendas alongside policy recommendations (91). Commissions on topics from NCDs to planetary health have influenced global health discourse and research priorities.

**WHO research agendas** for specific diseases and health system challenges identify priority questions aligned with WHO strategies and member state needs (92). Disease-specific blueprints, including the WHO R&D Blueprint for epidemics, prioritize research needed for epidemic preparedness and response.

## 29.8 Open Science and Data Sharing
The open science movement promotes transparency, accessibility, and reproducibility in research, with potential to accelerate scientific progress and democratize knowledge access (93). In global health, open science principles intersect with equity concerns, data sovereignty, and the practicalities of research in resource-limited settings.

### 29.8.1 Open Science Principles and Practices
**Open access publishing** makes research outputs freely available without subscription barriers. Mandates from funders including the Gates Foundation, Wellcome Trust, and European Commission require open access publication of funded research (94). While open access addresses reader-side barriers, article processing charges may create barriers for authors without funding to cover publication costs.

**Preprints** enable rapid sharing of research findings before peer review, accelerating dissemination while enabling community feedback. The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated preprint value for rapid evidence sharing, though also highlighted risks of misinterpretation of non-peer-reviewed findings (95). Preprint servers including medRxiv and bioRxiv have become established components of the scientific communication ecosystem.

**Open data** makes research data available for verification and reuse, enhancing reproducibility and enabling secondary analyses. Funder mandates increasingly require data sharing, though implementation varies. Open data policies must balance transparency with privacy protection, data quality assurance, and recognition of data generators' interests.

**Open methods** including registered protocols, analysis plans, and code sharing enhance reproducibility and reduce selective reporting (96). Protocol registration on platforms including PROSPERO (for systematic reviews) and clinical trial registries makes planned methods transparent before results are known.

### 29.8.2 Data Sharing in Global Health
The **FAIR principles** (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) provide a framework for data management that enables reuse while leaving access decisions to data stewards (97). FAIR principles have been widely adopted by funders and journals, though implementation remains challenging particularly for older datasets and those from under-resourced settings.

**Data repositories** provide infrastructure for data sharing, from general-purpose repositories like Zenodo and Dryad to domain-specific repositories for genomic, clinical trial, and survey data (98). Choice of repository affects data discoverability, long-term preservation, and access control options.

**Data governance** encompasses policies and processes for data collection, storage, access, and use. **Data use agreements** specify conditions for data access, protecting privacy while enabling legitimate research (99). Governance frameworks must balance scientific openness with privacy protection, recognizing that anonymization may be insufficient to prevent re-identification particularly for genetic data or small populations.

### 29.8.3 Equity in Knowledge Access
**Research4Life** provides free or low-cost access to academic literature for institutions in low- and middle-income countries, addressing financial barriers to knowledge access (100). Programs including HINARI (health), AGORA (agriculture), and OARE (environment) provide access to millions of journal articles and books.

**Publication fees** for open access journals may disadvantage researchers without funds to cover charges, potentially creating new inequities while addressing reader-side access (101). Fee waivers exist but are inconsistently applied and may be stigmatizing. Alternative models including diamond open access (no fees for readers or authors) address this concern but require sustainable funding.

**Predatory journals** exploit open access models by accepting articles with minimal review for payment, threatening research quality and wasting resources. Researchers in low- and middle-income countries may be particularly vulnerable to predatory publishers. Efforts to identify predatory journals and educate researchers aim to protect research integrity while avoiding unfair stigmatization of legitimate journals from the Global South.

### 29.8.4 Data Sovereignty and Community Ownership
The **CARE principles** (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) complement FAIR principles by centering people and purpose in data governance (102). Developed for indigenous data governance, CARE principles have broader relevance for global health research where data about communities should serve those communities' interests.

**Indigenous data sovereignty** asserts the rights of indigenous peoples to govern data about them, their territories, and their resources (103). Sovereignty movements in New Zealand, Australia, Canada, and globally have developed frameworks including Te Mana Raraunga (Māori Data Sovereignty Network principles) and the CARE principles. These frameworks challenge assumptions that data should be freely shared for scientific progress, instead asserting community authority over data.

**Community data trusts** provide governance structures for community ownership of data (104). Trusts hold data on behalf of communities, with community-determined policies for access and use. This model enables data sharing for beneficial purposes while protecting against misuse and ensuring community benefit.


## Case Studies in Global Health Research

**Case Study 29.1: INDEPTH Network - Building Longitudinal Research Infrastructure Across Africa and Asia**

The International Network for the Demographic Evaluation of Populations and Their Health (INDEPTH) represents a transformative approach to building sustainable research infrastructure in settings where vital registration systems are incomplete. Founded in 1998, INDEPTH brought together existing Health and Demographic Surveillance Systems (HDSS) spanning 18 countries across Africa, Asia, and Oceania to enable collaborative research, methodological development, and South-South knowledge exchange. HDSS platforms continuously monitor demographic events including births, deaths, and migrations in defined geographic areas, providing denominators for health indicators and platforms for nested studies, with over 40 million person-years of observation accumulated.

The network grew from founding members in Africa and Asia to encompass over 40 member centers. Each HDSS maintains longitudinal surveillance of defined populations, with regular household visits to capture vital events. Verbal autopsy methods enable cause-of-death determination in settings without medical certification, providing crucial mortality data. Standardized methods across sites enable cross-site comparisons and pooled analyses. INDEPTH established working groups addressing specific research domains including maternal and child health, adult health, migration, and data sharing. The iSHARE platform enables data sharing across sites while maintaining local data ownership.

INDEPTH sites have generated landmark evidence on mortality levels and trends, causes of death, health transitions, and intervention effectiveness. The network provided crucial data for global health metrics including the Global Burden of Disease study. During pandemics including H1N1 influenza and COVID-19, HDSS platforms enabled rapid assessment of mortality impacts. Beyond research outputs, INDEPTH has built substantial research capacity, training hundreds of researchers through workshops, exchanges, and collaborative projects. Sites provide research training environments for students from local and international universities, and leadership development programs have produced research leaders now heading major institutions across Africa and Asia.

INDEPTH has evolved toward greater sustainability through diversified funding, fee-for-service arrangements, and integration with national health information systems. The experience demonstrates several principles for global health research infrastructure: longitudinal surveillance platforms enable multiple studies more efficiently than standalone projects; South-South collaboration builds capacity and generates relevant evidence; standardized methods enable synthesis while local adaptation maintains relevance; and local ownership ensures sustainability beyond external funding.

**Case Study 29.2: NIH Fogarty International Center - Five Decades of Research Capacity Strengthening**

The Fogarty International Center, established in 1968 as part of the US National Institutes of Health, has been a pioneering institution in global health research capacity strengthening. The Center has trained over 7,000 scientists from more than 130 countries, evolving from supporting individual researchers to comprehensive programs addressing individual, institutional, and system-level capacity.

The Fogarty Global Health Research Training Program exemplifies the Center's approach. Begun in 1988 to build capacity for HIV research, the program supports partnerships between US institutions and counterparts in low- and middle-income countries. Training combines mentored research experience with coursework, typically including time at US institutions followed by return to home countries for dissertation research. Program evolution has emphasized in-country training, recognizing that capacity is best built where it will be used. Regional training programs enable trainees to remain closer to home while accessing high-quality education. Beyond the flagship training program, Fogarty supports infrastructure development through programs addressing research ethics, laboratory capacity, and mobile health.

Evaluations document substantial trainee productivity following program completion. Trainees have published in high-impact journals, obtained competitive grants, and assumed leadership positions in academic, government, and international organizations. Multiplier effects occur as trainees mentor subsequent generations, extending program impact. Institutional impacts include strengthened research management, improved ethical review capacity, and enhanced laboratory infrastructure at partner sites. Some partner institutions have evolved from dependent recipients to independent research leaders hosting their own international collaborations. Challenges include trainee retention in low-resource settings, where competing opportunities and inadequate research infrastructure may drive emigration.

Long-term sustainability requires eventual independence from Fogarty support. The Fogarty experience demonstrates the value of long-term commitment to capacity strengthening; meaningful change requires decades, not project cycles. Partnership models that respect local priorities and build institutional capacity outperform approaches focused solely on individual training. Alumni networks sustain impact and enable ongoing collaboration, and balanced approaches combining training abroad with in-country research maintain home country connections while providing access to resources.

**Case Study 29.3: WHO HIV Treatment Guidelines - From Research Evidence to Global Policy**

The evolution of WHO HIV treatment guidelines illustrates how systematic evidence synthesis, stakeholder engagement, and implementation considerations can transform global health practice. From initial cautious recommendations for limited antiretroviral treatment to current "treat all" guidance reaching 39 million people living with HIV globally, the guideline trajectory reflects both advancing evidence and evolving values about treatment access.

The first WHO antiretroviral treatment guidelines (2003) recommended treatment initiation at CD4 counts below 200 cells/mm3, reflecting efficacy evidence and concerns about feasibility in resource-limited settings. Subsequent updates progressively expanded treatment eligibility as evidence accumulated and implementation experience grew. The landmark 2015 guidelines recommended treatment for all people living with HIV regardless of CD4 count, based on evidence from the START and TEMPRANO trials demonstrating benefits of early treatment. Methodological rigor increased over time, with the GRADE approach adopted alongside explicit evidence profiles and evidence-to-decision frameworks. Systematic reviews commissioned through Cochrane and other groups provided the evidence base.

Moving from guideline publication to country implementation required sustained knowledge translation efforts. WHO supported country adaptation through guidance documents, technical assistance, and regional workshops. Civil society organizations, particularly networks of people living with HIV, advocated for guideline adoption and held governments accountable for implementation. Implementation challenges varied by context, including supply chain and workforce constraints, regulatory barriers, and cost considerations. Living guidelines approaches now enable rapid incorporation of new evidence, with living systematic reviews providing continuously updated evidence syntheses.

Civil society, particularly communities of people living with HIV, has been instrumental throughout the guideline process. Community representatives serve on guideline development groups, ensuring that lived experience informs recommendations. The HIV guideline experience demonstrates the power of combining rigorous evidence synthesis with stakeholder engagement and implementation support, and that implementation feasibility must be considered alongside efficacy evidence.

**Case Study 29.4: Maori Health Research - Indigenous Data Sovereignty and Community-Led Research in Aotearoa New Zealand**

Maori health research in Aotearoa New Zealand represents a global exemplar of how indigenous communities can reclaim research sovereignty, develop culturally grounded methodologies, and ensure research serves community priorities. The Maori research movement emerged from broader indigenous rights activism and critique of research that had historically objectified, exploited, and misrepresented Maori communities.

Kaupapa Maori research refers to research by Maori, for Maori, grounded in Maori philosophy and values. Key principles include tino rangatiratanga (self-determination), taonga tuku iho (cultural aspiration), ako Maori (culturally preferred pedagogy), kia piki ake i nga raruraru o te kainga (socioeconomic mediation), whanau (extended family structure), kaupapa (collective philosophy), and Te Tiriti o Waitangi (the Treaty of Waitangi as foundation). The approach developed through the work of scholars including Linda Tuhiwai Smith, whose book "Decolonizing Methodologies" (1999) became foundational globally for indigenous research movements. Institutional development supported Kaupapa Maori research, with Maori health research units established at universities, governed by Maori and accountable to Maori communities.

Te Mana Raraunga, the Maori Data Sovereignty Network, articulates principles for governance of data about Maori, including recognizing Maori rights and interests in data, ensuring Maori governance of Maori data, and ensuring data serves Maori aspirations. Practical implementation includes requirements for Maori governance of research involving Maori data, consultation protocols for secondary data analysis, and development of Maori-governed data repositories.

Kaupapa Maori research has produced substantial scholarship addressing Maori health priorities while developing methodological innovations applicable beyond Aotearoa. The approach has influenced indigenous research movements globally, with the CARE principles for indigenous data governance drawing on Maori experience. The Maori experience demonstrates that indigenous research frameworks can produce knowledge that is both locally relevant and globally valuable, and that decolonizing research requires fundamental shifts in power and knowledge production, not merely procedural modifications.

## Conclusion

Global health research encompasses diverse methodologies suited to different questions, from observational studies characterizing disease burden to implementation science bridging efficacy and real-world effectiveness. Implementation science provides frameworks for understanding how evidence-based interventions can be effectively delivered at scale, with particular attention to context, adaptation, and sustainability. Knowledge translation mechanisms move evidence into policy and practice, requiring sustained relationships between researchers and decision-makers, attention to political context, and capacity for evidence synthesis and interpretation.

Research ethics in global health addresses distinctive challenges arising from inequalities between researchers and participants, with growing emphasis on community engagement, benefit sharing, and decolonizing approaches that transform rather than merely reform research relationships. Research capacity strengthening requires investment at individual, institutional, and system levels, with sustainability dependent on local ownership and diversified support.

Systematic reviews provide the evidence base for guidelines and policy, with methodological advances including living reviews and evidence gap maps enhancing relevance and timeliness. Research priority setting processes balance scientific opportunity, health burden, equity considerations, and stakeholder perspectives. Open science principles promise to enhance transparency and access, though their application must be balanced with data sovereignty concerns and equity in knowledge production.

Throughout, global health research is evolving toward greater equity in research partnerships, centering community priorities and knowledge, and ensuring research serves the populations it studies.
